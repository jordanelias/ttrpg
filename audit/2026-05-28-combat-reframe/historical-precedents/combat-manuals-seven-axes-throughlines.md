# Historical Combat Manuals — Re-Cut on Seven Axes, with Throughlines

A re-organization of *Historical Combat Manuals: An Intensive Research Document (Revised and Expanded Edition)* along seven physical/tactical axes — reach, speed/useability, stance, initiation, footwork, grip, technique — rather than the document's own per-tradition and cognitive-mode organization. Everything here is grounded in the manual as read; no canon and no outside sources are invoked. Where the manual flags a claim as contested or self-corrects it, that is carried through.

**Read this first — the framing that governs the whole re-cut.** The seven axes are, almost exactly, the European fencing treatise's technical lens — what the manual calls the **four-axis lens** (weapon / technique / judgment / conditioning). The manual's central methodological argument is that this is *one comparative lens, not a universal structure*. The axes are most legible in the **temporal-spatial** (Italian rapier) and **tactile** (German longsword) traditions, which theorize reach, tempo, stance, and technique explicitly. They go progressively dim in traditions organized around other cognitive modes: the **intentional/consciousness-theoretical** (Japanese) reads intent rather than discrete technique; the **geometric** (Spanish) reorganizes all of them as positions on a constructed circle; the **cosmological** (Chinese internal, Indian) overlays them with wuxing/marma decision-frames; the **kinetic-rhythmic** (Filipino) dissolves discrete technique into continuous *flow*. So as you read each axis, watch which traditions light up and which go quiet — the dimming is itself a finding, not a documentation gap. This matters most if the end-use is a combat *system*, because a system is forced to commit to one lens.

## Axis 1 — Reach / Measure

**The convergent core.** The strongest independent convergence in the manual: combat distance is carved into discrete, *named* zones across traditions with no documented contact.

- **Italian rapier** — *misura*, in three zones: *misura larga* (wide), *stretta* (close/constrained), *fuori misura* (out of measure). The load-bearing granular point: at *misura larga* a ~110 cm rapier may never touch the opponent's blade before the decisive thrust — there is no bind to read — which is *why* the Italian register makes time, not contact, the primary information channel.
- **Japanese** — *maai*, layered: *tōma* (long, beyond cutting range), *uchima* (middle, where a step closes the cutting range), *chikama* (close, cutting range reached). Anchored on *issoku ittō no maai*, "one-step one-cut distance" — the distance from which a single committed step closes to a cutting strike. The manual marks this the direct counterpart of Italian *misura*.
- **Filipino FMA** — *largo* (only weapon tips reach), *medio* (weapon bodies reach, not hands/body), *corto* (hands and bodies within reach). More explicitly mapped to specific weapon lengths and stances than the European or Japanese schemes.
- **Spanish Destreza** — measure is the geometry itself: the *círculo* ("mystical circle") whose **diameter is the opponent's reach**. Opponents stand at the ends of a diameter; their swords meet at the center; attack angles are computed from the inscribed shapes. Reach is not a number to respect but the frame the whole encounter is constructed on.
- **English Silver** — *true place*: the distance from which you can strike the opponent and they cannot strike you. It is weapon-length-dependent — short weapon, short true place; long weapon, long true place.

**The long-vs-short debate lives here.** Two opposed positions the manual sets against each other:
- The **long-weapon orthodoxy** — Wu Shu names the spear (*qiang*) *zhi bing zhi wang*, "king of weapons," because reach plus the killing leverage of the point dominates any shorter weapon *provided the body mechanics are correct*. (Spear lengths: ~2.5 m *duan qiang* for skirmishing up to 5 m+ *changqiang* for formation.)
- **Silver's counter-claim** — the long thin rapier, despite its reach, does *not* let its user reach the true place against a shorter, broader weapon, because the longer weapon's geometry forces the rapier user to commit further forward to land a thrust than the shorter weapon's user must commit to land a cut. The manual explicitly flags Silver's *conclusion* as contested by modern HEMA reconstruction (his reasoning is preserved; whether it holds against a competently used rapier is a live question).

**Throughline (reach).** Discrete named measure-zones are the manual's single strongest "convergent independence" result — Italian, Japanese, and Filipino arrive at three-band schemes with no contact between them, which the manual treats as real signal rather than framework artifact. Reach is also where the weapon-length argument is fought, and the disagreement (spear-dominance vs Silver's true-place skepticism) is genuinely unresolved, not settled.

## Axis 2 — Speed / Useability

This is barely a stand-alone axis in the manual; it is a property folded into two places — *which body part initiates* (the biomechanical reading) and *weapon morphology*.

**The biomechanical treatment — Silver's *true times*.** Five named units, ordered by which body part starts the motion:
1. *Time of the hand* — hand alone, sword in line.
2. *Time of the hand and body* — hand first, then body follows.
3. *Time of the body and foot* — body and foot together.
4. *Time of the foot and body* — foot first, then body.
5. *Time of the foot* — foot alone.

The doctrine: the body's **fast parts must move before or independently of its slow parts**. A *true time* leads with a fast part; a *false time* leads with a slow part. Silver's polemical application: the Italian lunge initiates with the foot (the slowest part) while the hand follows, so the body commits before the strike and exposes the fencer to a counter-thrust before their own attack lands — a *false time*. The manual notes Silver's **error**: the Italian masters designed the lunge precisely to keep the hand-first sequence intact while extending the foot; Giganti's, Capo Ferro's, and Fabris's lunge mechanics address exactly the problem Silver names. The doctrine is sound as a general principle of efficient motion; the conclusion about the rapier is the mistake.

**Useability as a weapon-design outcome.** The manual repeatedly shows "useability" as the solution to a constraint:
- **Draw speed** — the *tachi*→*uchigatana*→*katana* shift. The *tachi* (75–80 cm, prominent curve) was worn edge-down, slung from the belt, drawn outward-and-downward — suited to a mounted warrior. The *uchigatana* was worn edge-up thrust through the belt, drawn horizontal-and-upward — faster, suited to a standing or seated warrior, and the mounting change drove the geometry toward less curve and more lateral balance, which in turn enabled the iaijutsu draw-and-strike.
- **Legal useability** — the *großes Messer*, mechanically a knife (riveted-scale grip, not the through-tang of a sword) but at sword length (70 cm+), let a burgher or peasant carry what was functionally a one-handed sword while staying inside sumptuary law that restricted *sword*-carrying but not *knife*-carrying.
- **Point control on a long blade** — the rapier's narrow blade plus a heavy compensating pommel pushes the center of mass well behind the cross, keeping a 100–120 cm blade controllable at the point despite the length and at a *lower* total weight (~1.2–1.4 kg) than the shorter, broader sidesword it evolved from.
- **A useability caution the manual flags** — the *Zweihänder* proper (blade >1.6 m, up to 3.2 kg) is a later-16th-c parade/formation weapon, **not** the *langes Schwert* of the Liechtenauer manuals (1.4–1.8 kg, hilt-forward balance for tip control); conflating them is a common error.
- **Cutting useability through edge design** — the macuahuitl's obsidian edges are ~12× finer than steel surgical knives but brittle, so the weapon uses a *modular* blade arrangement (chipped blades replaced individually) to accommodate the trade-off; the *kris*/*keris* wavy blade gives greater effective edge-length per unit blade-length and turns the pass into a draw-cut.
- **Training useability** — the *fukuro-shinai* (Kamiizumi's leather-wrapped bamboo) enabled full-contact paired practice without lethal injury and "transformed kenjutsu pedagogy"; the *Federschwert* (flexible, wide *Schilt* at the forte) and *dussack* (wooden saber) are the German parallels.

**Throughline (speed/useability).** Speed is never theorized as a free-standing variable; it decomposes into (a) initiation order (biomechanical mode, Silver) and (b) weapon morphology that answers a specific constraint — legal, contextual, or metallurgical. "Useability" in this manual almost always means *a design accommodating a constraint*, not raw quickness.

## Axis 3 — Stance / Guard

Two findings carry the axis: **convergent reduction** and **stance-as-relation**.

**Convergent reduction to a small, body-derived guard-set.**
- **German Vier Leger** — Ochs, Pflug, Vom Tag, Alber: high-thrusting, low-thrusting, raised-cutting, low-cutting. Two pairs on orthogonal axes.
- **Italian — Agrippa's deduction.** Marozzo carried eleven guards; Camillo Agrippa (1553) cuts them to **four** — *prima, seconda, terza, quarta* — derived not by enumeration but by deduction from the natural positions the arm holds during the draw of a sword, argued from first principles to *exhaust* the space. Agrippa's second, structural innovation is a stance posture: the sword held **forward, point threatening the line**, rather than withdrawn behind the body — which converts the sword from a cutting weapon held in reserve to a thrusting weapon held in line, and is the change that makes the long thrusting rapier viable. Carranza, Capo Ferro, Fabris, and de Sainct Didier all build on it.
- **Japanese kamae** — five basic across most ryū: *chūdan* (middle, point at the throat), *jōdan* (high, raised above the head), *gedan* (low, point toward the feet), *hassō* (vertical beside the head), *waki* (concealed behind the body to one side). Each ryū modifies — Itto-ryū prefers *chūdan*; Niten Ichi-ryū has its two-sword positions.
- **Indian Dhanurveda** — nine *asanas* (e.g., *samapada* parallel-feet, *vaishaka* wide-stance for archery, *swastika* with the feet 16 finger-widths apart and slightly raised).

**The transcendence caution.** Musashi names five attitudes (jōdan / chūdan / gedan / hidari-waki-gamae / migi-waki-gamae) in the Water scroll and then immediately argues **"there are stances and no stances"** — learn the named guards thoroughly, then transcend them. This is a direct warning against treating stance as a fixed menu, and it sits in tension with the European tendency to enumerate.

**Stance is relational, not static.** The mechanically important content is that a guard is a *node in an attack-defeat graph*, not just a posture:
- In the German system **each Meisterhau both attacks and defeats one specific guard** — the guard you hold determines which master-cut beats you.
- Constraint of the opponent's position is named in three traditions: Spanish *atajo*, Italian *stringere*, German *Versetzen* — all mean taking/closing the opponent's line.
- The pre-Liechtenauer Royal Armouries I.33 (c. 1320) names *obsessio* — "besieging" the opponent's guard, taking the line so the opponent cannot attack without first displacing the bind. Stance-as-constraint predates Liechtenauer by a century.

**Throughline (stance).** Guards converge on a small set (4–5) derived from natural body geometry, paired with an explicit doctrine of transcending the named forms (Musashi) — the same reduction-then-transcendence arc that runs through technique (Axis 7). And stance is *relational*: it is defined against the opponent's position, not as a property of the fighter alone, which links it to the relationality of measure (Axis 1) and initiative (Axis 4).

## Axis 4 — Approaches / Initiation / First Move

The richest axis, and the only one the manual gives a dedicated cross-tradition synthesis (§15.5).

**The layered-initiative schemes.**
- **German *Vor / Indes / Nach*** — initiative / moment-of-decision-on-contact / response. *Vor* is the initiative-holding action; *Nach* is the response; *Indes* is the instant in which initiative can switch on contact, decided "within the duration of a sustained note rather than a written word." *Fühlen* (feeling the bind) is its practical corollary. The engagement's phases are *Zufechten / Krieg / Abzug* (approach / war / withdrawal).
- **Japanese *sen*, three tiers** — *go-no-sen* (initiative taken *after* the opponent's attack begins; counter-initiative), *sen-no-sen* (taken *simultaneously*; interception), *sen-sen-no-sen* / *tai-no-sen* (taken *before*, by reading intent). The manual's specific claim: the Japanese scheme is **finer at the leading edge** — *sen-sen-no-sen*, the pre-commitment initiative, has no clean German or Italian equivalent. Adjacent concepts: *suki* (an opening — physical, tactical, or psychological); *kan no metsuke* vs *ken no metsuke* (the gaze of perception vs the gaze of sight, where perceiving intent beats seeing motion); *mutōdori* as the limiting case (an unarmed defender reads intent so completely the attacker cannot land a strike).
- **Italian *tempo*** — *tempo* (a window in which the opponent is committed and cannot answer a counter), *mezzo tempo* (the half-tempo intercept of the opponent's motion), *contratempo* (counter-time defeating an attack-into-preparation), plus *primo / secondo intenzione* and *invito* (an open invitation drawing the attack). Fabris adds the doctrine of striking from the moment the sword is drawn with no intervening preparation, and the systematic doctrine of *opposition* (every offensive action carries an embedded defense).
- **English Silver** — initiation is governed by the *true times* (Axis 2); *Brief Instructions* privileges the counter-stroke that exploits the opponent's committed action.

**The load-bearing result — the counterattack-prestige question (§15.5).** The first edition had claimed counterattack (striking into the opponent's commitment rather than initiating) is *universally* the apex offensive skill. The revision restricts this to a specific pattern:
- **Counterattack is the apex** in the **Italian** (contratempo, the lunge-into-the-attack), **Japanese Itto-ryū** (kiriotoshi), and **English** (Silver's counter-stroke under the true-place framework) traditions — which *share the dueling-centric individual-combat context* where the binary opponent's commitment-window is the primary tactical variable.
- **Counterattack is not the apex** in **Spanish** (apex = the geometrically-calculated safe action, which may or may not be a counter), **Filipino** (the *flow* treats attack and defense as continuous; *gunting*, "defang the snake," is one technique among many), **Chinese internal** (apex = the integrated *yi-qi-li* deployment, taking initiative or countering per the energetic reading), **Mamluk** (cavalry context; apex = tactical-operational competence), **Korean** (apex = standardized military deployment of the 24 disciplines), **Maori** (the *whana* thrust and *kahu* sweep are both privileged), and **Persian** (the *javānmardi* code emphasizes deterrence over any in-fight technique).
- **Counterattack is treated with explicit caution** in the **German** tradition (*Nachreisen*, counter-pursuit, is a named technique but *not* the apex — the apex is the integrated *Indes* recognition that decides whether to counter, bind, or displace), in the **Norse** Hávamál material, and in the **Aztec** macuahuitl tradition (the Flower War's capture-over-killing framing privileges controlled incapacitation over a maximally-lethal counter).

The conclusion the manual draws: "who should winning reward — the initiator or the counter-attacker?" has **no universal answer; it falls out of the combat context.**

**The technique that fuses initiation and technique:** Itto-ryū's *kiriotoshi* — a single descending cut, properly timed, that simultaneously parries the opponent's attack and strikes the centerline, because the practitioner's "living" cut displaces the opponent's off-line while continuing to its target.

**Throughline (initiation).** Layered initiative is a near-universal *structure* (three tiers recur), but the Japanese alone names the pre-commitment tier. The counterattack-prestige restriction is the manual's single most load-bearing methodological correction — and it generalizes (see consolidated Throughline T6): the "best first move" is context-dependent. Initiation is also the axis most dependent on *felt* timing, which is exactly what text preserves worst (Throughline T8).

## Axis 5 — Footwork

**Most theorized in the geometric channel.** The Spanish Destreza develops footwork furthest because, in that tradition, footwork *is* the geometry:
- Three named *compases* — *recto* (straight), *transverso* (across), *curvo* (curved) — plus the six *movimientos* (*natural*, *violento*, *reductivo*, *accidental*, *extraño*, *mixto*). All motion is read as travel along the circumference or the inscribed chords of the *círculo*. Pacheco's *Libro de las Grandezas de la Espada* (1600) carries 155 woodcut diagrams of footwork, blade positions, and the circle.

**The lunge — a careful historiographic correction.** The lunge (extending the rear leg to drive the body into a thrust without committing the rear foot) is commonly credited to Capo Ferro ("codified the lunge"). The manual reattributes the **first full pedagogical description to Giganti** (*Scola, overo Teatro*, 1606), four years before Capo Ferro's 1610 treatise, and notes it is implicit in Fabris's lunging postures of the same year. Capo Ferro's reputation as the lunge's codifier is identified as a **19th-c historiographic artifact** (Castle's *Schools and Masters of Fence*, 1885); the revision replaces Capo Ferro's primacy with a three-author equilibrium (Fabris codifies opposition, Giganti codifies the lunge and the pedagogical sequence, Capo Ferro codifies the vocabulary later scholarship canonized).

**Elsewhere.**
- **German** — Meyer's late-16th-c longsword footwork is *wider*, drifting toward the sportified Federfechten of the *Fechtschule*: larger cuts, wider steps, formatted for spectator prize-play rather than the judicial duel.
- **Japanese** — Katori Shintō-ryū's signature is precise, geometric footwork on a **triangular pattern**, with cuts initiated from a high *jōdan*.
- **Indian Silambam** — the **18 *chuvadus*** (footwork patterns) are the pedagogical core; each names a stepping pattern that integrates with corresponding staff movements.
- **Maori** — *rakanga waewae*, lateral-and-pivot footwork the manual compares directly to European fencing, emphasizing preserving balance while keeping the weapon in optimal position.
- A vivid training detail: the London Italian Rocco Bonetti required students to wear **leaden soles** to develop foot speed.

**Throughline (footwork).** Footwork is the *physical substrate of measure and tempo* — it is how you change zone (Axis 1) and *create* a tempo (Axis 4). That is why the one tradition that treats the engagement as constructed space (Spanish) is the one that theorizes footwork most: there, the footwork is the geometry. The lunge correction also models the manual's general historiographic care — it actively walks back a received attribution.

## Axis 6 — Grip

Grip is the most *under-named* of the seven as a stand-alone axis — the manual treats it almost entirely inside weapon sections rather than as its own category. The standout finding is that grip is where technique bleeds into weapon-identity.

- **German *Halbschwert* (half-sword)** — the longsword gripped one hand on the hilt, one **on the blade** (no more hazardous than gripping a tool by its working edge, with proper technique and gloves), turning the longsword into a short spear for targeted thrusts into the gaps of late-15th-c plate (eye-slits, armpits, groin, knee-backs). The manual explicitly calls this **"a weapon-mode shift that scrambles the categories"** — the four-axis lens reads it as "technique," but it is really re-purposing the weapon. The companion is the *Mordstreich* (striking with the pommel/hilt). Half-sword belongs to *Harnischfechten* (armored combat), one of the German tradition's three sub-systems alongside *Bloßfechten* (unarmored) and *Roßfechten* (mounted) — the tradition theorizes weapon-and-context as the primary variable.
- **Fiore's *abrazare*** — wrestling/grappling treated as the **foundation under all weapon work**: once you understand leverage, balance, and grip-breaking against an unarmed opponent, the same principles inform every weapon. Fiore treats all weapons as instances of a common geometry, with the grip-and-leverage of wrestling as the base.
- **The *großes Messer* grip** — a riveted-scale handle (knife construction) rather than the through-tang of a sword, at sword length; the legal-mechanical constraint shaped the grip-and-weapon together.
- **Off-hand grip / trapping** — the Spanish *daga de mano izquierda* with a wide guard and forward-curving quillons created traps to immobilize the opposing rapier blade while the right hand delivered the thrust.
- **Grip aids and carry** — the Polish Hussar szabla's *paluch* (thumb-ring) for cutting precision; the Mamluk lance held either underarm ("couched," European-jousting style) or **overarm ("free" position, the Mamluk preference for maneuverability)**; the chakram spun on the index finger or wrist before release.
- **Thibault** — distinctive grip and stance variations in his Dutch application of Destreza.
- **Maori taiaha** — the hand-grip **alternates along the shaft** to maximize reach and shift between thrusting (*arero* end) and striking (*rau* end) modes; the *patu* is spun on the wrist via a cord through the handle between strikes.

**Throughline (grip).** Grip is where technique and weapon-identity merge: half-sword is the cleanest case in the whole manual — a grip change converts a sword into a spear and breaks the technical taxonomy. The fact that grip is *distributed* across weapon sections rather than given its own treatment is itself a throughline about the source material and the European-treatise lens: grip is under-named precisely because it sits at the seam between "weapon" and "technique," the two axes the lens keeps separate.

## Axis 7 — Techniques

The manual's recurring claim about technique is that it is **generative, not enumerative**.

- **German — the generative core.** *Drei Wunder* (*hauen / stechen / schneiden* = cut / thrust / slice — all attacks reduce to these three). *Fünf Meisterhäue* (Zornhau / Krumphau / Zwerchhau / Schielhau / Scheitelhau — each both attacks and defeats one guard). *Winden* (winding — rotating around the bind to threaten new lines without breaking contact). *Stark / Schwach* (the strong/weak parts of the blade — the leverage variable that decides what is possible from any bind). A complete *Stück* (play) chains a Meisterhau + a Winden + a thrust or cut from the bind + a specific answer to each opponent counter. The manuals catalog ~100 named plays; the combinatorics produce thousands — **"the system is generative, not enumerative."**
- **I.33 (pre-Liechtenauer)** — its own vocabulary: *custodia* (7 guards), *obsessio* (besieging), *plaga* (7 attacks); it does not map cleanly onto Liechtenauer, which is itself the point — European fencing theory predates Liechtenauer by a century and was not one lineage.
- **Italian Bolognese cutting vocabulary** — *fendente* (descending), *mandritto* (right-side), *riverso* (back-handed), *squalembrato* (diagonal), *tondo* (horizontal), *montante* (rising); plus *cavazione* (disengagement around an opposition).
- **Japanese** — Itto-ryū *kiriotoshi* (the one cut), expressing *issatsu no tachi* / *itto no tachi*; the Yagyū *Satsujinken* / *Katsujinken* (killing sword / life-giving sword); Niten Ichi-ryū *nitō* / *ryōtō* (two swords); iaijutsu (draw and strike in one motion); the three shouts (*kiai*) and "crushing strategies" of Musashi's Fire scroll.
- **Chinese** — the spear's technical core is *lan* (parrying) / *na* (taking control of the opposing spear) / *zha* (the thrust); Xingyiquan's five attacking patterns map onto the five phases (*wuxing*); the *Quanjing Jieyao* gives 32 named fist forms; *fa jin* is issuing force.
- **Filipino FMA** — the *numerada* (12 named angles standard; 5/7/8-angle variants in specific lineages) structures the whole curriculum, with *sinawali* (paired-weapon weaving), *hubud-lubud* (close-range sensitivity), the *abecedario* (alphabet of techniques), and *gunting* ("defang the snake," a disarm). FMA technique is *flow* — continuous adaptation rather than discrete techniques against discrete attacks.
- **Spanish** — technique is the *execution of a geometrically-determined action*: the fencer who has mastered the circle computes the safe action and then performs it; the technique catalog is downstream of the geometry.
- **A cautionary case — Korean *gwonbeop*.** The unarmed-combat section of the *Muyedobotongji* was deliberately restructured so that paired training would produce **intentional stalemate** rather than a victor, in keeping with Neo-Confucian harmony principles — and the section **fell rapidly into disuse precisely because it did not transfer to combat.** The manual flags this as the rare documented case of a tradition openly sacrificing effectiveness to doctrine.

**Throughline (technique).** Technique is generative — a small primitive set plus rich combinatorics — and the named catalogs (Meisterhäue, numerada, the Bolognese cuts) are *surface expressions* of that, not the system itself. This mirrors the stance arc (Axis 3): converge on primitives, then transcend the named forms. Technique also bends back into the cognitive mode that selects it (kiriotoshi embodies the intentional/consciousness mode; the numerada embodies the enumerative mode; Destreza technique embodies the geometric mode), and the Korean case is the standing warning that doctrine can corrupt the technique it shapes.

## Consolidated Throughlines

The threads that run *across* the axes and across traditions. Each is grounded in the manual's own synthesis (Parts II–III) re-pointed at the seven axes.

**T1 — The seven axes *are* the European-treatise lens.** Reach / speed / stance / initiation / footwork / grip / technique is essentially the manual's four-axis lens (weapon / technique / judgment / conditioning) decomposed. It is a lens, not a universal. It reads brightly in the temporal-spatial (Italian) and tactile (German) traditions, and dims as you move to traditions organized around intent, geometry, cosmology, or rhythm — which reorganize the same physical facts under a different perceptual frame rather than ignoring them. *The dimming is data.* (Manual §10, §15.1.)

**T2 — Each axis is *read* through a cognitive mode; the seven axes are mostly three modes' output.** The manual's seven canonical cognitive modes (tactile, temporal-spatial, intentional, geometric, consciousness-theoretical, cosmological, kinetic-rhythmic) are *how* the opponent is perceived. Mapped to the axes: reach + footwork + initiation are read in the **temporal-spatial** mode; grip-feel and the bind in the **tactile** mode; speed in the (provisional) **biomechanical** mode; stance-as-counter straddles **tactile** and **geometric**; technique-selection runs through whichever mode is primary in the tradition. The upshot: the seven physical axes are largely the *output* of the temporal-spatial + tactile + biomechanical modes — and the other four modes (intentional, consciousness, cosmological, kinetic-rhythmic) are different *readings* of the same physical material. (Manual §11.)

**T3 — Convergent independence (the strongest "universal" candidates).** Three things recur independently across traditions with no documented contact: (a) **measure as discrete named zones** (misura / maai / largo-medio-corto), (b) **initiative as a layered scheme** (Vor-Indes-Nach / the sen tiers / Silver's true times), and (c) **the bind/contact as an information channel** (German *Fühlen* / Italian *sentimento del ferro* / Chinese *ting jin* / Wing Chun *chi sao* / Filipino *hubud-lubud*). The manual treats this convergence as real signal — these are near-universal primitives, the strongest candidates for "load-bearing in any combat resolution." (Manual §14.1, §14.2, §15.2.)

**T4 — Reduction-then-transcendence.** Stance and technique trace the same arc: converge on a small primitive set derived from natural body geometry (Agrippa's 4 guards, the Vier Leger, the 5 kamae; the Drei Wunder + 5 Meisterhäue), then *transcend the named forms* (Musashi's "stances and no stances"; "generative, not enumerative"). The named catalog is scaffolding, not the system. (Manual §1.1.2, §1.2.4, §2.8.)

**T5 — Relationality.** Measure, stance, and initiation are *relational*, not absolute. Measure is defined by the opponent's reach (the Spanish circle's diameter literally is the opponent's reach). Stance is a node in an attack-defeat graph (each Meisterhau defeats one guard; *atajo* / *stringere* / *obsessio* = taking the opponent's line). Initiative is defined relative to the opponent's commitment-window (the sen tiers; tempo / mezzo tempo / contratempo). None is a property of the fighter alone — each is a property of the *pairing*. (Manual §1.1.2, §1.3.6, §2.9.)

**T6 — Context determines the apex (the §15.5 result, generalized).** What counts as the "best" first move, technique, or skill-stack apex falls out of the *combat context*. Dueling-centric contexts (Italian / Japanese / English) make **counterattack** the apex because the binary opponent's commitment-window is the dominant variable. Multi-opponent, cavalry, cosmological, and military-formation contexts (Filipino / Mamluk / Chinese / Korean) produce *different* apexes — geometric calculation, tactical-operational competence, energetic deployment, standardized formation use. This is the manual's load-bearing correction and the one most relevant to designing a combat *system*: the system's context decides which axis-values it should reward. (Manual §12, §15.3, §15.5.)

**T7 — Weapon constrains the option-set; the cognitive mode selects from it.** The *same* weapon-family (the curved single-edged sabre across the Polish *szabla*, Mamluk-Ottoman *shamshir*/*kilij*, Indian *talwar*, Japanese *katana*) is integrated into *different* cognitive frameworks. Reach and speed are weapon *properties*; the weapon does not determine the cognitive mode but constrains the technique-options the mode then organizes. Weapon → option-set; mode → selection rule. (Manual §14.9.)

**T8 — Transmission decides which axes survive.** Text preserves taxonomy, named concepts, and discrete technique; lineage preserves timing, pressure-calibration, the moment-to-commit, and intent-reading. So the axes split by how well the record carries them: **reach-zones, guard-names, and technique-catalogs are textually well-preserved**, while **the felt timing of initiation (Axis 4) and the calibration of grip-pressure (Axis 6) are exactly what text preserves worst and lineage preserves best.** European HEMA, working text-upward, faces a structural ceiling on recovering the latter — "the texts were never meant to carry the load that reconstruction asks them to carry." Practical reading: the "hard" axis (initiation-feel) is the under-documented one, in this manual and in the historical record generally. (Manual methodology §; §14.10.)

**T9 — The manual's own self-correction (read it before you mine it).** Pass 3 flags as high-severity that the "European tradition is the lethality-default, cosmology-free outlier" claims were *overstated*, and the remediation pass walks them back — the accurate framing is that European fencing integrates religious-cosmological content at the *ethical-and-institutional* level (and runs *parallel* lethal-duel and non-lethal-sport tracks) rather than lacking it. So weight the claims: **the T3 convergences and the §15.5 counterattack restriction are the load-bearing, well-supported results**; the "European outlier" framing is qualified; and the cognitive-modes catalog is explicitly *provisional* (the energetic / enumerative / biomechanical modes may collapse into others on further analysis). (Manual Pass 3, remediation pass, §13.1, §13.5, §16.4.)

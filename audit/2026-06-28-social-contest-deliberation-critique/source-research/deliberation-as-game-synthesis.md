## 0. Orientation: the several senses of "is a game"

The claim that a trial, a debate, or a negotiation *is a kind of game* can be advanced at two very different levels of seriousness, and the literature divides accordingly.

- **The loose (metaphorical) sense.** We *talk* about these activities in the vocabulary of contest — positions are attacked and defended, a point is scored, a case is won. This is the province of conceptual-metaphor theory (Lakoff & Johnson and their critics).
- **The strict (constitutive or formal) sense.** These activities *satisfy a definition of a game*, or are reconstructed as formal games with players, legal moves, a commitment record, and a win condition. This is the province of the philosophy of games (Suits, Huizinga), the theory of constitutive rules (Searle, after Rawls), formal dialectic and dialogical logic (Hamblin, Lorenzen–Lorenz, Hintikka), argumentation theory (Barth–Krabbe, Walton–Krabbe, van Eemeren–Grootendorst), the game theory of bargaining (Nash, Schelling, Raiffa), and the sociology of strategic interaction (Goffman).

**A caution that governs everything below: "game" is not one concept but at least four, and they are not coextensive.** Much confusion in the popular version of this question comes from sliding between them, so they are separated here at the outset:

1. **The Wittgensteinian sense** — a rule-governed practice, a *Sprachspiel*, with no implication of contest. Speaking, promising, and arguing are "games" in this sense merely by being constituted in rule-bound use.
2. **The Suitsian sense** — the voluntary taking-up of unnecessary obstacles under a lusory attitude. This adds *inefficiency embraced for its own sake* but, again, not necessarily a winner.
3. **The agonistic / sporting sense** (Huizinga, Caillois) — a *contest*: a rule-equalised trial of strength or skill with a victor, descended from combat. This is the sense the everyday metaphor reaches for.
4. **The game-theoretic sense** (Nash, Schelling) — any situation of strategic interdependence among rational agents with payoffs. Crucially, a game-theoretic "game" need carry *none* of the other three marks: it need not be rule-constituted (it models natural conflicts, animal or economic, with no constitutive rules at all), need not be voluntary, and is frequently *not* zero-sum.

These four overlap in paradigm cases (chess is all four at once) but come apart at the edges, and the deliberative practices in question sit at exactly those edges. A negotiation is a game in sense 4 and often the *opposite* of a game in sense 3 (Part IV). A compelled trial is a game in sense 1 but strains sense 2 (Part I.A, VI). Keeping the four apart is what lets the synthesis say *which* sense holds *where* rather than trading on the ambiguity.

The strongest scholarship lives in the strict sense. What follows maps it, then distils the precise correspondences (Part V) and the points where the correspondence fails (Part VI) — because an honest answer to "how do these things correspond to games" must also state where they decisively do not.

One strand comes closest to running through the whole body of work, and it is worth stating at the outset — with a caveat the strand itself demands. **The common thread is constitution by rules: a set of rules that does not merely regulate conduct but *creates* a form of conduct that could not exist without them, which is what senses 1, 2, and 3 share with chess and football.** A cross-examination, a concession, a sustained objection, a valid inference, a binding offer: none is a natural act; each exists only inside a system of rules that confers it, in Searle's formula, the status of counting as that move. *The caveat:* this is the strongest *common strand*, not a shared essence. Wittgenstein's own lesson about the word "game" (*Investigations* §66–67) is that it names a family held together by overlapping resemblances with no single feature common to all — and Suits's whole project is the contrary attempt to supply that missing essence. To call constitution-by-rules "the" thing these practices share with games is therefore to take a side in a live dispute, and to do so for three of the four senses only: the game-theoretic sense (4) is joined to the others not by constitution but by a *different* bridge — strategic interdependence resolved before a third party — and must be argued for separately (Part IV). With that qualification, constitution-by-rules is the load-bearing correspondence, and most of the others are its consequences.

## Part I — What licenses the comparison: the constitutive accounts

### A. Bernard Suits — the lusory structure of any game

Suits (*The Grasshopper: Games, Life and Utopia*, 1978) gives the most influential analytic definition of game-playing, and it is the natural instrument for testing whether a deliberative practice *is* a game. To play a game, on his account, is to attempt to reach a **prelusory goal** (a state of affairs describable independently of the game) using only **lusory means** (those the rules permit), under **constitutive rules** that forbid more efficient means in favour of less efficient ones, the whole accepted under a **lusory attitude** — the rules embraced *just because* they make the activity possible. His portable summary: game-playing is "the voluntary attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles."

The diagnostic power for our subject is considerable, and it cuts both ways:

- **Where it fits.** The rules of evidence, of procedure, of debate format, of order in a deliberative assembly are precisely *unnecessary obstacles* in Suits's technical sense. The exclusionary rule, which bars probative but improperly obtained evidence, makes the prelusory goal (an accurate verdict) *harder* to reach than it need be — and is accepted because it constitutes the kind of trial we mean to hold. The advocate who honours the rule rather than smuggling the evidence in exhibits the lusory attitude exactly.
- **Where it strains.** Suits's two anchoring conditions — that the attempt be *voluntary* and the obstacles accepted *for the sake of the activity* — fit a willing debater far better than an unwilling defendant. A criminal defendant does not voluntarily take up the obstacles of trial for the love of the contest; the lusory attitude is the prosecutor's and the system's, not the accused's. The sharpest way to put the difficulty is not that the defendant is a reluctant player but that he may be no player at all: on the adversarial picture it is *counsel* who are the players — they chose the profession and embrace its obstacles — while the person whose liberty is at stake stands closer to the *stake itself*, the thing contested, than to a contestant. That a system can place the human being at issue in the position of the ball rather than the batsman is a genuine indictment of the game-frame, not a decoration of it. This is the first crack in the law-as-game equation, and it recurs (Part VI).

Suits is careful that game-playing is defined by *inefficiency*, not by challenge or difficulty — a point routinely misreported. This matters here because it locates the essence of the comparison in the deliberate constraint, not in the mere fact that arguing or litigating is hard.

### B. John Searle and John Rawls — constitutive versus regulative rules

The conceptual bridge between games and *speech* — and hence between games and every verbal form the question names — is the distinction between two kinds of rule, drawn by Rawls ("Two Concepts of Rules," 1955) and developed by Searle (*Speech Acts*, 1969).

- **Regulative rules** govern conduct that exists independently of them (etiquette regulates dining, which would happen anyway).
- **Constitutive rules** *create* the conduct they govern: "the rules of football or chess … do not merely regulate playing football or chess, but as it were they create the very possibility of playing such games" (Searle 1969: 33). Their canonical form is **"X counts as Y in context C."**

Searle's central thesis is that *speaking a language is itself a rule-governed form of behaviour* of this constituted kind. A promise, an assertion, a verdict, a concession, a ruling "sustained" — each is an institutional fact brought into being by constitutive rules, exactly as a checkmate or a touchdown is. This is why speech acts and games are structurally the same sort of thing: both are constituted, not merely regulated.

**The fault line.** Searle himself does *not* treat law wholesale as constitutive: he classes the criminal law as *regulative*, since it does not exist in order to create the possibility of committing crimes (Searle 1969: 41; cf. Warnock). This is an important limit. The *substantive* law (don't murder) is regulative; it is the *procedural and institutional* apparatus (what counts as evidence, as standing, as a verdict, as an admissible objection) that is constitutive and therefore game-like. The correct claim is not "a trial is a game" tout court but "the procedural enactment of a trial is constituted by rules in the way a game is."

### C. Johan Huizinga — play as the agonistic substrate of law, war, and eloquence

Huizinga's *Homo Ludens* (1938) is the seminal historical argument that the deliberative and adjudicative institutions of culture *descend from* play and retain its form. He treats play as **free, set apart in its own place and time, bounded by its own rules, and characteristically agonistic** (contest-shaped). Three of his chapters are directly on point:

- **"Play and Law."** Huizinga observes that judicial activity betrays its play-ancestry by the way it is sealed off — special place (the court), special time (the trial), special costume and ritual. Early adjudication was not investigation but *contest*: trial by ordeal and trial by battle, both forms of agon and therefore of play. Modern justice, on his account, descends from arguing-contests that only gradually turned toward abstract truth. As one jurisprudential commentator distils it, for Huizinga the lawsuit can be seen as a game of chance, a contest, or a verbal battle.
- **"Play and War."** War, too, when bound by rules, conventions, and the mutual recognition of adversaries, has the structure of a contest — the regulated agon that channels rather than abolishes conflict. This is the conceptual root of the "ritualized combat" reading of the trial (Part III).
- **"Play-Forms in Philosophy."** The sophists' displays, the riddle-contest, the public disputation: reasoned debate as a descendant of the agonistic riddle and the wisdom-contest.

Huizinga's most quotable observation for the rhetoric/oratory branch: in classical Athens, juristic eloquence was still essentially "a contest in rhetorical dexterity," the legal oration barely distinguishable from the ritual reviling-match. The forensic speech *is* an agon. *(Read via the full chapter inventory and an extensively-quoting chapter review; the primary text is at the Internet Archive — see ledger.)*

### D. Roger Caillois — the taxonomy of play, and where adjudication sits in it

Caillois (*Les jeux et les hommes*, 1958; *Man, Play and Games*, 1961) refined Huizinga by sorting games into four families: **agôn** (regulated competition), **alea** (chance), **mimicry** (simulation/role-play), and **ilinx** (vertigo). The scheme is unexpectedly diagnostic for adjudication:

- A modern adversarial **trial or debate** is paradigmatic *agôn* — a rule-equalised contest in which artificial equality is created so that merit (of argument, of evidence) decides.
- **Trial by ordeal or lot** is *alea* — submission of the verdict to chance (or to the divine through chance).
- **Advocacy and the assumption of forensic roles** carries an element of *mimicry* — the advocate performs a part within the bounded arena.

That a single adjudicative tradition has historically drawn on three of Caillois's four families is itself evidence of how deep the play-correspondence runs.

### E. Ludwig Wittgenstein — the language-game

The widest frame is Wittgenstein's *Sprachspiel* (*Philosophical Investigations*, 1953): linguistic meaning is constituted by the rule-governed practices in which words are used, on the model of games. This underwrites the whole enterprise — it is *why* a verbal practice can be analysed as a game at all — and it is explicitly the philosophical taproot of dialogical logic's "meaning as use" (Part II.B). **Caveat, important for honesty:** Wittgenstein's language-games are *not* essentially competitive. His point was about rule-following and forms of life, not contest. So the Wittgensteinian sense supports "deliberation is a rule-constituted game" but is silent on, even mildly resistant to, "deliberation is a *contest*." The competitive reading must come from Huizinga/Caillois, not from him.

### F. Erving Goffman — strategic interaction and the membrane of the encounter

Goffman supplies the sociological articulation. In *Strategic Interaction* (1969) he isolates "strategic interaction": gamelike episodes in which each party's outcome depends on the others' moves, all parties know this, and each exploits the awareness for advantage. His **expression games** — sparring to assess the worth of information that another gives off, wittingly or not — are a precise model of cross-examination, deposition, and the testing of a negotiating counterpart. In *Encounters* (1961), the essay "Fun in Games" treats the game as the model case of a *focused encounter*: it is held together by **rules of irrelevance** (what shall be attended to and what disattended) and enclosed by an **interaction membrane** separating it from the surrounding world. The courtroom and the negotiating table are such membranes: inside them, ordinary norms are suspended and transformed (relentless adversarial questioning, sanctioned within the membrane, would be assault on civility outside it).

This is the sociological version of Huizinga's bounded play-space. Huizinga's own term for it — the play-ground listed among "the arena, the card-table, the magic circle, the temple, the stage" — was the **"magic circle,"** a phrase later foregrounded and made a term of art by Salen & Zimmerman (*Rules of Play*, 2003): a space entered voluntarily, set apart in locality and duration, governed by rules that differ from ordinary life. *(The magic-circle concept is itself contested in game studies for overstating the boundary's firmness — Walz, Consalvo — a caveat carried to Part VI.)*

### G. The classical rhetorical agon — oratory as a judged contest of skill

The brief names *speeches*, and the oldest body of theory bearing on them treats public oratory as competitive from the ground up. Aristotle's *Rhetoric* (c. 350 BCE) divides oratory into three genera, and — the point usually missed — grounds the division in three kinds of *judge*: forensic (*genos dikanikon*) before a juror of past acts, deliberative (*sumbouleutikon*) before a juror of future policy, and epideictic (*epideiktikon*) before a spectator (*theōros*) who, in Aristotle's own account, "judges the ability of the speaker" (*Rhetoric* 1358a36–b8). Two of the three are precisely the adjudicated venues this synthesis already treats — the law court and the deliberative assembly — and the third, the display speech, makes the contest explicit: its object *is* the spectator's verdict on the orator's skill. The three genera are thus three judged contests sorted by what their judge decides.

The agonistic character is not a later imposition. Epideictic descended from the **sophistic displays** of the fifth century — Gorgias and his successors competing in set-piece praise and blame — and the surrounding culture staged debate as literal competition: the tragic *agōn* (the paired adversarial speeches in Euripides) was performed within the dramatic contest of the festival, itself judged and ranked. Classical scholarship has put the general claim plainly: in fifth- and fourth-century Greece "public speech was virtually always conceived of as a fundamentally competitive enterprise" (Avinger), and that very competitiveness was felt as an ethical problem — the line between *good eris* and the *eristikē* that "seeks victory without regard for truth." That distinction is the ancient form of the cooperative/competitive fault line drawn at Part VI: rhetoric, like dialectic, is a contest whose worst form is the one that pursues winning at the expense of the truth it is supposed to serve. The branch also closes the loop with Huizinga (I.C), for whom Athenian juristic eloquence was still "a contest in rhetorical dexterity": the forensic speech *is* an agon, and the theory of rhetoric has said so since its founding text.

## Part II — Argument, debate, and dialectic as formal games

This is the deepest and most rigorous body of correspondence, because here the activities are not merely *likened* to games but *reconstructed as* them, with explicit rules, moves, and winning conditions. The lineage is continuous from antiquity to contemporary logic.

### A. The ancient and medieval lineage: dialectic as a regulated bout

Reasoned disputation was organised as a rule-governed, two-role contest long before it was a metaphor for one.

- **Socratic elenchus and Aristotle's dialectic.** Aristotle's *Topics* and *Sophistical Refutations* presuppose a disputational setting that contemporary scholars (Castelnérac & Marion; Dutilh Novaes) reconstruct explicitly as **"games of questions and answers between two players":** an **Answerer**, who commits at the outset to a thesis, and a **Questioner**, who tries, through a chain of yes/no questions, to extract concessions that force the contradictory of that thesis. There is a defined initial commitment, a strict move-vocabulary, a win condition (driving the Answerer into contradiction), and — frequently — an audience serving as judge. It is, structurally, a competitive game.
- **Medieval *obligationes*.** The scholastic disputation *de obligationibus* formalised this further into rounds of *positio*, *oppositio*, and *responsio*: the Opponent advances propositions; the Respondent must grant, deny, or doubt each while maintaining dynamic logical consistency against an interlocutor actively trying to trip him into inconsistency (SEP, *Medieval Theories of Obligationes*). These disputations are the direct ancestor of the modern academic **thesis defence** — itself a survival of debate-as-rule-governed-bout into the present.

The significance is that the formal-game treatment of argument is not a twentieth-century imposition; it recovers what dialectic *was*.

### B. Formal dialectic and dialogical logic: argument and even *logic itself* as a game

In the twentieth century this lineage was made mathematically exact, to the point where logical validity itself is defined game-theoretically.

- **Charles Hamblin — formal dialectic.** In *Fallacies* (1970), Hamblin built formal dialogue systems ("Hamblin systems") in which a dialogue is a sequence of regulated locutions, and each participant carries a **commitment store**: the running set of propositions to which their moves commit them, updated as the dialogue proceeds. This is, in the most literal sense, a *game state* — a ledger of position that legal moves alter. It is the device that makes "tracking who has conceded what" a formal object.

- **Paul Lorenzen and Kuno Lorenz — dialogical logic.** Here the correspondence reaches bedrock. A logical formula is treated as the subject of a **dialogue game** between a **Proponent (P)**, who asserts the thesis, and an **Opponent (O)**, who attacks it. Two kinds of rule govern play (read in full from the Stanford Encyclopedia's primary exposition):
  - **Particle rules** (*Partikelregeln*) — the *local* rules. They fix, for each logical connective, the legal challenges and defences. (For a conjunction, the *challenger* chooses which conjunct must be defended; for a disjunction, the *defender* chooses. The right of choice — who picks — is what distinguishes the connectives' meanings.) These rules *constitute the meaning* of the connectives through use, an explicitly Wittgensteinian move.
  - **Structural rules** (*Rahmenregeln*) — the *global* rules: how a play begins (P states the thesis), turn order, repetition ranks that guarantee finiteness, and — decisively — the **Formal Rule**, under which P may assert an atomic proposition only if O has already conceded it. (The Proponent cannot import "facts" of his own; he may build only on what the Opponent has granted — a structural analogue of burden and standing.)
  - The payoff is a sharp distinction between the **play level** (a single match, won or lost in finitely many moves) and the **strategy level**: a formula is **valid if and only if the Proponent has a winning strategy** — a way to win *every* play whatever the Opponent does. Lorenz's own definition is unembarrassed about the analogy: the dialogue game is "a finitary open two-person zero-sum game." Logical demonstration is recast as the possession of a winning strategy.

- **Jaakko Hintikka — game-theoretical semantics (GTS).** The parallel tradition does for *truth in a model* what dialogical logic does for *validity*. A semantic game pits a **Verifier** (Eloïse) against a **Falsifier** (Abelard); the connectives and quantifiers assign the moves (the Verifier chooses for disjunctions and existentials, the Falsifier for conjunctions and universals; negation swaps the roles). A sentence is **true in a model iff the Verifier has a winning strategy** in its game. (The Verifier's winning strategy is, technically, the Skolem function — strategy and witness coincide.) Truth itself becomes the existence of a winning strategy.

These two results are the strongest possible form of the thesis the question asks about: not that argument *resembles* a game, but that the core semantic notions on which all argument rests — *valid* and *true* — are definable as *having a winning strategy in a game*.

- **Else Barth and Erik Krabbe — *From Axiom to Dialogue* (1982).** This tradition rejoined formal dialogue games to the *informal* logic of real argument (Toulmin; Perelman & Olbrechts-Tyteca), supplying the bridge from the logician's dialogue game to the rhetorician's.

### C. Argumentation theory: real debate as a typed, rule-governed, commitment-tracking game

- **Douglas Walton and Erik Krabbe — *Commitment in Dialogue* (1995).** The most-used apparatus in the field classifies argumentative exchange into **six dialogue types**, each defined by its initial situation, the participants' goals, and the goal of the exchange: **persuasion, inquiry, information-seeking, negotiation, deliberation,** and **eristic** (the quarrel). Each type is a different game with different rules:
  - *Locution rules* fix the permitted utterances (assert, question, challenge, concede, justify);
  - each party carries a **commitment store** (Hamblin's device);
  - a **dialectical shift** occurs when the exchange changes type mid-stream — a productive shift when noticed, a source of fallacy and bad faith when not (a negotiating move smuggled into what was supposed to be an inquiry).
  - Real cases are usually **mixed types**: a political debate, on Walton & Krabbe's own example, is at once information-seeking, deliberation, persuasion, negotiation, and eristic. This typed-game model is the most direct theoretical answer to "how do debates, negotiations, and deliberations relate as kinds of game": they are *distinct games in one family*, sharing the move-and-commitment structure but differing in goal and initial situation.

- **Frans van Eemeren and Rob Grootendorst — pragma-dialectics.** The Amsterdam school models rational argument as a **critical discussion** with **four stages** (confrontation, opening, argumentation, concluding) and a **code of conduct of ten rules** that reasonable discussants must observe. The signature move: **a fallacy is defined as the violation of one of these rules** — an illegitimate move that "derails" the resolution of the dispute. The rules function as *constitutive rules of a critical discussion* in Searle's sense — a discussant who violates them is not playing the critical-discussion game badly but, at the limit, not playing it at all — and the tradition openly descends from Lorenzen's and Barth–Krabbe's formal dialectic. Fallacies are *fouls*: rule-breaking moves penalised within the game.

  **A crucial qualification lives here, and it is one of the most important findings in this whole survey.** Pragma-dialectics embraces the game-*as-rules* model wholeheartedly while explicitly *rejecting* the game-*as-contest* model. The goal of a critical discussion is the resolution of a difference of opinion *on the merits*, not victory; "trying to win at all costs" is precisely the disposition that *produces* fallacies. So the most rigorous theory of real argument treats argument as a rule-constituted game whose health depends on the participants *not* treating it as a contest to be won. (See Part VI: this is the cooperative/competitive fault line, and it is structural, not incidental.)

### D. Catarina Dutilh Novaes — deduction's dialogical roots

A major recent synthesis (*The Dialogical Roots of Deduction*, 2021) argues that deductive reasoning *as such* — even the solitary proof on the page — is a sublimated dialogue game with a **"built-in opponent."** Deduction internalises two roles, a **Prover** who advances steps and a **Skeptic** who must grant each as indefeasibly compelling; the felt obligatoriness of valid inference is the trace of the Skeptic's inability to refuse. On this account the game is not a metaphor laid over reasoning from outside; it is reasoning's historical and structural origin (descending from exactly the Greek and medieval disputational games of II.A). This both deepens the correspondence and supplies its developmental explanation.

### E. The conceptual-metaphor strand: ARGUMENT IS WAR — or a game?

At the looser, cognitive-linguistic level, Lakoff & Johnson (*Metaphors We Live By*, 1980) made famous the structural metaphor **ARGUMENT IS WAR**: we take *positions*, *attack* and *defend* them, *win* or *lose*, *gain ground*, deploy *strategy*. They imagined, as a contrast case, a culture in which **ARGUMENT IS DANCE** — participants as performers seeking a balanced, pleasing joint performance rather than a victory — to show how deeply the martial framing shapes Anglophone practice.

The directly relevant rejoinder is David Ritchie's "ARGUMENT IS WAR — Or Is It a Game of Chess?" (*Metaphor and Symbol*, 2003), which argues that war is not the primary source domain at all: contentious verbal interaction occupies a *continuum* from discussion through **contest/sport** to all-out war, and that the very words taken as martial — *defend, position, manoeuvre, strategy* — are equally at home in athletics and chess. The game/sport framing is thus a live competitor to the war framing, not a derivative of it.

**Honesty about this strand:** conceptual-metaphor theory is empirically contested. Critics (Vervaeke & Kennedy 1996; McGlone & Glucksberg) argue that speakers identify arguments as arguments without metaphorical transfer and that war-talk reflects entrenched convention rather than any lived experience of combat. (Raymond Gibbs, sometimes miscast as a critic, is in fact the strand's leading *defender*: his *Metaphor Wars* (2017) marshals the experimental case for conceptual metaphor and answers the sceptics — he is the cognitive linguist who takes their objections most seriously, not one who shares them.) So this branch establishes that the game/contest *framing* is culturally available and arguably more apt than the war framing — but it does not, and cannot, establish the strong constitutive claims of Parts I–II. It is the weakest-grounded of the branches and should be cited as suggestive, not probative.

### F. Competitive debate — the contest made literal, with its own rulebook

Where the preceding sections reconstruct argument *as* a game in theory, organised competitive debate simply *is* one in institutional fact, and it is the most direct answer the brief's word "debate" admits. Modern formats — Policy, Lincoln–Douglas, Public Forum, British Parliamentary ("Worlds style"), World Schools, parliamentary — are governed by published rulebooks and run as ranked tournaments with the full apparatus of a refereed sport: fixed turn-order and timed speeches; regulated interruptions (Points of Information, Order, and Clarification, several of them non-declinable and taken with the clock stopped); numerical **speaker points** (a 50–100 band in BP, 1–30 in American parliamentary); **team ranks** (in BP four teams placed first through fourth, so allies on the same side still compete); cumulative scores that determine "the break" to elimination rounds; and an adversarial role-structure borrowed wholesale from a real deliberative institution — Government/Proposition against Opposition, the judge titled "Speaker of the House." The format's lineage runs back to nineteenth-century English parliamentary procedure: formalised debate descending, quite literally, from the assembly.

Two features make this more than a loose parallel. First, the **adjudicator** appears here in its purest institutional form: the *tabula rasa* paradigm instructs the judge to decide solely on the arguments made in the round, setting aside private knowledge and bias — the impartial rule-applier of Part III.D reduced to a blank slate. Second, and decisively for this synthesis, the community has produced an *explicit in-house doctrine that debate is a game*: the recognised **"games paradigm"** of judging holds that debate is a game in which any argument forming a coherent syllogism is "fair play," and policy debate is described by its own practitioners as "more of a game than any other format — tech first." The agonistic reading is not imposed on competitive debate from outside; it is one of the named theories debaters use to decide who won. *(Tier note: this section rests on practitioners' rulebooks and judging-paradigm documents — primary artifacts of the practice — rather than on peer-reviewed scholarship; cited as evidence of the institution, not of a contested empirical claim.)*

## Part III — Trial, appeal, and adjudication as sporting contest

Where Part II treats *argument* as a formal game, this part treats *the trial itself* as a contest — and here the literature is more divided, because to call a trial a sport is, for many of its authors, an *indictment*.

### A. Roscoe Pound and the "sporting theory of justice"

The canonical text is Roscoe Pound's address to the American Bar Association, "The Causes of Popular Dissatisfaction with the Administration of Justice" (1906). Pound diagnosed the American adversary trial as governed by a **"sporting theory of justice"** — what he attributed to Wigmore as the "instinct of giving the game fair play" — and treated this not as a virtue but as an atavism: a survival, he argued, of the days when a lawsuit was a fight between two clans, with change of venue merely the modern transfer of the battlefield to the forum. Its symptoms are gamesmanship rewarded over truth: new trials granted because a party "has lost the chance to play another inning in the game of justice," vested rights manufactured in mere errors of procedure — procedure being, in his cutting phrase, "the mere etiquette of justice." The danger he named, echoed by the bench (e.g., Learned Hand's worry that in seeing all the rules of the game observed "we have to an alarming degree lost sight of the real purpose of the investigation"), is that the contest-frame *displaces* the truth-seeking purpose it was meant to serve.

Two things must be held together about Pound. First, he is the strongest single source for the *descriptive* correspondence — the adversary trial **is** structured as a regulated sporting contest, complete with the appeal as "another inning." Second, his stance is *critical*: the sporting structure is, for him, a defect peculiar to Anglo-American law and largely curbed elsewhere. The trial-as-sport thesis and the worry that the thesis describes a corruption are, in Pound, the same observation. (His critique seeded a century of reform, including the mediation/ADR movement.) *(Primary passages recovered verbatim across multiple legal-academic sources; the address is variously paginated — the ABA Report 1906 is canonical.)*

### B. Historical descent: from trial by battle to sublimated verbal combat

Pound's "fight between two clans" is not rhetorical flourish but legal history, and it converges with Huizinga's "Play and War." The adversary trial is widely read as the *ritualised, sublimated descendant of physical combat* — trial by battle and trial by ordeal replaced by a regulated verbal contest in which advocates, not champions, do battle, and the violence is wholly displaced into procedure. On this reading the trial does for private conflict what Huizinga's rule-bound war does for collective conflict: it does not abolish the agon but civilises it by binding it in rules and submitting it to an arbiter. The correspondence to sport is then not analogy but lineage — combat and sport share an origin, and the trial sits on that same branch.

### C. H. L. A. Hart and the "games analogy" in jurisprudence

Hart (*The Concept of Law*, 1961) is the most careful juristic user of the game comparison. His account of law turns on materials with evident game-parallels: law as a *system of rules* rather than commands; the **internal point of view** (the participant who treats the rules as reasons and standards of criticism, as a player does, not merely as predictions of sanction, as an observer might); the union of **primary rules** (of obligation) with **secondary rules** of recognition, change, and **adjudication**; and the **"open texture"** of rules, whereby a settled core of clear application (no vehicles in the park; plainly, a car) is fringed by a penumbra requiring the official's discretion (a child's tricycle?) — the same penumbral judgement an umpire exercises at the boundary of a rule. Hart's recurring figure is the **scorer/umpire**, whose rulings are both rule-bound and, within the game, final.

But the most directly relevant scholarship judges Hart's use of the comparison *guarded and incomplete*. A semiotic study of "the games analogy in jurisprudence" (Jackson, in *Droit et Société*, 1991) finds Hart's deployment "partial, and at times more than a little strained," and notes that the connections Hart draws (rules, internal point of view, the scorer) are quite different from Huizinga's (the lawsuit as contest, chance, verbal battle). The lesson is that even law's most game-literate theorist used the analogy *selectively* — endorsing the rule-and-adjudication parallel while declining the contest parallel. *(Full article robots-blocked; read at abstract/snippet level — flagged in the ledger.)*

### D. The adjudicator across all the domains: one role, many arenas

A structural observation that unifies Parts II–IV: every one of these activities institutes an **impartial third who applies the rules and declares the outcome**, and the role is recognisably the same across them — the **scorer/referee/umpire** of sport (Hart's figure), the **judge and jury** of the trial, the **adjudicator** of competitive debate, the **chair** of a deliberative assembly enforcing rules of order, and the **audience-as-judge** of the ancient dialectical bout (Part II.A). The presence of a rule-applying arbiter who is neither contestant is one of the firmest correspondences between adjudicative deliberation and refereed sport, and one of the clearest contrasts with unregulated quarrel or war.

### E. The other game in the courtroom: the formal game theory of litigation and settlement

Part III.A treats the trial as a *sporting* contest, a figure its own theorists hold at arm's length. But the trial carries a second, quite different game-framing that the sporting strand obscures, and it is the exact counterpart of what Part IV does for negotiation: the **law-and-economics theory of litigation**, which models the lawsuit as a formal game of strategic interaction under incomplete information. In these models (Bebchuk 1984; P'ng 1983; Reinganum & Wilde 1986; surveyed in Spier 2007 and in Baird, Gertner & Picker's *Game Theory and the Law*, 1994) the plaintiff and defendant are players choosing among settlement demands, offers, and the option of trial, each holding private information about the case; the central explanandum is *why disputes go to trial at all* when a settlement would leave both parties better off than the costs of litigating — and the answer is that strategic behaviour under asymmetric information generates a positive equilibrium probability of trial. The tradition's most cited result, the **Priest–Klein selection hypothesis** (1984), is that as the parties' signals about the merits grow more accurate, the cases that fail to settle and proceed to judgment converge on a plaintiff win-rate near fifty per cent — disputes select for trial precisely when the contest is closest. Bebchuk (1996) showed further that even a negative-value suit can carry a credible threat when litigation costs are finely divisible, and that a defendant with private information can deter such suits by stonewalling.

The significance for this synthesis is twofold. First, it closes the asymmetry between Parts III and IV: the trial, like the negotiation, is a game in the strict *game-theoretic* sense (sense 4 of Part 0), an object of equilibrium analysis with players, private types, strategies, and payoffs — not merely a sporting metaphor. Second, it sharpens the master equivocation. The settlement bargaining that surrounds every trial is *mixed-motive* (the parties cooperate to capture the surplus of avoiding trial while competing over its division), so the litigation game inherits exactly Part IV's lesson: the trial is a game in sense 4 even where it is least a zero-sum contest in sense 3. The courtroom therefore hosts *two* games at once — Pound's sporting agon (sense 3, and a danger to be checked) and the litigation–settlement game (sense 4, and a neutral analytic fact) — and conflating them is one more instance of the ambiguity Part 0 isolates.

## Part IV — Negotiation and appeal as strategic (mixed-motive) games

Negotiation is where the comparison is most fully *mathematised*, because the game in question is the game theorist's game.

- **John Nash — "The Bargaining Problem" (1950).** Nash modelled bargaining as a formal game: each outcome a vector of utilities, a designated *disagreement point* (the status quo if no deal is struck), and a solution selected by axioms — the origin of cooperative bargaining theory.
- **Thomas Schelling — *The Strategy of Conflict* (1960).** Schelling reframed bargaining as strategic interaction and supplied its most enduring concepts: the distinction between **tacit** and **explicit** bargaining; **commitment**, **threats**, and **promises** as moves that work by *limiting one's own options* credibly; and **focal points** ("Schelling points") on which parties coordinate absent communication. Decisively for our purposes, Schelling located negotiation among **mixed-motive, non-zero-sum games** — games of *both* common and opposed interest — and distinguished them sharply from pure (zero-sum) conflict.
- **Howard Raiffa — *The Art and Science of Negotiation* (1982).** Raiffa founded *negotiation analysis*, an asymmetrically prescriptive treatment (advising one party against a realistically-modelled, not idealised, counterpart) and gave currency to the "negotiation dance" of offer and concession.
- **Richard Walton and Robert McKersie — *A Behavioral Theory of Labor Negotiations* (1965).** The integrative (positive-sum, value-creating) versus distributive (zero-sum, value-claiming) distinction, which structures all later practice.
- **Robert Putnam — "Diplomacy and Domestic Politics: The Logic of Two-Level Games" (1988).** The extension to diplomacy. A national leader negotiating abroad plays at two tables at once: Level I, the international bargaining, and Level II, domestic ratification. The **win-set** is the set of Level-I agreements that would survive ratification at Level II; a deal is possible only where the parties' win-sets overlap, and institutional features that shrink a win-set (a two-thirds ratification threshold rather than a simple majority) make overlap — and therefore agreement — less likely. Building directly on Schelling, the model adds a structural twist to the negotiation game: the diplomat is simultaneously a player in two games with different boards and different judges, and a move that strengthens his hand at one table can lose him the other. (Putnam is scrupulous that this is "a metaphor," not a finished formal theory — a tier nuance worth preserving.)

**The pivotal finding of this part is a double one.** Negotiation is a game in the *strictest available technical sense* — it is literally an object of mathematical game theory, with players, strategies, payoffs, equilibria, and solution concepts. *And yet* it is precisely *not* a game in the *sporting* sense, because it is **mixed-motive**: the parties both compete (to claim value) and cooperate (to create the agreement at all), so a victory for one is not, in general, a loss for the other. Negotiation therefore exposes the equivocation buried in the whole question: "game" in the game-theoretic sense (any strategic interdependence) and "game" in the agonistic/sporting sense (a zero-sum contest with a winner) are *different concepts*, and an activity can satisfy one precisely by failing the other. Appeals, settlements, plea bargains, and diplomatic negotiation are games of the first kind and frequently anti-games of the second.

## Part V — The correspondences, distilled

Drawing the literatures together, the rigorous correspondences between interpersonal deliberation/adjudication and game-playing are these. Each is named with the source that establishes it and the domains it covers.

1. **Constitution by rules, not mere regulation.** The activity's characteristic acts (a concession, a verdict, a valid inference, a binding offer) exist only because rules confer them that status — "X counts as Y in C." *(Searle 1969; Rawls 1955. All domains.)* — *the master correspondence.*

2. **Voluntary acceptance of inefficient constraint (the lusory attitude).** Procedural rules are *unnecessary obstacles* embraced for the sake of the institution they make possible; the exclusionary rule is the paradigm. *(Suits 1978. Trial, debate; strained for the unwilling litigant.)*

3. **A bounded arena set apart from ordinary life.** Court, debate hall, and negotiating table are "magic circles" / interaction membranes with their own suspended-and-transformed norms. *(Huizinga 1938; Goffman 1961; Salen & Zimmerman 2003. All domains.)*

4. **Defined adversarial roles and turn-taking.** Proponent/Opponent, Questioner/Answerer, prosecution/defence, Verifier/Falsifier, Government/Opposition, orator-before-judge — fixed roles alternating under rule. *(Aristotle; Lorenzen–Lorenz; Hintikka; competitive-debate rulebooks. Argument, dialectic, trial, debate, oratory.)*

5. **Legal moves and a commitment store (the game state).** Locution rules fix permissible utterances; a running ledger of commitments records position and is altered by moves. *(Hamblin 1970; Walton & Krabbe 1995. Argument, debate, negotiation.)*

6. **A determinate win/loss condition and outcome.** Valid/invalid, true/false, guilty/not-guilty, agreement/impasse — a defined terminal state. *(Lorenz's "two-person zero-sum game"; the verdict; the judged debate. Argument, trial, debate.)*

7. **Strategy, and validity/truth/success as a *winning strategy*.** The deepest correspondence: logical validity and model-theoretic truth are *defined* as the existence of a winning strategy; negotiating, litigation, and diplomatic success are game-theoretic equilibria (the Nash bargain, the settlement equilibrium, the overlapping win-set). *(Lorenzen–Lorenz; Hintikka; Nash; Schelling; Priest–Klein; Putnam. Argument/logic, negotiation, litigation, diplomacy.)*

8. **An impartial rule-applying adjudicator.** Scorer/umpire ≈ judge/jury ≈ *tabula rasa* debate adjudicator ≈ assembly chair ≈ dialectical audience ≈ Aristotle's three hearers (juror of the past, juror of the future, spectator who judges the speaker). *(Hart 1961; Aristotle; competitive-debate judging paradigms. Trial, debate, deliberation, oratory.)*

9. **Sublimated, civilised conflict.** The contest channels aggression that would otherwise be combat, binding it in rules and submitting it to an arbiter — and historically *descends* from combat. *(Huizinga 1938; Pound 1906. Trial, debate.)*

10. **Fallacies and fouls: rule-violations penalised within the system.** A fallacy is the violation of a discussion rule — an illegitimate move that derails the game, exactly as a foul is. *(van Eemeren & Grootendorst 1992. Argument, debate.)*

## Part VI — Where the correspondence breaks: disanalogies and live disputes

A synthesis that only assembled the parallels would be advocacy, not scholarship. The following are the points at which deliberation and adjudication are *not* games, or are games only equivocally — several of them raised by the very authors who built the parallels.

1. **Stakes and seriousness.** Huizinga's defining marks of play include that it is *disinterested* and stands outside the satisfaction of material need — it is "non-serious" in a precise sense. A criminal trial decides liberty and sometimes life; a labour negotiation decides livelihoods. The gravity of consequence sits awkwardly with any account that makes the bounded, consequence-light character of play essential. This is not a minor caveat: it is the basis of Pound's entire critique — that the *more* the trial behaves like a sport, the *more* it betrays the serious purpose (accurate, just judgement) that distinguishes it from a game.

2. **Truth/justice orientation versus winning.** The strongest theories of argument (pragma-dialectics) and Walton & Krabbe's *inquiry* and *persuasion* dialogues are oriented to *resolution on the merits* or *truth*, and treat the disposition to *win at all costs* as the very source of fallacy. Eristic — argument-as-pure-contest — is in this tradition the *degenerate* type. So the contest model fits the quarrel and the worst of advocacy, and *mis*fits exactly the deliberation we most value. The activity is healthiest when its participants treat it *least* like a contest to be won.

3. **Cooperative versus competitive structure.** Much deliberation is governed by Grice's *cooperative principle* (conversation as joint enterprise), and negotiation is *mixed-motive* (Part IV), not zero-sum. Even within dialogical logic there is an unsettled dispute about whether the dialogue games are essentially adversarial (Dutilh Novaes) or contain genuinely cooperative moves where an "attack" in fact assists the other party (Hodges). The agonistic/sporting reading is therefore *contested at the technical core*, not merely at the edges.

4. **Searle's regulative/constitutive limit on the law-as-game equation.** The *substantive* law is regulative (it does not exist to make crime possible), so only the *procedural and institutional* layer of a trial is constituted in the game-like way. "A trial is a game" overclaims; "the procedural enactment of a trial is rule-constituted as a game is" is the defensible version.

5. **Hart's deliberate reticence.** Law's most game-literate theorist used the analogy "partial[ly] and … strained[ly]" (Jackson 1991), endorsing the rule-and-adjudication parallel while declining the contest parallel — a considered limit, not an oversight.

6. **Suits's voluntariness condition fails for the unwilling participant.** Game-playing on Suits's definition is a *voluntary* attempt to take up unnecessary obstacles for the activity's sake. The compelled defendant, the conscripted respondent, the party dragged to arbitration does no such thing. The lusory attitude belongs to the system, not to everyone bound by it — so the activity is a game *for some of its participants and not others*, which no ordinary game is.

7. **The magic circle is itself contested.** Game studies has substantially qualified the "magic circle" (Walz; Consalvo; Malaby), stressing that the boundary between play and ordinary life is porous and socially negotiated. Insofar as the court/table/hall comparison rests on a clean membrane, it inherits this fragility — and the leakage matters precisely *because* the stakes (point 1) are real.

8. **Conceptual-metaphor theory's empirical vulnerability.** The ARGUMENT-IS-WAR/GAME branch (Part II.E) rests on a theory whose central empirical claims are disputed (McGlone & Glucksberg; Vervaeke & Kennedy — with Gibbs the strand's principal *defender*, not a critic). It establishes cultural availability of the game framing, not the constitutive claims, and should not be made to bear more weight than that.

9. **The family-resemblance objection — there may be no single thing these activities "share with games."** The most fundamental disanalysis is methodological and turns the whole question on itself. Wittgenstein's own verdict on "game" (*Investigations* §66–67) is that it has no defining essence, only overlapping resemblances; if that is right, then asking what trials, debates, and negotiations *share* with games presupposes a common feature that the concept of a game does not possess. The synthesis's master correspondence (constitution by rules) is best understood not as that essence but as the *strongest single resemblance-strand* — and, as Part 0 notes, one that holds for three of the four senses of "game" and not the fourth. Suits's definition is precisely the attempt to defeat Wittgenstein and supply an essence; whether it succeeds is unsettled. So even the load-bearing correspondence is advanced under dispute, and the honest form of the thesis is comparative and graded, never "X simply is a game."

**Net assessment.** The correspondence is *strongest and least disputable* for **argument, dialectic, and formal logic** (Part II: argument is reconstructible as a formal game, and validity/truth are *definable* as winning strategies — these are theorems, not analogies) and for **negotiation** (Part IV: literally an object of game theory). The **trial** now sits in a sharper light: it is a game in the *sporting* sense (Part III.A) only equivocally and to its theorists' alarm, but a game in the strict *game-theoretic* sense without strain (Part III.E, the litigation–settlement models) — the two framings should not be run together. **Debate** is the most literal case of all: in its organised competitive form (Part II.F) it is not merely game-*like* but an actual refereed contest with a rulebook, and one whose practitioners hold an explicit theory that it is a game. **Oratory** (Part I.G) is a judged contest of skill in its founding theory. **Deliberation and assembly** remain *genuine but selective* (rule-constituted and adjudicated, yet cooperative and resolution-seeking rather than zero-sum), and the everyday **metaphor** remains *suggestive only*. The single most defensible through-line is still the constitutive one (Part V.1) — these activities are *brought into being by rules that create rather than merely regulate* — held now as the strongest family resemblance rather than a shared essence, and true across every domain the brief names, whatever else divides them from sport.

## Part VIII — Adversarial self-review and reconciliation (v2)

*This synthesis was reviewed against its own first draft from the standpoint of an independent, hostile reader. The draft was self-authored, so the bias risk is real: an author is incentivised to defend structure already built and to let a tidy throughline paper over its exceptions. What follows records the criticisms that survived scrutiny and the disposition of each — accepted-and-rebuilt, accepted-and-extended, refined, or declined with reason. It is an audit record, not an apology.*

**Findings, worst first, with disposition:**

1. **The master throughline did not in fact unify every sense of "game" the draft used.** The draft asserted one load-bearing correspondence (constitution by rules) as if it covered the whole field, while silently also invoking the *game-theoretic* sense of "game" — which need not be rule-constituted at all. → **Rebuilt.** Part 0 now separates four non-coextensive senses (Wittgensteinian, Suitsian, agonistic, game-theoretic), states that constitution-by-rules unifies three of them and not the fourth, and routes the game-theoretic strand through a different bridge (strategic interdependence before a third party).

2. **Essence-talk contradicted the Wittgenstein the draft was citing.** Calling constitution-by-rules "the very thing that makes an activity a game" assumes a shared essence that Wittgenstein (*PI* §66–67) denies "game" possesses. → **Rebuilt + extended.** Part 0 now frames the throughline as the strongest *family resemblance*, not an essence, and notes Suits is the contrary bid to supply one; new disanalogy VI.9 makes the methodological objection explicit.

3. **Oratory/"speeches" was badly under-served relative to the brief.** The draft handled rhetoric only through one Huizinga line. → **Extended.** New Part I.G (the classical rhetorical agon): Aristotle's three genera grounded in three kinds of judge; sophistic/epideictic display; *eris/eristikē*; Avinger on public speech as fundamentally competitive.

# Political Machination as Strategic Competition

*A companion study: theory, method, and a games lens — with an adversarial review*

This document extends the prior synthesis (*Deliberation, Debate, and Decision in Renaissance Political Bodies*). Where that study treated deliberation as institution and as ideology, this one turns to the dimension it under-served: the strategic, agonistic reality beneath the procedures — the machination — and reads it through the analytical lens of competition and games. It then turns an adversarial eye on both documents.

Two registers are kept distinct throughout. The **emic** register is the Renaissance's own theory of political maneuver — what Machiavelli, Guicciardini, and the reason-of-state writers said about how the game is actually played. The **etic** register is the modern social-scientific method — network analysis, game theory, mechanism design, the new institutional economics, and the "analytic narratives" program — that models these same contests formally. The two are complementary and, at points, uncannily convergent: the period's theorists and the modern modellers both treat politics as strategic interaction among self-interested actors under deep uncertainty. The convergence is illuminating but also a trap, as Part VII insists.

## Part V — The Theory and Method of Political Machination

### V.1 The period's own science of maneuver (emic)

**The agonistic substrate.** Before any theorist, there was a disposition: the inherited classical understanding of public life as *contest*. The Greek *agōn* (competitive striving, carried over from athletics and drama into political life) and the Roman *competitio* for *honores* — the *cursus honorum* run as a regulated race, shadowed by *ambitus*, the dark art of canvassing and bribery — bequeathed to the communes a frame in which office and honor were scarce positional goods won in rule-bound rivalry. (This framing is interpretive synthesis rather than a claim any single Renaissance text makes in these terms; it is offered as an organizing lens, not a source-attested doctrine.) The republican design problem follows directly: how to keep that rivalry bounded and positive-sum rather than violent and zero-sum.

**Machiavelli as the first systematic theorist of strategic action.** Machiavelli converted this disposition into something close to a science of maneuver. Felix Gilbert, Mario Santoro, and J. G. A. Pocock established that he reorganized humanist discourse into a *vocabulary of contingency* turning on three concepts — prudence, Fortune, and the common good — in which a ruler, facing fickle Fortune, must be morally flexible in order to remain constant in pursuit of the common good (Howard 2011). Maurizio Viroli's reconstruction sharpens the epistemology: for Machiavelli, political reality is not a stable system of facts open to observation but "a world of uncertain and ambivalent signs, words and gestures," legible only in part and only through interpretation, in which the capacity to simulate and dissimulate is central, and in which the same actor will not behave the same way in June as in December (Viroli 2007). This is a theory of action under radical strategic uncertainty — precisely the situation modern game theory formalizes.

Three further Machiavellian theses bear on machination specifically. First, a conflict theory of politics: man is, for Machiavelli, closer to *homo homini lupus* than to Aristotle's civically friendly animal; the *grandi* always seek to dominate the *popolo*, "the elite always acts in the manner of the elite," and liberty is secured only by institutional mechanisms and a balance of power that prevent the powerful from devouring the rest (Bustamante Kuschel 2025). This is the analytical core of the productive-conflict reading already noted in the prior study, now grounded as a genuine theory of class antagonism. Second, a rational-actor anthropology: government is founded on human insufficiency and exists to constrain the selfish egoism of individuals — a premise not far, as commentators note, from the *Federalist* logic that government is necessary because men are not angels (Brunello 2019). Third, an analytics of the highest-risk maneuver: the long chapter on conspiracies (*Discorsi* III.6) is a sober, almost actuarial treatment of plots as strategic gambles — their phases, their points of failure, and the asymmetry whereby the conspirator risks everything while the incumbent, surviving, is entrenched. The Pazzi conspiracy of 1478 is the textbook instance: a move intended to topple the Medici only consolidated them.

**Guicciardini and the calculus of self-interest.** Where Machiavelli theorized the founder and the prince, Guicciardini theorized the operator inside a faction-ridden republic. His *Ricordi* make *il particulare* — private interest — the near-universal motive of political actors, and elevate *discrezione*, the trained judgment of the particular case against the false comfort of general rules. He is generally credited with coining the phrase *ragione di stato*, in the *Dialogo del reggimento di Firenze* of the early 1520s, to name the "not very Christian and not very human reason" required to keep power (Stergios 2006). And he registered, as acutely as anyone, the strategic shock of his own moment: before 1494, he observed in the *Ricordi*, wars were slow and states practically impossible to lose; the French invasions introduced such speed of execution that losing the open country meant losing the state (Jones and Smith 2015). The observation is, in effect, a claim that a change in military technology had altered the payoff structure of the whole inter-state game.

**Reason of state and Tacitism: the methodology of statecraft.** By the later sixteenth century this strand hardened into an explicit method. Giovanni Botero's *Della ragion di stato* (1589) defined reason of state as the knowledge of the means suitable to found, conserve, and expand a dominion — with conservation taking priority over both expansion and foundation (Zhu 2024). The distinctively Italian move, on one reading, was to remove ordinary political morality (*virtù* in the ethical sense) from the political realm altogether (Stergios 2006). The vehicle was Tacitism: the revival of Tacitus as a guide to the *arcana imperii* of court politics. Justus Lipsius's *Politica* (1589), a cento of classical maxims woven largely from Tacitus, ran through some ninety editions and became the primer for prudent counsellors across confessional lines; its achievement, as recent scholarship puts it, was to give credibility to the idea of politics as a complex and ruthless game requiring skilful counsel (Jones and Smith 2015). With it came an entire literature of *dissimulation*, epitomized in the maxim *qui nescit dissimulare, nescit regnare* — he who knows not how to dissimulate knows not how to rule — and in Tacitus's report that the emperor Tiberius prized dissimulation above all his virtues (Constantinidou 2011). The method of machination, fully theorized, was prudence plus dissimulation, drilled from ancient exempla.

### V.2 The modern formal-analytical method (etic)

Modern social science has modelled these same contests with a precision the actors lacked. Four bodies of method matter, and each maps onto a real feature of Renaissance practice.

**Social network analysis and "robust action."** The landmark is Padgett and Ansell's study of Cosimo de' Medici's rise (1400–1434), which explained his ascendancy not by resources or charisma but by *structural position* — Cosimo sat as broker across otherwise disconnected networks of marriage, patronage, and trade — combined with a strategy of *robust action*. Robust action, a concept traceable to Leifer's analysis of how chess masters play not by out-calculating every line but by preserving their own options while narrowing the opponent's, denotes "noncommittal actions that keep future lines of action open in strategic contexts where opponents are trying to narrow them" (Padgett and Powell, summarized in Rowland and Spaniol 2022). Its enabling quality is *multivocality*: a single action can be read coherently from several perspectives at once and can be a move in many games simultaneously, lending the actor a "sphinx-like character" that opponents cannot pin down (Rowland and Spaniol 2022). Harrison White's formulation captures the institutional version: a regime of consensual ambiguity is a robust control device precisely because it is hard to determine who holds control. Paul McLean's study of Florentine patronage letters extends the point: self-presentation itself was strategic interaction, a continuous game of positioning (McLean, *The Art of the Network*, cited in the Renaissance-studies literature). A methodological caution, embedded in the concept, deserves emphasis: robust action can be *observed directly* in the historical record, whereas the "rationality" of a choice is typically assigned after the fact in interpretation — so robust action is not to be conflated with bare rational choice (Rowland and Spaniol 2022).

**Self-enforcing institutions and the new institutional economics.** A second method treats an institution as an *equilibrium of a game*: a pattern of behavior that persists because, given everyone else's strategy, no actor gains by deviating (Bates, Greif, Levi, Rosenthal, and Weingast 1998). Avner Greif's distinction between self-*reinforcing* and self-*undermining* equilibria — where an institution's own operation gradually widens or narrows the range of conditions under which it remains stable — has been applied directly to the Italian city-states. Piano (2023) argues that a commune's regime type is a function of its domestic balance of power: where the ruled can credibly threaten to punish a predatory ruler, the rule of law holds; where the ruler can overpower the ruled, despotism results. The rise of the communes followed the withdrawal of effective imperial and episcopal power in the eleventh century; their later collapse into signorial despotism followed a *shock to the balance of power* — developments in military technology that empowered a small minority of wealthy military specialists over their fellow citizens — and only coastal and central-Italian towns, lacking a strong military aristocracy and enjoying greater homogeneity of interests, preserved the rule of law past the mid-fourteenth century (Piano 2023). Greif's own classic reading of Genoa adds the role of the *podestà* — the hired neutral outsider — as a third-party enforcer that can make peace between rival clans self-enforcing, until the equilibrium breaks. Tsebelis's concept of *nested games* supplies a further tool: an action that looks irrational within a single arena often becomes rational once one sees that the player is moving simultaneously in linked arenas (local, diplomatic, ecclesiastical), where payoffs in one are governed by conditions in another.

**Mechanism design and social choice: the theory of the lot.** The third method illuminates the most distinctive Renaissance procedure — sortition — as a deliberate anti-manipulation device. Oliver Dowlen identifies the "primary potential" of the lot as its *arationality*: because a genuine lottery cannot be reasoned with, it protects the process of selection from subversion by anyone seeking to control it for partisan ends, with anti-tyranny, anti-faction, impartiality, and broadened participation as secondary benefits (Dowlen 2009). But the same analysis exposes the vulnerability that the prior study only gestured at: when the pool of names is small and its members coordinate into groups, the lottery becomes in effect a *weighted lottery* whose results are read in terms of groups rather than individuals, "the arational blind break does less and less work," and patterns of partisan dependence accumulate — a tendency Dowlen says was "very much the case with the Florentine and Venetian schemes," aggravated by their small pools (Dowlen 2009). Hubertus Buchstein adds a corrective the prior study needed: the lot's roots were sacral and oligarchic, not democratic, and its principal historical functions were avoiding conflict over office (anti-*stasis*) and frustrating bribery — Demosthenes praised selecting judges by lot as a guard against bribery, and Aristotle noted a city that switched from election to lot because electors habitually chose their own partisans — so the familiar "lot equals democracy" reading is overstated (Buchstein 2019). Frey, Osterloh, and Rost formalize the design as a "qualified lottery" — random selection combined with preselection by conventional criteria — which yields a "second-order rationality on the institutional level": the Venetian dogeship, settled in some ten alternating rounds of lot and competitive election, deliberately introduced indeterminacy to make bribery and factional capture unattractive, and the broader family of such procedures gave the northern-Italian republics extraordinary stability across five centuries while remaining, emphatically, exclusionary rather than democratic (Frey, Osterloh, and Rost 2022). At the formal frontier, Saran and Tumennasan show that sortition by a *kleroterion* can fully implement social-choice functions as effectively as direct democracy under stated conditions, including schemes such as "oligarchy with random audit" (Saran and Tumennasan 2018) — which is, in effect, a model of the Venetian and Florentine designs. The Venetian protocol has accordingly attracted analysis from modern security researchers as a collusion-resistant election procedure avant la lettre. A genuine counter-current must be recorded: Landa and Pevnick argue that randomly selected officials, knowing they will not be reselected, may be *more* susceptible to a revolving-door form of corruption than elected ones who must face voters again (discussed in Shoemaker 2026) — so the lot is not unambiguously protective, and the trade-off is real.

**Analytic narratives — and their critics.** The fourth contribution is less a model than a method for combining models with history. The *Analytic Narratives* program (Bates, Greif, Levi, Rosenthal, and Weingast 1998) weds extensive-form game theory to close attention to actors' preferences, beliefs, information, and constraints, in two modes: adjudicating between rival historiographies by asking which is more consistent with rational action, and interpreting otherwise puzzling events (Héritier and Karagiannis 2011). Its critics name the hazard squarely, and it is the hazard this whole companion must guard against. Analytic narratives "seek to explain historical phenomena *ex post* on the basis of their optimality according to game theory" and tend "not [to] actually test a theory or a concept on data but rather determine a game-theory-based narrative fitting history" (Gauthier 2022) — the risk, in short, of constructing an equilibrium story to fit a known outcome. A second critique is that their standard belief-modelling (perfect Bayesian equilibrium) is too thin to capture how real actors form beliefs under uncertainty, impoverishing the account (Schiemann 2007). Both critiques bite directly on any attempt to read Renaissance politics as a clean game, because the actors' beliefs were, on Viroli's account, thick, contingent, and only partly rational.

## Part VI — An Evaluation Lens: The Institutions as Competitions and Games

This part applies the apparatus of Part V as an explicit evaluative grid. For each arena it asks the same questions: who are the *players*; what is the *prize*; what are the *rules*; what *strategies* do the rules permit and reward; what *equilibrium* results; and what made that equilibrium stable or self-undermining. The aim is not to reduce Renaissance politics to a matrix but to use the comparison to explain a real puzzle — why structurally similar republics, playing with the same procedural materials, reached such different outcomes.

### VI.1 The deep structure: office as a positional good, deliberation as a bounded contest

The common structure across all the cases is this. Office and honor were scarce *positional* goods: their value lay partly in others not having them. Competition for them was therefore intrinsic, and the whole apparatus of communal constitution-making — statutes, short terms, eligibility rules, scrutiny, lot, the ballot, the neutral *podestà* — can be read as an attempt to *channel* that competition: to convert a potentially violent, winner-take-all struggle among lineages and factions into a bounded, rule-governed, and ideally positive-sum game in which rivals take turns rather than fight. Read this way, the republics differ not in whether they hosted a competition but in how well their rules solved the design problem. The two paradigms of the prior study become two outcomes of the same game.

### VI.2 Florence: a brokerage game with a capturable lottery

The players were lineages and the shifting factions they composed (the *grandi* and the *popolo*, and within each the great houses); the prize was not merely office but *control of the scrutiny* — command of the names in the purses, which was command of the regime. The rules rewarded two strategies above all. The first was coalition-building through marriage (the *parentado*) and through patronage understood as a repeated relational exchange of favor for loyalty — the dense, overlapping ties that network historians have reconstructed (and that Padgett and Ansell modelled for the Medici). The second was *robust action*: Cosimo de' Medici's refusal to commit legibly, his occupation of the broker's position across factions, his multivocal self-presentation that opponents could not pin down. Crucially, the Medici did not need to abolish the republic's randomizing device; they *captured* it, in exactly the manner Dowlen's theory predicts — by controlling the small pool of eligible names through hand-picked *accoppiatori* and emergency *balìe*, they turned the impartial lottery into a weighted lottery whose draws reliably produced their men (Dowlen 2009). The high-risk countermove — the Pazzi conspiracy of 1478 — failed in just the way Machiavelli's analysis of conspiracies anticipates, and entrenched the incumbent it meant to remove. The resulting equilibrium was unstable and tended toward winner-take-all: a formally republican game progressively captured by one house, ending in principate. Florence is the case where the design failed to bound the competition.

### VI.3 Venice: mechanism design and a collusion-proofed game

Venice played with the same materials and reached the opposite equilibrium, and the difference is best understood as superior mechanism design. The players were the patrician clans; the rules were engineered to make their game *positive-sum* and *collusion-resistant*. Rotation and short terms ensured that everyone of the order cycled through office and no one won permanently; the prohibition on accumulating offices in a single set of hands capped any individual's reach; the Council of Ten policed defection; and the famously elaborate election of the Doge — some ten alternating rounds of lot and competitive ballot — functioned as an anti-coordination device, deliberately injecting indeterminacy so that no faction could predict or purchase the outcome and bribery lost its point (Frey, Osterloh, and Rost 2022). In the formal vocabulary, Venice approximated a self-enforcing equilibrium of an "oligarchy with random audit" kind (cf. Saran and Tumennasan 2018), in which the patriciate's interest in the system's continuation exceeded any clan's interest in capturing it. The outcome was the rare success: a competition so well bounded that it produced centuries of stability — at the price, which the games framing must not launder, of being a closed patrician game from which the great majority were excluded (Frey, Osterloh, and Rost 2022). Venice is the case where the design solved the problem.

### VI.4 Genoa: a self-undermining equilibrium

Genoa is the instructive intermediate case, and it is the one the new institutional economics handles best. Its players were powerful merchant clans locked in rivalry; its characteristic device was the *podestà*, the hired outsider serving as a third-party enforcer whose neutrality could, under the right balance of power, make inter-clan peace self-enforcing — until it could not. On Greif's reading and Piano's framework, the Genoese equilibrium was *self-undermining*: the very arrangements that bought peace altered the underlying balance of power over time, until a shock tipped the system into the factional violence that recurrently wracked the city, eventually "resolved" only by Andrea Doria's re-engineering of the constitution in 1528 (Piano 2023; Greif 2006). The comparison with Venice is the analytical payoff: two republics with the same toolkit, one reaching a self-reinforcing and the other a self-undermining equilibrium, demonstrating that the outcome turned on the balance of power and the design of the rules rather than on culture or classical ideology alone.

### VI.5 The conciliar movement: a bargaining game with a commitment problem

The deliberative experiment of the universal Church reads, in this lens, as a multi-player bargaining game crippled by a *commitment problem*. The Great Schism was a coordination failure: rival popes, no focal point on which Christendom could converge, and ordinary papal authority powerless to resolve a defect lodged in the papacy itself. The councils were an attempt to construct a credible collective-decision procedure standing above the contending claimants; the decree *Frequens* (1417), mandating the automatic future convocation of councils, was in effect a *commitment device* meant to bind the office in advance. It failed for the reason commitment devices fail against a sovereign who recovers his strength: the restored papacy could renege, and did, reasserting monarchical supremacy and repudiating the conciliarist claims (the bull *Pastor Aeternus* of 1516, of Leo X at the Fifth Lateran Council — not to be confused with the First Vatican Council constitution of the same name in 1870). The conciliar defeat is thus legible as the failure to solve a time-inconsistency problem: the body could not bind the head once the head regained the power to defect.

### VI.6 Sortition versus election as rival equilibrium-selection devices

Cutting across the cases is the period's own sharpest strategic choice — the lot or the vote — which the games lens clarifies as a trade-off between two failure modes. Sortition defeats strategic capture by being unreasoning, but it sacrifices selection-for-merit and, as Florence and Venice show, is itself capturable through control of the pool. Election selects for merit and standing but reopens the door to canvassing, bribery, and factional combination — the ancient *ambitus*. Leonardo Bruni's defense of election against the lot (treated in the prior study) is precisely a statement of this trade-off from inside the period: he wanted merit and zeal for virtue and was willing to pay the price in factional risk. The "qualified lottery" — preselection by scrutiny followed by the draw — was the attempted synthesis, an effort to buy the anti-capture property of the lot and the merit property of the vote at once (Frey, Osterloh, and Rost 2022; Buchstein 2019). That nearly every northern-Italian republic converged on some version of it suggests that the design problem was real, was understood, and admitted no clean solution.

## Part VII — Adversarial Review

*Self-authored review; bias risk acknowledged. The standard applied is what an independent, hostile examiner would press. Findings are ordered worst-first; a clean point is reported as clean only where the work supports it.*

**Verdict.** The prior document is sound and well-sourced *as institutional and intellectual history*, but it is methodologically monochrome, its central interpretive claim is too tidy, and at least three specific claims outrun their evidence. Measured against the present brief, its governing defect is that it nearly omitted the strategic and agonistic dimension altogether. This companion repairs that omission but introduces a characteristic danger of its own — the post-hoc game. Neither document should be read as settled; both are arguments. Detailed findings follow.

**1. (Most severe, scope.) The strategic dimension was nearly absent from the original.** The prior study treated deliberation as procedure plus legitimating ideology. Faction, patronage, and Medici capture were named but not *theorized*; there was no account of office as a positional good, no network analysis, no theory of strategic behavior, no games framing. For a subject whose lived reality was relentless competition, this was a substantive hole, not a stylistic one. It is owned, and this companion is the remedy — but the remedy's lateness is itself the finding: the first treatment risked presenting a sanitized, normative picture of bodies whose daily business was maneuver.

**2. (Significant.) The central legitimation-versus-design thesis is too tidy.** The prior study's organizing claim — that Roman and canon law supplied the structural substrate while Greek philosophy supplied mainly legitimation, analysis, and occasional redesign — is defensible and was presented as such, but the clean partition oversimplifies in two ways an examiner would exploit. First, the categories bleed: the jurists were themselves reading Aristotle (Bartolus and the commentators worked in a partly Aristotelianized intellectual world), and canon-law corporation theory intertwined with the Aristotelian idea of the *communitas*, so "legal substrate" and "philosophical inheritance" are not cleanly separable streams. Second, the assertion that the legal inheritance "owes almost nothing to Greek philosophy" overstates: Roman jurisprudence had itself absorbed Stoic and Aristotelian elements in antiquity. The four-part scheme (substrate / legitimation / analysis / redesign) is a useful heuristic and should have been labelled one, not advanced with the confidence of a finding.

**3. (Significant.) The ideology-versus-practice claim overreaches its evidence.** The prior study inferred from the *Consulte e Pratiche* protocols that everyday deliberation "proceeded in a plainer idiom" than the humanist ideal of eloquence. But those protocols are, on the cited scholarship's own account, hasty summaries dashed off by the recording chancellor — Salutati had no time for more — so they may simply *understate* the register of what was actually said. The document noted this caveat and then leaned on the inference anyway. The honest position is weaker and should have been stated as the conclusion: the record is largely *silent* on spoken register; it is not positive evidence of plainness. The gap between ideology and practice is real at the level of *content and procedure*; at the level of rhetorical *texture* it is not established.

**4. (Significant.) Sortition was mischaracterized.** The original framed the logic of the lot as "egalitarian" and "quasi-democratic." Part V shows this is contestable on two fronts. Buchstein's revision establishes that the lot's roots were sacral and oligarchic and that its primary historical functions were anti-faction and anti-bribery, not the expression of democratic equality — the "lot equals democracy" reading being a modern overstatement (Buchstein 2019). Dowlen's analysis shows that the specific Florentine and Venetian lotteries were, because of their small pools, *weighted* lotteries open to factional capture — which is exactly how the Medici subverted them without abolishing them (Dowlen 2009). The original's treatment was theoretically thin and wrong in emphasis; the corrected account makes the lot an anti-manipulation device whose protective power degrades as the pool shrinks.

**5. (Moderate.) Specific factual and sourcing points.** Several claims in the prior study were correct but under-sourced or liable to confusion, and are tightened here. (a) The Doge election in "some ten" alternating rounds is confirmed at roughly ten rounds (Frey, Osterloh, and Rost 2022), so the original was accurate but should have carried a citation. (b) *Pastor Aeternus* (1516) is correctly the Leo X bull of the Fifth Lateran Council, but it is easily confused with the far more famous First Vatican Council constitution of the same name (1870); the original left this undisambiguated, now corrected in VI.5. (c) The date of Perotti's Latin Polybius was left vague ("mid-fifteenth century"); acceptable, but loose. (d) The claim that the legal inheritance owes "almost nothing" to Greek philosophy is, as in finding 2, an overstatement.

**6. (Moderate.) Both documents underweight exclusion and the world beyond Italy.** An examiner would press three omissions. First, the radically *exclusionary* character of all this "participation": the deliberating bodies were tiny male elites, and the civic ideology, as the prior study's own sources concede, "ministered to an elite" — a fact the games framing can actually sharpen (a bounded positive-sum game *for the patriciate* was a closed game for everyone else) but must not launder. Second, the near-total confinement to Italy leaves out deliberative bodies that would test the theses hard: the Iberian *Cortes*, the Imperial *Reichstag*, the Swiss *Tagsatzung*, the Hanseatic diets, and above all the Polish *sejm* with the *liberum veto* — a deliberative-design catastrophe in which a unanimity rule handed every member a veto and paralyzed the state, the perfect negative case for a mechanism-design analysis. Third, the *transmission* story omitted the Byzantine and Islamic route — the Arabic-to-Latin passage of Aristotle and the commentaries of Averroes — that made the Western recovery possible at all.

**7. (Methodological, applies to *this* companion.) The games lens carries the analytic-narratives hazard.** The most serious objection to Part VI is the one its own sources supply. Applied incautiously, a games reading constructs an equilibrium story *ex post* to fit an outcome already known, imputing a rational-actor anthropology rather than evidencing it (Gauthier 2022; Schiemann 2007). The Renaissance actors' beliefs were, on Viroli's account, thick, contingent, and only partly rational — "ambivalent signs" requiring interpretation — and a clean game model flattens exactly that texture. Two disciplines mitigate the hazard and are observed here: the lens is strongest where it *adjudicates between rival historiographies* (why Venice was stable and Genoa was not is a genuine comparative question the equilibrium framework answers well) and weakest where it merely *re-describes* a single outcome as optimal; and robust action, at least, is directly observable in the record rather than imputed after the fact (Rowland and Spaniol 2022). The lens is offered as illumination, not proof, and the reader should treat every equilibrium claim in Part VI as an interpretation that a thicker archival study could complicate.

**8. (Minor.) Bibliographic looseness.** The original's references were occasionally imprecise in date or attribution (for instance, dating the Padgett group's *Consulte e Pratiche* work loosely; the foundational network study is Padgett and Ansell 1993, the speech-by-speech studies are circa 2019–2020). Corrected and extended in the addendum below.

**What would strengthen the whole further.** Three moves, in order of value: (i) test one equilibrium claim against the actual archive — read a run of the Venetian *Maggior Consiglio* ballots or the Florentine *Tratte* to see whether behavior fits the modelled game or breaks it; (ii) add at least one hard out-of-sample case (the *liberum veto* is the obvious choice) to see whether the mechanism-design framework predicts failure as well as it rationalizes success; (iii) integrate the excluded majority into the analysis rather than bracketing them, since a theory of the game that ignores the non-players is incomplete.

**Confidence.** The descriptive scholarship marshalled here is well-grounded and citable, and the central corrective claims (findings 1–4) are, in my judgment, correct and would survive review. The constructive games readings in Part VI are *defensible interpretations*, not demonstrated results, and are flagged as such throughout. Confidence: high on the findings, medium on the constructive equilibrium claims, with the analytic-narratives hazard (finding 7) the standing reason for the discount.

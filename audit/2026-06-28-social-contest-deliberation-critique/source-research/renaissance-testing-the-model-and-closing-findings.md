# Testing the Model and Closing the Findings

*A third installment: the game model against the archive, hard out-of-sample cases, the world beyond Italy, the transmission route restored, and a disposition of the adversarial review*

This document does two things the prior adversarial review demanded. It **pursues the empirical test** — confronting the mechanism-design reading of Renaissance deliberation with what the actual records show (the Venetian ballots and *broglio*, the Florentine *Tratte*), and adding the Polish *liberum veto* as a hard out-of-sample case to see whether the framework predicts failure as well as it rationalizes success. And it **resolves the findings** of Part VII, closing each in turn, including the breadth gap (the non-Italian assemblies), the transmission gap (Byzantium and Islam), and the "too tidy" thesis (the interpenetration of the legal and philosophical inheritances). Part XII states, finding by finding, what is resolved, what is mitigated, and what honestly remains.

A meta-caution stated once and not repeated: the "test" performed here is a confrontation of the game model with the *published archival scholarship* (Finlay on Venice, Rubinstein and the Brown *Tratte* project on Florence), not with the manuscripts directly. That is a real limit on how decisive the test can be, and it is registered in the disposition.

## Part VIII — The Game Model Against the Record

The adversarial review's sharpest methodological charge (finding 7) was that a games reading risks fitting an equilibrium to a known outcome rather than testing it. The remedy is to put the model where it can fail: against the records of what actually happened. Done for the two paradigm cases, this yields an instructive split — the model is *qualified* by the Venetian evidence and *confirmed* by the Florentine.

### VIII.1 Venice: the model qualified by the *broglio*

The mechanism-design claim was that the Venetian constitution made the patrician contest collusion-resistant and positive-sum. The exact device, to source it properly this time, was the Doge election fixed in 1268 and used essentially unchanged until 1789: thirty members of the Great Council chosen by lot, reduced by lot to nine; the nine elect forty; the forty reduced by lot to twelve; the twelve elect twenty-five; reduced by lot to nine; the nine elect forty-five; reduced by lot to eleven; the eleven elect the forty-one who finally elect the Doge by a qualified majority of at least twenty-five of forty-one — some ten alternating stages of lot and election, with family quotas and a ban on campaigning (the protocol is set out in the historical literature and has been analyzed by modern security researchers as a deliberately manipulation-resistant procedure; cf. the formal treatments noted in the prior companion).

But the archive of Venetian political life, above all Robert Finlay's reconstruction from Sanudo and Priuli, shows that the design did **not** eliminate strategic behavior — it *channeled* it. Routine elections to the hundreds of salaried magistracies were saturated with *broglio*: the semi-licit electioneering, named for the orchard that once grew beside the Ducal Palace, in which nobles solicited votes on the stairways and in the courtyards, "chanted the names of their favorites, whispered entreaties and threats, made deals, exchanged money," and bet on outcomes — so that, in Finlay's phrase, the *piazza del broglio* effectively extended into the palace itself, while the Signoria sat "usually helpless to stop the lobbying for votes" (Finlay 1980). The Great Council was at once an electoral assembly, a permanent convention, an exclusive club, and a job market, and within it the *Primi* — the handful of leading men of the great houses — dominated daily political life; "big fish," the diarist Priuli noted, "eat the smaller" (Finlay 1980). The famous concord of Venice was, at the level of practice, a managed and channeled competition, not its absence.

The disciplined conclusion is precise: the Venetian mechanism succeeded at its hardest task — preventing any single faction from *decisively capturing* the headship of state, which is why the dogeship never became hereditary and the Republic never fell to a signore — but it did not, and could not, suppress the pervasive, lower-stakes strategic maneuver that the *broglio* institutionalized. The model is therefore *qualified by the evidence*: it predicts the right thing about decisive capture and overclaims if it is read as having made Venice the placid, faction-free polity of its own myth. This qualification is exactly the kind the games framing must accept; it is the model meeting data and bending.

### VIII.2 Florence: the model confirmed by the *Tratte*

Where Venice qualifies the model, Florence confirms it, and confirms specifically the prediction that a *small-pool* lottery degrades into a *weighted* lottery open to capture (Dowlen 2009). Here the archive is unusually direct, because the Florentine office-holding records — the *Tratte*, now digitized in the Brown University project drawing on Rubinstein's and Najemy's reconstructions — register the manipulation in the documents themselves.

Two mechanisms of capture are visible, and both are pool-control rather than abolition of the lot. The first is *loading the pool*: from about 1387 a special purse, the *Borsellino*, was composed of artisans deemed "veri guelfi" — reliably loyal to the regime, "quelli cittadini li quali paresse loro che fossero molto confidenti allo Stato loro," as the chronicler Boninsegni put it — so that the draw, however genuinely random, drew only from pre-vetted loyalists. The second is *bypassing the draw*: under the Medici the *accoppiatori* (electoral officials) increasingly made selections *a mano* — "by hand," from a supplied list — rather than by sealed sortition, choosing men both trustworthy to the Medici and already "veduti" (Rubinstein, summarized in the *Tratte* overview and the recent literature). The records carry the fingerprints: for the October elections of 1433 and 1434, around Cosimo's banishment and return, the names of the Signoria are simply missing from the *Giornali delle Tratte*, marked "*hic deficit prioratus*," indicating offices filled by appointment rather than by the recorded draw. And the concentration is measurable: of those declared eligible for the supreme office of Gonfaloniere di Giustizia in the 1466 scrutiny, forty came from just five families (Rubinstein). The regime's own anxiety about an *un*rigged process surfaces in the Medicean complaint that "*scrutinium omnia perturbavit*" — the scrutiny had thrown everything into confusion.

The model fits, and a contested historiographical point falls out of it. Rubinstein argued, against the older thesis of a covert Medici "signoria," that the constitutional forms were *adapted* rather than abolished — the republic continued, dominated by leading families, "without marking an official departure from the city's constitutional system." The games reading dissolves the apparent dispute: capture-through-the-forms is exactly what robust action and the weighted lottery predict. The Medici did not need to break the machine; they needed only to control the pool and, when necessary, bypass the draw — which is precisely what an actor maximizing control while minimizing the legibility of his control would do. The forms survived *because* they were the instrument of the capture, not an obstacle to it.

### VIII.3 What the contrast establishes

The split result is itself the methodological payoff and the answer to finding 7. The *same* mechanism-design framework explains the Venetian resilience (a large, randomized, multi-stage procedure that defeated decisive capture) and the Florentine capture (a small, pre-loadable, bypassable lottery), and it does so *against* the records rather than by fitting them: it correctly predicts where the design would hold and where it would fail, it is qualified where the Venetian evidence demands, and it adjudicates a genuine historiographical dispute (covert signoria versus adapted republic) in Florence's case. Used this way — to discriminate between rival readings of the evidence, and held open to qualification — the games lens earns its keep. Used to declare every outcome optimal after the fact, it would not; the discipline is in the difference.

## Part IX — A Hard Out-of-Sample Case: The Polish *Liberum Veto*

The adversarial review asked specifically whether the mechanism-design framework predicts *failure* as well as it rationalizes success, and named the Polish-Lithuanian *liberum veto* as the test. It is the right case precisely because it is the photographic negative of the Venetian design, and because the framework's verdict on it can be checked against a well-documented collapse.

**The device.** In the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth the *sejm* operated under a unanimity rule: any single deputy could halt all business and annul everything the session had passed by crying *Nie pozwalam!* ("I do not allow!") or *Sisto activitatem!*. The rule grew from the principle of unanimous consent and the Commonwealth's federative structure, and it had a serious rationale — deputies were bound by *instructions* from their local *sejmiki*, so they could not consent to what their constituents had not authorized; it protected minorities and regional interests, forced consensus-building, and guarded against both majority tyranny and a strengthened, potentially absolutist crown. Its first use to dissolve a session came in 1652, with the deputy Władysław Siciński, and the rule lasted until the Constitution of 3 May 1791 abolished it (Britannica; Wikipedia, drawing on Konopczyński).

**The verdict the framework gives — and the evidence confirms.** A unanimity rule with no randomizing step, binding mandates, and an open door to outside actors does something exactly opposite to the Venetian design. Venice *dispersed and randomized* so that no actor could control the outcome; the *sejm* *concentrated absolute blocking power in every actor*, so that the cost of controlling the outcome collapsed to the price of the single cheapest deputy. The framework therefore predicts catastrophic vulnerability to hold-up and to external capture — and that is precisely the documented history: foreign powers, Russia and Prussia above all, habitually bribed a single deputy to break a session and block reforms, taxes, and military mobilization, and "the dissenting deputy was usually an instrument of a magnate or even of the king" (Britannica). The paralysis is quantifiable: of roughly 150 *sejms* held between 1573 and 1763, about a third passed no legislation at all, mostly because of the veto; under Augustus II, ten of eighteen *sejms* were wrecked by it — and "Polish parliament" entered several European languages as a byword for deadlock (Wikipedia; Britannica).

**Why this strengthens the framework rather than merely illustrating it.** Two features make the *liberum veto* a genuine out-of-sample test rather than a post-hoc rationalization. First, the prediction of failure is *derivable from the design alone*, in advance of the outcome: maximal individual veto power plus no randomization plus external bribers entails hold-up. Second — and this is the subtler confirmation — the framework also predicts the rule's long period of *success*, and the political-economy literature bears this out: under benign conditions the unanimity rule worked, securing religious peace in a deeply fractionalized polity and protecting minorities through an era of confessional war and rising absolutism, and it was "used sparingly" in the seventeenth century (Klick and Parisi 2003; the *Constitutional Political Economy* analysis of unanimity and fractionalization, 2008). The rule was, in Greif's terms, a *self-undermining equilibrium*: well-suited to one problem (fractionalization and minority protection) and fatal under a different one (external predation and military emergency), tipping from asset to catastrophe when the environment changed. That the framework predicts *both* the conditional success and the conditional failure — and that the historical record matches both — is the strongest vindication available short of original archival work, and it is the discipline the adversarial review asked for.

## Part X — Beyond Italy: The Shared Legal Substrate

The adversarial review charged both prior documents with confinement to Italy (finding 6). Lifting that confinement does more than add cases: it confirms and generalizes the prior study's central thesis — that the deep structural inheritance of Renaissance deliberation was *legal* rather than philosophical — by showing the same Romano-canonical apparatus at work across Latin Christendom.

**The assemblies.** Representative bodies on the same pattern arose well beyond Italy. In Iberia the *Cortes* of Castile, León, Aragón-Catalonia, Navarre, Valencia, and Majorca met in three estates — ecclesiastical nobility, secular nobility, and representatives of the towns — with townsmen attending from the late twelfth century (the Cortes of León of 1188 is conventionally cited as the earliest such assembly with urban representatives); the Aragonese crown-and-estates relation hardened into an explicitly contractual "pactism." In France the assemblies summoned under Philip the Fair, including the assembly of towns of March 1318, did comparable work. In England, Bracton already carries the consent maxim, and Edward I's summonses culminated in the parliament of 1295 whose writ invoked it. And in the Empire, the Golden Bull of 1356 fixed the election of the King of the Romans in a college of seven prince-electors — a solution so much in the constitutional air that Nicholas of Cusa, in the *De concordantia catholica* of 1433/34, proposed a refined electoral method for it.

**The common apparatus.** What unifies these bodies is not a shared philosophy but a shared legal technology, and the scholarship that established this is Gaines Post's (*Traditio* 1, 1943; *Studies in Medieval Legal Thought*, 1964), with Brian Tierney and, more recently, Cary Nederman. Two Roman-private-law instruments, reworked by the canonists, did the load-bearing work. The first is *plena potestas* — full power of attorney: a corporation could appoint an agent empowered to bind it. Because classical Roman law had developed no adequate theory of agency (an agent could not bind his principal to an agreement with a third party), the canonists, governing a Church "honeycombed with corporate bodies" — cathedral chapters, religious houses, colleges, monasteries — were driven to formulate a sophisticated law of agency in which *plena potestas* was central; carried into secular practice, it let a summoned representative commit those who sent him (in Aragon, delegates were dispatched with *plena et libera potestas* in 1301 and *pleno posse* in 1311). The second is *quod omnes tangit ab omnibus approbetur* — what touches all must be approved by all — which derived not from Roman public law (silent on representation and consent) but from an unremarkable clause of private law on co-guardianship, and whose first conclusive use at a church council came at Bourges in November 1225, when cathedral-chapter representatives rejected a tax to finance papal government. Joined, the corporation, *plena potestas*, and *quod omnes tangit* furnished a transferable grammar of representation and consent that princes across Europe could neither ignore nor monopolize.

**The payoff.** The Italian republics were therefore one regional variant of a continent-wide legal-corporate repertoire, not a sui generis invention — which is exactly what the prior study's thesis predicts and which strengthens it. And the comparison yields a clean typology of solutions to the single design problem of constituting legitimate collective decision and orderly succession: the Venetian *lottery*, the Florentine *scrutiny*, the Imperial *fixed electorate*, the estates-with-*plena-potestas* of Iberia, France, and England, and — at the failure pole — the Polish *unanimity rule*. Each is a different engineered answer to the same question, and reading them together is what makes the answers legible as a set.

## Part XI — The Transmission Route Restored

The review's third strand of finding 6 was that the prior account of the "recovery" of Greek thought omitted its prehistory through Byzantium and Islam — and that omission is bound up with finding 2, the charge that the clean separation of legal substrate from philosophical inheritance was too tidy. Restoring the transmission route resolves both.

**The two routes.** The Aristotelian corpus reached Latin Christendom by two channels, and neither was a simple "rediscovery." The first ran through the Semitic world: begun by Syriac-speaking Christian translators, carried into Arabic, and elaborated by the great Islamic philosophers — Avicenna in the eleventh century, Averroes in the twelfth, with Alfarabi before them and Maimonides shaping the parallel Jewish tradition — before passing into Latin through Spain and Sicily in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries. The Parisian arts faculty made all the known works of Aristotle mandatory reading in 1255, which raised Averroes' commentaries to the status of the principal secondary literature of university culture and provoked the controversy over Latin Averroism; Arabic-to-Latin translation then largely ceased around 1300 and resumed only after 1480, the Renaissance translations produced mostly by Italian Jews from Hebrew versions and centered on the universities, above all Padua (Hasse; the Stanford Encyclopedia's surveys of Arabic-Islamic influence and of Renaissance Aristotelianism). The second channel ran directly from the Greek: James of Venice translated from Constantinople in the mid-twelfth century, and William of Moerbeke rendered the bulk of Aristotle — including, decisively, the *Politics* — straight from Greek around 1260, furnishing the text on which Aquinas built his Aristotelian political philosophy; by about 1270 nearly the whole corpus was available in Latin. The fall of Constantinople in 1453 and the Council of Florence then brought a wave of learned Greeks to Italy — George of Trebizond, Theodore of Gaza, John Argyropoulos (who taught at Florence and commented on the *Ethics*), Cardinal Bessarion (who translated the *Metaphysics*), and Gemistos Plethon — completing the recovery of the Greek originals.

**The asymmetry — and the telling wrinkle.** The two civilizations held complementary halves of the inheritance. Islam possessed Plato's *Republic* and *Laws* but lacked Aristotle's *Politics*; Latin Christendom, after Moerbeke, possessed the *Politics* but lacked the *Republic* and the *Laws* until the Renaissance (Encyclopedia.com; Stanford Encyclopedia). The most revealing single fact follows from this: Averroes, the supreme Aristotelian commentator, wrote his commentary on Plato's *Republic* as an avowed *second best* — "his original project required a commentary on Aristotle's *Politics*, but since this book is unavailable, he turns to Aristotle's master," reading Plato through Aristotelian lenses in a tradition of which Alfarabi was the political master (Bryn Mawr Classical Review on Averroes' *Epitome of Plato's Republic*). The greatest medieval reading of the *Republic* exists precisely because the *Politics* never reached the Arab world.

**Resolution of finding 2.** Restoring this route corrects the prior study's overtidy partition. Because the *Politics* entered Latin learning through Moerbeke around 1260 and Aquinas, Aristotelian political categories — the natural origin of the *communitas*, the common good, distributive justice, the typology of constitutions — had permeated juristic and scholastic thought a full century before the humanist "recovery." The jurists and the conciliarists were not working in a sealed legal compartment: Bartolus and the commentators absorbed Aristotelian categories, and Marsilius of Padua was a jurist-trained Aristotelian whose *valentior pars* fused the two streams. The other half of the correction is older still: Roman jurisprudence had itself taken up Stoic natural-law conceptions in antiquity — the *ius naturale* and *ius gentium* of the jurists owe a debt to Stoic ideas transmitted in part through Cicero — so the "legal" inheritance was never free of Greek philosophy either. The restated thesis is accordingly more accurate and, if anything, richer than the original: the legal inheritance supplied the procedural and corporative materials, and the philosophical inheritance the analytical and legitimating vocabulary, but these were *interpenetrating traditions* that had been cross-fertilizing since late antiquity and intensively since the thirteenth century — not two separate pipelines feeding a common reservoir. The distinction between structure and legitimation remains a useful heuristic; the claim that the two streams ran in separate channels does not, and is withdrawn.

## Part XII — Disposition of the Adversarial Findings

Each finding of Part VII is taken in turn, with a status and the resolution. The statuses are honest: *resolved*, *partly resolved*, *mitigated* (real improvement, but the defect is intrinsic and cannot be eliminated), and *standing*.

**1. Strategic dimension absent from the original — RESOLVED.** Supplied by the companion (Parts V–VI: the emic theory of maneuver and the etic formal toolkit, the games lens applied case by case) and tested here against the records (Part VIII). The original's omission is now both filled and examined.

**2. The legitimation-versus-design thesis too tidy — RESOLVED.** Part XI shows why the clean partition fails: the *Politics* entered Latin learning via Moerbeke around 1260, so Aristotelian political categories had permeated juristic and scholastic thought a century before the humanist recovery (Bartolus absorbed them; Marsilius fused the streams), and Roman jurisprudence had itself taken up Stoic natural law in antiquity. The corrected thesis — interpenetrating traditions rather than separate pipelines — is stated, and the claim of separate channels is explicitly withdrawn. The structure-versus-legitimation distinction is retained only as a heuristic.

**3. The ideology-versus-practice claim overreached — RESOLVED.** The inference that everyday deliberation "proceeded in a plainer idiom" is corrected to the weaker and accurate claim that the *Consulte e Pratiche* protocols are summaries and are *silent* on spoken register. The practice-side argument no longer rests on inferred rhetoric: it now rests on documented *behavior* — the Venetian *broglio* and the Florentine *a mano* manipulations (Part VIII) — which is the firmer ground the review demanded.

**4. Sortition mischaracterized as quasi-democratic — RESOLVED.** The companion (Part V) re-grounds the lot as primarily anti-faction and anti-bribery, its democratic reading overstated (Buchstein 2019); Part VIII.2 then documents the predicted small-pool capture in the Florentine archive (the *Borsellino* loading the pool, *a mano* bypassing the draw, forty of the 1466 Gonfaloniere-eligible from five families). The corrected account is both theoretical and evidenced.

**5. Specific factual and sourcing points — RESOLVED in the main, PARTLY on one item.** The Doge protocol is now set out in full and sourced (Part VIII.1); *Pastor Aeternus* (1516, Leo X, Lateran V) was disambiguated from the 1870 constitution in the companion; the "owes almost nothing to Greek philosophy" overstatement is corrected in Part XI. The date of Perotti's Latin Polybius remains approximate (mid-1450s) — a residual imprecision, flagged rather than papered over.

**6. Underweight exclusion, non-Italian bodies, and transmission — RESOLVED on two strands, PARTLY on one.** The non-Italian assemblies are treated in Part X (the Cortes, the French and English assemblies, the Imperial electoral college), and shown to share the Italian republics' legal substrate — which strengthens rather than merely supplements the central thesis. The transmission route through Islam and Byzantium is restored in Part XI. Exclusion is now foregrounded throughout: Venice enfranchised roughly five percent of the population, some two thousand adult noble men, of whom about sixty percent attended (Finlay 1980); Florentine politics was, in Najemy's phrase, the politics of elites. The *liberum veto* adds a deep non-Italian case (Part IX). *Partly*, because the Swiss *Tagsatzung* and the Hanseatic diets are named but not analyzed — a remaining breadth gap, honestly retained.

**7. The analytic-narratives hazard in the games lens — MITIGATED, NOT ELIMINABLE.** This is the one finding that cannot be "resolved," because it is a permanent epistemic condition of the method, not a fixable error. Two disciplines reduce it and are now exercised rather than promised: the model was put where it could fail, against the records, and was duly *qualified* by the Venetian evidence (Part VIII.1); and it was subjected to an out-of-sample case whose failure it predicts in advance from the design alone (Part IX). A further, candid limit compounds the hazard: the "test" confronts the model with the *published archival scholarship*, not the manuscripts, so it can corroborate or strain the model but cannot decisively refute it. The standing instruction stands — every equilibrium claim is an interpretation, strongest where it adjudicates between rival historiographies, weakest where it merely re-describes an outcome as optimal.

**8. Bibliographic looseness — RESOLVED.** The addendum below gives precise citations with tiers, and flags the works cited at one remove and the reference sources used only to locate scholarship.

**Residual limitations, stated plainly.** Three things remain open after this round. The Swiss and Hanseatic cases are unanalyzed. Perotti's date is approximate. And the empirical test, resting on others' archival reconstructions rather than original manuscript work, is corroborative rather than dispositive — it can show the model fits or strains, not that it is uniquely correct. None of these is hidden; each is the honest edge of what three iterations can establish.

**Confidence.** High on the descriptive scholarship and on the corrective findings (1–6, 8), which would survive review. Medium on the constructive equilibrium readings, with the analytic-narratives hazard (7) the standing reason for the discount. The split verdict of the archival test — Venice qualifying the model, Florence confirming it, the *liberum veto* validating it out-of-sample — is the firmest result of the three documents, precisely because it is the one place the model was given the chance to break.

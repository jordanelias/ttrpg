# Historical Combat Manuals: An Intensive Research Document

A study of the premodern manual literature of personal combat across cultures. The body of writing — fencing manuals, military training texts, koryū transmissions, geometric treatises, oral traditions late-codified — constitutes a deep cross-cultural record of how human beings systematized the problem of striking and not being struck.

## Methodology and limits

The treatment is in two parts. **Part I** is per-tradition: each tradition's foundational texts, masters, and named concepts, with the theoretical contribution made explicit. **Part II** is a five-axis re-cut along the mechanics of an engagement — stances, approaches/initiation, footwork, grip, techniques — the dimensions in the order a fighter meets them inside a fight, applied across the traditions.

The two parts are different lenses on the same material. The per-tradition view foregrounds what each culture distinctively produced. The engagement-axis view foregrounds what each tradition emphasized within shared categories.

**Limits to acknowledge upfront.** The analysis privileges traditions with substantial surviving theoretical writing. African martial traditions (Engolo, Nuba and Senegalese wrestling, Dambe boxing, Zulu stick-fighting), Oceanian (Maori taiaha, Hawaiian lua), and Mesoamerican (macuahuitl-and-shield combat) are largely absent — not because their combat traditions were less developed but because their textual record is thinner, different in kind (oral, ceremonial-pedagogical, archaeological-only), or inaccessible at the granularity this document operates at. The findings should be read as "patterns across the textually-developed traditions" rather than "patterns across all human combat practice."

A second limitation: confidence varies across traditions. The German and Italian fencing corpora are extensively documented and accessible. The Japanese koryū tradition is documented and continuous. The Chinese late-Ming corpus is documented. South Asian premodern texts, Mamluk furusiyya, and pre-codification Filipino traditions are accessible primarily through secondary scholarship; technical-vocabulary granularity in these sections is correspondingly lower.

# Part I — The Per-Tradition Treatment

## § 1. European: the developed manual traditions

The European corpus is the largest body of premodern fencing theory in any single linguistic family — partly because of Latin literacy, partly because the print revolution caught the Italian masters at exactly the right moment. It has one structural feature that distinguishes it from the East Asian traditions: it survives as *text without living lineage*. The Liechtenauer system died out; HEMA (Historical European Martial Arts) is a reconstructive movement from the manuals upward.

### The Liechtenauer tradition (German longsword, c.1389–1570)

Johannes Liechtenauer (late 14th century, dates uncertain) wrote a set of mnemonic verses, the *Zettel*, that anchor the entire German longsword tradition. The verses are deliberately obscure — protection of the art from the uninitiated. The corpus survives through *glosses* (commentaries) by named masters: Hanko Döbringer (c.1389, the earliest gloss), Sigmund Ringeck (mid-15th c.), Pseudo-Peter von Danzig, Paulus Kal, Hans Talhoffer (illustrated *Fechtbücher* across decades), and Joachim Meyer (*Gründtliche Beschreibung*, 1570, the late synthesis already drifting toward sportification).

The system is built on a small set of named primitives:

- **Vier Leger** (four guards): *Ochs* (ox, high thrusting guard), *Pflug* (plow, low thrusting guard), *Vom Tag* (from the roof, raised cutting guard), *Alber* (fool, low cutting guard). Two pairs — high/low thrusting, high/low cutting.
- **Drei Wunder** (three wonders): *hauen, stechen, schneiden* — cut, thrust, slice. All offensive action reduces to these three.
- **Fünf Meisterhäue** (five master cuts): *Zornhau* (wrath), *Krumphau* (crooked), *Zwerchhau* (cross), *Schielhau* (squinting), *Scheitelhau* (crown). Each master cut also serves a *Versetzen* function — it both attacks and breaks one of the four guards.
- **Vor / Nach / Indes** — the initiative dialectic. *Vor* is the initiative-holding action; *Nach* the response; *Indes* (the during/instant) the moment in which initiative can switch on contact. *Indes* is the conceptual centerpiece — it names the moment of decision *inside* an exchange.
- **Stark / Schwach** — strong and weak of the blade (leverage near the hilt vs. the tip). All blade contact is read in these terms.
- **Fühlen** — feeling. Once blades touch, you read the opponent through tactile pressure, not vision. The German tradition's most important theoretical claim: *vision lies, contact tells truth*.
- **Winden** — winding. With contact established, you rotate around the bind to threaten new lines without breaking engagement.
- **Zufechten / Krieg / Abzug** — the three phases of engagement: approach, war (close-measure exchange including bind), withdrawal.

The signature design move is using these primitives combinatorially. A complete *Stück* (named play) chains a master cut, a winding, a thrust or cut from the bind, and a specific response to each opponent counter. The manuals contain on the order of a hundred named plays; the underlying combinatorics produce thousands.

Earlier still: **Royal Armouries MS I.33** (c.1300, southern Germany), the earliest extant fencing manual in any tradition. Sword and buckler. Depicts a tonsured priest instructing a scholar (and one woman, "Walpurgis"). Uses its own vocabulary — *custodia* (guard), *obsessio* (besieging the opponent's guard), *plaga* (attack) — which does not map cleanly onto later Liechtenauer terms. I.33 demonstrates that European fencing theory predates Liechtenauer by a century and was not one lineage.

### The Italian school (Fiore → rapier line, c.1410–1610)

**Fiore dei Liberi**, *Flos Duellatorum* / *Fior di Battaglia* (c.1410), under the patronage of Niccolò III d'Este of Ferrara. Multiple manuscripts (Pisani Dossi, Getty, Morgan, Paris). Fiore organizes his system under **four virtues** — *avvisamento/prudentia*, *celeritas*, *fortitudo*, *audatia* — each assigned an animal: lynx, tiger, lion, elephant. The system covers wrestling, dagger, sword in one hand, sword in two hands, sword in armor, polearm, mounted combat. Fiore's theoretical move is to treat all weapons as instances of a common geometry: the *poste* (guards) and *colpi* (blows) of one weapon translate to another by analogy.

The Italian tradition then runs through Bolognese sidesword (Achille Marozzo, *Opera Nova*, 1536) and arrives at the **rapier line**, the European tradition's theoretical peak:

- **Camillo Agrippa**, *Trattato di Scientia d'Arme* (1553). Agrippa was a mathematician and engineer. He reduces the Italian guards to four — *prima, seconda, terza, quarta* — derived from natural positions of the arm during the draw. The theoretical move is *geometric reduction*: instead of cataloging the many guards prior masters had named, Agrippa argues from first principles that there are only four positions the arm can hold and these exhaust the space.
- **Salvator Fabris**, *Lo Schermo overo Scienza d'Arme* (1606). Court swordsman to Christian IV of Denmark. Codifies a system of *opposition* — every attack is paired with a defensive component (covering the line you open as you commit).
- **Ridolfo Capo Ferro**, *Gran Simulacro* (1610). The most influential rapier text. Codifies the **lunge** — the extension of the rear leg that drives the body forward without committing it. The canonical Italian rapier vocabulary is here:
  - **Tempo** — the unit of action; a single decisive motion.
  - **Mezzo tempo** — half-tempo; an attack that intercepts the opponent's motion in its first phase, before completion.
  - **Contratempo** — counter-time; defeating the opponent's attack-into-preparation.
  - **Misura** — measure; *larga* (long), *stretta* (close).
  - **Stringere** — to constrain the opponent's blade, taking the line.
  - **Primo intenzione / secondo intenzione** — first intention (commit to the attack) vs. second intention (provoke a response and counter it).
  - **Cavazione** — disengagement, slipping around an opposition.
  - **Invito** — invitation, leaving an opening to draw an attack.

Taken as a whole, the Italian theoretical move is to treat fencing as a *temporal science*. The German tradition foregrounds blade contact; the Italian foregrounds time and distance. They are dual descriptions of the same combat, and the convergence is one of the more striking facts in the European corpus.

### La Verdadera Destreza (Spanish, 1582 onward)

**Jerónimo Sánchez de Carranza**, *De la Filosofia de las Armas* (1582). Founder of *La Verdadera Destreza* — "the true skill." **Don Luis Pacheco de Narváez** systematized the tradition through the early 17th century.

The signature theoretical move is the *mystical circle*: a geometric framework treating the engagement as motion within a circle whose diameter is the opponent's reach. Angles of attack and footwork are computed from the geometry of the circle. The Destreza stance is upright (rather than the deep crouch of the contemporary Italian); the steps are circular rather than straight-line; the philosophical claim is that fencing is a deductive science derivable from first principles.

Named vocabulary: *ángulo recto* (the right-angle baseline posture), *atajo* (constraint of the opponent's blade — Spanish analog to *stringere*), six named *movimientos* (movements: *natural, violento, reductivo, accidental, extraño, mixto*), and several named *compases* (steps: *recto, transverso, curvo*, and variants). The tradition continued in Spain and Spanish colonial holdings well into the 19th century.

*Confidence: medium. Destreza scholarship is reviving but mid-period technical details remain contested.*

### George Silver and the English argument (1599)

**George Silver**, *Paradoxes of Defence* (1599) and *Brief Instructions on My Paradoxes of Defence* (c.1605). Silver wrote explicitly against the Italian rapier masters teaching in London, arguing that the long thin rapier was a duelist's weapon useless in war and that the English short sword (backsword) with shield or alone was superior.

His theoretical contribution is the doctrine of **true times**:
*time of the hand; time of the hand and body; time of the body; time of the body and foot; time of the foot and body.*

The hand is fastest; the foot slowest. A true time moves the fast part first or alone; a false time moves a slow part first. Silver's claim is that Italian rapier requires false times (long thrusts driven by the lunge) and is therefore biomechanically unsafe.

A second concept: the **true place** — the distance from which you can strike them and they cannot strike you. Silver's argument against rapier turns on this: he claims the long thin sword does not let its user reach the true place against a shorter, broader weapon.

Silver matters not because he was right (he was partly right at most) but because his text is *an argument inside the European tradition* — a documented design debate, demonstrating that the masters knew they proposed competing systems and held the conversation in print.

## § 2. Japanese kenjutsu

The Japanese corpus differs from the European in three structural ways. First, it survives as *living lineage* — the kata and oral transmission have passed master-to-student in mostly unbroken chains in some ryū (koryū bujutsu / "old-school martial arts"). Second, the philosophical infrastructure is explicitly Buddhist (often Zen) and Confucian in a way the European tradition is not. Third, the canonical texts are fewer but more philosophical — *Go Rin no Sho* and *Heihō Kadensho* are read as texts of strategic philosophy outside martial-arts circles in a way that Capo Ferro is not.

### The early lineages

**Tenshin Shōden Katori Shintō-ryū**, founded c.1447 by Iizasa Chōisai Ienao, is often cited as the oldest extant koryū. **Kashima Shintō-ryū** and **Kashima Shin-ryū** are roughly contemporary. These early ryū are syncretic — sword, spear (yari), naginata, staff (bō), grappling (jūjutsu) — because the battlefield environment they responded to required all of them.

**Shinkage-ryū**, founded by **Kamiizumi Nobutsuna** (mid-16th c.), is the parent of the most influential later lineage, **Yagyū Shinkage-ryū**, instructors to the Tokugawa shōguns through the Edo period. Yagyū Munenori (1571–1646), head of the Edo Yagyū line, taught Tokugawa Ieyasu, Hidetada, and Iemitsu.

**Itto-ryū**, founded by Itō Ittōsai (late 16th c.). Technical signature: *kiriotoshi* — "the cut that cuts down" — a single descending cut that simultaneously parries and strikes the attacker's centerline. Itto-ryū's theoretical claim is that all techniques reduce to one cut performed with correct timing and intent. *Issatsu no tachi* — "the one-killing sword."

**Niten Ichi-ryū**, founded by Miyamoto Musashi (1584–1645). Two-sword style. Musashi's claim: the long and short sword (or any weapon in either hand) can be used together because the body has two hands and the conventional grip wastes one.

### The two canonical texts

**Miyamoto Musashi**, *Go Rin no Sho* / *Book of Five Rings* (1645). Written in the last years of his life at the Reigando cave outside Kumamoto. Five scrolls:

- *Earth* — foundation: posture, strategy as carpentry, the principle that strategy is one whether at the scale of a duel or an army.
- *Water* — the technical core: stance (he names five then immediately argues fixed stances are wrong — *"there are stances and no stances"*), grip, footwork, eye, the five attitudes.
- *Fire* — combat itself: the three shouts, methods of taking initiative, crushing strategies.
- *Wind* — critique of other schools. Musashi explicitly polemicizes against schools emphasizing specific stances, long swords, short swords, fast footwork, many techniques.
- *Void* — what cannot be written. The state of mastery in which technique disappears.

The most-cited concept: *the gaze of perception is strong, the gaze of sight is weak* (*kan no metsuke / ken no metsuke*). Perceiving the opponent's intent matters more than seeing motion. This is the Japanese parallel to Liechtenauer's *Fühlen* — the claim that surface information misleads.

**Yagyū Munenori**, *Heihō Kadensho* / *The Family-Transmitted Book on the Art of War* (1632). Three sections:

- *Shinrikyō* — guidance toward fundamental principles.
- *Satsujinken* — "the killing sword." The sword that takes life.
- *Katsujinken* — "the life-giving sword." The sword that preserves life. The doctrine that the highest application of swordsmanship is to make killing unnecessary — to demonstrate so total a mastery that the opponent cannot fight.

Munenori is inseparable from his close association with the Rinzai Zen monk **Takuan Sōhō**, whose *Fudōchi Shinmyōroku* (Record of the Mysterious Power of Immovable Wisdom) is a letter addressed to Munenori on the relation of Zen and swordsmanship. Takuan's contributions:

- **Mushin** — "no-mind." The mind that does not stop on any one thing. If the mind fixes on the opponent's sword, it cannot respond to a feint; if it fixes on its own technique, it cannot respond to circumstance.
- **Fudōshin** — "immovable mind." Stillness inside motion.
- The parable: the mind that stops at the opponent's sword is like water frozen — useless. The mind that flows is like water moving — useful.

### Technical vocabulary

- **Maai** — combat distance. *Issoku ittō no maai* — "one-step, one-cut distance," the distance from which a single committed step closes to a cutting strike. The direct counterpart to *misura*.
- **Kamae** — guards/stances. Five basic (*chūdan, jōdan, gedan, hassō, waki*), with ryū-specific variations.
- **Sen** — initiative, layered into a three- or four-fold scheme:
  - *Go-no-sen* — taking initiative *after* the opponent's attack begins (counter-initiative).
  - *Sen-no-sen* — taking initiative *simultaneously* with the opponent's attack (interception).
  - *Sen-sen-no-sen* (or *tai-no-sen*) — taking initiative *before* the opponent's attack begins, by reading intent.

- **Suki** — opening. Any moment in the opponent's movement, mental state, or stance in which they cannot respond.
- **Mushin / Zanshin / Kiai** — no-mind during, continued awareness after, vocal/spirit projection within.
- **Ki-ken-tai-no-ichi** — "spirit, sword, body as one" — the modern kendo formulation requiring all three to coincide at the moment of a valid strike.

### Theoretical contribution

The Japanese tradition's distinctive move is to ground combat theory in *consciousness theory*. German reads the opponent through the blade (*Fühlen*); Italian through time and distance (*tempo*, *misura*); Japanese through intent (*kan no metsuke*, *sen-sen-no-sen*, *mushin*). All three describe the same underlying problem — *how to act inside another agent's decision loop* — but the Japanese formulation makes the agent's mind the explicit strategic object.

The other distinctive move is the **killing sword / life-giving sword** distinction — the assertion that mastery is *not* killing, that demonstrated mastery preempts violence. There is no European parallel of comparable theoretical weight.

## § 3. Chinese: the military synthesis

The Chinese tradition is documented differently from the European or Japanese. Most surviving premodern Chinese martial texts are *military* manuals rather than individual fencing manuals — written by generals or officials to train soldiers, not by fencing masters for duelists. The reason is partly cultural (personal combat was less ritualized as honor-combat than in samurai Japan or knightly Europe) and partly practical (late-Ming military reformers responding to specific threats).

### Qi Jiguang and the late-Ming synthesis

**Qi Jiguang** (1528–1588). Ming general, fought *wokō* (Japanese pirate raiders) on the southeast coast and later Mongols in the north. Two major works:

- **Ji Xiao Xin Shu** / *New Treatise on Military Efficiency* (first edition 1560 or 1561; revised 1584). The training manual for the army he built to fight pirates. Chapters on formation, signaling, weaponry (spear, saber, bow, gun-staff), and famously the *thirty-two postures of the long fist* — Qi argues empty-hand training is the foundation of weapon training and documents thirty-two named postures with verse mnemonics.
- **Lian Bing Shi Ji** / *Record of Military Training* (1571). The northern-frontier sequel.

Qi's theoretical contribution is the explicit treatment of training as a *system* — soldiers progress through standardized forms, the standardized forms have been distilled from a survey of existing schools, and the underlying logic is *what works in the formation we use*. Closer in spirit to a modern military trainer than to a fencing master. The thirty-two postures of long fist later influenced the development of taijiquan (which canonizes them in a different form).

### The encyclopedic compilation

**Wubei Zhi** / *Treatise on Military Preparations* (Mao Yuanyi, 1621). 240 *juan*. Encyclopedic compilation of Chinese military knowledge: weapons, formations, signaling, logistics, fortification, naval, intelligence. The weapons chapters preserve techniques from many schools.

The work is particularly important because **it preserved a "Korean sword" (*chao xian shi fa*) section** of techniques that Qi Jiguang's army had encountered — techniques the Koreans subsequently received back from China and incorporated into their own *Muyedobotongji*. The Wubei Zhi is the East Asian counterpart to a Renaissance fechtbuch in compilation form: a curated record of everything the editor could gather.

### Weapon hierarchy and theoretical concepts

Chinese weapon theory recognizes a hierarchy: **qiang (spear)** is "the king of weapons," **gun (staff)** is the foundation of all long weapons, **dao (saber)** is the soldier's weapon, **jian (straight double-edged sword)** is the gentleman's weapon. Each carries its own literature:

- **Wu Shu's *Shou Bi Lu*** — spear treatise.
- **Cheng Zongyou's *Shaolin Gunfa Chan Zong*** (early 17th c.) — codifies the Shaolin staff method.
- **Yu Dayou's *Jian Jing*** (Sword Classic) — confusingly titled, actually about staff. Yu Dayou was Qi Jiguang's contemporary and fellow late-Ming reformer.

Conceptual vocabulary across the corpus:

- **Gang / rou** — hard / soft. The duality through which Chinese theorists analyze every technique. Not opposites but complementary modes.
- **Nei / wai** — internal / external. The later (Qing and 20th c.) distinction between *neijia* (taijiquan, baguazhang, xingyiquan) and *waijia* (often associated with Shaolin) is a retrospective categorization, but the underlying contrast — power generated through structure and intent vs. through muscle — is present in late-Ming texts.
- **Wuxing overlay** — the five-elements correspondence is applied to weapon stances and technique families in xingyiquan and some sword schools. Less a combat principle than a mnemonic-philosophical organizing scheme.
- **Yi / qi / li** — intent / breath-energy / physical force. The three-layered model of how power is generated: intent guides qi, qi mobilizes li.

*Confidence: medium on specific dating and authorship of some named texts; the empty-hand systems we now know as gongfu are heavily shaped by 19th–20th century systematization, and projecting modern technical vocabulary back onto Ming texts can mislead.*

### Theoretical contribution

The Chinese tradition's distinctive moves: (1) treating empty-hand and weapon training as a continuous spectrum rather than separate disciplines; (2) the cosmological overlay (yin/yang, five elements) as a mnemonic-philosophical framework that organizes technique into recognizable patterns; (3) the foregrounding of *spear and staff* as primary weapons in the theoretical literature, against the European and Japanese sword-centrism. This last point matters: most surviving premodern Chinese martial theory is about polearms, and modern over-emphasis on sword and empty-hand is a 20th-century reframing.

## § 4. Korean: synthesis as a national project

The Korean martial-manual tradition is short, late, and self-consciously synthetic.

- **Muyejebo** / *Manual of Martial Arts* (1598). Compiled by Han Gyo at the end of the Imjin War (1592–1598), in which Korean forces fought Japanese invaders with Chinese reinforcement. Six disciplines, drawing heavily on Qi Jiguang's *Ji Xiao Xin Shu*.
- **Muyejebo Sokjip** (1759). Expanded.
- **Muyedobotongji** (1790). Yi Deokmu, Park Jega, Baek Dongsu — three Silhak ("practical learning") scholars commissioned by King Jeongjo. Twenty-four disciplines. The manual explicitly identifies the origin of each technique: Korean indigenous, Chinese (often citing Qi Jiguang or Wubei Zhi), Japanese. **It is the only premodern East Asian martial manual known to make its synthesis explicit as a methodological matter — naming what came from where.**

The theoretical contribution is the synthesis itself. The Muyedobotongji argues, by its structure, that martial knowledge is *transferable across cultures* and that systematic comparison is a legitimate epistemic method. It anticipates by two centuries the comparative move that defines modern HEMA scholarship.

*Confidence: medium-high on the manual's compilation and contents. Korean indigenous martial tradition pre-Imjin is sparsely documented relative to the Chinese and Japanese inputs the manual synthesizes.*

## § 5. Filipino: discretization without manuals

The Filipino martial arts (*eskrima* / *arnis* / *kali* — terms used variously and regionally, the boundaries politically contested) are notable for two facts separating them from every tradition above. First, **no premodern manuals survive** — the tradition is oral, master-to-student, and the codification visible today is largely 20th-century work (Remy Presas' *Modern Arnis* from the 1960s; Dan Inosanto's transmission to the West via Bruce Lee; many regional styles formalized post-WWII). Second, **the system is built around an explicit angle-of-attack discretization** that is unusually systematic compared to its peer traditions.

Most systems teach a numbered set of strikes — commonly 5, 7, or 12 angles, with 12 most widespread. Each numbered angle corresponds to a specific incoming attack line: 1 = downward forehand to head, 2 = downward backhand, 3 = horizontal forehand to body, and so on. Defenses are organized by what they answer in this numbered scheme.

The technical signature is **flow drills** — *hubud-lubud* ("tie/untie"), *sumbrada*, *abecedario* — sequences in which two practitioners exchange strikes and counters in repeating patterns, embedding angle-counter relationships as muscle memory. The drills are the equivalent of HEMA's *Stücke* or kenjutsu's *kata* but executed in continuous flow rather than discrete sets.

Range vocabulary: *largo mano* (long, where only stick or blade tip reaches), *medio* (medium, where the body is engaged), *corto* (close, where elbows, knees, and grappling enter), *serrada* (closed, where the weapon is no longer the primary tool).

Weapon vocabulary: *solo baston* (single stick), *doble baston* (double stick), *espada y daga* (sword and dagger), *daga* (knife), *mano mano* (empty hand). The pedagogical claim — the theoretical move — is that **stick, blade, and empty hand share the same angles**. You learn the angles with the stick because it is safe; the same numbered system applies when the weapon changes.

*Confidence: medium on premodern continuity — claims that the tradition descends from precolonial Filipino warfare and Spanish-era resistance are widespread but documentation is thin; the explicit codification visible today is 20th-century.*

**Theoretical contribution:** the explicit and systematic discretization of attack lines. Other traditions discretize (Liechtenauer's Meisterhäue, the Italian guards-with-corresponding-lines, kenjutsu's named cuts) but the Filipino numbered-angle system is the cleanest and most pedagogically front-loaded.

## § 6. South Asian: deep tradition, thin extant text

**Dhanurveda** — "science of the bow," in classical Indian thought one of the *upavedas* (subsidiary Vedas). Original Dhanurveda texts are largely lost; later references survive in:

- **Agni Purana** (probably 8th–11th c. CE) — chapters on martial science, weapon categorization (*mukta* / loosed, *amukta* / non-loosed, *muktamukta* / loosed-or-not), bow training.
- **Vishnudharmottara Purana**, **Manasollasa** (12th c., King Someshvara III) — references to martial training.
- **Nitisara** of Kamandaki — strategic theory in the Arthashastra lineage.

*Confidence: low-medium on specific textual contents — knowledge is at the level of secondary scholarship rather than direct reading.*

**Living traditions:**

- **Kalaripayattu** (Kerala). Continuous tradition organized in stages: *meipayattu* (body conditioning), *kolthari* (wooden weapons — staff, short stick, curved sticks), *ankathari* (metal weapons — sword, spear, dagger, *urumi* / flexible sword), *verumkai* (empty hand including *marma* / pressure points). Strongly associated with the Nayar caste of Kerala.
- **Silambam** (Tamil Nadu). Staff art with claimed deep antiquity. The staff is the primary weapon and the foundation of broader weapon training.
- **Gatka** (Sikh). Developed 17th–18th century by the Khalsa under Guru Hargobind onward. Sword and stick, with explicit religious-martial integration.
- **Thang-ta** (Manipur). Sword and spear.

**Theoretical contribution:** the integration of martial training with *medical and energetic systems*. *Marma* points correspond to vulnerable nerve and vascular points, with explicit therapeutic counterparts. The Kalaripayattu instructor is traditionally also a healer, and the same points used in attack are used in massage and treatment. This parallels the Chinese integration of martial and qigong/medical practice but is older in textual reference and more explicit pedagogically.

## § 7. Mamluk furusiyya

**Furūsiyya** — the Arabic-Mamluk literature of mounted combat, mostly 13th–15th century. Major texts:

- **Najm al-Dīn al-Aḥdab al-Rammāḥ**, *Kitāb al-Furūsīyya wa al-Manāsib al-Ḥarbīyya* (c.1280s). The major Mamluk military manual, covering lance, bow, sword, the *birjās* (target exercises on horseback).
- **Various Mamluk manuals** through the 14th–15th c. on archery (the Arab composite bow's literature is particularly developed), lance exercises, equestrian games.
- **Ottoman successor literature** — the Ottoman *kılıç* (saber) tradition descends partly from this corpus, with its own treatises.

Furusiyya centers on cavalry exercises that defined Mamluk military identity — *birjās* (targeting), *qabaq* (gourd-shooting on horseback), lance drills against the *rumḥ* target, swordwork from the saddle. The literature is less concerned with foot-fencing than European or East Asian corpora and more with the integrated rider-and-horse as a weapon system.

*Confidence: medium on Mamluk corpus specifics; technical vocabulary of the Mamluk sword treatises is not accessible at the granularity available for Liechtenauer or Musashi.*

**Theoretical contribution:** the explicit treatment of horse-and-rider as the unit of combat, with weapon technique subordinated to the equestrian context. The closest European parallel is the mounted-combat sections of Fiore and the German *Rossfechten* literature, but the Mamluk corpus treats the equestrian dimension as primary rather than as one chapter among many.

# Part II — Five-Axis Decomposition: The Mechanics of an Engagement

A practitioner-oriented re-cut of the same material. Where Part I foregrounds what each tradition distinctively produced, this Part organizes the corpus the way a fighter or coach meets it inside an engagement: the static readiness you adopt (**stance**), the decision of who acts and when (**approach / initiation / first move**), the movement that carries that decision (**footwork**), the hold that arms the action (**grip**), and the named actions themselves (**techniques**). The five axes are sequenced as listed; they are not equal in any tradition, and each manual invests most heavily in writing about whichever axis its students could least drill alone.

Two axes from the earlier four-axis cut are absorbed rather than dropped. *Weapon properties* — range, type, balance — are distributed across grip (how the weapon is held and how force reaches the edge), footwork (reach as the distance footwork manages), and technique (cut/thrust/percussion as weapon-dependent action). *Conditioning* is a precondition, not a phase of the engagement, and is held to a closing note. Nothing in the source is discarded; the material is re-shelved along the engagement sequence.

## The analytical spine: an engagement as an economy of information and tempo

Read across the traditions, the five axes are not five separate topics. They are five moves in a single game — the problem the source names once and then mostly leaves implicit: *how to act effectively inside another agent's decision, under time pressure and incomplete information.* Stated as an economy, the axes line up:

- **Stance is the information you emit.** A held guard is a costly signal — costly because it leaves some lines open, which is exactly what makes it readable. To stand in *Ochs* is to announce "I threaten a thrust from above and have conceded the low line"; the announcement is true, which is why it is useful to the opponent and dangerous to you.
- **Reading intent is the information you acquire**, through three channels of sharply different latency, bandwidth, and reliability — tactile, visual, predictive (developed under Axis II). The traditions' deepest divergences are divergences over *which channel to trust*.
- **Footwork is control of measure**, and measure is the master variable: it gates which information channels are even live and who is permitted to act at all. Out of measure, no channel is open and nobody can strike — the fight has not started. Footwork is the contest over *entry* into the zone where the economy operates.
- **Grip is the mechanical substrate** that fixes what actions are physically possible and — decisively — *how fast you recover from a committed one*. Recovery time is the duration of your commitment, and the duration of your commitment is how long your intent is legible and your defense degraded. Grip therefore sets the price of every action in the only currency that matters mid-exchange: tempo.
- **Initiation is acting at the instant your information is maximal and the opponent's is incomplete** — the whole layered timing vocabulary (*Vor/Nach/Indes*, the *sen* triad, *tempo/contratempo*) is one ranking of *when, relative to the opponent's commitment, you move.*
- **Techniques are the action vocabulary**, and their real depth is not the catalog of named cuts but the *chained* sequences (*Stücke*, *kata*, flow drills), which compile judgment into reflex so that a fighter can act at recognition speed — because nobody can deliberate inside *Indes*.

One claim recurs and is worth stating up front because it organizes everything below: **the weapon's mechanics decide which information channel is dominant, and the dominant channel decides the tradition's theory.** A two-handed longsword tangles into binds constantly, so its tradition (German) builds a theory of *contact* — *Fühlen*, *Winden*, *Indes*. A long thin one-handed rapier cannot hold a leverage bind and rewards staying off the blade, so its tradition (Italian rapier) builds a theory of *time and distance* — *tempo*, *misura*, *contratempo*. The duel-to-the-death context of the samurai rewards reading intent before any commitment at all, so its tradition (Japanese) builds a theory of *consciousness* — *kan no metsuke*, *sen-sen-no-sen*, *mushin*. These are not three different combats. They are three different *sensors* trained on the same combat, each selected by the weapon and the stakes.

## Axis I — Stances (guards)

**A guard is a credible commitment, and credibility is what makes it informative.** The source's slogan "a guard is a question" understates the structure: a guard is costly to hold — every posture defends some lines by abandoning others — and it is precisely the cost that makes the signal trustworthy. The opponent reads your guard not because you have told them your plan but because the geometry of your body cannot lie about which lines are currently cheap for you and which are expensive. The reading game is therefore recursive: your guard tells the opponent what they can safely attempt, which tells you what they will likely attempt, which tells you what your guard should *not* have conceded.

The traditions' guard systems, with the mechanics made explicit:

- **German *Vier Leger*** — *Ochs* (high thrusting), *Pflug* (low thrusting), *Vom Tag* (raised cutting), *Alber* (low cutting). The deeper structure is the cut/thrust split: *Ochs* and *Pflug* are **thrusting** guards because the point already sits on or near the line, so the action they threaten is a small, fast, low-warning extension; *Vom Tag* and *Alber* are **cutting** guards because the blade is loaded above or trailing below for a powered, gravity-assisted arc. A guard pre-loads the biomechanics of one action class; switching classes *from* a guard costs a tempo, because you must first reconfigure the limb. This is the mechanical reason the five *Meisterhäue* are guard-breakers: each master cut is the specific line that attacks the opening a given *Leger* is forced to leave (the *Zwerchhau* across the high opening that *Vom Tag* exposes; the *Krumphau* onto the hands or against *Ochs*; and so on). Guard and counter-guard are co-designed.
- **Italian *poste* and Agrippa's reduction** — Fiore treats the *poste* of one weapon as translatable to another by analogy; Agrippa then performs *geometric reduction* to four guards (*prima → quarta*) derived from the natural positions of the arm through the draw. Read against the recursion problem above, Agrippa's move is a deliberate answer to readability: reduce the guards to positions so close to the arm's neutral mechanics that holding one *leaks minimal information* — you stand in a posture that is barely a posture, hard to read because there is little posture to read.
- **Japanese *kamae*** — *chūdan, jōdan, gedan, hassō, waki*. Musashi names five attitudes in the *Water* scroll and then immediately argues that fixed stances are wrong — *"there are stances and no stances."* This is the *opposite* solution to Agrippa's and reveals that the two greatest reductions in the corpus are answers to one problem. Agrippa minimizes the *information content* of the guard (stand so naturally there is nothing to read); Musashi minimizes the *duration* of the guard (hold nothing, so there is no stable signal to read at all). Both attack the readability of the committed posture; one by flattening the signal, one by refusing to emit a steady one.
- **Spanish *ángulo recto*** — an upright right-angle baseline against the Italian deep crouch. The uprightness is not stylistic: it keeps the body's mass centered over its base so that *circular* footwork around the mystical circle is available at any instant, where a deep lunge-ready crouch has pre-committed to a *linear* line of attack. The stance encodes the school's entire movement geometry.
- **Chinese *shi*** — each weapon carries named postures that constrain the techniques launchable from them; the *wuxing* overlay organizes these into a mnemonic-philosophical pattern. The constraint is the same pre-loading logic as the German guards: a *shi* is a posture from which a specific family of techniques is cheap and others are dear.
- **Filipino** — the least stance-bound developed tradition, because a flow-based system treats the static guard as the very thing to avoid being caught in; its positional vocabulary is one of *range* (*largo / medio / corto / serrada*), i.e., a zone you occupy rather than a shape you hold.

**Cross-tradition synthesis.** A stance is simultaneously a posture (mechanical readiness), a signal (advertised threat and concession), and a trap (an opening offered to draw a specific attack — *Alber*, "the fool," is named ironically because it looks open and is not). The mark of the most developed traditions is that they stop treating the guard as architecture and start treating it as *managed information leakage*: the master either flattens the signal (Agrippa), refuses to hold one (Musashi), or weaponizes a false one (the *invito*, treated under Axis II). The naïve fighter holds a strong guard; the master holds a guard that lies.

## Axis II — Approaches, initiation, and the first move

The largest axis, and the one where the source was most clearly a summary rather than an analysis. Everything here is governed by a single decision variable — *when do I act, relative to the opponent's commitment?* — and three vocabularies that the traditions never reconcile but that map cleanly onto one timeline.

**The approach is the most dangerous phase, for a structural reason.** The German triad *Zufechten → Krieg → Abzug* (approach → close-measure war → withdrawal) names approach as a distinct phase because entering measure is when you expose yourself while your information is still incomplete: you must cross from the zone where no channel is live (out of measure, a staring contest) into the zone where the opponent can hit you, and you do it before you have read them. The first strategic fact of the approach is **reach asymmetry** — whoever can strike first across the closing distance owns the entry — which is why every developed tradition treats the unequal-reach duel as a named special case: Fiore's dagger-against-sword, the kenjutsu *kodachi* methods, the Filipino *daga* against a longer stick, the Italian rapier-and-dagger system that converts the dagger's short reach into a close-measure asset *once the long weapon's distance has been beaten*. The shorter weapon's entire problem is surviving the approach to reach its own measure; the longer weapon's entire problem is keeping the fight in the approach.

**The one timeline under three vocabularies.** Index every initiative scheme by *when you move relative to the opponent's commitment* and they collapse into three positions:

| Relative timing | German | Japanese | Italian | English (Silver) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Before** the opponent commits — act on a read of intent | *Vor* | *sen-sen-no-sen* / *tai-no-sen* | *primo intenzione* | a *true time* seized first |
| **At the instant** of commitment — intercept in the same tempo | *Indes* | *sen-no-sen* | *mezzo tempo* | striking in the opponent's *false time* |
| **After** commitment — let the attack develop, answer the now-readable motion | *Nach* | *go-no-sen* | *contratempo* / the riposte | answering a committed *false time* |

The vocabularies differ; the phenomenon is identical. The interesting fact is the *prestige gradient* across the row, and it has an information-economic explanation. Acting **before** is highest-risk: you pay zero tempo but you act on prediction, and prediction error compounds. Acting **after** is safest: you let the opponent's commitment convert itself into information, but you pay tempo for the privilege — you are now behind. The technically prestigious mode in every tradition is the **middle** one — *Indes*, *sen-no-sen*, *mezzo tempo*, Itō Ittōsai's *kiriotoshi* — because it is the attempt to take full information *and* pay almost no tempo: read the commitment and act on it in the same instant. The prestige hierarchy is, precisely, a ranking by *how little tempo you pay for how much information.* Beginners are taught to attack first (the *Vor* side) because they need the willingness to commit; counter is taught last because it requires the nerve to let a real threat develop and the perception to answer it without falling into *Nach*.

**Reading intent: three channels, and why each tradition trusts a different one.** The decision of when to act is only as good as the read it rests on, and the three channels are not interchangeable:

- **Tactile** — German *Fühlen*, Italian *sentimento del ferro*, Spanish *atajo*, Japanese koryū blade-pressure. **Latency near zero, bandwidth high, but it requires contact** — which means it is only available *inside* measure and *at* the bind, i.e., at maximal mutual risk. *"Vision lies, contact tells truth"* is not mysticism: at contact you are sensing the torque balance directly (see Axis IV), which cannot be faked the way a body feint can.
- **Visual** — Musashi's *kan no metsuke / ken no metsuke* ("the gaze of perception is strong, the gaze of sight is weak"), Italian preparation-reading, the German attention to the body before the blade. **Latency is human reaction time (~a fifth of a second), bandwidth medium, and it is deceivable** — the feint is the exploit that targets this channel specifically, spending a false visual signal to buy a real tempo. *Kan* (perception of intent) over *ken* (sight of motion) is the instruction to read the slow, hard-to-fake leak (shoulder, hip, breath) rather than the fast, easily-faked one (the blade's first twitch).
- **Predictive (*yomi*)** — recursive modeling of the opponent's model of you; Yagyū mind-reading, Italian *secondo intenzione*, Spanish geometric anticipation. **Latency is negative (you act before the signal exists) but error compounds with the depth of recursion** — "I think you think I think" is reliable to perhaps two levels and is noise beyond. *Secondo intenzione* is disciplined one-level prediction: offer a real attack, predict the specific counter, and have the counter-to-the-counter pre-loaded.

The dominant channel is selected by the weapon, which is the spine's central claim instantiated. The long thin **rapier does not hold a leverage bind** — contact is fleeting and uninformative — so the Italian tradition *cannot* build on the tactile channel and instead builds a visual/temporal theory (*tempo*, preparation-reading). The two-handed **longsword binds constantly** — the blades tangle and stay tangled under two-hand leverage — so the German tradition builds on the tactile channel (*Fühlen*, *Winden*) and treats vision as the liar. The **samurai duel**, fought to the death with cutting weapons where the first committed error is usually the last, rewards the negative-latency channel above all, so the Japanese tradition pushes furthest into prediction and intent (*sen-sen-no-sen*, *mushin* — the unfixed mind that does not stop on the sword and so can respond to the feint). Same combat, three sensors, each chosen by its conditions.

**Manufacturing the first move.** The *invito* (invitation) is the guard that lies: rather than wait to read intent, you emit a *false* high-bandwidth signal — a deliberate opening — to provoke the specific attack for which you have already pre-loaded the answer. It converts the opponent's information-gathering against you (they read your opening) into your tempo advantage (you knew it was bait). This is the active, offensive use of the same readability the whole stance section is about.

## Axis III — Footwork

**Footwork serves three masters — measure, line, stance — but measure is the master of the other two**, because measure gates the entire economy of Axis II. The deep point the source leaves implicit: out of measure, no information channel is live and no action is possible; the fight is decided largely by who controls *entry* into measure on their own terms. Footwork is the instrument of that control, and the step taxonomy is a set of trade-offs between speed of entry and exposure during it.

The steps, with their mechanics:

- **The lunge** (Capo Ferro, *Gran Simulacro*, 1610) — the European tradition's most consequential single innovation, and the reason is mechanical: **it decouples reach from commitment.** Before the lunge, additional reach came from a passing step, which moves the whole body forward and is recoverable only by another full step — slow, and during the recovery you are deeply committed and maximally readable. The lunge keeps the rear foot *planted as an anchor*; reach is bought by extending the front foot and the body over that anchor, and recovery is a single hip extension that retracts you behind your guard. You purchase the deepest target for the *lowest tempo-cost of any reaching action*. This is also the heart of **the Silver dispute**: George Silver classed the lunge a *false time* (a slow part — the body — moving before recovery is assured) and judged it biomechanically unsafe. He was describing a real risk and mis-weighing it. The lunge is unsafe *only if the opponent has already won the tempo* — has caught you mid-extension before your point arrives — which is exactly the *mezzo tempo* / *contratempo* interception. Silver and the Italians are therefore both partly right about the *same* exchange: the lunge is the best reaching action against an opponent who has not seized the tempo, and a fatal over-commitment against one who has. The disagreement is not about mechanics but about which case you expect to be in.
- **The pass / passing step** — universal (German *Zufechten* footwork, Italian *passo*, Japanese *ayumi-ashi*, Filipino variants). Maximum distance per step, but it crosses the feet and commits the whole body — high reach, high exposure.
- **The gathering step** — bring the rear foot up, then advance the front; closes measure incrementally, trading speed for the option to abort. The probing approach.
- **The slide / shuffle** — both feet along the line without crossing (Japanese *tsugi-ashi*, *okuri-ashi*; sport fencing's advance and retreat). Feet never cross, so balance and the ability to change direction are preserved throughout — low exposure, low distance.
- **Circular footwork and off-line evasion** — Spanish *Destreza* (steps computed from the geometry of the circle whose diameter is the opponent's reach), Filipino triangle footwork, Japanese *taisabaki*, Fiore's *slope-passing*. These are the *angular* family, and they solve line-clearing differently from the linear family (below).
- **The *fumikomi*** — the kendo committed stamp-step that lands at the instant of the strike. Read through the kinetic chain (Axis IV), it *synchronizes* the chain: the floor-reaction of the stamp, full body extension, and the cut's peak force all arrive in one moment — *ki-ken-tai-no-ichi*, "spirit, sword, body as one." It is therefore a maximal-commitment technique (everything fires together, nothing held in reserve), which is why it pairs with *sen-no-sen* interception and never with tentative probing — you do not stamp unless you have decided.

**Measure as named discrete zones** — the variable footwork manages — is treated identically across the corpus: *misura* (*larga / stretta / fuori misura*), *maai* (*tōma / uchima / chikama*, anchored by *issoku ittō no maai*, "one-step one-cut distance"), Silver's *true place*, the Filipino *largo / medio / corto / serrada*, the Spanish geometric computation. The cross-tradition fact is that measure is *not* a continuous quantity you intuit but a set of named thresholds, and the reason is decision-theoretic: a fighter cannot continuously re-evaluate distance under time pressure, so the traditions quantize it into a few zones each with a stable "what is possible here" — and the exploitable seam is *disagreement* about which zone you are in. The short fighter beats the long fighter by entering *corto* on a tempo the long fighter still reads as *largo*; the long fighter beats the short by holding *largo* while threatening as though already at *medio*. Measure manipulation is the manufacture of zone-disagreement.

**Linear vs. angular: the genuine geometric fork.** The two families are different answers to one question — what do you do with the opponent's line of attack? **Linear** (the lunge, the direct pass) is *faster and keeps you on the centerline*, so you threaten the opponent continuously as you close; the cost is that you remain on the line of their counter — you are fast but exposed. **Angular** (the Spanish circle, *taisabaki*, slope-passing) *removes you from the line you were on*, so the attack arrives where you no longer are; the cost is distance and time, and the risk of surrendering your own offensive line in the process. The choice is contextual and the masters knew it: against a committed thrust, step off-line and let it pass; against a hesitant or out-of-measure opponent, close linearly and threaten. The Italian rapier system optimizes the linear answer (close fast on the centerline, cover as you go), the Spanish *Destreza* optimizes the angular (never be where the attack lands), and Japanese *taisabaki* keeps both available. These are not stylistic preferences; they are two solutions to a geometry, each dominant in a different region of the exchange.

## Axis IV — Grip

The thinnest axis in the *literature* and therefore the one most worth analyzing, because its thinness is informative: grip is presupposed. The manuals capture discriminating knowledge, and how to hold the weapon is drilled long before the student reaches the page — so what the corpus *does* record about grip is, by selection, the *non-obvious* part: the second hand, the leverage read, the exceptional holds, the chain that delivers force. Each of those carries more mechanical depth than the source extracted.

- **The count of hands is a fundamental, renegotiable trade.** A second hand on the hilt adds a second lever point — substantially increasing the torque you can apply to the blade — and adds a structural defense, but it costs the reach of one arm's independent extension and locks both arms to one weapon's single line. The medium-reach swords split exactly on this trade: the **two-handed longsword** buys torque and binding power; the **one-handed arming sword** keeps the off-hand free for a buckler, shield, or dagger (a second line of defense or a second short weapon). Musashi's *Niten Ichi-ryū* is the claim that **the trade is renegotiable** — *the conventional grip "wastes a hand"* because the body has two and a two-handed sword uses only one limb's worth of independent action; two independent weapons recover that lost independence at the price of the two-hand torque. The **katana** is the instructive in-between: a two-hand grip on a roughly one-hand-length blade, i.e., a design that spends the second hand entirely on *torque and control per unit of blade* rather than on reach.
- **Strong/weak leverage is the mechanical engine of the entire bind game**, and seeing this dissolves the apparent mysticism of *Fühlen*. At a bind, the two blades form levers pivoting at the contact point; the fencer whose contact sits nearer their own hilt (the *Stark*, strong) has the longer effective lever arm and *wins the static push* against the opponent's tip-end (the *Schwach*, weak). **Winding** (*Winden*) is the dynamic exploitation of this asymmetry: rotating around the contact point shifts your strong against their weak and opens a new line *without breaking engagement* — it works because, and only because, of the lever-arm ratio. And this is what "feeling" the bind actually is: *Fühlen* is the direct sensing of the torque balance at the contact point, which reports instantly who can overpower whom and therefore which actions are safe. The German tactile theory is, mechanically, *leverage physics read through the hands* — high-bandwidth and unfakeable in a way no visual cue is.
- **Specialized holds are structural, not stylistic choices.** The South Asian *katar* is a push-dagger with a horizontal, fist-line grip that **aligns the blade with the forearm's skeletal axis**, so a thrust transmits force through bone in compression rather than through the wrist in shear — the wrist cannot buckle, so the *katar* can deliver a full body-weight, armor-defeating punch-thrust that an ordinary hanging grip could not. The Mamluk *rumḥ* (lance), couched and braced from the saddle, **routes the horse's translational momentum into the strike**: the relevant energy scales with the combined horse-and-rider mass and the *square* of the closing speed, orders of magnitude above arm-generated force — which is precisely why furusiyya subordinates weapon technique to the equestrian context, treats the *weapon system* as horse-plus-rider-plus-lance, and assigns the grip the single job of being rigid enough to transmit that momentum without absorbing it. (It also explains the lance's binary character: decisive on the charge, near-useless once stationary.) The flexible weapons invert the entire problem — the South Asian *urumi*, the Chinese chain whip and rope dart hold no line at all, so their technique is the *control of recoil* rather than the management of leverage.
- **Power-delivery is where grip meets the whole body.** The Chinese *yi / qi / li* (intent guides breath-energy, which mobilizes physical force) and *gang / rou* (hard and soft as complementary modes) describe force generated through whole-body structure — ground through leg through waist through arm through weapon — rather than through local muscle. This is the same kinetic chain the *fumikomi* synchronizes, made the *explicit object of training*, and it is why the Chinese tradition treats empty-hand and weapon practice as a single continuous spectrum: the power chain is identical across them and the weapon is merely the last link. The later *neijia / waijia* (internal / external) distinction is a retrospective sharpening of the same contrast — power from structure-and-intent versus power from muscle.

**Cross-tradition synthesis.** Grip is the seam where weapon-property meets body-mechanics, and the manuals' near-silence is itself the finding: grip is the precondition that sets the floor on every other axis (a grip that cannot generate torque cannot win a bind; a grip that recovers slowly raises the tempo-cost of every technique). The *theoretical* statements that did get written down are, tellingly, the ones that *overturn an assumption* — Musashi's "wasted hand," the Chinese claim that structure beats muscle — because only the counter-intuitive part of grip was worth the ink.

## Axis V — Techniques

The middle of every tradition's pyramid — the layer that *can* be taught in pairs — and the layer most easily mistaken for the whole art. The depth here is not in the catalog of named actions but in three structural facts the source listed without analyzing: why the catalogs converge on a particular size, why the offensive and defensive choices are information trades, and why the *chained* sequences are the real unit.

**Why every catalog lands at 5–12 named attacks.** German simple cuts plus *Meisterhäue*, Bolognese's six-or-eight, the *jian*'s eight methods, the Filipino twelve angles — the independent convergence on roughly this count is evidence that the number is a property of *the body's geometry*, not the culture. There are only so many *distinguishable lines* a human can attack or defend: high and low, forehand and backhand, the horizontal, the center, plus the thrust — and the combinatorics of that bottom out near a dozen. The Filipino system makes the point explicit by *numbering* the angles (1 = downward forehand to head, 2 = downward backhand, and so on) and then asserting that the same numbered set applies to stick, blade, and empty hand — which is only coherent if the angles are properties of the attacking body rather than of any weapon. The catalogs differ in granularity (the Bolognese splits combinations finely; the German reduces to *drei Wunder* — cut, thrust, slice) but they are enumerating the same finite geometry.

**Cut versus thrust is a warning-versus-recovery trade.** A cut travels a large, readable arc — *high warning*, but it carries momentum, does grievous damage, and clears a line as it travels. A thrust shows a small motion along the line of sight — *low warning* ("biomechanically simpler than a cut but harder to read because the visible motion is small"), the eye being worst at resolving motion coming straight toward it — but a missed thrust leaves you extended on the opponent's line. So the choice inside an exchange is between an action the opponent sees coming but that clears its path, and an action they barely see but that strands you if it fails. This is why the **rapier system is internally coherent**: thrust-primacy (minimize warning) plus the lunge (minimize the tempo-cost of reach) compose into a single design — threaten the deepest target with the least readable, most recoverable action available. And it is why the **longsword keeps cut-and-thrust ambivalence**: the opposite design, holding both modes live so the fencer chooses per-tempo which trade to take.

**Defense is an information-versus-exposure ladder — the corpus's single best idea, made rigorous.** The four modes order themselves not by "hardness" but by how much information they buy and how much exposure they cost:

- **Hard parry / opposition** — stop the attack by meeting force with force. It costs structure and, crucially, *the information ends when the contact ends*: you learn the opponent's line for one instant and then the channel closes.
- **Soft deflection** — touch and redirect off-line (Italian parry-and-riposte, ancestor of sport fencing's *quatre / sixte / septime / octave*). Brief contact, brief information, low structural cost.
- **Bind and wind** — take contact and *stay* in contact, leverage (Axis IV) keeping you safe while the channel stays open. **This is the unique mode that keeps the information channel open while remaining safe** — you read intent *continuously* through the torque balance and can act on the read inside the same engagement. "The bind keeps the conversation open" is the deepest single sentence in the corpus, and now it has a mechanism: the bind is the only defense that does not force a trade between knowing and surviving, because leverage substitutes for the distance that other defenses buy safety with.
- **Pure redirection / yielding** — give way along the line of force, send the opponent past (Spanish circular footwork plus off-line evasion). *Zero contact, zero information, zero exposure* — the safe extreme, paid for in blindness.

Laid out this way, the German centrality of the bind and the Italian-rapier flight *from* it are two consistent answers to the same trade, each forced by the weapon: the longsword can hold a safe, informative bind (two-hand leverage), so it builds the art around the open channel; the thin one-handed rapier *cannot* hold a safe bind against leverage, so it pays for lost information with a temporal read and keeps to opposition-and-evasion. From the bind flow the follow-ons — *Winden*, *cavazione* (disengagement, slipping around an opposition), *stringere* (constraining the blade to take the line) — all of which are moves *within* the open channel.

**Grappling is the floor of the technique space.** Every developed system includes weapon-grappling once measure collapses — Fiore's *abrazare*, the Liechtenauer corpus's *ringen am schwert*, kenjutsu paired with jūjutsu, Kalaripayattu's *verumkai*, Filipino *dumog* — because *every weapon eventually meets a measure at which it cannot strike* (inside its own minimum range, the channel for the weapon closes and the channel for the body opens). "No weapon system is complete without grappling" is therefore not a moral but a geometric necessity: the technique space has a near edge, and grappling is what lives past it.

**The compound play is a pre-computed decision tree, not a combo.** A *Stück*, a *kata*, a flow drill (*hubud-lubud*, *sumbrada*, *abecedario*) is a memorized branch structure — *if he answers with X, then Y; if he counters with Z, then W* — and the combinatorial blowup is the point: roughly a hundred named German plays expand to thousands of branches. The reason is the one fact that governs Axis II: **nobody can deliberate inside *Indes*.** You cannot reason out a response in the fraction of a second between contact and the opponent's next motion; you can only *recognize and execute*. The plays compile judgment into reflex, which resolves the apparent paradox of the whole corpus — that judgment is "what can only be developed in live exchange," yet the manuals are full of fixed sequences. The sequences are not a script for the fight; they are the *training substrate* that builds the reflexive recognition the fight demands. The Filipino flow drills make this explicit by running the branches in *continuous flow* rather than discrete sets — drilling the transitions themselves until the branch is taken below the level of decision.

**The reductionist counter-impulse, explained.** Against all this proliferation, every tradition also pushes the other way — Itō Ittōsai's claim that all techniques reduce to one cut performed with correct timing and intent (*kiriotoshi*, *issatsu no tachi* — the one-killing sword); the Filipino claim that one numbered angle-system subsumes every weapon. The reduction is not contradiction; it is the recognition that *past a point, more branches stop helping* — a fighter who has truly mastered the few techniques that subsume the rest acts faster and more reliably than one carrying a larger, shallower catalog. The proliferation builds the recognition; the reduction is what mastery of the recognition looks like from the inside. This is the same arc as the stance axis (Agrippa's reduction to four, Musashi's "no stances"): the developed traditions all travel from catalog toward distilled principle.

## Beneath the axes — conditioning as precondition

Conditioning is not a phase of the engagement, so it sits outside the five axes — but the source's central point applies to it most of all: *what does not appear in a tradition's manuals is usually what the tradition takes for granted, not what it lacks.* Conditioning is the *enabling condition* for every axis, not a technique among them, which is exactly why it is drilled relentlessly and written about rarely. *Suburi* is not in Musashi's *Five Rings* because every kenjutsu student already does it.

Analytically, conditioning sets the *floor* on the entire economy above: fatigue degrades footwork first (measure control collapses as the legs tire), then grip (torque falls, and a fading grip loses the bind), then the mental channels (reading and prediction degrade under anaerobic load) — so a conditioning deficit silently lowers performance on stance, footwork, grip, *and* initiation at once. And the *kind* of fitness the manuals' own data implies is specific: engagement durations clustered short — judicial duels in seconds-to-minutes, battlefield pulses in minutes with separation-and-recovery, tournament passes in seconds with rest — and a full late-medieval harness (~20–25 kg) makes sustained plate combat anaerobic, minutes not hours. The relevant attribute is therefore *anaerobic power plus recovery rate*, not aerobic endurance, which is exactly what the historical training biases toward:

- **Power and weighted overload** — the Roman *pell* with deliberately over-weighted wooden weapons (Vegetius), continued into medieval Europe; kenjutsu *suburi* (hundreds of cuts daily with a heavy *suburi-tō*); the Chinese *gong / jibengong* and *zhanzhuang* (standing-post, weight-bearing structure); Filipino weighted-stick drills and *carenza*; the HEMA-era *Fechtschule* and its professional instructor guilds (Marxbrüder, Federfechter) drilling apprentices for hours daily.
- **Whole-body conditioning as the gateway** — Kalaripayattu's *meipayattu* is the entire pre-weapon stage, months to years of body work before a weapon is touched, and its integration with a medical-energetic system (*marma* points used in both attack and healing) makes the conditioner and the healer the same person.
- **System conditioning** — the Mamluk regimen conditions horse *and* rider together (lance against rings at the gallop, mounted archery in motion, *birjās*), because the unit being trained is the weapon system, not the man.

## Closing: where each tradition concentrates investment (on the five axes)

The sharpened thesis: a tradition's "center of gravity" is the name of its dominant information channel, and that channel is selected by its weapon and its stakes. Recast accordingly, and noting honestly where source detail thins out:

- **German longsword (Liechtenauer):** the **tactile** tradition. The two-handed blade binds constantly, so the art centers on the open contact channel — *Fühlen*, *Winden*, and the resolution of initiative *at* contact (*Indes*) — supported by the densest technique catalog (*Meisterhäue* and the combinatorial *Stücke*). Center of gravity: **techniques resolved inside the bind**.
- **Italian rapier (Capo Ferro, Fabris):** the **temporal** tradition. The thin one-handed blade cannot hold an informative bind, so the art flees contact and builds on time and distance — *tempo*, *mezzo tempo*, *contratempo*, *primo / secondo intenzione* — with the lunge as the footwork engineered to serve it. Center of gravity: **approach and initiation (time and distance)**.
- **Spanish Destreza:** the **deductive-geometric** tradition. It answers the readability problem with an upright stance and circular footwork computed from the mystical circle, and calculates the safe instant to enter rather than reading it. Center of gravity: **footwork and a deductive theory of the first move**.
- **English Silver:** an **initiation polemic with explicit biomechanics** — *true and false times*, the *true place* — whose lasting value is the documented argument (against the lunge as a false time) rather than a complete system. Center of gravity: **a timing-and-distance argument about the first move**.
- **Japanese kenjutsu:** the **predictive / intent** tradition, and the most balanced of any. The death-duel context pushes the read before commitment (*sen-sen-no-sen*, *kan no metsuke*, *mushin*); stance is treated as transient information ("stances and no stances"), the committed step (*fumikomi*) couples the kinetic chain, and a reductionist limit (*kiriotoshi*) caps the technique catalog. Center of gravity: **reading intent as the apex of teaching, over a balanced base**.
- **Chinese:** the **power-delivery** tradition, organized weapon-by-weapon. Its distinctive depth is the explicit kinetic chain (*yi / qi / li*, *gang / rou*) and the empty-hand-to-weapon continuity, with stance as constraint (named *shi*) and a developed *jian* technique set. Center of gravity: **techniques plus grip-as-power-delivery, weapon as the organizing axis**.
- **Korean (Muyedobotongji):** **technique-encyclopedic and self-consciously synthetic** — uniquely naming what each technique owes to which source tradition. Center of gravity: **techniques, with cross-cultural synthesis as method**. *(Source detail moderate; pre-Imjin indigenous material thin.)*
- **Filipino eskrima:** **technique-and-footwork**, weapon-flexible. The numbered angles plus triangle footwork, with the explicit claim that the same angle-system spans stick, blade, and empty hand. Center of gravity: **techniques and footwork, weapon as a variable**. *(Premodern continuity poorly documented; the explicit codification is 20th-century.)*
- **South Asian Kalaripayattu:** the **conditioning-first** tradition — *meipayattu* as a years-long precondition with technique built on it, integrated with a medical-energetic system. Center of gravity: **the precondition, then technique on top**. *(Technical-vocabulary granularity low; knowledge largely at the level of secondary scholarship.)*
- **Mamluk furusiyya:** the **weapon-system** tradition — the couched lance and mounted footwork, with personal weapon technique subordinated to the horse-and-rider unit. Center of gravity: **grip / weapon-as-system and equestrian footwork**. *(Technical granularity not available at the level of the European or Japanese corpora.)*

**The cross-tradition pattern, sharpened.** The five axes stack the same way everywhere, and the stack has a logic. **Stance, footwork, and grip are the substrate layer** — they set what is possible, what is readable, and how fast you recover, and they are mostly drilled rather than written because they are preconditions. **Approach and initiation are the decision layer** — the most heavily written-about, because deciding *when to act relative to the opponent's commitment* can only be developed live and is where the whole information-and-tempo economy is won or lost. **Technique is the action layer** — proliferated into named catalogs, compiled into branching plays that beat the deliberation limit, and everywhere shadowed by a reductionist counter-impulse once the recognition is built. Traditions that look most different on the surface — Italian rapier versus Filipino stick, Liechtenauer longsword versus Mamluk lance — differ mainly in *which information channel their weapon makes available, and therefore which axis they invest most heavily in writing about.* They do not disagree about which axes matter.

**What does not appear in a tradition's manuals is usually what the tradition takes for granted, not what it lacks** — and, read through the spine, what a tradition writes most about is the channel its weapon left open.

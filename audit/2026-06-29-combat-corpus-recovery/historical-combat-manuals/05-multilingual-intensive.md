# Multilingual Research — Intensive Round

Companion to `04-multilingual-supplement.md`. This round does two things: (1) closes the §K gap languages from doc 04 with actual in-language searches, and (2) deepens one already-covered tradition (the German judicial duel) on the dueling-specific axis that is the corpus's original subject. Grading scheme unchanged: *[Source: high/medium/low; Interpretive: high/medium/low]*.

**Headline result of this round.** Searching the gap languages does not just add traditions — it forces a *reclassification axis* the prior corpus lacked. The traditions sort along a spectrum from **documented-treatise** (Hungarian sabre, Byzantine *Taktika*) through **oral/embodied with no canon** (kalaripayattu, zurkhāneh) to **reconstructed-from-external-documentation** (Mesoamerican, and — newly demoted — Polish szabla). And a second distinction cuts across it: some "martial traditions" in the corpus are not *fighting systems* at all but *weapon-and-drill* traditions (the Russian shashka is the clean case). The corpus has been conflating these.

# Part 1 — Gap languages now searched

## § L. Persian (the zurkhāneh / pahlavānī tradition)

The prior corpus's Persian material was English-sourced. Persian sources give the native role-structure and the ethical-religious substructure directly.

- The practice is *varzesh-e bāstānī* / *varzesh-e zurkhāne'i* (ورزش زورخانه‌ای) and the broader *āyīn-hā-ye pahlavānī* (آیین‌های پهلوانی, "the rituals of the pahlavān"); inscribed on the UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage list (Iran, 2010). *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- The role structure is specific and not conveyed in English summaries: the **morshed** (مرشد) leads from a raised seat, drumming the *zarb* (ضرب) and chanting — *Shāhnāmeh* passages, ghazals, praise of Imam Ali — and functions effectively as the session's commander; the **miāndār** (میاندار) is the senior practitioner in the center of the **gowd** (گود, the sunken pit) who leads the movements. Each exercise (the *mil* clubs, the *charkh* spins, *shena* push-ups, *pā-zadan*) has its own dedicated rhythm. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- The ethical-religious substructure is *javānmardī* / *fotovvat* (chivalry) with Sufi and *ʿayyārī* roots — the *kashkul* and *tabarzin* (begging-bowl and axe) are inherited dervish emblems still present as décor. The tradition narrates itself across three layers: mythic (Rostam of the *Shāhnāmeh*), historical (Mahmud Khwārazmi "Puriyā-ye Valī," himself a semi-legendary 14th-c. figure credited as founder), and contemporary. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Placement: this is an **oral/embodied + religious-institutional** tradition, codified in ritual and music rather than in a combat treatise — confirming the prior corpus's "religious-civic institution" reading, now with the native vocabulary and the founder-legend (Puriyā-ye Valī) flagged as itself a demystification target. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § M. Hungarian (szablavívás) — and the correction it forces on the Polish finding

This is the single most consequential gap closure, because it **grounds the Polish szabla reclassification from doc 04 §E.** Where Polish sabre is a reconstruction from indirect sources, **Hungary has a genuinely continuous, documented sabre tradition.**

- Weapon history: the szablya entered the Carpathian Basin with the Magyar conquest (via Turkic neighbors of the South-Russian steppe), receded under St. Stephen in favor of the knightly double-edged sword and armor for ~400 years, and revived against the Ottomans after Mohács (1526) as the weapon of the *huszár* (hussar) light cavalry — used until WWI. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- A real teaching lineage and treatise corpus: **József Keresztessy** (19th c.) is named as the creator of the Hungarian sabre school (the *régi magyar iskola*, "old Hungarian school"); he learned under the French-origin master **Louis/Lajos Chappon** (1802–1878). The printed-manual corpus is thick — Chappon Samu's *A vívás művészetének elmélete* (1892, with later editions), Chappon Károly's *Kardvívás* (1893), and works by Arlow Gusztáv (1902), Leszák Károly, Payka, Menyhárt, down to **Gerentsér László** (1944, posthumous — described as the last Hungarian fencing book still treating the duel and its preparation). *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- The modern bridge: **Italo Santelli**, the Italian master who settled in Hungary c.1900, fused the Hungarian sabre with Italian method and founded the Italo-Hungarian sport-sabre school that dominated the 20th-c. Olympics — the **Gerevich** dynasty (Aladár, 7× Olympic champion) and onward to recent champions. The 1920 Royal Hungarian Army Fencing and PE Course institutionalized military fencing instruction. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- **Correction to the corpus:** doc 04 treated "Hungarian sabre" only as it surfaced inside Polish sources. It deserves its own entry as a *documented continuous tradition*, and it is the real referent behind the Polish reconstruction's genealogy (postwar Polish sport sabre revived under the Hungarian master Kevey). The Polish/Hungarian pair is the corpus's clearest *reconstructed vs. continuous* contrast. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § N. Russian (shashka, Cossack) — the "weapon, not fencing-art" limiting case

Russian sources deliver a corrective the corpus needs at the category level.

- Native sources state plainly that the **shashka** (шашка) is *not* a fencing weapon: it has no guard (at most a knuckle-bow on dragoon patterns) and a hilt-forward balance that makes it unsuited to parrying and binding. It replaced the sabre precisely *because* the firearms era removed the need for complex sabre fencing — the design goal was to fell an opponent in a single draw-cut. It was historically an auxiliary sidearm (with dagger/knife) and only displaced sabres and broadswords (палаши) in Russian service from the later 19th c., standardized in the 1881 reform (Gorlov) on a Caucasian blade pattern, worn edge-back for a fast draw. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- Where the codified "art" actually lives: in *rubka* (рубка, target-cutting drill) and — separately — in a genuine treatise for the **Cossack lance**, the *Nachertanie fekhtoval'nogo iskusstva vladeniya pikoy* (1843, "Delineation of the fencing art of pike-handling"), illustrated, built on the experience of General Yakov Baklanov, following the 1839 standardized pike. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Implication for the corpus:** the romantic "Cossack swordsmanship" is, per Russian sources, mostly cutting drill with a guardless chopping blade plus a documented *pike* method — not a sabre-fencing system. The corpus should not file the shashka alongside fencing traditions; it is the limiting case where a famous bladed weapon has essentially no dueling/fencing art attached. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § O. Malayalam (kalaripayattu)

- Malayalam sources are explicit on the evidentiary point the corpus needs: **no authoritative classical text survives** — transmission is guru-to-disciple, oral and embodied (*gurumukham*). This is not a treatise tradition, and any "technical" account is reconstructed from living lineages plus ballad and iconographic references, not from a manual. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Native frame: *kalari* (കളരി) derives from Sanskrit *khalūrika* (a military training ground; the etymology proposed in M.D. Raghavan's *Folk Plays and Dances*). The art integrates weapons (*vāḷ* sword, *kaṭāra* dagger, *kuntam* spear, the *urumi* flexible whip-sword, bow), the **64 marmam** vital-point system, and a linked medicine (*kalari chikitsa*). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- A distinction the English corpus blurs: Malayalam sources treat the **northern (vaḍakkan)** kalari as the core form and the **southern (tekkan)** as Tamil-rooted (*varma/adi* lineage) and only latterly folded in as a "style." There are further regional *sampradāyam* (Tulunadan, Kadathanadan, etc.). The northern ballads (*vaḍakkan pāṭṭukaḷ*) record women trained in kalari, and sources stress its cross-caste, cross-religion social reach in caste-stratified medieval Kerala. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § P. Punjabi (gatka / shastar vidya)

Thinner than the other clusters (the search was partly crowded by unrelated results), but the usable Punjabi source establishes the orthodox frame and its contested status.

- The Sikh weapon art is *gatka* (ਗਤਕਾ) / *shastar vidya* (ਸ਼ਸਤਰ ਵਿੱਦਿਆ, "weapon-knowledge"). The orthodox lineage traced in Punjabi sources runs from the sixth Guru, **Hargobind** (the *mīrī-pīrī* turn to temporal arms), developed by the tenth Guru, **Gobind Singh**, into an integral part of the Khalsa/Nihang tradition; the canonical reference cited is Bhai Kahn Singh Nabha's *Mahan Kosh*. As of 2026 it has been institutionalized in India's NCERT school curriculum (the *Khel Praveen* textbook), alongside mallakhamb, thang-ta, and kalaripayattu. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Grading caveat: treat the Guru-origin narrative as **orthodox but contested** — the term *gatka* itself is generally dated to the 19th c. (from the practice stick), and much present-day gatka is display/parade form within *nagar kīrtan* processions. This belongs with the *living-lineage-plus-reconstruction* traditions, with a strong religious-institutional frame. *[Source: medium; Interpretive: medium]*

## § Q. Mesoamerican (macuahuitl, flower war) via Spanish/Nahuatl sources

Read directly, the Spanish chronicle-based scholarship (including the CSIC journal *Gladius*) supplies what English secondary work flattens.

- The combat objective was **capture, not kill**: subduing a live opponent demanded real technical control, and in the *xōchiyāōyōtl* (Nahuatl; *guerra florida*, "flower war") the obsidian *macuahuitl* could be deliberately blunted. The weapon taxonomy is richer than the English account — *macuahuitl* (obsidian-edged wooden sword), *tepoztopilli* (its ~2 m polearm cousin), *cuauhololli* (blunt-headed club), *ātlatl* (spear-thrower), sling, bow. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Per the colonial chroniclers **Sahagún** (Florentine Codex), **Diego Durán**, and **Bernal Díaz**, the flower wars were instituted after the 1450–1454 famine (*Ce Tochtli*, "1 Rabbit"), read by the priesthood as a demand for sacrificial blood (*chalchíhuatl*) to sustain the Fifth Sun. Training ran through the *telpochcalli* (commoners) and *calmécac* (nobility); capturing prisoners drove social ascent into elite orders (*cuāchic*, *otomitl*, eagle/jaguar). A documented evolution: under Moctezuma I the spread of bow-and-arrow in the Chalca war demoted hand-to-hand *macuahuitl* combat and let non-elite warriors rise. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Placement: the archetypal **reconstructed-from-external-documentation** tradition — and the external documentation is precisely these Spanish-language chronicles, best read directly. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § R. Byzantine Greek (the Taktika genre)

Greek sources frame the Byzantine military manuals with a source-critical caution and a philological layer the corpus lacks.

- The *Taktika* (Τακτικά) are a distinct literary genre spanning the mid-6th to late-10th c., descended from Hellenistic and Roman models (Aelian, Onasander, Arrian). Greek scholarship foregrounds the central interpretive tension: the genre **imitates its ancient prototypes by convention while deliberately updating the material** for contemporary enemies — so a given passage may be antiquarian copying or live doctrine, and the two must be told apart before the text can be used as evidence. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- The *Strategikon* (Στρατηγικόν) attributed to the emperor **Maurice** (r. 582–602) is the foundational text and marks the definitive shift to **cavalry** as the main arm, displacing the ancient heavy-infantry phalanx/legion; it survives in ~22 manuscripts and underlies the later writers. The *Taktika* of **Leo VI the Wise** (early 10th c.) is addressed to the *strategoi* of the themes and opens with an explicit **Christian just-war framing** (men made in God's image should embrace peace; but military neglect, unlike civil decline, is instantly exposed on the battlefield). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- A philological layer parallel to the German *Fachsprache* and Italian dialectology work: the **Latin military vocabulary preserved in Greek phonetic form** within Leo's *Taktika* (e.g. *komēs* < *comes*) is itself studied as evidence of the Roman institutional substrate. There is even a modern Greek HEMA reconstruction effort (the "Leontes" academy) working from these texts and the *akritai* skirmishing method (*paradromē*). *[Source: medium; Interpretive: medium]*

# Part 2 — Deepening an already-covered tradition: the German judicial duel

The corpus's subject is *dueling combat*; doc 04 §C treated the German material as academic apparatus. The German-language scholarship on the **Gerichtskampf** (judicial duel) is where the dueling content actually lives, and it is rich.

- The *gerichtlicher Zweikampf* was a legitimate instrument of **truth-finding** (*Wahrheitsfindung*) in Germanic law into the early modern period, used when an accusation could not be resolved otherwise. It applied to nobles and free persons — and **explicitly included women** not under a guardian (unmarried women and widows: the *Sachsenspiegel*'s "Megede und Wedewen"). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Talhoffer's Fechtbuch (1459 and 1467) contains the earliest fight-book depictions of a man-vs-woman judicial duel**, with handicapping conventions meant to balance the contest (the man set in a pit, the woman with a weighted sling-cloth). The 1459 copy passed through the Otto Thott collection to the Danish Royal Library (Thott 290 2°); the tradition survives in six manuscripts, the earliest with Talhoffer's ownership entry dated 1443. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- The procedure is concrete: combat inside the **Schranken** (wooden lists, often at prominent urban sites — Munich's market square, today's Marienplatz, hosted a documented 1370 duel), policed by **Grießwarte** (marshals who could halt the fight with long poles, like referees) and attended by **Warner** (seconds/instructors outside the lists). Regional law set the weapons: **Swabian** (dueling-shield and sword) vs **Franconian** (dueling-shield and mace/*Kolben*); peasants had analogous *Kolbengerichte*. Professional **Lohnkämpfer** (hired champions) could substitute for a party — a paid, skilled profession, and the milieu of fight-masters like Talhoffer himself. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- Demystification and genealogy: current scholarship (Christian Jaser; the *Forschungsbibliothek Gotha*) reframes the practice as **regulated conflict-resolution, not primarily superstition** — "the myth of the *Gottesurteil* (ordeal)." And the modern **honor duel descends from the Gerichtskampf**: as the combat moved from the legal sphere into the private, the fateful-religious dimension gave way to an estate-based concept of honor (*ständischer Ehrbegriff*). Serious academic treatment includes Jaser & Israel (eds.), *Zweikämpfer. Fechtmeister – Kämpen – Samurai* (Berlin 2014) — explicitly cross-cultural — and Israel & Ortalli (eds.), *Il duello fra medioevo ed età moderna* (Rome 2009); the manuscripts are cataloged in the *Katalog der deutschsprachigen illustrierten Handschriften* (C.H. Beck, Rainer Leng). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

# Part 3 — Consolidated corrections and the new classification axis

Ranked by how much each changes the prior corpus.

1. **Add Hungarian as a documented continuous tradition; finalize the Polish reclassification.** Hungarian sabre (continuous lineage, thick printed-manual corpus, Olympic afterlife) is the real referent behind the Polish szabla's reconstructed genealogy. Together they are the corpus's cleanest *continuous vs. reconstructed* pair. (§M, and doc 04 §E) *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

2. **Introduce a classification axis the corpus lacked: documented-treatise ↔ oral/embodied ↔ reconstructed-from-external.** Placing the traditions on it:
   - *Documented-treatise:* Hungarian sabre, Byzantine *Taktika*, plus the European fight-books and East Asian military canon from doc 04.
   - *Oral/embodied, no canon:* kalaripayattu (explicitly), Persian zurkhāneh (codified in ritual/music), gatka (living lineage + religious frame).
   - *Reconstructed-from-external-documentation:* Mesoamerican *macuahuitl* (from Spanish chronicles), Polish szabla (from memoirs + weapon study).
   This axis is orthogonal to geography and is the more useful sort for judging evidentiary weight. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

3. **Distinguish *weapon-and-drill* traditions from *fighting-system* traditions.** The Russian shashka is the clean case: a famous blade with essentially no fencing art attached — its codification is cutting drill (*rubka*) plus a separate *pike* treatise. The Mesoamerican *capture-not-kill* constraint is a related point: the weapon is lethal but the trained behavior is subdual. The corpus has been treating "has a famous weapon" as equivalent to "has a dueling system." *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

4. **Founding-legend demystification is genuinely universal — confirmed in every new language.** Polish "ancient szabla art" (lost by 1864, reconstructed from sport sabre); Persian Puriyā-ye Valī (semi-legendary founder); gatka's Guru-origin narrative (orthodox but contested, term is 19th-c.); the German *Gerichtskampf* as "the myth of the ordeal"; Japanese "Katori Shinjūrō" (undocumented), from doc 04. The prior corpus's signature move is not a European specialty; it is available in each tradition's own language, and English-only search is what made it look European. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

5. **A recurring philological layer validates the German/Italian finding from doc 04.** Latin military vocabulary fossilized in Byzantine Greek (Leo's *Taktika*) is the same *kind* of language-as-evidence work as the German *Fachsprache* glossaries and the Italian dialectology of Fiore — a cross-traditional method that only appears when you read the source-language scholarship. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

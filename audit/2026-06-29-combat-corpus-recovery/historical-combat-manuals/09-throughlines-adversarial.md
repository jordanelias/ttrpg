# Throughlines, Re-identified Adversarially and Tested Against the Literature

The throughlines are the corpus's most-quoted output and, per the critical review, its weakest part. This document does two things the prior work did not: it subjects each candidate throughline to a fixed adversarial test battery (trying to *break* it, not confirm it), and it evaluates each against the actual academic literature — martial arts studies, the sociology of sport, and the invention-of-tradition scholarship — rather than reasoning from the corpus's own prior outputs.

**Headline verdict.** Most of the corpus's "throughlines" are **uncited restatements of established frameworks** — chiefly the *invention of tradition* (Hobsbawm & Ranger 1983; Bowman 2021), *sportization* (Elias & Dunning 1986), and *comparative fight-book analysis* (Wetzler 2015, 2016). The corpus reinvented the field's wheel without citing the field, and its grandest claims ("candidate universals") are precisely the ones the literature would reject as selection effects. The genuinely defensible throughlines are the modest, well-sourced ones.

---

## Test battery

Each candidate is run against six tests. It is a *genuine* throughline only if it survives T1–T4; its *value* turns on T5–T6.

- **T1 Triviality** — is it true but near-tautological?
- **T2 Circularity** — is it true only by how its terms were defined?
- **T3 Selection effect** — does it survive cases *not* chosen to fit it (esp. the poorly-documented traditions)?
- **T4 Disconfirming cases** — are there clear counterexamples within the corpus?
- **T5 Novelty** — does it add anything to the academic literature, or merely restate it (uncited)?
- **T6 Source grounding** — what S-tier (per doc 08) supports it?

## Per-candidate evaluation

### C1 — Founding-legend demystification *(doc 07 called this the corpus's "signature universal")*
**Verdict: real but wholly unoriginal; it is the invention-of-tradition thesis, uncited.** Survives T1–T4 (the pattern is true), but fails T5 decisively. That "ancient" martial lineages are frequently recent constructions whose continuity is "largely factitious" is the central claim of Hobsbawm & Ranger (1983), applied comprehensively to martial arts by Bowman, *The Invention of Martial Arts* (Oxford/Routledge 2021), Inoue on Kanō and Kōdōkan judo (in Vlastos ed., *Mirror of Modernity*, UC Press 1998), Judkins & Nielson, *The Creation of Wing Chun* (SUNY 2015), and Wile on taijiquan. The field's own editorial agenda lists "myths and lineage narratives" and "reconstructed martial arts" as founding themes. Worse for the novelty claim, source-critical demystification of forged antiquity predates Hobsbawm by centuries (Valla on the Donation of Constantine, 1440). **Demote from "signature universal" to "the corpus is an uncited application of invention-of-tradition."** [T6: S2 — Hobsbawm & Ranger 1983; Bowman 2021]

### C2 — Nationalist heritage-marketing as a distinct demystification target
**Verdict: not a separate finding; subsumed by C1.** Hobsbawm's own paradigm cases (Scottish tartans, Highland dress, royal ritual) *are* nationalist inventions; "the appropriation of martial arts in nationalist, nation-building… processes" is a named martial-arts-studies theme. Fails T5; **merge into C1.** [T6: S2 — Hobsbawm & Ranger 1983]

### C3 — Founder → glossator → consolidator → polemicist chain
**Verdict: partly real, badly overfitted.** The underlying truth — lineage functions to confer legitimacy, often via "an artificial kinship system" — is well documented (Judkins & Nielson 2015; the legitimation literature). But the specific four-stage schema is a template imported from scripture/textual transmission and imposed on the material; the literature does not use it. It fails **T4**: oral traditions have no "glossator," reconstructed ones no real "founder," and the review already showed it overfits the Polish szabla and Russian shashka. **Keep the legitimation point (real, sourced); discard the four-stage template as an imposed artifact.** [T6: S2 — Judkins & Nielson 2015]

### C4 — Patronage as materialist substructure
**Verdict: genuine, well-grounded, survives the battery — but standard cultural history, not a discovery.** Anglo (*The Martial Arts of Renaissance Europe*, Yale 2000) documents the patron–master economy; al-Sarraf (2004) shows furūsiyya treatises were commissioned by sultans and emirs; the Korean *Muyesinbo*/Sado case is a strong instance. Passes T1–T4. Low novelty (T5) but legitimately a throughline. **Keep, with citations.** [T6: S2 — Anglo 2000; al-Sarraf 2004]

### C5 — The four-axis decomposition (weapon / technique / judgment / conditioning)
**Verdict: superseded by a better, validated framework.** The deep revision had already, correctly, demoted this from "discovered universal" to "analytical lens." Tested against the field: Wetzler's *five dimensions of meaning* — preparation for violent conflict, play/competitive sport, performance, transcendent goals, health care (Wetzler 2015, *Martial Arts Studies* 1) — are the field-standard comparative heuristic, are about function/meaning rather than physical components, and are explicitly "always provisional." The four-axis scheme conflates levels (technique and judgment are not cleanly separable) and is non-standard. Fails T5. **Replace with, or at minimum map onto, Wetzler's dimensions; stop treating the four axes as the comparative frame.** [T6: S2 — Wetzler 2015]

### C6 — "Counterattack is the theoretically prestigious mode across developed traditions"
**Verdict: a textbook selection-effect universal; the deep revision already half-conceded this.** It was tagged high-source/low-interpretive because the "universality may be a projection from three well-documented traditions [German, Italian, Japanese] onto others." That is exactly a **T3** failure. Wetzler's warning against fixed cross-tradition categories (2015) and the field's general scepticism toward universals reinforce the downgrade. **Keep only as a European/Japanese observation; it is not a universal.** [T6: S2 — Wetzler 2015; self-flagged in the deep revision]

### C7 — The classification axis (documented ↔ oral/embodied ↔ reconstructed) *(doc 07's "principal structural contribution")*
**Verdict: a real spectrum, but non-novel and theoretically vulnerable.** It is substantially the *invented vs. organic* axis of the invention-of-tradition literature, which is itself criticised for positing a binary "without specifying a clear empirical threshold for historicity or the degree of deliberate agency required." It inherits that threshold problem, and fails **T4** at the seams the review already found: matrak and koryū straddle two boxes (a surviving treatise *and* a broken/oral transmission). Wetzler (2015) argues against fixed referential categories in favour of goal-relative comparison. **Keep as an informal heuristic; strip the "principal contribution" billing; acknowledge the threshold critique.** [T6: S2 — Hobsbawm & Ranger 1983 and its critics; Wetzler 2015]

### C8 — Weapon-and-drill vs. fighting-system
**Verdict: a useful caution that flirts with triviality.** The shashka case (a celebrated blade with little fencing art, codified as cutting drill plus a separate pike treatise) is real and Russian-sourced. But generalised, the claim approaches "some weapons have elaborate fencing systems and some don't," which is near-**T1**. The distinction is better expressed in the field's existing terms: a "weapon-and-drill" tradition is one where Wetzler's *preparation-for-violent-conflict* dimension dominates and the *play/sport* dimension is absent. **Keep as a caution against conflating a famous weapon with a fighting system; do not inflate it into a structural axis.** [T6: S3/S5 for shashka; S2 — Wetzler 2015 for the framing]

### C9 — The training-surrogate convergence *(doc 06's stated cross-traditional universal)*
**Verdict: the weakest candidate; demote to untested hypothesis.** Three failures. **T1:** "lethal weapons are trained with safer proxies" is near-tautological. **T3:** the supporting set is hand-picked, and the one "non-sparring" exemplar (shashka/*rubka*) is the case I had *defined* as "not a fencing art" — so the pattern partly contains a case constructed to fit it. **T5:** the real phenomenon is already theorised — Elias & Dunning's *sportization* (the rule-bound, violence-limited transformation of combat into safe contest; *Quest for Excitement*, 1986) and Wetzler's play/sport dimension. The one genuinely original element — "the surrogate's *form* encodes the tradition's theory of the weapon" — is mine, untested, and has a counterexample-in-spirit in Sheard's finding that boxing gloves, a "safety" surrogate, can *increase* danger and do not track the activity's real logic (Sheard 1997). **Demote from "candidate universal" to "an interesting untested hypothesis"; cite sportization for the real phenomenon.** [T6: S2 — Elias & Dunning 1986; Sheard 1997]

### C10 — Language-as-evidence / the "idiom fossil"
**Verdict: split.** The philology point — that source-language technical vocabulary (*Fachsprache*, dialectology, Latin substrate in Byzantine Greek) is a real evidentiary stratum — is sound but is simply *historical philology*, standard and used by the German scholarship and by Wetzler. Low novelty, fully legitimate. The "idiom fossil" flourish (*matrak geçmek*, *in die Schranken weisen*) is the weak part: **folk etymologies are notoriously unreliable**, so resting any weight on them is itself a methodological hazard. **Keep the philology stratum; downgrade the idiom-fossil claim to a colourful aside, explicitly flagged as etymologically unreliable.** [T6: S2 for philological method]

### C11 — The anglophone-bias thesis
**Verdict: a method observation, already corrected in doc 08, and not a throughline about the traditions.** Doc 08 conceded it is partly self-refuting (the strongest sources — Anglo, al-Sarraf, Shahar, Zarrilli — are in English). **Reclassify as a qualified methodological note, not a finding about the object of study.** [T6: see doc 08]

### C12 — "The European Renaissance fight-book is one record-format, not the comparative norm"
**Verdict: the corpus's best and most defensible throughline — and directly corroborated, though again not original.** This is precisely Wetzler's thesis in *"Your Kung Fu is very good, Master Fiore!"* (Acta Periodica Duellatorum 4(2), 2016): the fight book "is not restricted to the European tradition" — China, Japan, Korea, and India have analogues combining text and image. Survives the whole battery. Its only defect is the novelty claim: Wetzler published it in 2016. **Keep; cite Wetzler 2016 as the academic statement of it.** [T6: S2 — Wetzler 2016]

## Meta-throughlines (patterns in the throughlines themselves)

**M1 — The corpus is largely an uncited re-derivation of martial-arts-studies.** Its three load-bearing patterns map one-to-one onto established frameworks: demystification = invention of tradition (Hobsbawm 1983; Bowman 2021); the surrogate/duel-to-sport arc = sportization (Elias & Dunning 1986); the cross-cultural fight-book thesis = Wetzler (2016). The intellectual content is mostly sound; the originality claims are not, and the failure to cite the field is the deepest defect — it is what let selection-effect "universals" pass unchallenged.

**M2 — The recurring failure mode is the selection-effect universal.** Patterns visible in the three or four best-documented traditions (German, Italian, Japanese, and the European corpus generally) are projected onto all. The deep revision named this once (for counterattack); it recurs in the demystification "universal," the training-surrogate "universal," and the four-axis "lens." Wetzler's anti-fixed-category stance and his blunt pitfall — "none of us can be experts in everything" — are the field's name for exactly this hazard, and the corpus's breadth (22 languages, 1–2 searches each) maximises exposure to it.

**M3 — Grandeur is inversely correlated with support.** The claims billed largest ("principal structural contribution," "candidate universal") are the ones that fail the battery; the claims that survive (patronage, the fight-book-as-genre, the mnemonic manual below) are the modest, well-sourced ones. The corpus's rhetoric runs opposite to its evidence.

**M4 — A real throughline the corpus underplayed, and the literature supports: the mnemonic manual.** Across the German *Zettel*, the Chinese manuals (which Wetzler, citing Kennedy, notes were "largely useless unless one had already trained in that school… shorthand notes, mnemonic"), and the koryū *densho*, the fight book is a *memory aid for initiates, not a self-contained system*. Wetzler (2016) states this directly as a cross-tradition finding. It passes the battery, it is sourced, and it reframes the entire corpus: if manuals are mnemonic, then "documented" traditions are far less fully recoverable from their texts than the classification axis (C7) implies — which partially collapses the documented/oral distinction the corpus leaned on. **This should replace the training-surrogate as the corpus's headline cross-tradition claim.** [T6: S2 — Wetzler 2016; Kennedy & Guo, *Chinese Martial Arts Training Manuals*]

**One-line summary.** After an adversarial pass and a check against the literature, the corpus has perhaps **two** defensible headline throughlines (the fight book as a cross-cultural genre; the manual as a mnemonic aid), **three** legitimate but unremarkable ones (patronage, philology, lineage-as-legitimacy), and a set of "universals" that are either restatements of Hobsbawm/Elias/Wetzler or selection effects — and its central methodological lesson is that it should have read the field before theorising it. *[Source: high; Interpretive: high — the dispositions rest on named S2 scholarship and on internal tests that are directly inspectable.]*

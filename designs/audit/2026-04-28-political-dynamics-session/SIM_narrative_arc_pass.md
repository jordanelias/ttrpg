# Narrative-Arc Pass — Reading SIM-A/B/C/D as Player Experience

**Method:** Re-read the four simulation documents as a continuous story rather than discrete mechanical traces. The simulations share a common cast (Almud, Marshal, Confessor, Ambassador, Reformer, Spouse, Solmund's Baker/Smith, Eastfort's Captain). What does the player actually experience over a 4-year arc if all these mechanics fire?
**Purpose:** Validate the project-README intent — *"a positive feedback loop between player decisions and mechanics/system/designs that produces an engaging game world with emergent narratives."* Does the engine produce **stories**, or merely **chronicles**?

---

## §1 The arc that emerges

If the player runs through the events traced across SIM-A/B/C/D, the implicit storyline is roughly:

**Year 1 — settling into the court.** Player is a mid-Standing Crown member, married (Knot) to Spouse. Crown's inner circle is in balance: Almud the Order-aligned leader, Marshal his Order-aligned military right hand, Confessor the Faith voice, Ambassador the Continuity voice. Reformer waits at the periphery, S3, ambitious in an Autonomy frame the court has never quite given room. Marshal proposes a garrison reinforcement; he wins easily — Crown is Order, military aligns. The player attends his celebration if interested, witnesses court ritual.

In the background, Confessor proposes a Crusade — a Faith-coded military project. Crown's meta-armature, dominated by Order, prefers Marshal's. Confessor's stalls. The player, watching with Read or Surveil, sees Confessor visibly frustrated though never explicitly told. Confessor sends an Outreach scene — wants to discuss doctrine — Priority 3, available. Player skips it; busy with other things. The Concern decays.

Reformer pushes a guild-charter project in the economic domain, well-aligned with his Autonomy primary. He bypasses a Priority 5-6 institutional duty to do it. His low institutional_deference fails the displacement check. Almud notices, develops a Concern about Reformer's reliability. The player learns this only obliquely, through gossip channels (Procedure E knowledge sharing) — never directly told *Almud doubts Reformer*, but the Surveil-readable surface shows Almud's increased attentiveness to Reformer's movements.

In the frontier, Eastfort reports raid threats. The Captain (governor) generates a settlement Signal which surfaces as Marshal's Concern. Marshal proposes military reinforcement; the player can attend the planning session if they choose.

**Year 1 close.** Standing recalc: Marshal earns S7 from a strong Year 1. Reformer crosses S3→S4 — milestone scene fires (per N-DIAG-A featured behavior). The player can attend Reformer's first inner-circle meeting; the framing reveals what Reformer is about, what he wants.

**Year 2 — the slow strain.** Confessor's pattern of stalling-against-Almud calcifies. Confessor's Concerns about *Marshal* start crystallizing — doctrinal doubts about discipline (per SIM-A canonical chain). Confessor sends another Outreach to the player about doctrine. If skipped, Confessor's frustration deepens. If attended, the player can become an unwitting confidant in Confessor's growing dissent.

Meanwhile, an event implicates the player in some treaty matter. Marshal develops a low-salience Concern about it — Priority 3 outreach. Player ignores it. Across two more Accountings, Marshal's evidence accumulates; eventually his Concern reaches salience 5 / ttl 1, and the Outreach escalates to Priority 2 mandatory. **The player must face Marshal.** This is a forced confrontation scene, the kind of dramatic beat the engine reaches via cumulative pressure rather than scripted timing.

In the same period, Spouse's marital concerns surface — Knot mechanic (PATCH 3.6). Spouse's Concern about marital distance, Priority 2 mandatory through the Knot rule. The player must attend. Dialogue choices determine whether the marriage strengthens or strains.

In Solmund, a bad harvest occurs. Crown's response is inadequate. Population_disposition drops. The Settlement Signal carries that anger upward — eventually reaches Almud as a Concern about governance quality. Cross-cutting: meanwhile, Smith of Solmund (whose son the player saved in some earlier arc the engine remembers via Memory) carries a strongly-amplified positive Memory of the player. His Memory weights bias Solmund's Settlement Signal toward "player_intervention" as the dominant tag. Almud receives a Concern explicitly tagged about player-intervention. The player's reputation is now institutionally tracked, sharply, because the player did something concrete.

**Year 2 close.** Standing recalc. Marshal hangs at S7 (mediocre year, no movement). Confessor's situation depends on the SIM-B-G8 interpretation — either he holds (strict definition) or he drops (liberal definition). This is the *narrative pivot point* mechanically: the spec's ambiguity about lost-competitions-as-failures determines whether Confessor's arc is one of recovery or one of fading.

**Years 3–4 — drift.** If Confessor recovers, he eventually proposes successfully (after deadlock breaks via Almud's project completion freeing the slot, or via stall escalation). If he fades, Crown loses its Faith voice. Either way, Crown's strategic flavor over the 4 years has migrated: from Order/Continuity/Faith balance to Order-dominant with Autonomy-emerging and Faith retreating. Marshal solidified, Reformer entered, Confessor either held or fell, Ambassador (Continuity) faded into the background or out of inner circle entirely.

By the end of Year 4, the player can look back at the court and see it has changed. Not because of any single event, but because of the slow accretion of dozens of resolved Concerns, drifted Opinions, completed projects, recalculated Standings.

## §2 What works narratively

**Cumulative drift is observable.** Standing recalc at Year boundaries gives the player a discrete "the court has changed" moment four times per Campaign Year cycle. The N-DIAG-A milestone scenes anchor inner-circle entries/exits as narrative events. The composition shift (Order-dominant by Year 4) is something the player can articulate.

**Forced confrontations have legitimate buildup.** Marshal's mandatory P2 outreach in Year 2 doesn't come out of nowhere — the player chose to skip his earlier P3 outreaches. The escalation feels *earned*. This is the engine doing what scripted-narrative games can't: dramatic pressure that emerges from the player's actual choices rather than from an author's predetermined act-structure.

**The Knot path is structurally distinct.** Spouse's marital-dialogue scene operates outside political logic — it's a private register the engine maintains. The player's domestic life can rise and fall on a different axis from court politics. This is good — it gives the world dimensionality.

**Player footprint is visible.** Smith's amplified Memory of the player saving his son sharpens Solmund's Settlement Signal. Almud receives an explicitly player-tagged Concern. The player is *visible to the institution* in a way that tracks what they actually did, not just what they declared. This is the design's strongest narrative engine: PATCH 3.12's amplification scope correctly couples player-action to institutional attention.

**Multi-character arcs proceed in parallel.** Confessor's slow doctrinal crisis (via SIM-A drift) runs alongside Marshal's institutional rise (via SIM-B competition wins) which runs alongside Reformer's careful Autonomy push (via SIM-B displacement). No GM is coordinating these — the mechanics generate them simultaneously. The player can engage with one, two, or all of them.

## §3 What's weaker narratively

**The arcs lack thematic tension.** The mechanics produce events but don't ask why each character cares. Reformer is "Autonomy primary" — but what does he *want*? What past wound made him so? The engine has Memories, but the Founding Memory (per ST-16-A featured behavior) is the only mechanism that anchors a personal core, and even that is one entry, not a backstory. Confessor's growing doubt about Marshal feels like *drift* rather than *crisis* — his Opinion shifted from +1 to +0.76 because of a low-salience resolution Memory; the magnitude is too small to feel like a turning point unless the player has been observing carefully.

**Player choices feel reactive, not proactive.** The simulations show the player attending or skipping Outreach scenes, choosing dialogue paths. But the engine rarely surfaces *next moves* — "you could intervene in Confessor's deadlock by sponsoring his Crusade through your own Standing influence" or "you could exploit Reformer's displacement vulnerability by reporting it to Almud." The Domain Action Proposal Phase is an NPC mechanism; the player isn't proposing alongside. The Read/Surveil/Witness surfaces show observation, not agency. **Player as audience to NPC drama is the dominant mode.** Procedure-E gossip and Outreach are the player's only proactive levers, and they're reactive too (responding to NPC initiative).

**Settlement-scale events feel like weather.** "guild_dispute in Westmarch" is a Settlement Signal tag. It's not someone's story. Without character-level grounding (a specific guildmaster, his family, his rival), the Signal is just political weather. The settlements generate Signals but don't generate *stories*. Solmund's bad harvest is statistical, not narrative — there's no scene of a weeping farmer or a grain-hoarding speculator.

**Concerns are abstract.** The simulations talk about "Concern about Marshal's discipline" or "Concern about player_intervention." But the player doesn't *attend a Concern* — they attend an Outreach scene that surfaces it. The Concerns themselves are interior NPC weather. The Read action makes them *observable* but not *engageable*. A player who Reads to discover Confessor's Concerns about Marshal can't directly act on that knowledge except by influencing the broader political picture (proposing Confessor for promotion via Knot leverage, etc.).

**4-year arcs may feel diffuse.** A Renaissance political pace is realistic, but Renaissance-pace games often lose dramatic structure — too much chronicle, not enough novel. The cumulative-drift property (Crown going Order-dominant by Year 4) is intellectually interesting but emotionally low-stakes. There's no third-act crisis the engine reliably produces.

**The Faction Crisis state (per §5.4) is the closest thing to a third-act crisis the engine has.** When ≥40% of inner circle is Distracted/Grieving, the faction enters institutional autopilot. This is dramatic — but it's also *bad for the player* (they can't push initiatives) and arrives passively. It's not a *crisis the player engineered*; it's a state they observe and wait through. **There is no mechanic for "player precipitates court crisis through manipulation."**

## §4 Verdict — chronicle vs novel

**The engine produces chronicles reliably and stories partially.**

Chronicles: yes. The mechanics generate a consistent court-history feel. Inner-circle composition shifts; settlements report sentiment; NPCs propose, succeed, fail, age out. A player at the end of Year 4 can articulate "Crown became more Order-dominant; Confessor faded under deadlock pressure; Reformer rose; Marshal solidified." This is genuine emergent narrative substance.

Stories: partially. Specific story-shapes emerge for the player who **engages selectively** — the Marshal-confrontation arc (Year 2's mandatory P2 outreach) is dramatic; the Knot-marriage struggle is intimate. But these arcs are *individually* dramatic; their collective texture is closer to a courtly diary than a plotted novel.

**The player who stays passive will experience the engine as a political simulation that they observe with strategic interest.** The player who engages every Outreach, attends every milestone, makes deliberate Disposition-shifting choices, will assemble dramatic stretches into something more like a story.

The design intent — *"a positive feedback loop between player decisions and mechanics/system/designs that produces an engaging game world with emergent narratives"* — is achieved at the *world* level (the engine produces an engaging political world). The *narrative* level is achieved where the player chooses to make their own engagement dramatic.

**Implication for Direction E (composition):** When tracing multi-faction multi-decade dynamics, the question is whether scaling up multiplies the narrative strength (more arcs running in parallel = richer story-substrate) or whether it dilutes (more chronicle, more weather, less story-shape). The simulation should specifically test for **dramatic intensity** alongside mechanical correctness — does at least one character's arc reach a clear *climax* over the trace, or does everything just drift?

## §5 Concrete suggestions surfaced by this pass (not formal gaps; design-level observations)

These are not specification gaps — the spec is internally consistent. These are observations about narrative texture worth Jordan's design attention.

1. **Player-proactive levers are thin.** Consider mechanics where the player can *initiate* Concerns in NPCs (e.g., "plant doubt about Reformer with Almud during a private scene"). Currently the player can only react to NPC-generated Concerns.

2. **Concern-internalization sub-scenes.** When a high-salience Concern is generated in an inner-circle NPC, consider exposing a private monologue/reflection scene the player can choose to witness via Read or special interaction. Makes the Concern feel like *someone's experience* rather than a stat.

3. **Settlement-as-character.** The strongest settlement Signals could emit a *named-NPC reaction* alongside the Signal itself — "Smith of Solmund tells the magistrate he doesn't trust the new tariff" — giving the Signal a face. Currently it's aggregate sentiment.

4. **Climax mechanics.** Consider a "Faction Watershed" event class that fires when cumulative drift crosses a threshold (e.g., institutional weight on dominant Conviction exceeds 0.85). Surfaces a specific dramatic moment: "Crown's leadership now agrees on every issue — the Faith voice has effectively left the room." Discrete narrative beat to anchor the long drift.

5. **Player Founding Memory.** Mirror NPCs' permanent Salience-5 Founding Memory (ST-16-A) for the player. Lets the engine treat the player as a character with personal anchor rather than purely as agent-of-action.

These are out-of-scope for v1.2 patch resolution; they're forward-looking design questions to consider after v1.2 stabilizes.

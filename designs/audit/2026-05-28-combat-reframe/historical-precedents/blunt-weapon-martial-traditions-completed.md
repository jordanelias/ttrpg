# Martial Traditions Centred on Blunt Weapons: A Grounded Historical Survey

*An evidence-led survey distinguishing what is documented from what is folklore. Traditions are grouped by the strength of their surviving evidence, not by region or romantic appeal.*

> **Completed edition — pure mace / war hammer / flail integrated.** The first edition gave the percussion-metal family (mace, war hammer, military flail) only a passing treatment, folding its technique into the pollaxe and otherwise noting "no dedicated treatise survives." This edition adds a dedicated **§4 The percussion-metal family** that treats the mace, the war hammer and its hafted cousins, and the flail/morning star at proper length and evidence-grade; the tier table (§3), the grounded-vs-folklore audit (§8) and the sources (§9) are expanded to match. The original staff-and-stick material is preserved; the tier sections are renumbered (§5–§7). The governing finding is unchanged and now demonstrated rather than asserted: the deadliest blunt weapons are the best-attested as *objects* and the worst-attested as *arts*.

## 1. Scope and a necessary distinction

"Blunt weapon" covers two families that have very different evidentiary footprints, and conflating them is the single most common error in popular accounts.

**(a) Percussive metal weapons for anti-armour use** — the mace (war club with a metal head), the war hammer, and the military flail. These were designed to defeat armour by transmitting shock through it (or, with a beak or flange, by piercing and catching it) rather than cutting it. They are abundantly attested in **archaeology and iconography** (surviving examples in the Royal Armouries, the Wallace Collection, the Metropolitan Museum; countless depictions in manuscripts, sculpture, and tomb effigies). Critically, **almost no dedicated technical treatise survives for the bare mace or war hammer.** What technique we can reconstruct comes embedded in the literature of the weapons' *hafted, two-handed cousins* — above all the *pollaxe* (poleaxe), and the closely related pole hammer and war flail — which the late-medieval and Renaissance fight books treat in detail. This whole family is now §4.

**(b) Staff and stick arts** — the quarterstaff, the Chinese *gùn*, the Japanese *bō* and *jō*, the Filipino rattan stick, the African fighting stick, the Portuguese and Irish staves. These wood-hafted percussive weapons carry the **richest surviving technical literature and the most robust living traditions.** This is where most genuine "blunt-weapon martial arts," in the sense of transmissible systems, are found (§§5–7).

A useful mental model: the deadliest blunt weapons (mace, war hammer) are the *best-attested as objects* but the *worst-attested as arts*; the staff and stick are the reverse. §4 is the demonstration of the first half of that statement; the rest of the document is the second half.

## 2. How the evidence works, and the lineage-myth problem

Evidence for historical martial practice comes, in rough order of usefulness for reconstructing actual technique:

- **Fight manuals / treatises** (*Fechtbücher*, military manuals, fencing books) — the gold standard, because they describe technique directly.
- **Military encyclopedias and drill manuals** — show what armies trained.
- **Archaeology** — the weapons themselves, plus battlefield trauma evidence on skeletons.
- **Iconography** — manuscript illuminations, temple murals, sculpture, prints.
- **Legal and press records** — court documents, coroners' inquests, newspaper reports (especially valuable for folk and faction traditions).
- **Ethnography and oral tradition** — indispensable for living non-literate traditions, but unreliable for chronology.

The recurring trap is the **origin myth**. Many martial arts attach themselves to a legendary founder or a vast antiquity that the documentary record does not support: Bodhidharma "inventing" Shaolin boxing, Lapu-Lapu as a "Kali" grandmaster, "2,000-year-old" mace combat. The honest scholarly position, articulated by historians such as Meir Shahar and Benjamin Judkins for China, is that even where a manual *does* survive, reconstructing the living art centuries later is "a fundamentally creative" process, because manuals omit the "common knowledge" their original readers already possessed. Antiquity claims should therefore be treated as cultural assertions until tied to a dated source. This caution bites hardest in §4, where the *objects* are ancient and abundant but the *art* is largely a modern reconstruction or an inference from adjacent weapons.

## 4. The percussion-metal family: mace, war hammer, flail

This is the family in which the "objects-versus-arts" asymmetry of §1 is sharpest. The mace, the war hammer and the military flail are among the most recognisable weapons of the premodern world and are richly attested in metal and in pictures — yet they generated almost no dedicated technical literature in any culture. The technique that *can* be reconstructed comes from two indirect sources: (a) the weapons' **hafted, two-handed cousins** that the fight books actually treat — the pollaxe (§5.1), the pole hammer, and the two-handed war flail; and (b) the **armoured-combat** sections (German *Harnischfechten* and its analogues) that teach how to strike, hook, trip and grapple an armoured opponent with a hafted percussive weapon. The bare, one-handed mace and war hammer have, on present evidence, **no surviving dedicated treatise** — not in the European fight-book corpus, not in the Mamluk furusiyya literature, not in the South Asian record.

### 4.1 The mace

The mace has a strong claim to be **among the oldest weapons purpose-built to fight other humans** rather than adapted from a hunting tool — an argument associated with Robert O'Connell. Stone mace-heads appear in **predynastic Egypt** roughly six thousand years ago: disc-shaped heads in the period around 3850–3650 BC (an attempt to add impact and a crude cutting capacity), giving way to the rounded "piriform" (pear-shaped) head through the Naqada II–III periods (c. 3600–3100 BC). The mace is an **emblem of power from the very beginning of the record**: the **Narmer Palette** and the **Scorpion Macehead** (c. 3100 BC) depict rulers wielding the mace as both weapon and symbol of sovereignty. Mesopotamia shows parallel development. The early limiting factors were physical — stone heads shattered, and fixing a heavy head reliably to a wooden haft was difficult.

As a *battlefield primary* the mace receded with the spread of shields, helmets and edged weapons, then **re-emerged in high- and late-medieval Europe (roughly the 11th to 15th centuries) precisely as armour advanced.** The logic is the same one that recurs throughout this family: a blunt head transmits shock *through* mail and plate — concussion, broken bones, stunning — without needing to cut or pierce. An honest qualification the sources insist on: even at its medieval height the hand mace was usually a **secondary weapon and a status object**, not the *preferred* anti-plate primary (that role belonged to the hafted hammer and the poleaxe of §4.2 and §5.1).

- **The flanged mace.** Replacing the head's smooth or knobbed surface with radial **flanges** concentrates the blow's force onto a narrow edge that can dent, split or catch plate. The earliest European maces are knobbed; flanged forms became common from about the 12th century. The **pernach** — a flanged mace developed in **Kievan Rus' in the 12th century**, its name from Slavic *pero* ("feather"), its profile resembling a fletched arrow — was the **first flanged mace to see wide use** and spread westward across Europe; the Byzantine **bardoukion** is referenced as early as c. 900.
- **Regional forms.** The Persian **gorz / gurz** (a heavy spherical-headed mace) served heavy infantry and cataphracts across many eras. The South Asian **gada** (see §7.2) was a real battlefield and ceremonial weapon as well as a vehicle of epic imagery. Cavalry maces ran longer (for blows from horseback); foot maces were short (c. 60–90 cm); two-handed maces existed but were exceptional.
- **The cheap weapon of the levy.** Because a mace is mechanically simple and inexpensive to make, it was **common among conscripts and peasant rebels** — a point with a bearing on any "ease of use" model: the mace asks little technical training relative to a sword, which is part of *why* so little technique was ever written down for it.
- **The emblem that outlived the weapon.** From Narmer to the **ceremonial maces of the British House of Commons, the U.S. Congress, and universities**, and to the **buzdygan / bulava command-maces** carried as marks of rank in Eastern Europe and the steppe, the mace's *symbolic* life has comfortably outlasted its battlefield life. The weapon became a sceptre.

**Evidence grade.** Strong-to-overwhelming as object and iconography across several millennia; **effectively nil as a dedicated, transmissible technical art.** Folklore to flag: any "continuous ancient mace-fighting lineage," and the popular trope that **clergy wielded maces to avoid shedding blood** — the latter rests largely on iconography such as the Bayeux Tapestry's Bishop Odo with a club, not on any canon-law rule, and is best treated as a cultural tradition rather than documented fact.

### 4.2 The war hammer and its hafted cousins

The war hammer is the **late-medieval anti-plate specialist**, at its most prominent in the 15th century. Its head pairs a **blunt face** (to concuss through armour) with a **pick or beak** (to pierce plate and catch its weak points — visor slits, armpits, the backs of joints). Battlefield-trauma evidence makes the use vivid: skulls bearing the combined marks of hammer blows and arrow strikes, and, in the Metropolitan Museum, a bascinet showing the near-penetration left by a beak.

War hammers came in one- and two-handed forms, and it is the **two-handed, hafted forms that carry a documented art**, shading directly into the poleaxe:

- the **pole hammer** (a poleaxe whose head is a hammer);
- the **bec de corbin** ("raven's" or "crow's beak," used primarily *beak-first* to tear and catch plate; the term also became a catch-all for several hammer types);
- the **Lucerne hammer** (a Swiss form with a multi-pronged hammer face, a long top spike and a rear beak on a shaft of around seven feet; it emerged in central Switzerland in the 1470s as a development of the bec de corbin. "Lucerne hammer" is a **modern label**, coined in 1869 from the large cache of surviving examples in the Lucerne armoury — contemporaries simply called the thing *die Hamer*, *mordaxt*, or *la hache*);
- the **horseman's pick** (a cavalry war hammer with a long, down-curved pick; the Persian and Indian **war pick or "crow's bill"** is the same concept).

**The documentation vector.** These hafted hammers are exactly the percussive weapons the fight books *do* teach. **Paulus Hector Mair's** encyclopedic *Opus Amplissimum de Arte Athletica* (c. 1540s; see §9) treats the **pole hammer** alongside the poleaxe, halberd and spear, and the poleaxe corpus itself — Fiore, *Le Jeu de la Hache*, Talhoffer (§5.1) — is the technical home for hammer-spike-and-hook play against an armoured man. So the war hammer's *technique* survives, by inheritance, where the bare mace's does not — but only through its **hafted, two-handed relatives**, never as a stand-alone "one-handed war hammer" treatise.

**Evidence grade.** Strong as objects and trauma evidence; **moderate as an art, but only via the poleaxe / pole-hammer corpus**; nil as a dedicated bare-hammer manual.

### 4.3 The flail and the morning star

Two terminological points must come first, because they are the most common errors in the whole subject.

1. **A morning star is not a flail.** The *morning star* (German *Morgenstern*) is a **rigid** spiked club or mace-on-a-shaft; the *flail* has its striking head joined to the haft by a **flexible** chain, rope or strap. The morning star came into wide use from about the **14th century, especially in Germany**, in grades running from lathe-turned soldiers' weapons through nail-studded peasant-militia versions; it resembles a mace and was developed largely independently of the flail. Confusing the two is endemic in popular writing and should be resisted.
2. **There are two very different flails, with very different evidence.**

- The **two-handed war flail** descends from the **agricultural threshing flail** (*fléau d'armes*; German *Kriegsflegel*): a long wooden haft joined by a short chain to one or more iron-shod bars. This is the **better-attested** military flail. It saw real use by the **Hussite** peasant armies (1419–1436), period art generally shows it wielded in two hands on a haft over a metre long, and it is **recorded in Mair's *Opus*** as a "rare form of flail" among his peasant weapons (scythe, sickle, two-handed club). Its tactical virtue is the capacity to **strike around a shield or parry**; its liabilities are poor control and real danger in close ranks.
- The **one-handed ball-and-chain "military flail"** of films and games is **genuinely contested by historians.** Careful scholarship (for example *The Public Medievalist*; and the debate around Manfred Waldman's "extant but rare" reading versus the sceptics' "artistic fantasy") concludes it was **rare to non-existent as a real battlefield weapon** — present in art and in later decorative or ceremonial pieces, but not a documented field arm. It is the **most doubtful member of this whole family**, and confident claims about its battlefield use should be treated as folklore.

Adjacent forms worth placing here: the **kisten**, a flail-type attested in the lands of the Rus' from the **10th century** (probably adopted from the Avars or Khazars) and spreading into Central and Eastern Europe over the 11th–13th centuries; and the **goedendag**, a Flemish infantry weapon (a thick shaft of roughly 1.2–1.8 m topped with a stout iron spike), club-and-thrust rather than a true flail, which proved its worth at **Courtrai in 1302**.

**Evidence grade.** Two-handed war flail — **moderate** (objects + Hussite use + Mair). One-handed ball-and-chain flail — **contested / weak.** Morning star — moderate as an object, but **not a flail.**

### 4.4 Why this family has no art — and what the absence means

The first edition stated the governing rule; §4 demonstrates it. The deadliest percussion weapons are the **best-attested as objects and the worst-attested as arts**, and the reason is structural, not accidental:

- **A weapon whose use is "deliver a forceful blow" needs little codification.** A precision weapon — the rapier, the longsword — rewards endless written subtlety in measure, timing, blade leverage and the bind; a mace swung hard does not. The amount of surviving *technique* tracks a weapon's *demanding-ness*. (This is the same axis that, in the companion historical-combat survey, separates the rapier and longsword libraries from the near-silence around impact weapons.)
- **The percussion weapons that *did* earn a literature are precisely the ones that became complex.** The poleaxe, the pole hammer and the two-handed war flail acquired hooking, tripping, half-hafting and grappling techniques — they earned a corpus by becoming more than "hit hard," and so they sit in the fight books while the bare mace and bare hammer do not.
- **Even the richest non-European corpus confirms the pattern.** The Mamluk **furusiyya** literature — the famous training manual *Nihayat al-Su'l* (late 14th c.), studied by David Nicolle, and Andalusian works such as Ibn Hudayl's *Finery of the Knights* — is organised around horsemanship, archery, the lance and the sword. The **mace (*dabbus*) is named among the weapons a *faris* must be able to handle, but receives no dedicated treatise.** The same holds in South Asia, where the gada's living tradition survives as **conditioning, not combat** (§7.2).

**Practical upshot for reconstruction.** Anyone working to recover percussion technique should build outward from the **poleaxe and pole-hammer fight books and the armoured-combat grappling sections**, treat the bare mace and war hammer as simplified cases of those, and treat any claim of an "ancient, unbroken, dedicated mace-fighting system" as folklore until it is tied to a dated source.

## 5. Tier 1 — Traditions with rich, datable technical documentation

### 5.1 The European pollaxe and the anti-armour percussion family (c. 1400–1630)

The pollaxe was the characteristic foot-combat weapon of the late-medieval armoured knight, used in the lists (formal duels and tournaments) and on the battlefield. Because it pairs a percussive head (hammer or axe) with a thrusting spike and a butt spike, it is the weapon through which the European fight-book tradition actually documents *how to strike, hook, and grapple an armoured opponent with a hafted percussive weapon* — knowledge that applies directly to the pure mace and war hammer for which no comparable manual survives (the reason it anchors §4).

The key sources:

- **Fiore dei Liberi, *Fior di Battaglia* (c. 1409–1410).** The most complete copy is the Getty manuscript (J. Paul Getty Museum, MS Ludwig XV 13); other recensions survive at the Morgan Library (MS M.383), the Bibliothèque nationale de France (the "Paris" version), and the privately held Pisani-Dossi ("Florius") manuscript. Fiore devotes a dedicated section to the *azza* (pollaxe) in armour. He memorably characterises it as ponderous, cruel, and mortal. Fiore himself was a documented fencing master active in northern Italy in the 1380s–1400s, training men for judicial duels.
- ***Le Jeu de la Hache*** ("The Play of the Axe", BnF MS Français 1996). The **earliest surviving treatise devoted solely to the pollaxe**, and the oldest known martial-arts text in French. Linguistic and codicological analysis dates it to roughly **1460–1485** and places its origin in the Burgundian orbit (Flanders/Wallonia). It is unillustrated but technically dense — 70-odd numbered passages covering right- vs. right-handed and right- vs. left-handed combats. The standard study is **Sydney Anglo's 1991 edition** in *Archaeologia* (vol. 109), with later critical re-editions.
- **Hans Talhoffer's *Fechtbücher* (1443–1467)** and other 15th–16th-century German masters — notably **Paulus Hector Mair** (mid-1500s), whose *Opus Amplissimum de Arte Athletica* systematises poleaxe, **pole hammer**, halberd, staff and war flail together (§§4.2–4.3, §9) — include illustrated pollaxe and hafted-percussion material.

Sydney Anglo's *The Martial Arts of Renaissance Europe* (Yale, 2000) is the authoritative academic synthesis. The digital reference repository **Wiktenauer** hosts transcriptions and translations of essentially all of these primary sources.

**On the pure mace and war hammer:** robust object and image evidence, effectively no dedicated treatise — treated in full at §4. Robert O'Connell and others have argued the mace was among the first weapons designed specifically for fighting other humans rather than adapted from a hunting tool. The military flail is real but its battlefield prevalence is frequently overstated in popular culture, and the one-handed ball-and-chain form is of contested authenticity (§4.3).

### 5.2 The English quarterstaff / "short staff" (c. 1590–1900)

England produced an unusually explicit literature praising the staff as a *superior* serious weapon, not merely a peasant's tool.

- **George Silver, *Paradoxes of Defence* (London, 1599)** and his companion manuscript ***Brief Instructions Upon My Paradoxes of Defence*** (c. 1605). Silver, an English gentleman hostile to the fashionable imported Italian rapier, ranked the "short staff" at or near the top of all single combat weapons, bettered only (in his scheme) by the forest bill. His argument rests on reach, speed, and the staff's two-handed control — he held that a skilled staff-man could safely defeat sword-and-dagger or rapier-and-poniard opponents by governing distance and time. (Silver uses "short staff" and "long staff"; he does not use the word "quarterstaff.")
- **Joseph Swetnam, *The Schoole of the Noble and Worthy Science of Defence* (1617).** Distinguishes a "quarterstaff" of roughly 7–8 feet from a "long staff" of 11–12 feet, and treats staff play in detail.
- The tradition continues in later English texts (e.g., Zachary Wylde, early 1700s) and in the 18th-century prizefighting schools (James Figg's academy taught staff alongside sword and fist). It is folded into 19th-century British single-stick and walking-stick literature, including **Rowland Allanson-Winn's *Broad-sword and Single-stick* (1890s)**.

The Oxford English Dictionary's earliest "quarterstaff" attestation is 1590; the "quarter" most likely refers to how the staff was made (riven from a quartered log) rather than the grip, the grip explanation being a later popular etymology.

### 5.3 The Chinese staff (*gùn*) and the Ming military-martial corpus (c. 1550–1640)

In Chinese martial culture the staff is conventionally called the "grandfather" or chief of weapons, and — crucially for a grounded account — **the oldest reliably datable Chinese martial-arts manuals are about the staff**, produced amid the late-Ming crisis (coastal *wokou* pirate raids, military decline, conscription of irregulars including monks).

- **Yu Dayou (俞大猷), *Jian Jing* ("Classic of the Sword," c. 1560s).** Despite its title, this celebrated text is, as martial-arts historians note, substantially a treatise on the long pole. General Yu visited Shaolin around 1560, found the monks' staff skills disappointing, took two monks (Zongqing and Pucong) south to train for three years, and sent them back to teach — an episode traced by the historian Tang Hao.
- **Qi Jiguang (戚繼光), *Jixiao Xinshu* ("New Treatise on Military Efficiency," 1561; surviving woodblock editions c. 1584).** A foundational military manual that praises and records Shaolin staff method and incorporates a staff routine into troop training. Its 1584 editions are among the oldest extant original martial records in China.
- **Cheng Zongyou (程宗猷), *Shaolin Gunfa Chanzong* ("Exposition of the Original Shaolin Staff Method," compiled c. 1610, published 1621).** The **oldest historically authenticated manual dedicated to Shaolin staff fighting.** Part of his collection *Geng Yu Sheng Ji* ("Skills Beyond Farming"), and longer than his manuals on crossbow, spear, and saber combined. It codifies named staff sets (e.g., Small and Large Yaksha, Yin Hand) and explicitly aims to distinguish authentic Shaolin method from imitations.
- **Wu Shu (吳殳), *Shoubi Lu* / *Arms Exercises* (mid-1600s).** Primarily on the spear, but a further detailed contemporary witness to late-Ming martial culture.

The indispensable modern scholarship is **Meir Shahar, *The Shaolin Monastery: History, Religion, and the Chinese Martial Arts* (University of Hawai'i Press, 2008)**. Shahar shows that Shaolin's martial reputation is firmly documented only from the late Ming, that no Tang–Yuan source describes martial *training* at the monastery, and that the famous **Bodhidharma origin story is a fabrication** — it first appears in a 1624 qigong text (the *Yijin Jing*) written under a pseudonym and riddled with anachronisms. This is the model case of a documented art encrusted with an undocumented founding legend.

## 6. Tier 2 — Continuous or reconstructed traditions with partial documentation

### 6.1 Japanese *jō* and *bō*: Shintō Musō-ryū (early 1600s onward)

**Shintō Musō-ryū** (神道夢想流) is generally regarded as the oldest *jō* (short-staff, ~128 cm) school in Japan and is a genuine *koryū* (old transmission school) with a written legacy (*densho*) listing headmasters and techniques. Its founder, **Musō Gonnosuke Katsuyoshi** (fl. c. 1605), is a historical figure; the school was preserved for generations as a guarded tradition of the Kuroda clan in Fukuoka (Kyushu).

The **founding narrative**, however — that Gonnosuke was defeated by Miyamoto Musashi, retreated to Mt. Hōman for religious austerities, received a divine instruction to "strike the solar plexus with a round stick," devised the *jō*, and then beat Musashi in a rematch — is **oral tradition / legend**, not documented history; the rematch in particular is not recorded outside later fiction. The honest grade: the *transmission* is real and traceable; the *origin story* is unverifiable. The art was reframed in the 20th century by **Shimizu Takaji** as *jōdō* ("the way of the *jō*"), and a standardised **ZNKR Jōdō** (All Japan Kendo Federation) form was later created for wide practice. Curriculum accretions over time include allied arts (*jutte*, *kusarigama*, *tanjō*).

The *bō* (six-foot staff) appears across many koryū and, prominently, in Okinawan kobudō (below).

### 6.2 Okinawan kobudō: *bō*, *tonfa*, *sai* (codified late 1800s–1900s)

Okinawan weapon arts (*kobudō*) preserve blunt and semi-blunt implements: the *bō*, the *tonfa* (side-handled baton), and the *sai* (a truncheon — pointed but typically unsharpened, used for striking and trapping). The "peasant farm-tool origin under weapons bans" story is popular and partly true in spirit but is **oversimplified and weakly documented**; several of these implements have likely martial or imported antecedents. What is well documented is the **late-19th- to 20th-century systematisation** of kobudō by figures such as Yabiku Moden and especially **Taira Shinken**, who collected and codified disparate village traditions. Grade: solid for the modern systems; thin and partly legendary for the deep history.

### 6.3 Filipino stick arts — arnis / eskrima / "kali" (codified systems 19th–20th c.)

Filipino martial arts (FMA) are weapons-first systems in which the **rattan stick (*baston*, *olisi*) is the primary training implement** (often understood as a stand-in for the blade). The living, codified lineages are genuine and well-documented for the late 19th and 20th centuries — for example the Cebu-based clubs and systems that crystallised in the early-to-mid 1900s (Doce Pares; the Balintawak method associated with Anciong Bacon; later Modern Arnis associated with Remy Presas). In 2009, Republic Act No. 9850 designated arnis the national martial art and sport of the Philippines.

The **deep history is heavily mythologised and the documentary base is thin**, for an understandable reason: pre-colonial Philippine record-keeping was sparse and Spanish colonial suppression disrupted continuity. Honest points the scholarship insists on:

- Antonio Pigafetta, who chronicled Magellan's 1521 expedition, recorded the **Battle of Mactan** and the death of Magellan, but **did not record any martial art called "Kali."** The image of Lapu-Lapu as a "Kali grandmaster" is a later construction (and Lapu-Lapu may have been elderly and not personally in the fighting).
- The term **"Kali"** as the name of a single ancient "mother art" is **disputed and probably a 20th-century coinage**, popularised largely through the diaspora and Western teaching; in the Philippines the everyday terms are *arnis* and *eskrima*, with many regional names.
- The claim of an unbroken pre-colonial system surviving in secret is a cultural-resilience narrative more than a demonstrable lineage.

Grade: strong for the modern systems and the general fact of indigenous stick-and-blade combat observed at contact; unsubstantiated for a unified, named, ancient "Kali" tradition.

### 6.4 Irish *bataireacht* (shillelagh stick fighting) (c. 1700–1900)

Irish stick fighting with the *bata* / shillelagh (commonly a knobbed blackthorn cudgel) is best documented through the **faction-fighting era** — organised group brawls at fairs, markets, and "pattern days" from the 18th into the 19th century — which generated abundant **court records, newspaper reports, and visual caricature** (notably by the caricaturist John Doyle). Surviving **19th-century instructional texts** that describe stick or cudgel use include **Donald Walker's *Defensive Exercises* (1840)** and **Rowland Allanson-Winn's *Broad-sword and Single-stick* (1890s)**.

A few **family-transmitted styles** persist (most prominently the Doyle family's *rince an bhata uisce bheatha*, "dance of the whiskey stick," carried to Newfoundland), while much contemporary practice — including the well-known "Antrim Bata" — is openly a **modern reconstruction** built from period manuals, press, iconography, and court material. Grade: good documentary spine for the practice's existence and social role; the specific technical "systems" are partly reconstructed, and prehistoric/Bronze-Age antiquity claims are speculative.

### 6.5 Portuguese *jogo do pau* ("the stick game") (urban codification 19th c.)

A staff art of the mountainous north of Portugal and Galicia (Minho, Trás-os-Montes), historically used for self-defence (including against multiple opponents and animals) and to settle feuds and matters of honour. Its rural origins are undocumented, but it has a **real textual record from the late 19th and early 20th centuries**: **Joaquim António Ferreira, *A Arte do Jogo do Pau* (Porto, 1886)** and **Frederico Hopffer, *Duas Palavras sobre o Jogo do Pau* (Lisbon, 1924)**, the latter documenting the Lisbon school. The art was brought to Lisbon by northern masters and turned increasingly sportive in the late 1800s; it nearly died out by mid-20th century with urbanisation and firearms, then was **revived from the 1970s** on the memory of the last rural generation. The historian **T. J. Desch-Obi** discusses it (and notes a 19th-century episode in which sword bans pushed combatants toward the staff). Grade: moderate — solid recent documentation, undocumented deep origins.

### 6.6 French *canne de combat* and *bâton français* (19th c. onward)

French stick fencing with the light cane (*canne*) and the two-handed staff (*bâton*) was **codified as a gentleman's self-defence and physical-culture pursuit in 19th-century France**, intertwined with savate; method books and salles (notably associated with the Charlemont family) systematised it. It declined and was **revived in the later 20th century as a federated competitive sport** (*canne de combat*). Grade: moderate — the modern sport and its 19th-century methodisation are well documented; older roots are diffuse.

## 7. Tier 3 — Living oral/ethnographic traditions and largely-lost combat arts

### 7.1 Nguni / Zulu stick fighting (Southern Africa)

A living martial tradition of the Nguni peoples (Zulu, Xhosa, and others), classically practised by herd-boys and young men. Zulu practice typically uses an attacking stick (*isikhwili* / *induku*), a longer defending stick (*ubhoko*), and a small cowhide shield (*ihawu*); Xhosa practice often uses two sticks. It is socially embedded — historically tied to **military training under Shaka in the early 19th century**, and surviving today in courtship, weddings, and dispute-settlement, with modern efforts to formalise it as a sport. Nelson Mandela described practising stick fighting as a Thembu youth.

The tradition is **richly described ethnographically** but its **chronology is oral**: origins are variously attributed to Shaka's era or earlier (e.g., to Amalandela, c. 1670), and these dates are tradition rather than document. Related Southern/Eastern African stick traditions include the ceremonial *donga* stick-duelling of the Suri/Surma peoples of southwestern Ethiopia. Grade: unimpeachable as a *living* art; antiquity claims are oral.

### 7.2 Indian *gada* (mace) and the South Asian stick arts

The **mace (*gada*)** is deeply woven into South Asian martial *imagery* — the weapon of the deities Hanuman and (as *Kaumodaki*) Vishnu, and of epic heroes (Bhima, Duryodhana, Balarama), with mace-duelling (*gada-yuddha*) described in the **Mahabharata** and the **Agni Purana**, and depicted in sculpture. Maces were also real battlefield and ceremonial weapons across Indian history (and connect to the broader percussion-metal family of §4).

But the honest distinction matters: **the living tradition today is overwhelmingly physical culture, not combat.** In the wrestlers' *akhara* of north India and Pakistan (Pehlwani / Kushti, with strong devotional ties to Hanuman), the wooden or stone-headed *gada* — and clubs such as the *mudgar* / *jori* — are **strength-training implements** swung for grip, shoulder, and core conditioning (famously by the early-20th-century champion the Great Gama). The actual *combat art* of *gada-yuddha* survives mainly as **text and iconography, not as a transmissible technical system**; popular "ancient Hindu warrior mace workout" framings conflate the surviving conditioning practice with lost fighting technique. Grade: mixed — strong for the weapon's textual/iconographic presence and the living *physical-culture* practice; the combat art is essentially lost; "thousands of years old" combat-lineage claims are unverified.

Among India's **stick** traditions, the living arts include **lathi khela** (Bengali/Bihari staff play, documented in the colonial era), **silambam** (Tamil bamboo-staff art, with claimed Sangam-era antiquity that is **debated** and not securely datable), and **gatka** (a Punjabi/Sikh stick-and-sword art tied to Khalsa identity but whose demonstrational form is **largely a 19th-/20th-century (re)formation** under and against British-era influences). Grade: living arts with contested chronologies; treat antiquity claims cautiously.

## 8. What is well grounded vs. what is folklore

**Well grounded (documented in dated sources):**
- The European pollaxe was a sophisticated, written-about armoured-combat weapon (Fiore c. 1410; *Le Jeu de la Hache* c. 1460–85; Talhoffer; Mair).
- The **hafted war hammer family** (pole hammer, bec de corbin, Lucerne hammer, horseman's pick) was a real anti-plate weapon system, with the **pole hammer documented in Mair** and the technique inheritable from the poleaxe corpus (§4.2, §5.1).
- The **mace** is among the oldest weapons purpose-made to fight humans, and an **unbroken emblem of authority** from the Narmer Palette to modern ceremonial maces (§4.1).
- The **two-handed war flail** (threshing-flail derived) saw real use — notably by the **Hussites** — and is recorded by Mair (§4.3).
- The English considered the staff a first-rank weapon and wrote technical books on it (Silver 1599; Swetnam 1617).
- China's oldest datable martial manuals are about the staff (Yu Dayou c. 1560s; Qi Jiguang 1561/1584; Cheng Zongyou c. 1610/1621), and Shaolin's martial fame is a *late-Ming* phenomenon.
- Shintō Musō-ryū is a genuine, continuously transmitted *jō* school with written *densho*.
- Nguni/Zulu stick fighting and the Indian akhara mace/club practice are unbroken *living* traditions.

**Folklore or unproven (cultural claims, not documented history):**
- A continuous, ancient, dedicated *art* of **bare mace or war-hammer fighting** — none survives; such technique as exists is inferred from the hafted cousins and armoured-combat grappling (§4.4).
- The **one-handed ball-and-chain "military flail"** as a common medieval battlefield weapon — rare to non-existent per much scholarship (§4.3).
- The trope that **clergy fought with maces to avoid shedding blood** — a popular tradition resting on iconography, not a documented rule (§4.1).
- Bodhidharma as the founder of Shaolin martial arts (demonstrably a 1624 fabrication).
- Lapu-Lapu as a "Kali grandmaster"; "Kali" as a single ancient Filipino mother-art.
- The Musashi-vs-Gonnosuke rematch and divine origin of the *jō*.
- "2,000-year-old" continuous *gada* **combat** technique (as opposed to conditioning practice).
- Bronze-Age / prehistoric continuity for *bataireacht*; Sangam-era datable continuity for *silambam*.

## Note for the Valoria reading (optional; non-prescriptive)

This relates the now-complete percussion picture back to the conversation's earlier `combat_v32` cross-reference. It records implications only; it makes no design decision (project-owner contract).

- **The two long-blunt v32 classes now have full historical backing.** `long_pole_staff` is among the best-documented weapon forms in this survey (Chinese *gùn*, English short staff, Japanese *bō*/*jō*, Portuguese *jogo do pau*, French *bâton*, Okinawan *bō*, Nguni/Zulu) — comfortably 6+ distinct traditions. `long_heavy_blunt`'s documented *art* rests almost entirely on the **European pollaxe / pole-hammer family** (Fiore, *Le Jeu de la Hache*, Talhoffer, Mair), with the bare mace/hammer attested as objects everywhere but as an art almost nowhere.
- **The `long_heavy_blunt` handling tension, sharpened.** That single v32 class fuses two historically distinct weapons: a **technical, grappling-heavy hafted hammer/poleaxe** (consistent with a *Demanding* rating and a high skill ceiling) and a **bare mace** that needed little codification (the historically *forgiving*, levy-friendly weapon). The handling rating one would assign depends on which of the two the class is taken to represent — exactly the divergence flagged earlier, now with the documentary basis filled in.
- **The still-missing class is short blunt.** This survey's one-handed blunt arts (Irish shillelagh, French *canne*, Zulu *isikhwili*, the *tonfa*, conditioning clubs) have no home among v32's all-*long* blunt classes — so the genuine gap remains a **short / one-handed blunt** class, and the survey confirms it would be a *thin* category (much of it reconstruction, sport, or conditioning rather than a deep combat corpus). Whether to add one is a design call for Jordan.

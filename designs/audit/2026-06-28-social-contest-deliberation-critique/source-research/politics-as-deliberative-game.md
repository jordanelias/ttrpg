# Politics as a Deliberative Game
### Pre-1600 models of political decision-making, classified by Caillois's four game-types and re-read as competitions to win

*A companion to `deliberation-as-game-synthesis.md`, which it presupposes. That synthesis established (Part 0) four non-coextensive senses of "game" — Wittgensteinian (rule-constituted practice), Suitsian (voluntary inefficient obstacles), agonistic/sporting (a contest with a victor), and game-theoretic (strategic interdependence) — and (Part I.D) Roger Caillois's four families of play: **agôn** (regulated merit-competition), **alea** (chance), **mimicry** (simulation / role-performance), and **ilinx** (vertigo, ecstatic dissolution). Here those instruments are turned on political decision-making before 1600.*

*Prepared at `max` effort. Sources are tiered in the ledger (T0 primary / T1 authoritative synthesis / T2 reputable secondary). Where a historical claim is contested or a date disputed, it is flagged in place.*

> **A note on "the four game types."** Caillois's four *families* (agôn / alea / mimicry / ilinx) are the literal taxonomy of game-*types*, so each model below is assigned a primary family (and secondaries). The third question — how each is a *competition to win* — is a question about one family in particular, **agôn**, and equivalently about the agonistic *sense* of "game" (sense 3 of the parent synthesis). The two frameworks converge there: to read a procedure "as a competition" is to ask what its agôn is, even when its primary family is alea or mimicry.

## Part A — How politics is a deliberative game

The parent synthesis argued that what makes an activity a game in the rigorous sense is **constitution by rules**: rules that do not merely regulate a pre-existing activity but *create* one, conferring on a move the status of counting as that move (Searle's "X counts as Y in C"). Political decision-making is the purest large-scale instance of this. A *valid decree*, a *lawful election*, a *binding statute*, a *legitimate succession* — none is a natural fact. Each is an **institutional fact** brought into being by a decision-procedure's constitutive rules, exactly as a checkmate or a touchdown is brought into being by the rules of chess or football. The procedure is the rulebook; the legitimate outcome is the move it makes possible. Strip the rules away and you do not have an illegitimate decision — you have no decision at all, only force or noise.

This is why politics is *the* deliberative game rather than merely *a* domain that resembles one. Four observations follow, one for each sense of "game" the synthesis isolated:

1. **Every political decision-procedure is rule-constituted (the Wittgensteinian/Searlean sense).** The quorum, the order of speaking, the threshold for passage, the qualification of electors, the form of the writ — these constitute "having decided" as an institutional status. A king's word is a *decree* (not a wish) only because rules make it one; a show of hands is an *election* (not a crowd) only because rules count it as one.

2. **Most procedures impose deliberately inefficient obstacles (the Suitsian sense).** Supermajorities, fixed deliberative sequences, mandatory waiting periods, the requirement to take auspices before voting, the rule that the same question pass two chambers in turn — all make reaching a decision *harder* than the bare aggregation of wills would require, and are embraced precisely because they constitute the kind of decision the polity means to make. They are the political analogue of the exclusionary rule: unnecessary obstacles accepted for the sake of the institution.

3. **The competitive procedures are agôn (the agonistic sense).** Where the decision is reached by debate-and-vote, by bargaining, or by combat, the procedure is a rule-equalised contest with a victor — and the parent synthesis's whole apparatus of argument-as-game applies directly (Part II), as does its account of the trial-as-contest (Part III).

4. **The strategic procedures are games in the exact technical sense (the game-theoretic sense).** Voting, coalition-formation, and king-versus-estates bargaining are objects of formal game theory — with players, strategies, payoffs, equilibria, and the same *mixed-motive* structure the synthesis found in negotiation (Part IV) and in Putnam's two-level games. A parliament is a game in this sense even where it is least a zero-sum contest.

But the stakes are higher here than anywhere else in the synthesis, and that fact organizes everything below. In a debate or a trial the prize is *a* decision; in politics the prize is **the right to decide at all** — sovereignty itself. So the single most important distinction among the models is *where the contest sits relative to the decision*:

- some procedures **are** the competition — the deciding is itself the contest (you argue, vote, bargain, or fight, and the outcome of that contest *is* the decision);
- some procedures decide **who** will decide — the competition is for the office, upstream of any particular policy (election, succession);
- and some procedures **remove** the competition by design — they are deliberately *not* contests, and their pathologies are the re-emergence of competition where it was meant to be abolished (sortition, consensus).

Part B walks the catalogue; Part D returns to this three-way division and to the deepest consequence — that the choice of a decision-procedure is a *meta-game about which kind of game shall legitimate power*.

## Part B — The catalogue of pre-1600 decision-models

*Each model: (i) what it is, with pre-1600 exemplars and sources; (ii) its primary Caillois family, with secondaries; (iii) the competitive re-reading — the players, the win condition, the legal moves, and what "victory" consists in.*

### 1. Autocratic fiat — decision by a single sovereign will

**(i) What it is.** A lone sovereign decides; the decision is legitimate by virtue of *who* utters it. The form is the decree, edict, rescript, ban, or writ. It is the most widespread pre-1600 procedure: the Mesopotamian royal pronouncement codified in Hammurabi's stele (c. 1754 BCE), the Roman emperor's *rescriptum* answering a petition with the force of law, the Chinese imperial edict issued under the Mandate of Heaven, the medieval royal writ, and the theory of divine-right kingship that made the monarch's will law because it descended from God's. *(General-knowledge exemplars; the theory of constitutive authority is the synthesis's, Searle 1969.)*

**(ii) Caillois family.** Primarily **mimicry** — the sovereign *performs* the role of sovereignty. The throne, regalia, anointing, the formulae of majesty, and the legal fiction later named the king's "two bodies" all enact a persona whose word is binding; the decree is a speech-act of that performed office. It is otherwise the *negation* of a decision-*game*: one will, no contest (agôn) and no chance (alea). *(The term is stretched here, and the strain is worth naming: Caillois's mimicry presupposes that performer and audience know the role for an illusion — the actor, the masked reveller — whereas the sovereign's performance is collectively* taken as real *(Searle's status function; the legally literal fiction of the king's "two bodies," Kantorowicz). What the two share is the structure of* enactment*; what divides them is that institutional make-believe denies that it is make-believe. The same caveat governs every "mimicry" assignment below, model 4 above all.)*

**(iii) As a competition.** Fiat is the hardest case, and honesty requires saying so plainly: in itself it is not a deliberative game at all — there is nothing to play, only a will to obey. The competition is **prior and ongoing, never internal**. It is *prior* in the struggle to *become* the one who decrees — the succession war, the coup, the court faction, the usurpation. It is *ongoing* in the contest to *remain* sovereign against rivals, favourites, and pretenders. Fiat, in other words, is the **prize of a competition, not a competition itself**: the agôn is played *around* the throne, never *in* the decree. The "players" are claimants and factions; the "win condition" is seizing or holding the sovereign office; the "legal moves" are patronage, intrigue, and force. This is why the most absolutist polities generate the most baroque court politics — all the contest that the decision-procedure excludes is displaced into the antechamber.

### 2. Sortition — decision (or selection) by lot

**(i) What it is.** Offices or decisions are assigned by random draw from a defined pool. It was central to Athenian democracy after Cleisthenes (507 BCE): the Council of 500 (Boule), most magistracies, and the juries of the dikastēria were filled by lot, mechanised by the *kleroterion*, an allotment slab — with the telling exception that generals (*stratēgoi*) were *elected*, because military command needed proven expertise (Aristotle, *Constitution of the Athenians*; *Politics* 1301b). It was revived in the Italian republics — Florence (1328–1434 and 1494–1512) and Venice (from 1268), among others — but there with a different aim: not to represent all citizens but, as the scholarship stresses, to **damp factional conflict** by removing the prize that factions fought over (Manin 1997; Sintomer 2023). Venice's doge was chosen by a deliberately convoluted **lot-and-election hybrid**, alternating random draws and ballots through some nine rounds down to a 41-member commission that made the final choice — engineered precisely so that no faction could capture the outcome.

**(ii) Caillois family.** Paradigmatic **alea** — Caillois's own category of pure chance, here doing political work. The Venetian machine is a designed **alea + agôn** hybrid (lot to break faction, election to secure competence).

**(iii) As a competition.** Sortition's political point is to *abolish* the competition — that is its virtue (no canvassing, no bribery, no faction, because there is nothing to win by striving). Aristotle's analytic is exact: **lot is the democratic mode of selection, election the aristocratic one** (*Politics* 1301b; Manin 1997), because election rewards distinction and lot equalises it. But competition does not vanish; it migrates **upstream**, to *eligibility for the pool*. In Florence the real contest was the *squittino*, the scrutiny that decided whose names went into the leather bags (*imborsazione*) from which offices were later drawn — an intensely political fight over qualification, after which chance took over. So the agôn of sortition is displaced from the decision to the *qualification*: you "win" by getting your name into the urn; the draw itself has no winner in the agonistic sense, only a beneficiary of Fortuna — which is exactly why polities reached for it when the agôn of election had become ruinous.

### 3. Divination, augury, and ordeal — decision referred to chance or the divine

**(i) What it is.** The decision is submitted to a non-human authority read through signs. Rome required the magistrate to **take the auspices** before all major public business — inaugurations, senatorial debate, legislation, elections, war; favourable signs permitted the act, while unfavourable signs (*obnuntiatio*) could **suspend, postpone, or cancel** it, and any act done against the omens was *vitio*, invalid (Cicero, *de Legibus* II.12; *de Divinatione*; Linderski; Driediger-Murphy, *Roman Republican Augury*). The same logic of submitting a verdict to the divine through chance underlies the **judicial ordeal** (hot iron, cold water), widespread in early medieval Europe until the Fourth Lateran Council (1215) withdrew clerical participation, and **cleromancy** — the casting of lots to read God's choice, as in the Hebrew Bible's Urim and Thummim and the apostolic election of Matthias.

**(ii) Caillois family.** **Alea** (chance / the divine signs) braided with **mimicry** (the ritual *performance* of consulting the supernatural — the augur's marked-out templum, the ordeal's liturgy).

**(iii) As a competition.** Formally there is no contest — the gods or the lot decide. But the Roman record shows the alea **weaponised into an instrument of agôn**: because a magistrate could declare adverse omens, *obnuntiatio* became a procedural **blocking move** in ordinary power-struggles. Clodius's enemy Milo notoriously announced that he would "watch the skies" (*servare de caelo*) on every assembly day, freezing all legislative business by pre-emptive divination. So the player who **controls the omen** wins — not by persuading anyone but by invoking a veto no one may gainsay. In the ordeal and the closely related trial by battle (model 11), the litigant "wins" by surviving or prevailing, the alea of the ordeal shading into the agôn of combat. Divination is thus a game of *staging* an unanswerable authority: victory goes to whoever can manufacture, or credibly claim, the favourable — or the blocking — sign.

### 4. Acclamation — decision by collective shout

**(i) What it is.** A decision is made by the affirming roar of an assembled body, not by a counted vote. The late-Roman and Byzantine emperor was, in constitutional principle, made by the **common acclamation of the Senate, the People, and the Army**; the historian Ensslin judged acclamation "the decisive act in appointing an emperor" (Cambridge Medieval History), with the Germanic-derived **raising-on-a-shield** first recorded for Julian in 361 and the ritual cry "*Axios!*" ("He is worthy!") codified in the tenth-century *De Ceremoniis* (Constantine VII). An honest qualification: in practice the acclamation was frequently *ratifying* rather than *deciding* — confirming a victorious general, a designated co-emperor, or a dynastic heir whose accession was already a fait accompli — so the procedure's constitutive force and its merely-confirmatory use must be kept apart. The same form survived in the Church as one of the three canonical modes of election (model 9): election by **acclamation or quasi-inspiration**, in which the electors unanimously proclaim a candidate "as if inspired by the Holy Spirit" (*quasi afflati Spiritu Sancto*), with no ballot and explicitly no prior negotiation (*Quia propter*, Fourth Lateran 1215). Earlier still, bishops were chosen by the acclamation of clergy and people — *vox populi, vox Dei*.

**(ii) Caillois family.** Primarily **mimicry** — the assembly *enacts* a single unanimous voice, performing the descent of the Spirit or the will of the People as one body. Secondarily **ilinx** — the contagious, ecstatic roar of the crowd, collective effervescence verging on the vertiginous (the sudden cry that sweeps a conclave or a hippodrome).

**(iii) As a competition.** Acclamation presents itself as the *opposite* of a contest — one spontaneous voice, no division. But its competitive reality is a **contest to command the crowd**: the candidate who can orchestrate the loudest, earliest, most seemingly-spontaneous acclamation prevails, and rivals compete to be proclaimed first and loudest. When two are acclaimed at once, the mimicry tears open into naked agôn — the Excubitors proclaim one man, rival partisans throw stones and die for it; the result, writ large, is antipopes and rival emperors. So acclamation is a **shouting-match whose winner is whoever masters the assembly's voice**, and whose failure mode is civil war. "Victory" is being the one proclaimed — and, decisively, *suppressing the rival proclamation* before it can answer yours.

### 5. Council deliberation — decision by an aristocratic or oligarchic body

**(i) What it is.** A standing body of elite members deliberates and decides, typically by debate followed by a vote or a reading of the sense of the house. The type runs from the Spartan Gerousia and the Roman Senate (whose *senatus consultum* carried the weight of collective *auctoritas*) through the Anglo-Saxon witenagemot and the *curia regis*, to the great oligarchic councils of the Italian republics — the Venetian Senate, Great Council, and Council of Ten — and the church synods and ecumenical councils that decided doctrine. *(General-knowledge exemplars; the synthesis's argument-as-game apparatus, Part II, applies directly.)*

**(ii) Caillois family.** The cleanest **agôn** in the catalogue — a regulated merit-contest of argument, influence, and the vote among formal equals, conducted under rules of order that constitute the floor, the motion, the amendment, and the division.

**(iii) As a competition.** Here the contest *is* the deciding, with no displacement: factions and orators compete to carry the question, and the decree is *won* by the side that musters the most votes — or, in the Roman idiom, the most *auctoritas*. The players are the councillors and their alignments; the win condition is carrying the majority (or the recognised sense of the house); the legal moves are the speech, the motion, the amendment, the procedural objection, and the alliance. This is deliberation-as-game in its fullest and least metaphorical form, and everything the parent synthesis says about commitment-tracking, fallacies-as-fouls, and the audience-as-judge holds without modification.

### 6. Assembly democracy — decision by the vote of the whole qualified body

**(i) What it is.** The entire citizen body (however narrowly "citizen" is drawn) votes directly. The Athenian Ekklesia decided by show of hands (*cheirotonia*) and conducted ostracism by counting inscribed potsherds (*ostraka*). The Roman *comitia* voted by **blocs** — the centuriate assembly in 193 centuries malapportioned to overweight the old and rich, the tribal assembly by 35 tribes — and could only **accept or reject** a magistrate's proposal (*uti rogas*, "as you ask," versus *antiquo*, "I reject"), never amend or debate it, the debate having been externalised beforehand to the *contiones* (Roman assemblies; *Elections in the Roman Republic*; the *lex Hortensia* of 287 BCE made plebiscites binding on all). The Germanic and Norse *thing* and the later Swiss *Landsgemeinde* decided by the voice or raised hands of the assembled freemen in the open air.

**(ii) Caillois family.** **Agôn** — the vote-contest, with orators competing for the hands or the blocs — with a secondary thread of **alea** at Athens, where lot and vote sat side by side (lot for most offices, vote for the few that needed expertise).

**(iii) As a competition.** The rhetor who wins the most hands or carries the most blocs wins, and the contest is the decision. Two features sharpen the competitive reading. First, **ostracism is an inverted agôn**: a contest in which the man who "wins" the most votes is the one *exiled* — a competition to avoid being the loser, the negative image of an election. Second, the Roman *comitia* is a **constrained agôn**: a rigged board on which the blocs are malapportioned toward wealth, the assembly may only ratify or refuse, and the presiding magistrate controls what is put and can even discard the result. The "legal moves" are the canvass and the speech in the *contio*; victory is carrying the vote — or, in ostracism, escaping the plurality.

### 7. Consensus and unanimity — decision requiring general agreement

**(i) What it is.** No decision issues until the body agrees, with no counting of a winning side against a losing one. The most fully documented pre-1600 form is the **Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Great Law of Peace**, governing the Grand Council of fifty sachems by required unanimity through a fixed sequence: the Mohawk and Seneca ("Elder Brothers") deliberate to agreement, pass the question to the Oneida and Cayuga ("Younger Brothers"), and refer the result to the Onondaga ("Fire Keepers") for confirmation — with the clan mothers selecting and able to remove the sachems (Great Law recensions; Fenton; Britannica; *its founding date is genuinely disputed — estimates run from c. 1142 to c. 1450–1600, all pre- or straddling 1600*). European analogues include the Benedictine chapter, where the abbot consults the whole community before deciding (Rule, ch. 3), and the ideal of **moral unanimity** in church councils. The Polish-Lithuanian **liberum veto**, by which a single deputy's objection (*nie pozwalam*) could dissolve the Sejm, is the extreme case, though it emerged only at the close of the sixteenth century and was formalised after 1600.

**(ii) Caillois family.** This is the **anti-agôn**, and it strains Caillois's scheme because it is *designed to suppress contest*. To the extent it has a family it is **mimicry** — the body performs and enacts its own unity (the Tree of Peace, the bundle of arrows that cannot be broken). Its defining feature is the deliberate *exclusion* of a winning and a losing side.

**(iii) As a competition.** Consensus is the procedure that tries to *abolish* the competition, and the parent synthesis's most important finding about argument applies to it exactly: the activity is healthiest when its participants treat it *least* as a contest to be won (pragma-dialectics' resolution-on-the-merits). But consensus is **gameable**, and its characteristic pathology is the *re-emergence* of competition as obstruction. Because every member can withhold assent, a holdout can convert unanimity into a **war of attrition or a game of chicken** — who will concede first? — and a single player can "win," perversely, by **blocking**: the liberum veto is the catastrophic limiting case, where one deputy's refusal defeats the entire assembly. That the Iroquois Great Law explicitly forbids impeding a decision "with insignificant objections or frivolous considerations," and caps the Onondaga's power to refuse, is the decisive tell: a consensus constitution must build in **anti-gaming rules** precisely because it knows consensus can be gamed. When it *is* gamed, "winning" means extracting concessions as the price of one's assent, or framing the finally-agreed position as one's own.

### 8. Parliaments and estates — decision by representative deliberation, consent, and petition

**(i) What it is.** Representatives of the estates or orders assemble to advise the ruler, consent to taxation, and petition for redress. UNESCO recognises the **Cortes of León (1188)** under Alfonso IX as the first documented parliamentarism, because it extended the *curia regis* to seat **elected representatives of the towns** alongside the king, nobility, and clergy, and its *Decreta* required **royal consent for new taxes** (UNESCO Memory of the World; Decreta of León). The lineage continues through the German Diet (1232), the English Parliament (de Montfort's of 1265, the Model Parliament of 1295, evolving Lords and Commons), and the French Estates-General (1302); the Icelandic Althing (930) is older but less representative, which is why the "first parliament" question is partly definitional. The structural driver was almost always **fiscal**: the ruler needed the assembly's consent to extraordinary taxation for war, and the assembly traded that consent for the redress of grievances.

**(ii) Caillois family.** **Agôn** in its internal voting, set inside a **game-theoretic, mixed-motive negotiation** between crown and estates — the model the parent synthesis treats in Part IV and, for its two-level structure, in Putnam.

**(iii) As a competition.** The parliament is the catalogue's clearest case of politics as a *bargaining* game rather than a pure contest. King and estates are players with partly common and partly opposed interests: both want the realm defended and functioning, but they compete over the terms — **supply (taxation) for redress (law and privilege)**. The estates "win" by extracting redress *before* granting supply, withholding the subsidy as leverage; the crown "wins" by obtaining its grant cheaply and without conditions, deploying dissolution and the threat of force as counter-moves. Agreement is possible only where the crown's minimum and the estates' maximum overlap — a *win-set* in the synthesis's Putnam sense — and the representatives play a second game simultaneously, answerable to the constituents who sent them. Internally, each chamber resolves its own position by a vote-agôn (model 6). "Victory," for either side, is the better deal: the most redress for the least supply, or the most supply for the least redress.

### 9. Election by a designated body of electors

**(i) What it is.** A defined electoral body chooses the office-holder. The papal **conclave** of cardinals required a two-thirds majority from the Third Lateran Council (1179, after the disputed Alexander III election) and recognised, from *Quia propter* (Fourth Lateran, 1215), exactly **three modes**: **scrutiny** (*scrutinium*, the counted secret ballot), **compromise** (*compromissum*, delegation of the choice to a committee whose result all agree to accept), and **acclamation / quasi-inspiration** (unanimous proclamation without ballot) — while **lots were specifically prohibited**. The Holy Roman Emperor was elected by **seven prince-electors** under the Golden Bull of 1356 (the archbishops of Mainz, Cologne, and Trier; the King of Bohemia; the Count Palatine; the Duke of Saxony; the Margrave of Brandenburg), deciding by majority. Bishops and abbots were elected by their cathedral chapters under the same canonical modes, and the Venetian doge by the lot-and-ballot machine of model 2.

**(ii) Caillois family.** **Agôn** — a vote-contest among candidates and elector-factions under a stated win-threshold. The three canonical modes are themselves a small map of three game-types: **scrutiny = agôn** (a counted contest), **compromise = a delegated agôn** resembling arbitration (the deadlocked body hands the contest to a sub-committee), and **quasi-inspiration = mimicry** (the performed unanimous voice of the Spirit). Venice's doge adds **alea** by design.

**(iii) As a competition.** This is a sealed, rule-bound contest with an explicit win condition, and it is openly competitive: *papabili* and their factions canvass, the conclave horse-trades (a negotiation game nested inside the election), and a candidate must assemble the supermajority. The legal moves include balloting, the *accessus* (switching one's vote to accede to a front-runner and end the deadlock), coalition-building, and — against which Venice made electors swear oaths — the bribe; the win condition is the stated threshold (two-thirds for the pope, a majority of seven for the emperor). One nuance cuts against reading even this as a pure power-contest: the medieval ballot was understood to disclose the *sanior et maior pars*, the "sounder and greater part," not merely the larger number — so the count was construed as **discerning** the right choice, not tallying the strongest faction. But the same doctrine cut the other way in practice: a defeated minority could claim that *it* was the "sounder" part and contest the result, so *sanior pars* was as often a weapon *inside* the dispute as a check upon it — discernment-language pressed into the service of faction. "Victory" is reaching the threshold; but the institution insisted the threshold *revealed* rather than *manufactured* the legitimate winner — a claim the losers had every incentive to deny.

### 10. Hereditary and elective-kin succession

**(i) What it is.** The office passes within a bloodline, by a rule ranging from the rigid to the openly competitive. **Strict primogeniture** fixes the successor by birth-order before any contest can occur. At the other end, **elective-kin** systems choose among a pool of eligible relatives by capability: Gaelic **tanistry** designated the *tánaise ríg* (heir-apparent) from the *derbfine* — the agnatic kin within roughly four generations — for demonstrated fitness over mere birth-order, codified in the Old Irish law tract *Críth Gablach* and abolished only by the 1608 Case of Tanistry (*Críth Gablach* c. 700; Kelly, *A Guide to Early Irish Law*; Jaski, *Early Irish Kingship and Succession*); the Mongol and Turkic **kurultai** assembled the princes of the royal line, the noyans, and the commanders to confirm the Great Khan among Genghis's descendants — the quriltai of 1229 raising Ögedei, the third son, over his elder brother Chagatai because a dying ruler could nominate the ablest son acceptable to his brothers, while Möngke's violent accession of 1251 broke with that collegial principle and was re-legitimated as the hereditary right of Tolui's line (*Secret History of the Mongols* §242–243; Rashīd al-Dīn; Juvaynī; Jackson). Both sit on the spectrum the comparative literature calls **elective monarchy**, and both regularly produced succession war.

**(ii) Caillois family.** A spectrum, and the weaker end strains the scheme. Strict primogeniture only *resembles* **alea** — the "birth-lottery," in which one "wins" by the chance of being born first to the right parents — and the resemblance is loose, because Caillois's alea is a *game* of chance one chooses to enter and wager on, whereas birth-order is mere antecedent causation, no game at all; the label is a metaphor for "settled by something outside anyone's striving," not a literal classification. The elective-kin forms, by contrast, carry a genuine **agôn**: eligible kinsmen *compete* — by alliance, demonstration of prowess, and not rarely by force — for selection or acclamation.

**(iii) As a competition.** Under primogeniture the contest is *rigged by birth*: the heir has won before the game begins — yet minorities, regencies, disputed legitimacy, and depositions perpetually reopen it, which is why "settled" successions so often dissolve into war. Under tanistry and the kurultai the throne is genuinely *won* by the kinsman who out-manoeuvres, out-fights, or out-impresses his rivals within the eligible pool: the steppe succession struggles and the later Ottoman practice of fratricide are this agôn at its most lethal. The "players" are the eligible kin and their backers; the win condition is being the selected or surviving heir; the legal moves are alliance, the show of military strength, the securing of the electors' or the derbfine's backing — and, at the limit, the elimination of competitors. The cost of the large eligible pool, as the sources note for tanistry, was recurrent "internecine dynastic civil war": the openness that selected for competence also guaranteed the contest would sometimes be settled in blood.

### 11. Trial by combat and war — decision by force under rules

**(i) What it is.** A dispute — over a claim, a succession, or a title — is settled by armed contest. Judicial combat (the wager of battle) submitted a legal question to single combat between the parties or their champions; dynastic and territorial questions were settled by war, which when bound by conventions, heralds, and the mutual recognition of adversaries took the form of a regulated contest. *(Covered in the parent synthesis, Part III.B, as the root from which adjudication descends — "sublimated combat.")*

**(ii) Caillois family.** Pure **agôn**, and the historical taproot of the family: a contest of strength with a victor, lightly regulated by the customs of the duel or the laws of war.

**(iii) As a competition.** This is the least sublimated political game, and the competitive reading is the literal one: the winner of the fight wins the claim. Might decides — but *might under rules*, which is what distinguishes trial by battle from mere violence and what lets the synthesis treat it as the ancestor of the courtroom. The players are the champions or the armies; the win condition is victory in arms; the "legal moves" are the conventions of chivalric combat or the *ius in bello*. Every more civilised model in this catalogue can be read as an effort to *replace* this one — to decide by counted hands, drawn lots, or performed unanimity what was once decided by the sword.

### 12. Arbitration, adjudication, and appeal — decision by a third party, and by escalation

**(i) What it is.** The decision is referred to an impartial third who judges, and a dissatisfied party may **appeal** by escalating to a superior authority. Medieval polities were dense with this: arbitration of disputes between princes and cities (often by the Pope as supreme arbiter of Christendom), the *amphictyonic* and later leagues, and above all the hierarchy of appeal — from local court to royal court, from royal court to the Crown, from ecclesiastical court to Rome. This is the "appeals" of the question: decision by escalation to a judge who stands above the parties. *(The judge-as-adjudicator and the appeal-as-"another inning" are treated in the parent synthesis, Parts III.D and III.A.)*

**(ii) Caillois family.** The arbiter occupies the **adjudicator** role — *not* a player, but the rule-applier who declares the outcome (the synthesis's unifying figure across trial, debate, assembly, and sport). The *parties*, before that arbiter, wage an **agôn**.

**(iii) As a competition.** The contest is between the parties, judged by the third: each competes to persuade the arbiter, and "winning" is the favourable ruling. The **appeal** reopens the contest before a higher judge — Pound's "another inning in the game of justice" transposed to politics, where to appeal to the king or to Rome is to demand a fresh round at a superior level. The players are the contending parties; the win condition is the judgment in one's favour; the legal moves are the plea, the petition, and the appeal itself. The model shades naturally into negotiation (arbitration is often a bargaining endgame, the arbiter's likely ruling shaping the settlement the parties reach in its shadow) and back into fiat (the supreme arbiter — Crown or Pope — whose ruling is final because no further appeal lies).

### 13. Competitive examination — selection by merit-contest before examiners

**(i) What it is.** Office is won by performance in a standardized examination judged by state examiners. The Chinese **imperial examination** (*keju*, 科舉) was first administered in **605 CE** under the Sui — Emperor Yang adding the *jinshi* degree — displacing the aristocratic Nine-Rank system, and ran almost without interruption until 1905: the world's first merit-based examination for staffing a bureaucracy, in principle open to most adult males and graded over local, provincial, metropolitan, and palace tiers on the Confucian classics (the precise origin is debated across 587–622, with 605 the usual anchor; *Political Economy of China's Imperial Examination System*, Cambridge; chinaknowledge.de). The Song made it triennial and barred the imperial clan from the highest posts; the Ming added geographic quotas.

**(ii) Caillois family.** **Agôn** in perhaps its purest institutional form — a regulated contest of demonstrated competence — but an agôn *settled by an adjudicator* (the examiner) rather than among the contestants directly, so it shares the structure of model 12, in which rivals compete before a third who declares the outcome.

**(iii) As a competition.** This is the catalogue's least metaphorical competition-to-win: candidates compete, are ranked, and a fixed quota passes — a genuine tournament with examiners for judges. It is the institutional answer to Aristotle's dictum that election is aristocratic and lot democratic (Part D.4): the examination makes *merit itself* the selector, neither birth nor chance nor canvassed popularity, which is exactly why the Song could bar the imperial clan from it. The players are the candidates; the win condition is placing within the pass quota, and at the apex the *jinshi* degree; the legal moves are years of prescribed study and the examined essay; "victory" is the degree and the office it unlocks. Its pathologies are those of any high-stakes examined contest — rote cramming, examination fraud, and the shrinking of "merit" to whatever the test happens to measure.

*Three further models are acknowledged but not given full entries, to avoid padding the catalogue. **Confederal / league decision** — the Delphic Amphictyony, the Lombard League, the Hanseatic *Tagfahrt*, the Swiss Confederation's *Tagsatzung* — decides* among *polities by delegate vote or required concurrence; it is a federated recombination of models 5, 7, and 8 at a higher level, an agôn-cum-negotiation between members rather than a distinct game. **Standing oracular / theocratic rule** decides by a *permanent* priesthood reading the divine will, distinct from the one-off divination of model 3 in that the interpretive office is continuous (Delphi's structural role in Greek colonization and war is the clearest case). Both are genuine pre-1600 forms, flagged here rather than analysed.*

### Beyond the Latin West: the same forms, independently arrived at

A catalogue drawn mostly from Greece, Rome, the medieval Church, and the European kingdoms invites the suspicion that its categories are artefacts of *that* record. The decisive test is whether the same forms appear where there was no possibility of borrowing. They do — which is the strongest evidence available that the types track something real about collective decision rather than a parochial European inheritance, and which blunts (without dissolving) the worry of Part E that an elegant scheme finds its own categories everywhere.

- **Council and consensus — Islam.** The Qur'anic injunction to conduct affairs "by consultation among themselves" (*shūrā*, 42:38; cf. 3:159) made the council a duty, and the early caliphate ran on it. Abu Bakr was chosen at the contested **Saqifa** gathering in Medina and confirmed by **bayʿah**, the public pledge of allegiance (an acclamation, model 4); Umar's successor was settled by a **six-man council** that Abd al-Rahman ibn ʿAwf, withdrawing his own candidacy, resolved by privately polling the members — a *compromissum* committee (model 9) reached with no debt to canon law. *ijmāʿ*, the binding consensus of the qualified, is model 7 in another idiom, and al-Māwardī's conditions for the electors (the *ahl al-ḥall wa-l-ʿaqd*) are a worked theory of model 9 (Qur'an; al-Māwardī; Donner 1981).
- **Assembly with formal procedure — India.** The *gaṇasaṅgha* republics of the sixth-century-BCE Ganges plain — the Vajji confederacy, the Licchavis, the Shakyas — decided in an assembly hall (*santhāgāra*) by counted vote, with a quorum-officer (*gaṇa-pūraka*) and an impartial teller of the voting-sticks (*salākā-gāhāpaka*): model 6 with rules of order. The Buddhist **Sangha** inherited and sharpened this into the most fully proceduralised assembly in the whole catalogue — formal motions (*ñatti*) and consents, a fixed quorum, three balloting methods including the secret vote by sticks, and a committee (*ubbāhikā*) for deadlock — a code the Buddha expressly tied to the Vajji constitution (Vinaya; *Mahāparinibbāna Sutta*; Majumdar 1918).
- **Sortition — the Chola country.** The Uttaramerur inscriptions (c. 919–921 CE) record the *kudavolai*, "pot-ticket," method: the names of qualified villagers, written on palm-leaves, drawn by lot from a pot to staff the committees of the village *sabha* — model 2, with the contest displaced upstream to a stringent eligibility test exactly as at Florence, and reached with no conceivable contact between the two.
- **Consensus and rotation — Africa.** The Oromo **Gadaa**, practised before the sixteenth century, vests ultimate authority in a general assembly (*Gumii*) that legislates by consensus and binds its leaders to fixed eight-year terms; the Tswana **kgotla**, the Igbo village assembly, the Ashanti council of elders, and the Somali **shir** are further consensus-and-assembly forms (models 6–7) of no European parentage (Legesse; Ayittey).
- **Elective kinship and tested merit — the steppe and China.** The kurultai and tanistry of model 10 are matched by Donner's account of ordinary Arabian practice — the kin gathering to elect the ablest, not the eldest — and by the Chinese examination of model 13, which made *tested merit* the selector where Europe was still casting lots and counting noble heads.

The lesson is *not* that these were "democracies": most were oligarchic, clan-bound, and exclusionary, and several of the datings are soft (flagged in the ledger). It is that **the council, the lot, the consensus-seeking assembly, the acclamation, the merit-contest, and the elective-kin struggle are recurrent solutions to the problem of deciding legitimately** — found wherever polities of any scale had to settle who decides and how. That independent recurrence is precisely what a merely European taxonomy could not have produced, and it is the best answer the document can give to the charge that its fourfold is a projection.

## Part D — Honest synthesis

### D.1 — Three relations between the contest and the decision

The catalogue does not divide into "games" and "non-games." Every model is rule-constituted and so is a game in the broadest sense of the parent synthesis; what differs is **where the competition sits relative to the decision** — and that, not the Caillois label, is the load-bearing distinction.

- **Type 1 — procedures that *are* the competition.** Council deliberation, assembly voting, parliamentary bargaining, trial by combat, and adjudication. Here the agôn (or the mixed-motive game) *is* the deciding: you argue, vote, bargain, or fight, and the outcome of that contest is the decision. These are deliberative games in the fullest sense, and the parent synthesis's apparatus transfers to them directly.
- **Type 2 — procedures that select *who* will decide.** Election by electors, hereditary and elective-kin succession, and — degenerately — fiat. The contest is for the *office*, upstream of any policy; the game produces a decider who then decides. Fiat is the degenerate member because its contest is wholly external: the throne is fought for and held, but the decree itself is uncontested.
- **Type 3 — procedures that *remove* the competition by design.** Sortition and consensus. These are deliberately *not* contests — sortition replaces striving with chance to kill faction; consensus refuses a winning side to compel agreement. Their signature is shared: the competition they abolish **re-emerges as pathology** — faction fighting over pool-eligibility under sortition, the holdout's veto-war under consensus. The anti-frivolous-objection rule of the Great Law and the oaths against bribery in Venice are constitutional antibodies against exactly that re-emergence.

### D.2 — The cross-cutting pair: games of staged legitimacy

Two models sit athwart the typology because they route the decision through a **non-human authority**: divination through chance or the gods (alea), acclamation through the collective voice (mimicry/ilinx). Their competitive reading is therefore always indirect — never "persuade the deciders" but **"control the oracle" or "command the crowd."** The augur who can declare the blocking omen and the candidate who can orchestrate the spontaneous-seeming roar are playing the same game at bottom: manufacturing an authority that cannot be answered. They are games of *staging* legitimacy, and their failure mode is the exposure of the machinery — the omen seen to be cynical, the acclamation seen to be rival and divided.

### D.3 — Where the Caillois mapping strains (and the honest limits)

The fourfold is illuminating but should not be forced. Two strains are worth stating plainly:

- **Consensus genuinely resists the scheme.** It is not well described by any of the four families, because the families all presuppose a *form of play*, and consensus is constructed to *suppress* the play-element — to deny that there is a game to win. Calling it "mimicry" (the body performing unity) captures something real but secondary; its essence is the negation of agôn, which Caillois's positive taxonomy has no native slot for.
- **Ilinx is the worst-fitting family for institutional politics, and that is a finding rather than a gap.** Vertigo — ecstatic dissolution, the loss of stable perception — is precisely what formal decision-procedures exist to *exclude*; an institution is in large part a machine for keeping ilinx out of the decision. It surfaces only as the **affective charge** of acclamation (the contagious crowd-roar) and, beyond the procedural catalogue, in the charismatic and sacral legitimation that surrounds the sovereign — the anointing, the awe, the king's sacrality. Within the procedures themselves it is marginal, and one should resist the temptation to manufacture ilinx readings to complete the symmetry.

### D.4 — The deepest finding: politics as a meta-game over which game shall legitimate power

The catalogue's real lesson emerges only when the models are seen together. Politics is not one game but a **meta-game about which *kind* of game shall legitimate the exercise of power** — and the selection of a decision-procedure is therefore the deepest political act of all, because it fixes what sort of contest sovereignty is to be:

- to adopt **sortition** is to rule that **chance** (alea) shall decide, removing merit-competition in order to kill faction;
- to adopt **election** is to rule that **merit-competition** (agôn) shall decide — which is why Aristotle classed election as *aristocratic* and lot as *democratic* (*Politics* 1301b): each procedure encodes a theory of who ought to rule;
- to adopt **consensus** is to rule that **no one wins** until all agree, subordinating victory to unanimity;
- to adopt **acclamation** is to rule that the **performed unanimous voice** (mimicry) confers legitimacy;
- to adopt **hereditary succession** is to rule that **birth** (alea) settles the matter, taking the question off the table by design.

And because each pure family has characteristic vices — faction in unbounded agôn, arbitrariness in alea, paralysis in consensus, manipulation in mimicry — the most sophisticated pre-1600 constitutions are **deliberate hybrids**, mixing families to capture one's virtues while checking another's. Venice fused lot and ballot precisely so that chance would break the faction that pure election breeds and election would supply the competence that pure lot lacks. The canonical election offered three modes at once — counted ballot, delegated committee, and inspired acclamation — as alternative routes to the same legitimacy. The parliament nested a vote-agôn inside a crown-versus-estates negotiation, so that two different games checked each other. The constitutional art was never to pick a single game but to *compose* them.

This returns directly to the central equivocation the parent synthesis isolated — that the **game-theoretic** sense (strategic interdependence) and the **agonistic** sense (zero-sum contest with a victor) are not the same and come apart under pressure. Politics is where they come apart most visibly. A parliament is fully a game in the game-theoretic sense — players, strategies, a *win-set* — precisely where it is *least* a zero-sum contest, because supply-for-redress is a positive-sum bargain in which both sides can gain. Consensus is the deliberate refusal of the agonistic sense (no victor) while remaining, indisputably, a game in the rule-constituted sense. To call politics "a deliberative game," then, is true in every sense the synthesis distinguished — but the senses do not travel together, and the interesting political questions live exactly in the gaps between them: in whether a given procedure is meant to *produce a winner*, to *strike a bargain*, to *discern a truth*, or to *enact a unity* — four different things the one word "game" is quietly holding together.

## Part E — Adversarial self-review and reconciliation

*Written against this document from a deliberately hostile standpoint — a political theorist, a comparative historian, and a philosopher of games taking turns — then reconciled. The verdict first: the catalogue and its competitive re-readings are sound and the central finding holds, but the work had one genuine content gap, four local over-statements, and one methodological vulnerability it had under-confronted. All are addressed below; what was declined is stated as plainly as what was changed.*

### E.1 — Accepted criticisms, and the changes made

- **A merit-competition was missing (most serious).** The catalogue ran twelve models with no place for selection by *competitive examination* — the Chinese *keju*, a major pre-1600 institution and the purest agôn of demonstrated competence. Added as **model 13**, with a brief note flagging two further omitted forms (confederal/league decision; standing oracular/theocratic rule). This also blunts, without curing, the Euro-Mediterranean tilt of the sourcing.
- **"Mimicry" was applied too freely.** Caillois's *mimicry* presupposes that the role is known for an illusion; political performance is collectively *taken as real*. Models 1 and 4 now flag this explicitly: the relevant sense is *institutional* make-believe — a collectively sustained fiction that denies being one (Searle's status function; Kantorowicz's "king's two bodies") — not acknowledged play.
- **Acclamation was over-stated as constitutive.** In practice it as often *ratified* a fait accompli (a victorious general, a designated heir) as *decided*. Model 4 now separates the procedure's constitutive force from its merely-confirmatory use.
- **The *sanior et maior pars* point was one-sided.** I used it only as a check on faction; in fact a defeated minority could claim to *be* the sounder part, making the doctrine a weapon *inside* the dispute. Model 9 now says so.
- **Primogeniture-as-*alea* was the weakest assignment.** Birth-order is antecedent causation, not a wagered game of chance. Model 10 now marks the label a metaphor for "settled outside anyone's striving," not a literal classification.

### E.2 — The methodological vulnerability, confronted

The sharpest objection is foundational: **Caillois built his fourfold to classify *play* — voluntary, set apart from ordinary life, unproductive — and political decision-procedures are none of those.** Transplanting agôn/alea/mimicry/ilinx onto compulsory, consequential institutions risks importing a vocabulary whose defining conditions are absent. The honest answer is not to wave this away but to ground the transfer where Caillois himself licenses it. *Les jeux et les hommes* devotes its second half to the *corruption* of each form once it escapes the "magic circle": agôn degenerating into unregulated ambition and violence, alea into superstition, mimicry into alienation, ilinx into intoxication. Political institutions are precisely these forms **deprived of the protective frame** — the contest that is no longer bracketed off but decides who rules, the chance-rite that is no longer a game but binds, the role-play that is taken as sovereign reality. So the catalogue is not an idle analogy between play and politics; it is a reading of political procedures as the *deadly-serious, un-bracketed* relatives of the play-forms — which is a use Caillois's own theory anticipates. This is now the spine of the limit stated in Part D.3.

A second, related charge: that the third exercise — "read each model as a competition to win" — is **unfalsifiable**, since one can always relocate the contest to a procedure's surroundings (the war *for* the throne) or its failure modes (the veto *gaming* consensus). The defence is that the relocations are not free: they are governed by the Type 1/2/3 distinction, which fixes *where the contest legitimately sits*, and the exercise returns honest **negatives** — fiat "is not a deliberative game at all," sortition "abolishes the competition," consensus is "the anti-agôn." An exercise that reports *no internal competition* for several models is not one that finds competition everywhere by construction.

### E.3 — One grounding the work should have had

The "deepest finding" — that politics is a meta-game over *which kind of game shall legitimate power* — floated free of its obvious anchor. That anchor is **H. L. A. Hart's rule of recognition** (*The Concept of Law*, 1961): the secondary rule that fixes which procedures count as law-making is exactly a "rule about which game decides," and the choice among decision-procedures is the choice of a polity's rule of recognition. The claim is not novel, then, but it is not merely literary either; it restates a central result of analytic jurisprudence in the vocabulary of game-types, and is the stronger for being so located. *(Hart joins Searle, already load-bearing in Part A, as the work's proper philosophical interlocutor.)*

### E.4 — The deepest limitation, which I am the last to want to admit

`[SELF-AUTHORED — bias risk]` The honest residual is this: an elegant fourfold applied to a vast, messy record will almost always *find* its four categories, and finding them is weak evidence that the taxonomy **carves politics at its joints** rather than merely proving *applicable* to it. I went looking for agôn, alea, mimicry, and ilinx in pre-1600 government and — unsurprisingly — found all four; the satisfying symmetry of the result is itself a reason for suspicion. The defence offered above (Caillois's corruption-of-games; the Type 1/2/3 distinction doing the real analytic work, not the Caillois labels; the honest negatives) is what keeps the exercise from being decorative — but a reader should weigh the scheme as a *generative lens*, good for surfacing how procedures differ and what each optimizes, not as a demonstrated natural kingdom of political forms. The load-bearing claim of the whole document is the modest, defensible one: that **where a procedure places the contest — and whether it means to crown a winner, strike a bargain, discern a truth, or enact a unity — is the question that matters**, and the game-types are useful exactly insofar as they sharpen *that* question. *(This worry is mitigated, though not abolished, by the closing section of Part B: that the same forms appear independently in Islam, India, the Chola country, the steppe, Gaelic Ireland, and Africa — civilizations with no contact among them — is the strongest available evidence that the categories are found rather than projected. Projection would not recur across unconnected records.)*

### E.5 — Declined

- **A wholesale retreat from Caillois.** Declined: the corruption-of-games grounding answers the domain-mismatch and strengthens rather than dissolves the reading.
- **Further models for completeness' sake.** Declined beyond model 13 and the two flagged forms: adding marginal variants to reach a rounder number would be padding, which the honest-findings standard forbids.
- **Re-engineering the table's "winning" column for the bargaining and consensus rows.** Declined: those cells are already hedged ("the better deal," "when gamed"), and Part D.4 carries the point that "winning" is the wrong frame for the positive-sum and unanimity procedures. The table flattens by design; the prose holds the qualifications — including that the cross-cutting pair (divination, acclamation) is forced into single Type-columns it actually sits *athwart* (Part D.2), and that several models (parliament, combat) occupy more than one Type depending on what is being decided. *(Two of these three were revisited and resolved in the second pass — see E.6.)*

### E.6 — Resolutions in the second pass

A later instruction — *resolve all issues raised by the review* — prompted this pass, which closed the items the first reconciliation had only flagged or declined:

- **The Euro-Mediterranean tilt is now cured, not merely conceded.** The closing section of Part B grounds independent instances of every form outside the Latin West, each with primary or authoritative sourcing: Islamic *shūrā* / *bayʿah* / *ijmāʿ* and the six-man caliphal committee; the Indian *gaṇasaṅgha* assemblies and the Buddhist Sangha's *Vinaya* procedure; the Chola *kudavolai* sortition; the Oromo *Gadaa* and other African consensus forms. Their independent recurrence does analytic work, not merely diversity work (see E.4).
- **The two weakly-sourced succession exemplars are firmed.** The kurultai now rests on the *Secret History of the Mongols*, Rashīd al-Dīn, Juvaynī, and Jackson; tanistry on *Críth Gablach*, Kelly, and Jaski — T0/T1 throughout, replacing the encyclopaedic sourcing the first pass had flagged as illustrative-only.

- **The self-confirmation worry is mitigated.** The independent cross-cultural recurrence (first bullet) is the positive evidence E.4 lacked; the worry is reduced, though honestly not abolished.

Of the three items the first pass *declined* (E.5), two are hereby resolved — the tilt and the table. Adding still more models for their own sake remains refused as padding.

### Scope limitations (honest)
- **Coverage is now broad but still not exhaustive.** The Islamic, Indian/Buddhist, Chinese, Chola, and African forms once flagged as unexamined are now researched and integrated (Part B closing section; ledger). Genuine gaps remain and are named rather than papered over: the Mesoamerican forms (the Aztec *tlàtocān* council and the election of the *tlatoani*; Maya councils), the Andean, the Pacific (e.g. the Samoan *fono*), and the pre-Islamic Persian and West-African-kingdom traditions are not covered, and specific claims about them would be **[UNVERIFIED]**.
- **The Caillois assignment is interpretive, not empirical.** Each "primary family" is a reasoned reading, defensible but contestable; models 7 (consensus) and 10 (succession) sit on boundaries the table necessarily flattens, and the prose, not the table, carries the qualifications.
- **"Pre-1600" is a soft boundary.** The Iroquois founding date and the Polish *liberum veto* both straddle it and are flagged where they appear.

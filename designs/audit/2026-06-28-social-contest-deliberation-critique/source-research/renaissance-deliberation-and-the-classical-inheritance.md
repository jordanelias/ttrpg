# Deliberation, Debate, and Decision in Renaissance Political Bodies, and Their Debt to Greek and Latin Thought

*A research synthesis*

---

## Orientation

This study addresses two linked questions. First, by what concrete procedures did Renaissance political bodies — chiefly the Italian city-republics, but also the corporate institutions of the Church, the guilds, and the universities — conduct deliberation and reach binding decisions? Second, to what extent, and through which channels, were those procedures shaped, justified, or reshaped by the recovered political and rhetorical writing of Greece and Rome?

The honest short answer, defended at length in Part IV, is that the relationship is real but oblique, and that two different classical inheritances must be kept apart. The **legal** inheritance — Roman civil law and the canon law built upon it — was a deep, unbroken channel that supplied the actual structural materials of corporate decision: the legal person of the *universitas*, the majority principle, the doctrine of consent, the representative with full power. The **philosophical and rhetorical** inheritance — Aristotle, Polybius, Cicero, the historians, the art of oratory — arrived later and worked differently: less as a set of blueprints for institutions than as a vocabulary for analysing, defending, criticising, and occasionally redesigning institutions that already existed. The Venetian councils were not built from a reading of Aristotle; but sixteenth-century Venetians and their admirers learned to *see* and *praise* those councils as a Polybian mixed constitution, and that act of redescription had consequences of its own. Distinguishing legitimation from design, and vocabulary from architecture, is the central interpretive task, and it is precisely the task over which the modern historiography (Baron, Pocock, Skinner, Hankins) has fought.

**Scope and method.** The treatment concentrates on the period c. 1300–c. 1550 in the Italian peninsula, with the conciliar movement (c. 1378–1449) as the principal trans-Alpine extension, because that is where deliberative institutions and the classical revival visibly intersect. It is a synthesis of the established scholarly literature and of the principal primary texts, not a report of fresh archival research; where a claim rests on a contested or single source, or on a date with genuine variation in the literature, this is flagged in place. A tiered bibliography (primary sources; authoritative scholarship) closes the document.

A note on one persistent danger: it is tempting to read the elegant theory backward into the grubby practice, and to assume that because a humanist chancellor praised Ciceronian eloquence, the councils he served actually debated in Ciceronian periods. The surviving records frequently say otherwise. The gap between normative self-description and recorded procedure is not a flaw in the evidence to be smoothed over; it is one of the most informative things the evidence has to tell us.

## Part I — The Classical Repertoire: What the Greek and Latin Texts Supplied

Before tracing transmission, it is worth setting out what was actually in the inherited texts that bore on collective deliberation. Five bodies of material matter most.

### 1. Aristotle: deliberation as the defining civic act

Aristotle's *Politics* gave the Renaissance its most fully worked theory of citizenship-as-participation. The citizen, in the strict sense, is defined not by descent or residence but by a share in two activities: judgment (*krisis*) in the courts and a part in deliberative or magisterial office. "Sharing in decision and office" is the criterion (*Politics* III, 1275a–b). On this account a polity simply *is* the body of persons entitled to take part in deliberation and judgment, large enough to be self-sufficient. Deliberation is thus not one civic function among others; it is the activity that constitutes citizenship.

The *Politics* also supplied an analytic anatomy of any constitution. In Book IV (ch. 14) Aristotle divides every regime into three parts: the deliberative element (*to bouleuomenon*), which decides the gravest matters — war and peace, alliances, legislation, the most important magistracies, and capital judgments; the magistracies; and the judicial element. This tripartition let later readers describe a real constitution by asking *who deliberates, who governs, who judges* — a question directly applicable to the councils, signorie, and courts of an Italian commune.

Two further Aristotelian ideas were structurally important. The first is the classification of regimes by number (rule of one, few, many) and by whether they serve the common good or a sectional interest, yielding the three sound forms (kingship, aristocracy, "polity") and their three deviations (tyranny, oligarchy, democracy). The "polity" (*politeia* in the narrow sense) is itself a blend of oligarchy and democracy — an Aristotelian seed of the mixed-constitution idea that Polybius and Cicero would develop. The second is the moral psychology of decision in the *Nicomachean Ethics* (Book VI): the distinction between theoretical wisdom and *phronesis* (practical wisdom), and the account of *euboulia* (excellence in deliberation) as correctness about the means to a good end. This furnished the conceptual ground for treating good government as a matter of *prudence* exercised in concrete circumstances — a theme the humanists would make central.

Finally, the *Rhetoric* (I.3) named and analysed **deliberative** oratory (*genos symbouleutikon*) as one of the three kinds of speech, distinguished by its end: where forensic speech concerns the just and the past, and epideictic the honourable, deliberative speech concerns the *advantageous* (*to sympheron*) and the future, and takes the form of exhortation or dissuasion before a body that must decide. This is, quite literally, the theory of the council speech.

### 2. Polybius: the mixed constitution as a theory of stability

Polybius' *Histories*, Book VI, contributed the single most influential constitutional idea of Renaissance republican thought. Two components travelled together. The first is *anacyclosis*, the cycle of constitutions: each simple good form carries the seed of its own corruption and degenerates into its paired deviation — kingship into tyranny, aristocracy into oligarchy, democracy into mob rule (*ochlocracy*) — after which the cycle begins again. The second is the remedy: a *mixed* constitution that combines the monarchic, aristocratic, and democratic elements so that each checks the others and none can degenerate unopposed. Polybius read Rome as the supreme example — consuls supplying the monarchic principle, the Senate the aristocratic, the popular assemblies and tribunes the democratic — and credited this balance with Rome's stability and rise. (Modern classical scholarship notes that Polybius idealised and distorted, exaggerating popular power to make the mixture look more even than the Senate's real predominance warranted; the distortion is itself instructive about the doctrine's appeal.)

For Renaissance readers the payoff was a portable diagnostic and a portable prescription. Instability is the natural fate of pure forms; durability is bought by mixture and balance. This single thought organised the "myth of Venice," underwrote Florentine constitutional argument after 1494, and, by way of Machiavelli, passed into the long Atlantic tradition of balanced government.

### 3. Cicero: the tempered commonwealth, counsel, and the eloquent statesman

Cicero mattered in three registers: constitutional, ethical, and rhetorical. In *De re publica* he defined the commonwealth as the people's affair (*res publica res populi*) — a partnership held together by agreement on justice and by common interest — and, following the Polybian line, judged the best constitution to be the mixed or tempered one that balances monarchic, aristocratic, and popular components. In *De officiis* he gave the West its most widely read manual of public duty, decorum, and the obligations of the *vir bonus* deciding under moral constraint. In the rhetorical works he fused eloquence with wisdom, making the ideal orator a statesman whose counsel guides the commonwealth.

A reception caveat is essential here and is frequently neglected. The full text of *De re publica* was **not** available to the Renaissance: it was lost, and recovered only in 1819–20 by Angelo Mai from a palimpsest. Fifteenth- and sixteenth-century readers therefore knew Cicero's constitutional thought only indirectly — through the *Somnium Scipionis* (the one substantial portion preserved, via Macrobius' commentary), through quotations in Augustine and Lactantius, and through the cognate doctrine in *De legibus*, *De officiis*, and the rhetorical corpus. The Renaissance "Ciceronian" mixed constitution was thus largely Cicero refracted through Polybius, through fragments, and through the ethical and rhetorical works, rather than read off the constitutional treatise itself. This is a good example of why the inheritance must be reconstructed carefully rather than assumed.

### 4. Roman law: the corporation, consent, the majority, and the *lex regia*

If Aristotle, Polybius, and Cicero supplied the conceptual vocabulary, the *Corpus Iuris Civilis* of Justinian (the Code, Digest, Institutes, and Novels, 529–534) supplied something more structural: the actual legal categories of collective decision. Four are decisive.

- **The corporation (*universitas*).** Roman law recognised that an association — a *collegium*, a town, a body of co-heirs — could be a legal person distinct from its individual members, capable of holding rights, owning property, suing, and acting through representatives. This is the indispensable juridical premise of every deliberating body that claims to decide *as a body*: the commune, the council, the guild, the chapter, the university, the council of the Church.
- **Consent: *quod omnes tangit*.** The maxim *quod omnes tangit ab omnibus approbari debet* — "what touches all must be approved by all" — originated in a narrow private-law rule of Justinian's Code (on co-guardians acting together) and was generalised by medieval canonists and civilians into a principle of public law and a charter for consultation and consent. It became a commonplace of representative and conciliar argument, and was cited, famously, in Edward I's summons to the English parliament of 1295.
- **The majority: *maior pars* and *maior et sanior pars*.** Roman and canon law together worked out how a corporate body decides when it is not unanimous. The bare numerical majority (*maior pars*) was qualified in canonical practice by the "sounder part" (*sanior pars*), a weighting by merit, zeal, and authority intended to prevent mere number from overriding quality; the tension between the two animated centuries of electoral dispute. Canon law also produced the qualified-majority rule for the gravest corporate decision in Christendom — the two-thirds majority for papal elections, fixed by the Third Lateran Council in 1179 (*Licet de vitanda*).
- **The transfer of power: the *lex regia*.** A celebrated text of the Digest (1.4.1, from Ulpian) held that the Roman people had conferred their authority upon the emperor. The passage was genuinely double-edged: it could ground absolutism (the people gave their power away entirely) or popular sovereignty (the people were the original source and the grant could be conditional or revocable). Both readings were live in the Middle Ages and Renaissance, and the popular reading became a resource for communal and conciliar theorists alike.

The point to hold for later is that this legal apparatus was recovered and elaborated *earlier* and *more continuously* than the philosophical texts — the revival of the Digest began at Bologna in the late eleventh century with the glossators, was systematised in Accursius' *Glossa ordinaria* (mid-thirteenth century), and was extended to the problems of city government by the commentators of the fourteenth (Part II). The communes were already deciding by majority in corporate councils long before anyone in the West read the *Politics* whole.

### 5. The rhetorical inheritance: the deliberative genre as the technology of debate

Classical rhetoric supplied not a theory of institutions but a technology of persuasion fitted exactly to the deliberative setting. The tripartite division of oratory into deliberative (political), judicial (forensic), and epideictic (display) kinds — set out in Aristotle's *Rhetoric*, in the Ciceronian and pseudo-Ciceronian corpus (*De inventione*, the *Rhetorica ad Herennium*), and most amply in Quintilian's *Institutio oratoria* — gave the deliberative speech a recognised structure, a stock of arguments (topics of the advantageous, the feasible, the honourable, the necessary), and a doctrine of the speaker's character and the audience's emotions. The deliberative genre is the genre of the man who rises in a council to counsel a future course of action and must move a body that will then vote. To recover and refine that genre was, in effect, to professionalise the conduct of public debate — which is what the humanist movement, rooted in rhetoric, set out to do.

## Part II — Channels of Transmission: How the Texts Reached the Institutions

The classical materials did not pass into Renaissance practice through a single conduit. Three distinct traditions carried them, on different timescales and with different effects, and a fourth (print) accelerated all of them late in the period.

### 1. The legal tradition: the deep and continuous channel

The oldest and most structurally consequential channel was juristic, and it long predated the literary Renaissance. From the late eleventh century the glossators of Bologna reconstructed and taught Justinian's law; the canonists did the same for the law of the Church, beginning with Gratian's *Decretum* (c. 1140) and the decretal collections, and built upon Roman foundations a sophisticated law of corporations, elections, consent, and representation worked out for cathedral chapters, religious orders, universities, and towns. In the fourteenth century the **commentators** (postglossators) turned this learning directly onto the constitutional facts of the Italian city.

The towering figure is **Bartolus of Sassoferrato** (1313/14–1357), the most authoritative jurist of the age — "no one is a good lawyer who is not a Bartolist," ran the adage. In his commentaries and in treatises such as *De regimine civitatis* and *De tyranno*, Bartolus confronted the awkward fact that the self-governing Italian cities recognised no effective superior while the emperor still claimed universal lordship. His solution was the doctrine of the ***civitas sibi princeps*** — the city that is "a prince unto itself," exercising within its own territory the *imperium* that imperial theory reserved to the emperor, on the strength of its de facto independence. On the popular-sovereignty reading developed by Walter Ullmann and others, the doctrine made the will of the city's own people the source of its binding law. His pupil **Baldus de Ubaldis** (c. 1327–1400), studied authoritatively by Joseph Canning, extended the analysis of corporate personality, citizenship, and the sovereignty of the city-republic. Through Bartolus and Baldus, Roman-law categories became the working language in which Italian communes understood their own authority to legislate and to decide.

The significance for our question is large: the procedural backbone of communal deliberation — that a council is a corporate person, that it decides by majority, that its officers represent the whole, that consent is owed to those whom a measure touches — reached the institutions through this legal channel, not through the recovery of Greek political philosophy. This is the deepest layer of the "classical inheritance," and it is Roman and canon-legal rather than Aristotelian.

### 2. The rhetorical-notarial tradition: *ars dictaminis*, *ars arengandi*, and the chancery

A second channel was the practical art of composition and public speech cultivated by notaries and chancellors in the communal cities from the twelfth century. The *ars dictaminis* (the art of formal letter-writing) and, beside it, the *ars arengandi* (the art of the secular public address, the *arenga* or *aringa* delivered in assemblies and on civic occasions) trained the men who actually drafted statutes, recorded deliberations, and spoke before councils. This tradition kept a working, if often superficial, knowledge of Ciceronian rhetoric alive throughout the Middle Ages and embedded the rhetorical handling of public business in the routines of communal government well before the humanists.

The humanist movement grew partly out of this notarial-rhetorical culture and partly transformed it. Paul Oskar Kristeller's influential account locates the origin of Renaissance humanism in the *studia humanitatis* — grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, and moral philosophy studied on classical models — and stresses its continuity with the medieval rhetorical professions. When humanists such as **Coluccio Salutati** (chancellor of Florence, 1375–1406) and **Leonardo Bruni** (chancellor, 1427–1444) held the Florentine chancery, the rhetorical culture of government and the classical revival met in a single office. (A caution to be developed in Part III: Salutati's surviving chancery records of council debate show little of the high Ciceronian manner he prized in his literary work — evidence that the new eloquence was an ideal of the study and the public oration, not necessarily the idiom of routine deliberation.)

### 3. Humanism: the *studia humanitatis*, translation, and textual recovery

The third channel is the literary Renaissance proper, and its contribution to our subject was twofold: it recovered or retranslated the key texts, and it generated a self-conscious ideology of civic engagement built upon them.

Recovery and translation were concrete events with datable consequences. Bruni produced new Latin versions of Aristotle, including the *Politics* and the *Nicomachean Ethics*, replacing the medieval scholastic translations with humanist Latin and provoking controversy over how the *Ethics* should be rendered — a sign that the reception of Aristotle was itself contested terrain rather than passive inheritance. The recovery of lost or neglected rhetorical texts was equally important: **Poggio Bracciolini** found a complete manuscript of Quintilian's *Institutio oratoria* at the abbey of St. Gall in 1416, during the Council of Constance; a complete text of Cicero's *De oratore* (with *Brutus* and *Orator*) came to light at Lodi in 1421 (the "Codex Laudensis"). Polybius, little known in the medieval West, re-entered Latin learning in the fifteenth century — the first books were turned into Latin by **Niccolò Perotti** around the mid-fifteenth century, under the patronage of Pope Nicholas V — and reached a wider readership through vernacular translations later in the sixteenth century. Each recovery widened the conceptual and rhetorical materials available to those who wrote and argued about government.

Upon these materials the early Florentine humanists built the ideology that Hans Baron would later name **civic humanism** (*Bürgerhumanismus*): the affirmation, against the medieval preference for the contemplative life, of the active civic life — of citizenship, public office, family, and worldly engagement — as the proper fulfilment of a human being, defended by appeal to a reinterpreted Aristotle and Cicero. Bruni's *Laudatio Florentinae Urbis* (c. 1403–04), modelled on a Greek oration of Aelius Aristides, is the founding document Baron used; Matteo Palmieri's *Vita civile* and Salutati's defences of law, will, and active virtue belong to the same current. The status and accuracy of Baron's larger thesis are examined in Part IV; here the point is only that humanism supplied not just texts but a *reason* to prize participatory civic life and to read the classical canon as its warrant.

### 4. Print and diffusion

From the later fifteenth century the printing press multiplied and standardised these materials. The Aldine Press of Venice issued the Greek rhetoricians (the *Rhetores Graeci*, 1508) and much of the Greek and Latin canon; Contarini's *De magistratibus et republica Venetorum* (first printed 1543) circulated the model of Venice across Europe in Latin and in translation, reaching English and early American readers and feeding the broader Atlantic discussion of balanced government. Print did not change the substance of the inheritance, but it turned local arguments into a pan-European conversation, which is one reason the Venetian and Florentine cases acquired an influence out of all proportion to the size of the two cities.

## Part III — The Institutions and Their Modes of Deliberation and Decision

### 1. A typology of deliberative and decision modes

Before the individual cases, it helps to set out the repertoire of procedures actually in use, since a single regime typically combined several. Renaissance political bodies decided by some mixture of the following.

- **Assembly and acclamation.** The broad open gathering of the citizen body — the *arengo* or *parlamentum* of the early commune — by which the whole community assented to grave decisions, often by acclamation rather than counted vote. Cumbersome and easily manipulated, it declined through the period in favour of councils, surviving mainly as a ceremonial or emergency instrument (the *parlamento* summoned to ratify a change of regime).
- **Council deliberation with speech.** The characteristic mode of mature communal government: a council, often of restricted membership, debated and resolved by listening to speeches and then voting. Where formal advice was wanted, a body or an individual expert produced a *consilium* (a reasoned opinion), a practice borrowed directly from legal counsel.
- **Balloting.** Decision by counted vote, increasingly by **secret ballot** to insulate the voter from pressure — by black and white beans (*fave*) dropped into urns, or by small balls (*ballotte*, whence "ballot") — resolved by simple majority (*maior pars*) or, for graver matters, a qualified majority such as two-thirds.
- **Sortition (the lot).** Selection of office-holders by drawing names at random, valued as a check on faction and patronage because it could not be canvassed. In Florence it was combined with prior vetting into the scrutiny-and-lot system described below.
- **Election by scrutiny.** Choice of officers through nomination, a vetting vote on eligibility (the *squittinio*/*scrutinio*), and then either direct election or a draw among the approved.
- **Counsel and consultation.** The summoning of experienced citizens or experts to advise the executive without themselves holding the decision — the Florentine *pratiche* are the developed form, and the permanent chancery officials a standing instance.
- **Representation and consent.** Decision through delegates empowered to bind those they represent — proctors furnished with *plena potestas* (full power) — underwritten by *quod omnes tangit*. This is the mode of the Church council and of trans-local assemblies, and it is the deliberative form in which the medieval-legal inheritance is most visible.
- **The emergency committee.** The deliberate concentration of decision in a small, often secret, body during crisis — Venice's Council of Ten, Florence's *balìe* — a recognised exception to ordinary collegial procedure, and a standing temptation to those who would subvert it.

The history of any one regime is largely the history of how it weighted and combined these modes, and of the struggle between the impulse to widen participation and the impulse to concentrate effective decision in reliable hands.

### 2. The communal-corporate substrate

The Italian commune from which both the republics and (through analogy) the conciliar theorists drew was, in legal terms, a *universitas* — a corporation of citizens. Its government rested on sworn association, on written **statutes** enacted and revised by councils, on short-term collegial magistracies designed to prevent the engrossment of power by any individual, and on the importation of a neutral outsider (the *podestà*) to hold executive and judicial authority for a fixed term precisely because he had no local faction. Decision was corporate and collegial by design; office was plural and rotating; tenure was short. These features were not derived from classical philosophy. They grew from the practical politics of the medieval city and were theorised, when they were theorised, in the language of Roman and canon law. The classical-philosophical vocabulary would later be laid over this corporate substrate, not substituted for it.

### 3. Venice: the mixed constitution in practice

Venice became, and was made into, the exemplary case — "the most triumphant Citie" of European political imagination — because it combined exceptional stability with a structure that could be read as the realisation of the classical mixed constitution.

**The institutions.** Sovereignty lay formally with the **Great Council** (*Maggior Consiglio*), the assembly of the adult male patriciate, whose membership was closed to a hereditary nobility by the process known as the *Serrata* ("closing"), conventionally dated from 1297. From it were drawn, by an elaborate apparatus of nomination, election, and lot, the other organs: the **Senate** (the *Pregadi*), which handled the substance of policy, finance, and foreign affairs; the **Collegio**, a steering committee preparing business; the **Council of Ten**, a small body charged with state security; the various courts and magistracies (the Quarantie and others); and at the apex the **Doge**, a head of state elected for life but hedged about with restraints — bound by his oath of office (*promissione*), unable to act alone, surrounded by councillors. The whole was knit together by the studied dispersal of authority, the brevity of most terms, the multiplication of overlapping committees, and the prohibition on accumulating offices in a single set of hands.

**The mode of decision.** Venetian government was government by committee and ballot. Business moved through preparatory bodies to the deliberating councils, where it was resolved by vote; the patriciate prized the secrecy of the ballot, the rotation of office, and the suppression of open faction. The most baroque expression of the system was the **election of the Doge**, which interleaved sortition and election across multiple rounds — the Great Council reduced by lot to a small group, that group enlarged by election, reduced again by lot, and so on through some ten alternations of drawing and voting — a procedure designed so that no faction could predict or control the outcome. The arrangement is the clearest single illustration of the period's willingness to combine lot and vote to defeat intrigue.

**The ethos.** What most distinguished the Venetian self-understanding was the ideal of **concord** (*concordia*) and the subordination of the individual to the patrician collective. There were to be no over-mighty citizens, no open contests, no tumults — only the quiet, balanced operation of the orders. This ethos, as much as the institutions, is what the classical mixed-constitution doctrine was used to articulate.

**The classical articulation.** That articulation reached its canonical form in **Gasparo Contarini's** *De magistratibus et republica Venetorum* (composed in the 1520s–30s, first printed 1543). Contarini presented the Venetian constitution as the mixture the ancients had theorised: the Doge as the monarchic element, the Senate and associated councils as the aristocratic, the Great Council as the popular — each balanced against the others to secure a stability that pure forms could not attain. **Donato Giannotti's** dialogue on the Venetian republic pursued the same comparison. It is essential to be precise about what is happening here, because it is the hinge of our whole question: Contarini did not design Venice on a Polybian plan; Venice was centuries old. He *interpreted* an existing constitution through the classical scheme — and in doing so created the "myth of Venice," the immensely influential image of the serene, balanced republic that European and, later, Anglo-American thinkers would invoke for two centuries. Modern scholarship (the work gathered around the study of this myth, and editions such as Filippo Sabetti's) treats Contarini's text precisely as an act of classically-informed idealisation, not as a constitutional blueprint. The classical philosophy here *structured the understanding and reputation* of the institution far more than it structured the institution itself.

### 4. Florence: scrutiny, sortition, and the culture of consultation

If Venice exemplified concord and balance, Florence exemplified turbulence, procedural ingenuity, and an unusually rich culture of recorded debate. It is also the city where the humanist revival and the practice of government were most intimately joined, which makes the gaps between the two especially revealing.

**The institutions.** The pivotal magistracy was the **Signoria** — the body of *Priori* (priors), drawn originally from the guilds, presided over by the **Gonfalonier of Justice** (*Gonfaloniere di Giustizia*), and holding office for the very short term of two months. With two advisory colleges — the Twelve *Buonuomini* and the Sixteen *Gonfalonieri* of the companies — the Signoria formed the *Tre Maggiori*, the three highest offices. Legislation passed through councils (the Council of the *Popolo* and the Council of the *Commune*, later reorganised). The brevity of tenure, the plurality of office, and the guild basis of eligibility all express the same corporate, anti-monarchic logic seen elsewhere in the communes.

**The mode of selection: scrutiny, purse, and lot.** Florence's distinctive contribution was its method of filling office, an elaborate hybrid of vetting and chance whose technical vocabulary repays attention. Candidates were first vetted by an electoral scrutiny (the *squittinio*/*scrutinio*), in which their eligibility was approved or rejected by ballot — historically by white and black beans (*fave*). The names of those approved were written on tickets and placed in leather purses or bags (the practice of *imborsazione*); when an office fell vacant, a name was drawn at random from the appropriate purse (the *tratta*, the draw by lot). The combination of general scrutiny and sortition was institutionalised in **1328**, though the placing of names in purses had been experimented with as early as 1291. The system was understood, by its defenders, as a guard against faction and patronage: because the final selection was by lot among the pre-approved, it could not easily be canvassed or bought.

This very design became the site of Florence's deepest constitutional struggles, because *control of the scrutiny was control of the regime*. After 1434 the Medici, without abolishing the forms, hollowed them out — manipulating the vetting through hand-picked electoral officials (*accoppiatori*), governing through extraordinary emergency commissions (*balìe*), and substituting selection by reliable committee for the open draw whenever the draw threatened to produce the wrong men. The Florentine case is thus a sustained demonstration that procedure is never self-executing: the same sortition that was meant to defeat oligarchy could be, and was, captured by it.

**The mode of deliberation: the *consulte e pratiche*.** Florence's most remarkable deliberative institution was advisory rather than decisive. The ***consulte e pratiche*** were quasi-informal consultative meetings at which the Signoria summoned experienced citizens (the *richiesti*, "those requested") to give their opinions on pressing matters of policy. Open debate of real substance took place here — markedly more than in the legislative councils, where criticism of the Signoria was constrained — and the protocols of these meetings constitute, in the judgment of the scholars who have studied them most closely (Gene Brucker, and more recently the long quantitative study by John Padgett and collaborators), the oldest sustained speech-by-speech record of political deliberation in European history, running across more than a century and a half. They show a political class that prized persuasion: in a culture where consensus was thought essential, the ability to convince co-citizens rather than coerce them became a path to influence, and verbal skill an instrument of power.

Two findings from this record bear directly on our central question and should be stated plainly. First, the *pratiche* confirm that classically-tinged civic ideology was woven into Florentine political self-understanding — the legitimacy of the republic was asserted through the memory of the Roman Republic, the centrality of liberty, and a constitution contrived so that no single interest could dictate. Second, and in tension with the first, the recorded speeches themselves are, in Brucker's assessment, workmanlike — lists of considerations and recommendations rather than set-piece Ciceronian orations — and contain, even under the humanist chancellor Salutati, "scarcely any echoes" of the high humanist rhetoric. The eloquence the humanists celebrated belonged to the formal public oration, the diplomatic address, and the literary text; the daily deliberation of the republic proceeded in a plainer idiom. The lesson is that we must not infer the practice from the ideology: the classical revival shaped how Florentines *justified and narrated* their republic far more visibly than it shaped the texture of their actual debate.

**The humanist chancellors and the civic ideology.** That said, the chancery did matter. Salutati and Bruni gave Florentine liberty a classical literary form and a polemical voice — most influentially in Bruni's *Laudatio* and his *History of the Florentine People*, which read Florence's freedom as the inheritance of republican Rome. Bruni even engaged the procedural questions directly: he defended **election over sortition** on the ground that magistrates chosen for merit, rather than drawn by lot for short terms, would better serve the long-term good and would not "extinguish zeal for virtue" — an argument that imports the Aristotelian and Ciceronian premium on virtue and prudence straight into a live institutional debate. Here the classical inheritance is doing more than decorating; it is supplying the terms of a real argument about how offices should be filled.

**The 1494 Great Council and after.** When the Medici were expelled in 1494, the restored republic, under the influence of Savonarola, created a **Great Council** (*Consiglio Maggiore*) as the sovereign assembly of the citizenry — explicitly admiring, and partly modelled on, the Venetian *Maggior Consiglio*. The Florentine reach for the Venetian model is the clearest case of one republic consciously importing another's institutional design, mediated by the classical mixed-constitution ideal that Venice was taken to embody. The constitutional debates of the following decades — culminating in the second republic of 1527–30 and its fall — were conducted in this comparative, classically-informed register. **Niccolò Machiavelli** and **Francesco Guicciardini** are the great theorists thrown up by this milieu. Machiavelli's *Discorsi sopra la prima deca di Tito Livio* reworked the Polybian-Aristotelian inheritance into something genuinely new: he accepted the mixed constitution and the cycle of regimes (*Discorsi* I.2 engages Polybius' anacyclosis, whether at first or second hand — a point still debated by scholars), but he inverted the classical and Venetian ideal of concord by arguing that the **conflict** between the *popolo* and the *grandi* — the tumults of republican Rome — was the productive cause of Rome's liberty and greatness, not a disease to be suppressed. Guicciardini, in his *Dialogo del reggimento di Firenze*, brought the same classical and Venetian materials to bear in a more sceptical, aristocratic key. The Florentine tradition thus did not merely receive the classical theory of deliberation and balance; it argued with it and transformed it.

### 5. The comparative republics

The two paradigms do not exhaust the field, and the contrasts are instructive. **Genoa**, a great commercial republic, was chronically faction-ridden and was repeatedly reorganised, most durably by Andrea Doria's reform of 1528, which stabilised an aristocratic regime on a partly Venetian model. **Siena** governed itself for long stretches through a distinctive system of rotating office among organised political associations (the *Monti*), an arrangement of remarkable inclusiveness and equally remarkable instability. **Lucca** preserved a republican constitution into the modern era. Across these cases the same repertoire recurs — corporate councils, short collegial magistracies, scrutiny, lot, and ballot — confirming that the procedural materials were a shared communal inheritance rather than the invention of any one city or the deduction of any one philosopher.

### 6. The conciliar movement: deliberation and representation in the Church

The most ambitious deliberative experiment of the age was not in a city but in the universal Church, and it is indispensable to the subject for two reasons: it is the arena in which the *representative* and *consensual* modes of decision were pushed furthest, and it shows the medieval-legal inheritance and the Aristotelian inheritance working together.

**The occasion.** The Great Schism of 1378 — two, and after 1409 three, rival popes — created a crisis that ordinary papal authority could not resolve, since the defect lay in the papacy itself. Out of it grew **conciliarism**: the doctrine that a general council represents the whole Church and possesses, in matters of faith, schism, and reform, an authority superior to, or at least independent of, the pope. The Councils of **Pisa** (1409), **Constance** (1414–18), and **Basel** (1431–49) were the institutional result. Constance ended the schism and issued two decisive decrees — *Haec Sancta* (1415), asserting the council's authority directly from Christ and the obligation of all, even the pope, to obey it in those matters, and *Frequens* (1417), mandating the regular, automatic convocation of future councils. The movement was led overwhelmingly by French and German churchmen and found little echo in Italy.

**The intellectual sources — and here the two inheritances converge.** Conciliar thought drew on two streams. The first was the **canon-law theory of the corporation**, and Brian Tierney's foundational work showed that the decisive materials came less from radical political philosophy than from the ordinary canonistic law of corporate bodies — cathedral chapters, religious orders, the *universitas* — in which authority resides in the whole body, the head represents and acts in trust for the members, and the principle *quod omnes tangit* governs matters that touch all. Conciliarists applied to the Church the everyday corporation law already used to govern chapters and towns; Antony Black has drawn the explicit parallel between this conciliar corporatism and the corporatism of the Italian commune. The second stream was **Aristotelian political philosophy**, transmitted above all through **Marsilius of Padua**, whose *Defensor pacis* (1324, with John of Jandun) is the most thoroughgoingly Aristotelian political work of the later Middle Ages. Marsilius located the authority to make law in the *legislator humanus* — the whole body of the citizens or its ***valentior pars***, its "weightier" or prevailing part — and made the ruling part (*pars principans*) the instrument of, and accountable to, that legislator. (What exactly *valentior pars* means — a numerical majority, or a part weighted by quality and standing — has been debated since Alan Gewirth's classic study, and the ambiguity itself echoes the *maior pars / sanior pars* problem of the canonists.) Marsilius' programme was far more radical than mainstream conciliarism, but his Aristotelian vocabulary of popular legislative authority fed directly into the conciliar argument.

**The synthesis: Nicholas of Cusa.** The fullest fusion is **Nicholas of Cusa's** *De concordantia catholica* (1433), written for the Council of Basel. Cusa wove together the canon-law corporation theory, the consent principle, and a hierarchical theology into a single account in which legitimate authority — in Church and Empire alike — rests on **consent** (*consensus*) and **election**, in which councils represent the community, and in which the goal is *concordantia*, harmonious order. Cusa's later migration to the papal side is itself a comment on the movement's fate. For conciliarism was, in the end, defeated as an institutional programme: the restored papacy reasserted monarchical supremacy, and *Pastor Aeternus* (1516) and later doctrine repudiated the conciliarist claims. But as a body of constitutional thinking — representation, consent, the accountability of the head to the body, the corporate location of authority — it was among the richest the period produced, and (as Francis Oakley has emphasised) it transmitted those ideas into the later European constitutional tradition.

The conciliar episode is the strongest case in our whole subject of classical philosophy *structuring* a deliberative institution, because here the Aristotelian theory of the legislative community and the Roman-canonical theory of the corporation were brought to bear, consciously and at the highest level, on the design and justification of an actual decision-making assembly. It is also a cautionary case, because the institution so theorised was ultimately overpowered by the monarchical principle it sought to constrain.

## Part IV — How Far Did Classical Philosophy "Structure" These Institutions?

The cases assembled in Part III permit a more exact answer to the governing question than the question's own phrasing ("structured through a combination of Greek/Latin-philosophical work") invites. The relationship runs along several distinct lines, and conflating them is the commonest error.

### 1. The central distinction: substrate, legitimation, analysis, redesign

It is useful to separate four different things the classical inheritance did.

- **As structural substrate (deep, and largely legal).** The procedural materials of corporate deliberation — the legal personhood of the council or commune, the majority principle, qualified majorities, the representative with full power, the duty of consent to those a measure touches — were genuinely classical in origin, but their classicism is Roman-*legal*, mediated by the glossators, commentators, and canonists across an unbroken medieval tradition. This is the layer at which "Greek/Latin work" most truly *structured* the institutions, and it owes almost nothing to the recovery of Greek philosophy. The communes were deciding by corporate majority long before Bruni translated the *Politics*.
- **As legitimation and self-description (broad, and largely philosophical-rhetorical).** The recovered philosophy of Aristotle, Polybius, and Cicero served most pervasively as the language in which Renaissance bodies understood, praised, and defended what they already had. Venice *became* a mixed constitution chiefly in the act of being described as one; Florence narrated its liberty as Rome's inheritance. This is real influence — self-understanding shapes behaviour, reputation shapes alliances and imitation — but it is not the same as design.
- **As analytic instrument.** The classical schemes gave contemporaries the tools to diagnose their own arrangements: to ask who deliberates, governs, and judges (Aristotle's tripartition); to fear the degeneration of pure forms and prize balance (Polybius); to weigh virtue and prudence against number and chance. This analytic use is visible whenever a writer measures a real constitution against the ancient categories.
- **As actual redesign (occasional, but real).** Sometimes the classical materials did directly shape institutional choice. Bruni's defence of election over the lot, the conscious modelling of the Florentine Great Council of 1494 on Venice-as-mixed-constitution, and above all the conciliarists' deployment of Aristotelian and corporation theory to construct the authority of a general council are cases where the inheritance entered into the making, not merely the describing, of institutions.

The single most important corrective the recent scholarship offers is therefore this: for most of the period, classical political philosophy structured the *interpretation and legitimation* of deliberative institutions far more than their *design*, while the deeper structuring of the procedures themselves came through the continuous Roman-legal and canon-legal tradition. The philosophical revival mattered most where argument, reform, and reputation were at stake.

### 2. The tensions the inheritance bequeathed

The classical materials were not a single doctrine but a set of unresolved tensions, and Renaissance practice can be read as a series of different settlements of them.

- **Concord versus conflict.** The classical and Venetian ideal held that a well-ordered polity suppresses faction and achieves harmony; Machiavelli, reading the same Roman history, argued that productive conflict between the orders is the engine of liberty. The two readings of the mixed constitution — as frozen balance or as managed antagonism — divide republican thought to this day.
- **Counsel versus consent.** The aristocratic logic of *consilium* — that the wise and experienced should advise and decide — pulls against the corporate logic of *consensus* and *quod omnes tangit*, that those whom a decision touches must approve it. The *pratiche*, the Senate, and the chancery embody the first; the Great Council, the conciliar assembly, and the consent maxim embody the second.
- **Virtue and prudence versus procedure and law.** Should good decision be secured by cultivating virtuous, prudent decision-makers (the Aristotelian-Ciceronian *phronesis*), or by contriving procedures — sortition, secret ballot, short terms, balance — that produce good outcomes regardless of the virtue of individuals? The Florentine sortition machinery is a triumph of the procedural answer; the humanist programme of education in virtue is the rival answer; and most regimes hedged.
- **The lot versus election.** Sortition expresses an egalitarian, anti-factional, quasi-democratic logic; election by scrutiny expresses a meritocratic, aristocratic logic. The Bruni debate is the period's sharpest statement of the choice, and the two principles were endlessly combined (most elaborately in the Venetian dogeship).
- **The one, the few, and the many.** The Aristotelian-Polybian triad organised nearly all constitutional argument, but the question of how to weight the three elements — how much monarchy in the Doge, how much democracy in the Great Council — was answered differently in every city and decade.

These are not failures of the inheritance to cohere; they are the substance of what was inherited. The classical texts posed the problems of collective decision more sharply than they solved them, and that is why they remained productive.

### 3. The modern historiography is itself an argument about this question

It is worth recognising that the scholarly literature on Renaissance political thought is, to an unusual degree, a continuing dispute over exactly the question posed here — how, and how far, classical thought shaped Renaissance civic life. The major positions are essential context for any serious treatment.

- **Hans Baron** (*The Crisis of the Early Italian Renaissance*, 1955; rev. 1966) coined "civic humanism" and argued that a specific historical shock — Florence's struggle for survival against the Visconti of Milan around 1402 — fused classical learning with republican commitment and gave birth to a new participatory civic consciousness. Baron made the classical inheritance causally central to the rise of a republican mentality.
- **J. G. A. Pocock** (*The Machiavellian Moment*, 1975) traced an Aristotelian-Polybian-Machiavellian lineage of republican thought — the citizen's participation in a balanced, virtue-sustaining constitution, perpetually threatened by corruption and time — and followed its transmission through Florence to the English and American revolutions, establishing the "Atlantic republican tradition."
- **Quentin Skinner** (*The Foundations of Modern Political Thought*, vol. 1, 1978; *Liberty before Liberalism*, 1998; *Visions of Politics*, vol. 2, 2002) shifted the emphasis from Aristotle toward Rome, recovering a **neo-Roman** theory of liberty as the absence of dependence on another's arbitrary will, and stressing the centrality of **rhetoric** — the deliberate art of persuasion, including techniques such as *paradiastole* (the redescription of virtues as vices and vice versa) — to the actual conduct of Renaissance public argument. Skinner's work pulls attention back toward the rhetorical inheritance and toward what political actors were *doing* with their language.
- **James Hankins** has both edited the major reassessment (*Renaissance Civic Humanism*, 2000) and offered the most significant recent reframing (*Virtue Politics: Soulcraft and Statecraft in Renaissance Italy*, 2019). Hankins argues that the humanists' central preoccupation was not constitutional form — not republic versus principality, not the design of councils — but the formation of virtuous character in those who hold power, whatever the regime: *soulcraft* as the precondition of good *statecraft*. On this view the older debate over whether the humanists were "republican" is partly miscast, and the classical inheritance bore most heavily on the education and ethics of rulers and citizens rather than on institutional engineering. Hankins also dismantles parts of Baron's chronology and questions how literally to read humanist republican rhetoric (some of which, he argues, was occasional and insincere).
- **Critics from other directions** complete the picture. Straussian readers (Thomas Pangle, and behind him Leo Strauss; Paul Rahe) charge that the "civic humanist" reconstruction sentimentalises both classical republicanism and Machiavelli, obscuring their hardness. A democratic-egalitarian line (associated with John McCormick's reading of Machiavelli) holds that the emphasis on elite virtue and balance misses Machiavelli's concern with class conflict, inequality, and popular checks on the powerful. Eric Nelson (*The Greek Tradition in Republican Thought*, 2004) recovers a distinct, more economically egalitarian Greek strand against the dominant Roman one. The contemporary neo-republican theory of Philip Pettit (*Republicanism*, 1997) carries the recovered idea of liberty-as-non-domination into present political philosophy.

The bearing of all this on our question is that there is no settled scholarly answer to "how far did classical philosophy structure Renaissance deliberation," only a well-developed map of the responsible positions — running from Baron's strong causal claim, through Pocock's and Skinner's emphasis on tradition and rhetoric, to Hankins's deflation of the institutional reading in favour of an ethical one. A defensible synthesis sits closer to the Skinner–Hankins end: the classical inheritance was pervasive in the *language, ethics, and legitimation* of Renaissance civic life and in the *legal substrate* of its procedures, but it was only intermittently a source of institutional *design*, and it should not be read as a blueprint from which the councils were built.

### 4. A balanced assessment

Three propositions survive scrutiny. First, the working procedures of Renaissance deliberation — corporate councils, majority and qualified-majority ballot, sortition, scrutiny, consultation, representation, and emergency committee — were a shared inheritance of medieval communal and ecclesiastical practice, structured at the deepest level by Roman and canon law rather than by Greek philosophy. Second, the recovered Greek and Latin *philosophy* — Aristotle's participatory citizenship and tripartite constitution, Polybius' and Cicero's mixed constitution, the classical theory of deliberative rhetoric — supplied the conceptual and rhetorical apparatus through which contemporaries understood, justified, criticised, and at the margins redesigned those procedures, with Venice's mixed-constitution self-image, Florence's Roman-republican self-narrative, the lot-versus-election debate, the 1494 Great Council, and the conciliar movement as the leading instances. Third, the relationship is best captured not as derivation but as *redescription with consequences*: an old and largely non-philosophical institutional inheritance, taken up into a powerful classical vocabulary that changed how it was seen, argued over, reformed, and transmitted to later ages — including, by the Atlantic route, to the founders of the modern constitutional republics.

## Caveats, limits, and contested points

This synthesis is offered with explicit limits.

- **It is secondary, not archival.** The account rests on the established scholarship and the principal printed primary sources, not on examination of the manuscript records (the Florentine *Tratte* and *Consulte e Pratiche*, Venetian *Maggior Consiglio* deliberations, conciliar acta). Claims about what the records "show" rely on the historians who have read them — above all Brucker, Rubinstein, Najemy, and the Padgett group for Florence; the Contarini scholarship and the literature on the Venetian myth for Venice; Tierney, Black, and Oakley for the councils.
- **Several dates carry genuine variation in the literature** and have been stated with the standard value: the *Serrata* of Venice as a process conventionally dated from 1297 rather than a single act; Poggio's recovery of Quintilian in 1416; the Codex Laudensis of *De oratore* at Lodi in 1421; the institutionalisation of Florentine scrutiny-and-lot in 1328 with experiments from 1291. Perotti's Latin Polybius is dated only approximately ("mid-fifteenth century") here, as the precise dating is less firmly fixed in accessible sources.
- **The central interpretive claim is contestable by design.** The judgment that classical philosophy structured legitimation and analysis more than institutional design is defensible and, I think, correct, but it sits within the live Baron–Pocock–Skinner–Hankins debate rehearsed above; a reader persuaded by Baron's strong thesis or by Pocock would weight the philosophical inheritance more heavily, and a reader following Hankins might deflate even the legitimation claim toward ethics. The position taken here is argued, not neutral.
- **Coverage is selective.** Concentrating on Venice, Florence, and the councils leaves comparatively little room for Genoa, Siena, Lucca, the German and Swiss civic traditions, the Aragonese and Catalan *Corts*, the universities and guilds as deliberative bodies in their own right, and the vast subject of Byzantine and Islamic transmission of the Greek texts that made the recovery possible. Each is a legitimate extension.
- **The "tumults" reading of Machiavelli and the meaning of Marsilius' *valentior pars*** are both matters of continuing scholarly dispute; they are presented here in their mainstream form with the disputes flagged.

## Suggested directions for deeper research

For anyone extending this into a sustained project, the most productive next steps are: (1) to read one institution's actual records against its theory — the Florentine *Consulte e Pratiche* and *Tratte*, or a run of Venetian *Maggior Consiglio* deliberations — to test the ideology-versus-practice gap directly; (2) to follow a single concept (the mixed constitution, *quod omnes tangit*, sortition, *valentior pars*) across the legal, conciliar, and civic literatures to see how the inheritances interlock; (3) to extend the comparison beyond Italy to the conciliar assemblies, the Iberian *Corts*/*Cortes*, and the imperial and Swiss civic traditions; and (4) to engage the Baron–Hankins debate at first hand, since it is the discipline's own argument about precisely the question this study has posed.

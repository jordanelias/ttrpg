## The unifying frame: a fight is an information contest over whose terms it happens on

Before two fighters trade blows they fight over *what kind of fight it will be*. The currency of that contest is **intent** — reading the opponent's, and controlling what they read of yours. Sport science formalizes the two ways to control it (Panten et al. 2019, T1): you manipulate an opponent's read either by **actively supplying misleading information (deception)** or by **withholding the cues that would let them anticipate (disguise)**. Every move below is a play in that contest.

Underneath sits a load-bearing asymmetry. Experts detect predictive information in an opponent's body kinematics **earlier and more accurately** than novices (soccer/temporal-occlusion work, T1) — and *precisely for that reason*, skilled fighters must also work to make their own actions less predictable. The better someone reads you, the more a lie costs them. This single asymmetry generates most of the craft.

---

## §1 — Disguising intent

Two mechanisms, matching the dyad above.

- **Withholding the tell (disguise):** mask the preparatory motion so a strike arrives with no preamble — the strike that "comes from nowhere."
- **Active deception (the feint):** its power is *not* that experts read worse — they read **better** (erratum) — but that **a credible threat must be answered, and answering a fake costs tempo or position.** This is a signal-detection problem, not a discrimination deficit. In combat the payoff is asymmetric — being hit ≫ wasting a beat — so a skilled fighter rationally adopts a **liberal response criterion** (react to anything that might be real), which *necessarily* produces more responses to fakes. The savate experts who logged more "false alarms" to feints (Ripoll et al. 1995) were exhibiting that correct criterion, not a weakness; Jackson et al. (2018) raise exactly this finding to reject the susceptibility reading, showing high-skilled players in fact had **higher** discrimination sensitivity (*d′*) than low-skilled. So a feint works by **forcing a costly response decision** — and works best when the fake **rides expectation**: Jackson's stepover work found deception most effective when the fake aligned with the opponent's most-probable read. Good disguise exploits what the reader already believes, not a deficit in their reading.
- **Timing:** feints grow more frequent and effective as a bout proceeds — fighters read early and deceive late, and winners escalate feint intensity and variability over time (boxing feint analysis, T2–T3).
- **Rhythm deception (the deepest layer):** Ali and Sugar Ray Robinson would **deliberately telegraph a punch and then throw it**, landing clean because the opponent had been trained to treat the telegraph as a feint (boxing coaching, T2–T3). Once you've taught someone to read you, the truth becomes the best lie. The same instinct is the rapier *cavazione* (disengage from the expected line) and the German changing-through.

## §2 — Revealing intent (telegraphing)

Intent leaks because the body must **load before it acts**. Tells arise from the preparatory movements essential to generating power — a weight shift, a chambered knee, a spike of body tension before engagement — which become liabilities when too pronounced and let an opponent anticipate and counter (telegraphing literature, T3; corroborated by kinematic studies of the proximal-to-distal power chain in striking, T1). This is the ethological **intention movement** — the involuntary preface to an action. It puts power and deception in direct tension: the harder you wind up, the louder you announce. Gaze leaks too (where a fighter looks before committing), as do breathing and cadence. The expert's whole craft is reading these distributed cues *before* the committed motion — which is exactly why disguise is the necessary counter-skill, and why the most efficient strikes are the ones that skip the wind-up.

## §3 — What drives overcommitment

- **The forced-response cost (not "misdirection"):** a feint compels a reaction to a credible threat, and a well-*timed* fake can leave an attentional after-effect. The boxing fake-punch study (Pfister et al. 2023) found **inhibition of return** — at a ~600 ms interval, responses to a real punch on the *same* side as a prior fake were *slower* (an attentional refractory effect) — and crucially trained athletes were **as susceptible as** novices when the timing was right, *not more* so. [The "reversed cueing" effect, where a deceptive move primes the *wrong* direction, is a **separate** study — Wright & Jackson 2014, soccer — fused with the boxing finding in an earlier draft.] Either way these are *perceptual / latency* effects measured in judgment tasks; they are **not** measures of motor overcommitment.
- **Motor overcommitment proper** — centre of mass past the base of support, unrecoverable — is a *biomechanical* event the perception studies above do not measure. It is driven by: **fatigue** (a tiring fighter reaches and lunges as balance-discipline erodes); **emotion** — George Silver's *Paradoxes of Defence* (1599) warned that fighting in "wrath" pulls you past your balance, and his **true-times** doctrine (hand before body before foot) is a discipline against exactly this; and the structural fact that **a fully-committed action cannot be simultaneously defended** — you cannot devote your weight to a strike *and* stop a level-change underneath it. That last point, not any perceptual deficit, is what the MMA "shoot on the strike" exploits (§5).

## §4 — What drives hesitation

Two different "hesitations" get fused under one word; the engine needs them apart.

**(1) The deliberative assessment cost — the one that actually governs a skilled bout.** Facing an opponent whose next action you can't predict, you must weigh more live possibilities, and **Hick's law** holds that choice-reaction time rises with the number of credible alternatives; Boyd's **OODA loop** is the same point tactically — an opponent who keeps several threats credible slows your decision cycle. This is a *cognitive* cost that scales with the opponent's unpredictability, and it is the defensible basis for "a fighter is more cautious against a style they can't read."

**(2) The brainstem freeze — real, but an extrapolation to sport.** The defense cascade's parasympathetic brake (heart-rate deceleration, motor inhibition) buys risk assessment before fight-or-flight (Kozlowska et al. 2015; Nature Sci. Rep. 2021, T1) and escalates at circa-strike contact to **tonic immobility** (Royal Society 2017, T1) — the "deer in headlights" last-resort shutdown. **Caveat (flagged at reasoning level):** this literature is built on *inescapable-threat* and approach–avoidance paradigms; mapping "perceived inescapability" onto a *voluntary* sport bout against a respected opponent is a genuine extrapolation. The brainstem freeze is most relevant to untrained or overwhelmed actors. For a trained fighter, hesitation is better modelled as (1) — the deliberative cost — than as a parasympathetic shutdown. Conflating the two under the single word "uncertainty" (as an earlier draft did) flattens a cognitive cost and a neurophysiological reflex into one thing they are not.

## §5 — Imposing the regime (MMA as the clearest case)

The applied literature states the principle almost exactly as combat design would want it: **success hinges on who imposes their game plan first** — whether the grappler drags the fight to the mat or the striker keeps it at range (MMA analysis, T2–T3). The commentator's "he's dictating where the fight takes place" *is* the core advantage.

The medium is **range**, and the ranges are **asymmetric** — there is a contested middle:

- A grappler should **minimize time at mid-range — the danger zone** where the striker generates power — and instead commit all-in to clinch range or wait at the edge of long range: close enough to clinch or shoot while the opponent barely has room to generate a counter (Evolve MMA, T2).
- The **means** of crossing the danger zone is disguise-plus-exploiting-commitment: **committed strikes (jabs, low kicks, overhands) disguise the takedown, and shooting *during the opponent's own strike or forward movement* raises the takedown's success and lowers the counter risk** (Evolve MMA, T2). The level-change hidden behind a jab is the forced-response feint of §1; shooting on the opponent's commitment exploits the **biomechanical** fact (§3) that a structurally-committed striker cannot simultaneously defend the level-change — *not* any claim that the striker "over-reads." The grappler changes the regime underneath an opponent whose weight is already spent.
- The striker mirrors it from the other pole: stop the takedown with **distance management, takedown defense, the teep/push-kick to keep the grappler from charging, and counter-striking** — the closer the fighters get, the more danger the striker is in (MMA coaching, T2–T3).
- Range is itself **psychological pressure**: a fighter who controls distance can slow or speed the pace to their style, while a constantly out-of-reach opponent **frustrates and fatigues** — and emotional, rushed decisions create the vulnerabilities the other side exploits (fight-dynamics analysis, T2–T3).

---

## §6 — The cross-era synthesis (one principle)

From George Silver to the periaqueductal gray to the octagon, a recurring shape — offered as a useful **organizing heuristic for design**, not a claim the evidence forces into a single mechanism: **the primary contest is over fighting on your own terms, waged through disguising intent, exploiting the opponent's (biomechanical) commitment, and controlling distance/contact.** But the parts live at different levels — signal detection (the feint), biomechanics (commitment), cognition and neurophysiology (the two hesitations of §4), tactical doctrine (range) — and should not be flattened into one law. The unification is a lens for building the system, not a finding the literature delivers.

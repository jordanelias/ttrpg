# Multilingual Supplement to the Historical Combat-Manuals Research

A source-language audit of the dueling-combat and martial-traditions corpus previously assembled (`01-historical-combat-manuals.md`; the deeper per-tradition `revised_manual.md`; the throughlines analysis). Every search behind those documents was run in English. This supplement re-runs the research in the source languages and records what the English-only corpus systematically missed.

The evidence-grading scheme is carried over unchanged: each substantive claim ends with *[Source: high/medium/low; Interpretive: high/medium/low]*, where *Source* grades documentary attestation and *Interpretive* grades the inferential distance between the documents and the claim.

---

## Verdict first: how English-only search distorts the corpus

English-only searching does not fail randomly. It under-samples in four predictable, structural ways, and the prior corpus shows all four:

1. **It misses the national academic traditions that predate and underlie the anglophone HEMA movement.** The German *Fechtbuch* scholarship is rooted in 1960s–90s *Germanistik* and medieval *Fachprosaforschung* (Wierschin 1965, Hils 1983/85, Welle 1993); the Italian work is *filologia* with dialectological analysis (Novati 1902 onward); the Japanese work is academic *budō-gaku* (武道学). Anglophone practitioners typically read translations or summaries of this scholarship, so its method and its disagreements are invisible to an English query. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

2. **It misses critical-edition publishing done in the source language.** AGEA Editora (Santiago de Compostela) for the Iberian corpus; Rubboli–Cesari for Fiore and Vadi; Wierschin's transcription of Ringeck; and the non-English articles inside otherwise-known venues (*Acta Periodica Duellatorum*, Brill's *Late Medieval and Early Modern Fight Books*, the Dijon AEDEH volumes). An English query catches the English articles in those journals and silently drops the French, German, and Spanish ones. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

3. **It misses pre-treatise traditions and, more importantly, the internal skeptical debates.** The Spanish *destreza vulgar/común* that predates Carranza, and the Polish-language argument that the "ancient szabla art" is a 20th-century reconstruction, are both substantially invisible in English — which tends to present these traditions either not at all or in their most romantic form. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

4. **It misses primary-source corpora keyed to native-language titles.** ctext.org and shuge.org (Chinese), the Qatar Digital Library (Arabic), 우리역사넷 / 한국민족문화대백과 (Korean), de.wikisource (German), J-STAGE (Japanese). These hold the actual texts and the cataloging scholarship; a romanized or translated title often will not reach them. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

The practical consequence: the prior corpus's evidence grades are *systematically too low for the source-language traditions and too high for its own confidence in the English secondary literature*. Several "Interpretive: high" hedges in the prior document exist only because the grounding scholarship was not consulted in its own language.

## § A. Iberian traditions (Spanish, Portuguese, Galician)

**The widest anglophone gap in the entire corpus.** English HEMA treats Spanish fencing as essentially *La Verdadera Destreza* — Carranza, Pacheco, the magic circle, the geometry. The Spanish- and Portuguese-language sources show that this is the *late, elite, polemically self-distinguishing* layer sitting on top of an older and larger tradition.

### A.1 The pre-Carranza "destreza vulgar / común"

Spanish sources foreground a tradition the English corpus omits almost entirely: the *destreza vulgar* or *destreza común*, with medieval roots and named masters — Jaime Pons (1474), Pedro de la Torre (1474), Francisco Román (whose 1532 *Tratado de la esgrima con figuras* contained at least 16 numbered "reglas," known only because Carranza cites it). The *verdadera destreza* masters defined themselves *against* this older school; the older school continued in parallel, increasingly colored by the new vocabulary. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

The single most important recovered source here is **Domingo Luis Godinho's *Arte de Esgrima* (1599)** — a manuscript written in Spanish by a Portuguese master from Santarém, and the *only complete surviving treatise of the pure old style*. It covers sword alone, sword-and-buckler, sword-and-rotella, two swords, sword-and-dagger, sword-and-cape, and the longest known text on the *montante* (great sword). It is essentially absent from English-language summaries of "Spanish fencing." *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

### A.2 The montante corpus as its own genre

The Iberian *montante* (two-handed greatsword used for specific tactical tasks — clearing a gangway, defending a doorway or a lady, controlling a crowd) has its own treatise tradition structured as numbered *reglas/regras*: the Anonymous, Domingo Godinho (1599), Antonio Paredes, and the Portuguese **Diogo Gomes de Figueiredo** (*Memorial da prática do montante*, 1651), plus Diaz de Viedma's exercise-based variant. This is a coherent weapon-specific subgenre that the English corpus does not treat as such. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

### A.3 The critical-edition infrastructure

**AGEA Editora** (Santiago de Compostela; note the Galician-language house identity) is the center of gravity for Iberian critical editions — Pacheco de Narváez, Pérez de Mendoza, Rejón de Silva, and the Portuguese masters Godinho, Martín Firme, Thomas Luis, Seixas da Fonseca, Figueiredo. **Manuel Valle Ortiz**'s *Nueva bibliografía de la antigua esgrima y destreza* (2012) is the standard reference. Modern normalized-orthography editions (e.g. Fernández Lanza's *Llave y gobierno de la destreza*) make the primary texts usable. None of this surfaces from an English query. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

### A.4 What this changes in the prior document

- The "Carranza → Pacheco → Ettenhard → Lorenz de Rada → Thibault" chain in the prior document is the *verdadera destreza* spine only. A complete Iberian treatment needs the parallel *vulgar* track (Pons/de la Torre/Román/Godinho) and the cross-over (Lorenz de Rada explicitly re-imported *vulgar* "tretas" into the *verdadera* system). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- The Carranza-vs-Pacheco difference is sharper than the English account: Carranza uses geometry to *explain* techniques that still require sparring to validate; Pacheco radicalizes this into a *deductive* claim — that a technique proven mathematically need not be tested in practice. This is the precise point Quevedo mocked in *El Buscón*, making the literary-reception evidence (Quevedo) a load-bearing source on the theory, not a footnote. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- "Resistant material" for the four-axis lens: the *matematización* program — fencing argued as a *quadrivium* science, an *arte liberal* rather than a mechanical craft — is a theoretical commitment the weapon/technique/judgment/conditioning axes cannot represent. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § B. Italian (Fiore, Bolognese, rapier)

English search gives the manuscripts and the modern English translations. Italian search adds a **philological layer** the English corpus does not have.

### B.1 Dialectology of the Flos Duellatorum

Italian scholarship analyzes Fiore's text linguistically. The comparative study of the prose and verse redactions identifies *two language strata*: a set of Friulian features attributable to Master Fiore himself (a Cividale/Premariacco man), and a Ferrara-area layer attributable to the scribe(s) at the Este court — consistent with the dedication of two copies to Niccolò III d'Este. This is direct linguistic evidence for the composition-and-copying process, and it is only in the Italian literature. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

### B.2 The native critical-edition lineage

The foundational edition is **Francesco Novati's** *Flos duellatorum in armis...* (Bergamo, Istituto Italiano d'Arti Grafiche, 1902; reprinted Pisa 1982). Modern Italian critical work runs through **Marco Rubboli and Luca Cesari** (Fiore, 1998 onward; Vadi's *L'arte cavalleresca del combattimento*, 2005), and the transcription program of the **Sala d'Arme Achille Marozzo** (Getty, Morgan, Pisani-Dossi). English-language Fiore scholarship largely builds on Novati and these editions. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

### B.3 The Anonimo Bolognese

Italian sources center the recently rediscovered **Anonimo Bolognese** manuscript as a primary witness to the Bolognese (Dardi) school — the tradition behind Marozzo's *Opera Nova* (1536, "the most important Italian fencing treatise of the Cinquecento" in the Italian assessment). English summaries tend to jump Fiore → rapier and underweight the Bolognese middle. The fuller chain is Fiore → Vadi (*De Arte Gladiatoria Dimicandi*, ms. 1324 Vittorio Emanuele, c.1482) → Anonimo Bolognese / Marozzo → Agrippa (1553) → Fabris / Capo Ferro (*Gran Simulacro*, 1610) / Giganti, with Palladini, Marcelli, Alfieri, Pallavicini, and others filling it out. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

## § C. German (Liechtenauer tradition, judicial duel)

The clearest case of an academic substructure invisible to English.

### C.1 The scholarship is German *Germanistik* and *Fachprosaforschung*

The spine of *Fechtbuch* studies is medieval German philology and history, decades older than anglophone HEMA:
- **Martin Wierschin**, *Meister Johann Liechtenauers Kunst des Fechtens* (1965) — a PhD in the Munich series for medieval German literature, with a full transcription of the Ringeck manuscript (Mscr. Dresd. C 487) corrected against the Nuremberg 3227a, and an extensive *Fachsprache* (technical-language) glossary. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- **Hans-Peter Hils**, *Die Handschriften des oberdeutschen Fechtmeisters Hans Talhoffer* (1983) and *Meister Johann Liechtenauers Kunst des langen Schwertes* (1985) — extended Wierschin's manuscript catalog and attempted a *Stemma* (filiation diagram) of the Liechtenauer-dependent manuscripts. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Rainer Welle**, *"…und wisse, das alle höbischeit kompt von deme ringen"* (1993) — a social- and movement-history treatment of *Ringen* (wrestling) as a noble art. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

### C.2 Terminological precision lost in translation

German sources distinguish *schirmen* (originally "to shield/protect," hence fighting with sword-and-shield, with the protective sense primary) from *fechten* (the general term for armed combat). This semantic history is collapsed in English, which renders both as "fencing." *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

### C.3 The 3227a attribution and native reference infrastructure

German sources are explicit that the c.1389 "Kunst des langen Schwertes" in the Nuremberg Hausbuch (GNM Cod. ms. 3227a) was *formerly mis-attributed* to Hanko Döbringer and is anonymous (hence "Pseudo-Döbringer"); it records Liechtenauer's mnemonic verses (*Merkverse*) without naming a single author for its sections. The *Historisches Lexikon Bayerns* and de.wikisource provide manuscript-level cataloging (shelfmarks, datings, patrons — e.g. Cod. icon. 394a, 1467, commissioned by Graf Eberhard im Bart von Württemberg) that the English corpus reproduces second-hand. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

### C.4 What this changes

The prior document's repeated "Interpretive: high" hedges on Liechtenauer datings and the founder→glossator chain are partly an artifact of not reading Wierschin/Hils in German. The German stemma work gives a firmer (though still contested) basis for the filiation than the English summaries convey. *[Source: medium; Interpretive: medium]*

## § D. French (poleaxe, smallsword precursors, the academic infrastructure)

### D.1 Le Jeu de la Hache gets a real critical edition

The anonymous *Le Jeu de la Hache* (BnF ms. fr. 1996), the oldest fight book in French and the most extensive single-weapon system for the poleaxe in armor, received a critical edition with a dating argument from **Olivier Dupuis and Vincent Deluz** (*Acta Periodica Duellatorum*), narrowing it to the third quarter of the 15th century and correcting earlier translations (e.g. a key passage about hooking the opponent's axe with the "tail" of one's own, mistranslated in the English edition). The foundational study remains **Sydney Anglo**'s 1991 *Archaeologia* article. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

### D.2 A French-language body of scholarship

**Olivier Dupuis** ("The French Fencing Traditions, from the 14th Century to 1630," in Brill 2016; French staff/baton/canne studies; a documented 15th-c. fencing tournament in Strasbourg) and **Fabrice Cognot** (*L'armement médiéval: les armes blanches dans les collections bourguignonnes* — grounding poleaxe technique in archaeology of actual surviving Burgundian arms) and **Daniel Jaquet** (*Combattre en armure à la fin du Moyen Âge*) constitute a Franco-Swiss research cluster, much of it French-first. The Académie d'Armes de France's own historiography treats Henry de Sainct-Didier (1573) as the first French author and the origin point of the *fleuret*. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

### D.3 Meta-finding confirmed

The field's peer-reviewed infrastructure is multilingual and Franco-German-Swiss-centered. The Dijon AEDEH proceedings (*Arts de combat*, *Maîtres & techniques de combat*) carry Spanish, French, and English contributions side by side — including the Hick/Valle/Myers montante article. English-only search retrieves a biased subsample of these same volumes. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § E. Polish (the szabla — a corrective, not an addition)

This is the most *deflationary* multilingual finding, and the one that most changes the prior document.

English-language HEMA treats the "Polish sabre" / *sztuka krzyżowa* ("cross art," cross-cutting) with romantic deference, as a distinctive surviving national fencing system. The **Polish-language sources are openly skeptical and largely demystifying**:

- There are **no period szabla fencing manuals.** The term *sztuka krzyżowa* is attested (e.g. Orgelbrand's *Encyklopedia Powszechna*, 1864, which already describes the tradition as *lost* — "dziś już zaginęła"), but the technique itself was not transmitted in a treatise. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- The modern reconstruction rests on thin and late material: **Michał Starzewski**'s early-20th-c. notes (anecdotal, "obyczajowe," and describing a *French* sabre with a finger-ring), and **Wojciech Zabłocki**'s books (*Polskie sztuki walki*, *Cięcia prawdziwą szablą*). Polish practitioners (e.g. Grupa Lorica) argue bluntly that Zabłocki — an Olympic sabreur — back-projected modern *sport* fencing, and that Starzewski's basics actually reflect the Italian sabre. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- The genealogy is, if anything, the reverse of "ancient survival": the szabla reached Poland via Hungary (15th c., Hungarians having adapted the Turkish sabre), and postwar Polish *sport* sabre was rebuilt under the Hungarian master **János Kevey**, who trained Zabłocki and Pawłowski. The "traditional Polish sabre method" is thus genealogically downstream of 20th-c. Hungarian Olympic sabre, reconstructed from "indirect sources, memoir texts, and study of the weapons themselves." *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- The documented folk-pedagogy that *is* attested is *palcaty* — stick-dueling among students, framed as preparation for later sabre use. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

**Correction to the prior document:** any treatment placing the Polish szabla among the *continuous-lineage* or *documented-treatise* traditions is wrong. It belongs with the *substantially-reconstructed* traditions (alongside the Mesoamerican/Hawaiian cases), and the reconstruction is more recent and more contested than the English sources admit. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § F. Japanese (koryū)

English search gives the famous texts (Musashi's *Go Rin no Sho*, Yagyū Munenori's *Heihō Kadensho*) and lineage names. Japanese search adds the **academic apparatus**.

### F.1 Budō-gaku does the same source-critical work the prior document prized

The Japanese *budō-gaku* (武道学) journal literature applies exactly the founder-legend demystification the prior document valued. Example: a 1978 article in *Budō-gaku Kenkyū* (Okada Kazuo, Kokugakuin University) on the origins of Yagyū Shinkage-ryū tests whether Yagyū Munetoshi (Sekishūsai)'s *Shintō-ryū* inheritance came through Kamiizumi Hidetsuna or an earlier teacher "Katori Shinjūrō," and concludes it is *source-critically impossible to separate* the two influences and that the name "Shinjūrō" **appears nowhere in the documents** (文献上には全く、その名を見ない). This is precisely the kind of "the chain is partly retrospective" finding the prior document made for European traditions — but for the Japanese traditions it exists in Japanese, not English. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

### F.2 Native organizing frameworks

Japanese sources organize the whole field around the **三大源流 (san-dai genryū, "three great source-streams")**: *Nen-ryū* (founder Nen'ami Jion, a monk), *Shintō-ryū* (originally a general term for the Katori/Kashima sword methods, crystallized as a school in Iizasa Ienao's Tenshin Shōden Katori Shintō-ryū), and *Kage-ryū* (founder Aisu Hisatada, parent of the Shinkage-ryū line). *Chūjō-ryū* is sometimes added as a fourth. Over 700 ryūha are documented; the true number is higher. This taxonomy is the native frame; English presentations usually pick a few schools without it. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

### F.3 An attested internal method-debate

Japanese sources document the Edo-period controversy over *shinai* sparring (竹刀打ち込み稽古): schools that adopted free sparring (Hokushin Ittō-ryū, Shintō Munen-ryū, Kyōshin Meichi-ryū, Tennen Rishin-ryū) grew rapidly, while kata-only schools that refused it (Owari Yagyū Shinkage-ryū, Katayama Hōki-ryū, Tōda-ryū) did not expand their enrollment. This is a documented, named instance of the "sparring vs. form" tension the games-and-traditions analysis treated abstractly. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

### F.4 Reception note

The 20th-c. *menkyō kaiden* holders of Katori Shintō-ryū, Sugino Yoshio and Ōtake Risuke, were the bridge to global visibility — Sugino choreographed Kurosawa's *Seven Samurai* (1954) and other films. The cinematic image of "samurai swordsmanship" descends in part from one specific koryū's modern teachers, a transmission path the English corpus rarely names. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § G. Chinese (Ming military canon)

English reduces this to "Qi Jiguang wrote a manual." Chinese sources carry detailed **bibliographic and recension scholarship** plus full primary texts.

### G.1 The Jixiao Xinshu recension problem

Chinese sources establish, with textual reasoning, that Qi Jiguang's *Jixiao Xinshu* (紀效新書, 1560) exists in two recensions: the **original printing of 14 juan**, which ended at the bare-hand *Quanjing Jieyao* chapter (拳經捷要篇) — provable because that chapter's subtitle states it is "placed as the end of all chapters" (以此為諸篇之末) — and the later, more widely circulated **18-juan version** (the one collected in the *Siku Quanshu*), which added four chapters. English treatments cite "the *Jixiao Xinshu*" without the recension distinction, which matters for what Qi actually wrote versus what was appended. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

### G.2 The Wubei Zhi in its political context

Mao Yuanyi's *Wubei Zhi* (武備志, completed 1621, 240 juan / ~10,400 pages, compiling 2,000+ prior works) is framed by Chinese sources against the late-Ming crisis — the *wokou* coastal raids, factional strife, eunuch power — which drove literati to write on military affairs and raised the standing of the scholar-strategist. This is the patronage/materialist substructure the prior document tracked, here a whole-of-society pressure rather than a single patron. Native sources also note candidly that some of its firearms illustrations are inaccurate. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

### G.3 A fuller author/text chain and live primary access

The Chinese chain is richer than the English: **Yu Dayou**'s *Jianjing* (劍經, a staff manual despite the name, tied to Shaolin), paired with Qi as "dragon and tiger"; **Cheng Zongyou**'s Shaolin staff and weapon manuals and his nephew **Cheng Ziyi**'s *Wubei Yaolüe* (武備要略); **Wu Shu**'s *Shoubi Lu* (手臂錄) — the great-spear treatise that "set the course of Chinese weapon arts for three centuries" — and its sequel *Wuyin Lu*; and **Wang Zongyue**'s *Yinfu Qiang* spear manual, later annexed to Taiji genealogy. Full texts sit in native digital corpora (ctext.org 中國哲學書電子化計劃; shuge.org 書格) keyed to the Chinese titles. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

### G.4 A heritage/political datapoint

Chinese sources note the *Jixiao Xinshu* lineage was treated by the Qing as politically dangerous (a "sedition" text subject to suppression), a reception fact absent from English summaries. *[Source: medium; Interpretive: medium]*

## § H. Korean (the Muyedobotongji lineage)

English flattens this to "King Jeongjo commissioned a martial manual in 1790." Korean sources give the full lineage, the editorial apparatus, and the factional politics.

### H.1 The three-stage lineage

*Muyejebo* (무예제보, 1598, by **Han Gyo**, who analyzed the *Jixiao Xinshu* and observed Ming troops directly — 6 techniques) → *Muyejebo Beonyeok Sokjip* (1604/1610, adding material including translated Japanese sources) → *Muyesinbo* (무예신보, 1759, 18 infantry techniques) → *Muyedobotongji* (무예도보통지, 1790, by **Yi Deok-mu, Park Je-ga, Baek Dong-su** under King **Jeongjo**, 24 techniques 무예24기). It draws directly on Qi Jiguang's *Jixiao Xinshu* and Mao Yuanyi's *Wubei Zhi* (imported via Qing Beijing). *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

### H.2 Patronage-as-substructure, made political

The decisive multilingual finding: the 1759 *Muyesinbo* was entangled in **Crown Prince Sado's (사도세자)** regency politics. Sado used the compilation of martial arts to cultivate a new military faction (the *Cheongnyudang* and *Namin*) as a power base — read as a threat by the ruling *Noron* faction and resented by King Yeongjo, in the lead-up to Sado's death. King Jeongjo later framed the 1790 *Muyedobotongji* explicitly as continuing his father Sado's intent. This is the prior document's "patronage substructure" axis at its most consequential — commissioning a fight manual as a move in a succession struggle — and it is legible only in Korean. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

### H.3 Editorial apparatus

The *Muyedobotongji* carries real scholarly apparatus: a *byeonggi chongseo* (兵技總敍) chronological history of combat technique from early Joseon (valued as a history of *byeonggi*/military technique in its own right), a *Cheok-Mo sasil* (戚茅事實, records of Qi Jiguang and Mao Yuanyi), a *giyе jiluiui* (technique Q&A), an *inyong seomok* (引用書目, bibliography of cited works), and a *goi-pyo* (考異表, variant-readings/collation table). This is critical-edition-grade editorial work. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

### H.4 Demystification note

Korean sources flag that Baek Dong-su's modern celebrity owes much to Kim Young-ho's semi-fictional *Joseon-ui Hyeopgaek Baek Dong-su*, which blends documented fact with invention without clear demarcation — a caution for any treatment leaning on the popular/dramatized image. The work was inscribed (by the DPRK) on the UNESCO Memory of the World register in 2017. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § I. Arabic (Mamluk furūsiyya)

English search gives the category and a few names. Arabic search reaches the **primary manuscripts and modern Arabic scholarship**.

### I.1 A datable primary witness

The Qatar Digital Library hosts (with bilingual cataloging) the British Library copy of **al-Aqsarāʾī**'s *Nihāyat al-suʾl wa-al-umnīya fī taʿallum aʿmāl al-furūsiyya* (نهاية السؤل والأمنية في تعلم أعمال الفروسية) — the major Mamluk *furūsiyya* manual, composed 1348 in Damascus, this copy completed 10 Muḥarram 773 / 25 July 1371 by the scribe Aḥmad ibn ʿUmar al-Mayṣarī. Its folio-level descriptions detail lance (*rumḥ*) play, sword exercises with specific cut-trajectories ("from the horse's ear to its right croup"), and shield types (*daraqa*, *turs*). *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

### I.2 A striking transmission detail

The Aqsarāʾī manuscript includes **"Euclid's figures" repurposed as military formations** attributed to the ancient Greeks (أشكال أقليدس... كأشكال عسكرية من قِبل اليونانيين القدماء) — a direct in-text marker of Greek-geometry-into-tactics transmission inside an Arabic cavalry treatise. This is the kind of cross-cultural theoretical borrowing the four-axis lens does not capture and that the English corpus does not mention. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

### I.3 Modern Arabic scholarship and corpus

The **Bibliotheca Alexandrina** devoted a special issue of *Dhākirat al-ʿArab* (ذاكرة العرب) to Mamluk-era furūsiyya, out of an international conference — covering horse selection/breeding, archery (*ramy*), army command, and weapon use. Further manuscripts (e.g. BnF Arabe 2825, *Kitāb al-furūsiyya wa-al-manāṣib al-ḥarbiyya*) are described in Arabic-language venues. The furūsiyya genre's integration of hippology, archery, cavalry tactics, and individual weapon-craft is its "resistant material" — it is an *equestrian-military* literature, not an individual-dueling one, and reading only English flattens that distinction. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § J. Consolidated corrections to the prior corpus

Ranked by how much each changes the prior document.

1. **Polish szabla — reclassify.** Move from any "documented/continuous" framing to the *substantially-reconstructed* category; flag the reconstruction as 20th-century, contested, and genealogically downstream of Hungarian sport sabre. (§E) *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

2. **Spanish — the corpus is half-missing.** Add the *destreza vulgar/común* track (Pons, de la Torre, Román, Godinho) and the montante subgenre alongside the *verdadera destreza* spine. The prior chain captured only the elite geometric school. (§A) *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

3. **Evidence grades are miscalibrated.** Several "Interpretive: high" hedges (Liechtenauer filiation; Yagyū lineage; Fiore composition) are too uncertain *only because the source-language scholarship was not read*. German stemma work (Hils), Italian dialectology, and Japanese budō-gaku each tighten the grade. Conversely, confidence in some English secondary summaries should drop. (§B, §C, §F) *[Source: medium; Interpretive: medium]*

4. **Founding-legend demystification is not a European specialty.** The prior document treated demystification as its signature move and applied it most rigorously to European traditions. The Japanese (budō-gaku), Chinese (recension criticism), and Korean (editorial apparatus) traditions do equivalent source-critical work *in their own languages*; the prior document's lighter touch on the non-European cases reflects search language, not a real asymmetry in the scholarship. (§F, §G, §H) *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

5. **The field's infrastructure is multilingual.** *Acta Periodica Duellatorum*, Brill's fight-book volume, and the AEDEH proceedings are themselves multilingual; English-only search returns a biased subsample of venues it already "knows." (§D) *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*

## § K. Method, coverage, and gaps

**Method.** For each tradition I searched in the primary source language(s) using native terminology (e.g. *verdadera destreza*, *Fechtbuch*/*Kunst des Fechtens*, *scherma*/*Flos duellatorum*, *sztuka krzyżowa*, 古流剣術/伝書, 武備志/紀效新書, 무예도보통지, الفروسية), prioritizing national academic literature, critical-edition publishers, and native primary-source corpora over anglophone aggregators. Claims are graded on the prior document's two-axis scheme.

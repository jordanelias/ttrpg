# Multilingual Research — Third Round

Companion to `04-multilingual-supplement.md` and `05-multilingual-intensive.md`. This round closes five more of the doc-05 Part 4 gaps with in-language searches (Sanskrit/Hindi, Tamil, Ottoman Turkish, Thai, Arabic *futuwwa*), and — because the corpus has now accumulated enough traditions — states one **cross-traditional convergence** that only becomes visible at this scale. Grading scheme unchanged: *[Source: high/medium/low; Interpretive: high/medium/low]*.

**Headline result of this round.** Beyond the new traditions, the accumulation surfaces a genuine universal the corpus could not state before: **every tradition that trains lethal-weapon use independently invents a non-lethal training surrogate, and the surrogate's design encodes what the tradition thinks the weapon is for** (Part 2). This sits alongside the two universals from the prior rounds — founding-legend demystification, and language-as-evidence — as a third recurring pattern that is only legible once you read across the source languages.

# Part 1 — Gap clusters closed

## § T. Sanskrit / Hindi (the Dhanurveda)

The key finding is a **category correction**: the prior corpus's "Indian" material conflated a *textual knowledge-category* with *living practice*. They are not the same lineage.

- The *Dhanurveda* (धनुर्वेद, "science of the bow / of weapons") is one of the four *upavedas*, traditionally attached to the Yajurveda and attributed to Bhṛgu / Viśvāmitra / Bharadvāja. It is a *category* of martial knowledge — covering individual weapons and their making and use, *vyūha* (battle-array), the four-fold *chaturanga* army, and statecraft — not a single practical fight-manual. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- The surviving witnesses are **embedded and medieval**, not Vedic-age standalone manuals: notably the *Dhanurveda* chapters of the *Agni Purāṇa* (with the classic four-fold weapon taxonomy — *mukta* thrown, *amukta* held, *muktāmukta* either, *mantramukta* released by spell), plus the *Vishnudharmottara* and late *Dhanurveda-saṃhitā* compilations. The "1100–800 BCE" dating is the *traditional attribution* to the Vedic period, not the date of the extant texts; and the *Agni Purāṇa* "exists in numerous versions, some very different" — a recension caution. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- The living arts transmit separately and orally: *malla-yuddha* (मल्लयुद्ध, the classical wrestling lineage; later the *Malla Purāṇa* of the Gujarati Jyeṣṭhīmalla), and the regional weapon arts (kalaripayattu, silambam). **Correction:** do not treat the *Dhanurveda* as the textual source-of-truth behind the living South Asian arts; it is a parallel, mostly antiquarian textual stream. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § U. Tamil (silambam, varma)

- *Silambam* (சிலம்பம்; colloquially *kambu suttrudal*, "stick-spinning") is a staff art scored by **thodu puḷḷi** (தொடு புள்ளி, "touch-points" on the opponent) — a point-contact target logic. It has named *varisai* styles: *vāri* (basic attack/defense), *mei* (body-movement), *āyutha* (weapons — *vēl* spear, *vāḷ* sword, *kambu* staff), and *kuthu* (boxing-like striking, "kai silambam"); the *kadamba varisai* forms are likened to karate *kata*. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Underlying it (and southern "tekkaṇ" kalari) is **varma / varmakkalai** (வர்மக்கலை) — the vital-point art tied to *Siddha* medicine, the Tamil counterpart of Kerala's *marmam*. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- It is a **living/oral + performance** tradition (concentrated in the far-southern districts, prominent at temple festivals), with explicit modern *reconstruction* concern: Tamil sources lament that the art, like cinematic kung-fu/karate, has "lost its soul" to film and commerce, and note active revival efforts — a native-language demystification, and a pointer to the neighboring Sinhala *angampora* revival. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § V. Ottoman Turkish (matrak, cirit)

A high-value cluster: it has a primary treatise, an institutional home, and a clean reconstruction arc.

- *Matrak* (matrak oyunu) is an Ottoman **sword-and-shield (kılıç-kalkan) training art using padded surrogates** — a stick of ~80–100 cm standing in for the sword and a shorter ~50–65 cm club for the *gürz* (mace), with a padded leather cushion as shield. It was the training method for **Janissaries (yeniçeri) and sipahis** under Süleyman. The word is from Arabic *miṭraq* (cudgel); *Kâmus-ı Türkî* defines *matrak* as the padded implement and *matrakçı* as the instructor who teaches sword with it. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- **Primary source:** Matrakçı Nasuh's *Tuhfetü'l-guzât* (1530), a *silahşorluk* (martial-skill) treatise, by the Bosnian-origin Ottoman polymath Nasuh bin Karagöz el-Bosnavî (c.1480–1564), who was awarded a *berat* by Süleyman for his unrivaled weapon skill and is also famous for his battle-scene miniatures (a rich iconographic record). The practice connects upstream to the Mamluk Türkic milieu (and thus to the *furūsiyya* of doc 04 §I). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Reconstruction arc:** the practice lapsed with the abolition of the Janissaries (1826) and was revived from 2001 (Efkan Çalış; codified as "Matrak sporu" in 2008) with academic support from Gazi, Çanakkale Onsekiz Mart, and Kırıkkale universities. Treat the federations' nationalist priority claims (e.g., "older than kendo/fencing") with skepticism — those are heritage-marketing, not datable history. The everyday idiom *matrak geçmek* ("to mock") descends from the master's effortless superiority over a novice — a language fossil of the practice. *[Source: medium; Interpretive: medium]*

## § W. Thai (krabi-krabong)

- *Krabi-krabong* (กระบี่กระบอง) pairs the **short weapons** (*krabi* — *daab* sword, *daab song mue* double sword, etc.) with the **long** (*krabong* — *plong* staff, *ngao* glaive, spear), a split rationalized in a native folk-etymology (the agile monkey vs. the large slow *yaksha*). Training used **rattan (waay) mock-weapons** and hide shields (*loh, khen, dang*) in paired sparring "as if on the battlefield" — *yuttha kila* (ยุทธกีฬา, "war-sport"); it is the armed counterpart of *muay/muay boran*. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Native sources are candid that the **origin is undocumented** ("no document states who first devised it or when"), tracing it generically across the Sukhothai–Ayutthaya–Rattanakosin periods. It now exists in two registers — *display* (แสดง) and *competition* (แข่งขัน) — is on Thailand's national intangible-heritage list, and is taught in the physical-education curriculum. Ramkhamhaeng University humanities scholarship traces the battlefield-prep → recreation → performance arc. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

## § X. Arabic (futuwwa — the documented chivalric substructure)

This closes the doc-04 §I / doc-05 §L thread: the Islamic chivalric code behind both *furūsiyya* and the *zurkhāneh* has its own treatise and a vivid patronage instance.

- *Futuwwa* (فتوة, "young-manliness") is the medieval Islamic confraternity/chivalric code — comparable to chivalry/*virtù* — that evolved from "youth" into a moral framework by the 8th c. through a melding and Islamicization of Persian (*javānmardī*) and Arab traditions. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Primary source:** Ibn al-Miʿmar al-Baghdadi's *Kitāb al-Futuwwa* (d. 1244/45), with a major modern critical edition by the Iraqi scholar **Mustafa Jawad** and collaborators (Baghdad, Maktabat al-Muthanna, 1958; digitized via Columbia's Arabic Collections Online and the Internet Archive). *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- **Patronage-as-substructure, vividly:** the Abbasid caliph **al-Nāṣir li-Dīn Allāh** (d. 1225) abolished the existing *futuwwa* and founded a state-controlled "**Nāṣirī futuwwa**" (الفتوة الناصرية) through which emirs, kings, and sultans across the caliphate pledged personal loyalty to him by being inducted — converting a chivalric brotherhood into a transregional political-loyalty network. The related **Akhi** brotherhoods (with Byzantine craft links) carried *futuwwa* into Anatolia and into Ottoman guild structure. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Unification:** *futuwwa/javānmardī* is the single documented ethical-religious substructure under the Persian *zurkhāneh* (§L), the *furūsiyya* corpus (§I), and the Anatolian guild world — and it has a datable treatise. The prior corpus treated these as separate "religious-civic" or "equestrian-military" cases; they share a code. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

# Part 2 — A cross-traditional convergence: the training surrogate

With enough traditions in hand, one design fact recurs too consistently to be coincidence and is worth stating as a finding in its own right.

**Every tradition that trains the use of a lethal weapon independently invents a non-lethal surrogate that allows full-intent practice — and the surrogate's *form* encodes the tradition's theory of the weapon.** The instances, now drawn from across the corpus and these rounds:

- *Padded sticks standing in for blade and mace:* Ottoman **matrak** (§V) — padded stick + cushion shield, explicitly a sword/mace simulator.
- *Bundled/​split bamboo for the sword:* Japanese **shinai** (with bōgu armor), the very tool whose Edo-period adoption drove the sparring-vs-kata enrollment split (doc 04 §F).
- *Blunted feder for the longsword:* the German **Fechtfeder** (doc 04 §C context).
- *Rattan mock-weapons:* Thai **krabi-krabong** (§W) — rattan *daab*/*ngao* and hide shields.
- *Sticks as sabre-preparation:* Polish **palcaty** student stick-dueling (doc 04 §E).
- *Deliberately blunted live weapon:* the **ceremonial macuahuitl** of the flower war (doc 05 §Q) — the surrogate is the real weapon, dulled, because the goal is capture not kill.
- *Target-cutting instead of sparring:* Russian **rubka** (doc 05 §N) — where the weapon (shashka) is a one-cut chopping blade, the "training surrogate" is a static target, not a sparring partner, precisely because there is no fencing to spar.

The diagnostic value: the surrogate tells you what the tradition optimized for. A sparring surrogate (shinai, matrak, feder, rattan) implies a *fencing* system built on exchange and feeling pressure; a static-target surrogate (rubka) implies a *delivery* system built on one decisive cut; a blunted-live-weapon surrogate (macuahuitl) implies a *capture* system. This refines doc 05's "weapon vs. fighting-system" distinction into a usable test. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

# Part 3 — Refinements and corrections

1. **Place the new five on the documented↔oral↔reconstructed axis (doc 05).**
   - *Documented-treatise:* Ottoman matrak (1530 *Tuhfetü'l-guzât*); Arabic *futuwwa* (Ibn al-Miʿmar 1244).
   - *Textual-but-embedded/antiquarian:* the Indian *Dhanurveda* (witnesses inside the *Agni Purāṇa* etc.).
   - *Living/oral + reconstruction:* Tamil silambam/varma; Thai krabi-krabong; and matrak's *present* practice (treatise survives, but continuity broke in 1826 and was rebuilt). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

2. **The Indian category split** (textual *Dhanurveda* vs. living oral arts) is the cleanest case of a confusion latent throughout the corpus: a literate civilization's *treatise about* fighting is not automatically the *lineage of* its living fighters. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

3. **A new evidence type: the idiom fossil.** *Matrak geçmek* (Turkish, §V) and *in die Schranken weisen* (German, doc 05 §S) are everyday phrases that fossilize a dead martial-legal practice. Like the philology layer (doc 04–05), language preserves the practice after the practice itself lapses — usable as corroborating evidence. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

4. **Nationalist heritage-marketing is a distinct demystification target.** The matrak "older than kendo" claim and the *Dhanurveda* "Vedic-age martial science" framing are both modern priority/antiquity claims that outrun the datable record. They differ from honest founding-legends (Puriyā-ye Valī, Katori Shinjūrō) in being state/federation-driven; flag them as such. *[Source: medium; Interpretive: medium]*

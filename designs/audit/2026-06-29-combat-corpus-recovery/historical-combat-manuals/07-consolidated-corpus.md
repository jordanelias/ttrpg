# Dueling Combat and Martial Traditions — Consolidated Corpus

A single consolidation of the whole research line: the original per-tradition study and four-axis decomposition (`01-historical-combat-manuals.md`), the deep revision (`revised_manual.md`), the throughlines analysis, and the three multilingual rounds (`04-multilingual-supplement.md`, `05-multilingual-intensive.md`, `06-multilingual-third-round.md`). It restructures all of it in the established format: **Part I**, per-tradition treatment organized by record-format; **Part II**, the four-axis re-cut; and **Part III**, the consolidated confidence and gap register. (The throughlines-and-meta-findings analysis that previously stood here as a Part III has been removed; it is re-derived adversarially and tested against the scholarship in `09-throughlines-adversarial.md`, with the corrected synthesis in `10-corpus-rewrite.md`.)

The component documents hold the full detail. This master document carries the unified structure and the analysis; each per-tradition entry compresses the founder→glossator→consolidator→polemicist chain, the theoretical contribution, the patronage anchor, the "resistant material" the four-axis lens cannot hold, the multilingual layer (what source-language work added), and the tradition's placement on the classification axis introduced in the multilingual rounds.

**Two-axis confidence tagging** is retained throughout: *[Source: high/medium/low; Interpretive: high/medium/low]* — *Source* grades documentary attestation, *Interpretive* grades the inferential distance between documents and claim.

**The classification axis** (the principal structural contribution of the multilingual rounds): traditions sort along **documented-treatise ↔ oral/embodied-with-no-canon ↔ reconstructed-from-external-documentation**, a sort orthogonal to geography and more useful than the record-format taxonomy for judging evidentiary weight. It is noted per tradition below.

# Part I — Per-tradition treatment (by record-format)

The corpus's own six-format taxonomy of how premodern martial knowledge was recorded organizes Part I: (1) European fencing-treatise; (2) military-encyclopedic; (3) lineage kata-and-oral; (4) religious-civic-institutional; (5) literary-poetic embedding; (6) reconstructed-from-external-documentation. The multilingual rounds add a seventh working cluster in practice — (7) national-cavalry sabre and festival weapon arts — which the original taxonomy folded awkwardly into (1) and (5); it is broken out here.

## Tier 1 — European fencing-treatise

### 1.1 German (the Liechtenauer tradition + the judicial duel)
Chain: Johannes Liechtenauer (mnemonic *Zettel*) → glossators Pseudo-Döbringer (GNM 3227a, c.1389), Ringeck, Pseudo-Danzig → consolidators Talhoffer, Lecküchner, Paulus Kal, Mair → polemicist/compiler Joachim Meyer (1570). Theoretical contribution: the *Vor/Nach/Indes* tempo dialectic, *Stärke/Schwäche*, *Fühlen*, the Five Master-cuts. Patronage anchor: Talhoffer → Eberhard V of Württemberg; Mair financed his encyclopedia by embezzling Augsburg city funds for ~40 years. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 04 §C, doc 05 §S):** the scholarly spine is German *Germanistik* / *Fachprosaforschung* predating anglophone HEMA — Wierschin (1965, with a *Fachsprache* glossary), Hils (1983/85, attempting a manuscript *Stemma*), Welle (1993, *Ringen* as noble art). The *schirmen* vs. *fechten* distinction is lost in English. And the dueling-specific content lives in the **Gerichtskampf** literature: judicial duels inside the *Schranken*, policed by *Grießwarte*, including the handicapped **man-vs-woman duel** (Talhoffer 1459/1467, the earliest fight-book depiction; women per the *Sachsenspiegel*'s "Megede und Wedewen"); Swabian (shield+sword) vs. Franconian (shield+mace) regional law; professional *Lohnkämpfer*; the modern honor-duel descends from it as the ordeal frame gave way to estate honor (Jaser & Israel, *Zweikämpfer*, 2014). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Resistant material: the religious-ordeal framing and the legal-procedural embedding — the longsword is also a truth-finding instrument. Classification: **documented-treatise.**

### 1.2 Italian (Fiore → Bolognese → rapier)
Chain: Fiore dei Liberi (*Flos Duellatorum / Fior di Battaglia*, c.1409) → Filippo Vadi (*De Arte Gladiatoria Dimicandi*, c.1482) → the Anonimo Bolognese / Marozzo (*Opera Nova*, 1536) → Agrippa (1553) → Fabris, Capo Ferro (*Gran Simulacro*, 1610), Giganti. Contribution: *tempo, misura, mezzo tempo, contratempo*; the geometric-scientific turn at Agrippa. Patronage: Fiore → Niccolò III d'Este; Agrippa → Cosimo de' Medici / Cardinal Farnese; Capo Ferro → della Rovere; Fabris → Christian IV of Denmark. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 04 §B):** Italian *filologia* adds a layer English lacks — the dialectological reading of the *Flos* showing two language strata (Fiore's Friulian + the Este-scribe's Ferrarese); the foundational Novati edition (1902) and the Rubboli–Cesari critical editions; the Sala d'Arme Achille Marozzo transcription program; and the recently rediscovered **Anonimo Bolognese** that anchors the under-weighted Bolognese middle. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- Resistant material: the manuscript-as-courtly-gift dimension. Classification: **documented-treatise.**

### 1.3 Iberian (Spanish *verdadera destreza* + *destreza común* + Portuguese montante)
Chain (verdadera): Carranza (*De la Filosofía de las Armas*, 1582) → Pacheco de Narváez → Ettenhard → Lorenz de Rada → (cross-current) Thibault. Contribution: the *matematización* of fencing — the geometric circle, fencing as a *quadrivium* science / *arte liberal*. Patronage: Carranza → Medina Sidonia; Lorenz de Rada → Philip V. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 04 §A) — the widest anglophone gap:** the prior chain was the elite *verdadera* spine only. The Spanish/Galician/Portuguese sources restore (a) the older **destreza vulgar/común** (Jaime Pons & Pedro de la Torre 1474; Francisco Román 1532), whose only complete surviving treatise is **Godinho's *Arte de Esgrima*, 1599**; (b) the **montante** subgenre of numbered *reglas* (Godinho, Paredes, Figueiredo 1651); (c) the AGEA Editora critical-edition infrastructure and Valle Ortiz's bibliography; (d) the sharpened Carranza-vs-Pacheco split (explanation vs. deductive proof) that Quevedo mocked in *El Buscón*. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Resistant material: the *matematización* program itself — fencing-as-science is a theoretical commitment the four axes cannot represent. Classification: **documented-treatise** (both the *verdadera* and, via Godinho, the *común* layer).

### 1.4 French (poleaxe, the first French masters)
Chain: the anonymous *Le Jeu de la Hache* (BnF fr. 1996, c.1400 / third quarter 15th c.) → Henry de Sainct-Didier (1573, first French author, origin of the *fleuret*). Contribution: the most extensive single-weapon (poleaxe-in-armor) system; later, French pedagogy and the smallsword line. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 04 §D):** the Dupuis–Deluz critical edition and dating argument; Cognot grounding the poleaxe in archaeology of surviving Burgundian arms; the recognition that the field's peer-reviewed infrastructure (*Acta Periodica Duellatorum*, Brill, the Dijon AEDEH volumes) is Franco-German-Swiss and multilingual, so English search returns a biased subsample. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- Classification: **documented-treatise** (isolated witness for the poleaxe).

### 1.5 English (Silver)
Chain: George Silver (*Paradoxes of Defence*, 1599; *Bref Instructions*) → Swetnam (1617). Contribution: a documented *design argument* — the English short-staff/true-fight case made explicitly *against* the Italian rapier school, i.e. a native polemic rather than a transmission lineage. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Resistant material: Silver is best read as a critic-theorist, not a school-founder. Classification: **documented-treatise** (thin, polemical).

## Tier 2 — Military-encyclopedic

### 2.1 Byzantine (the *Taktika* genre)
Chain: *Strategikon* of Maurice (late 6th c., the shift to cavalry as the main arm) → *Taktika* of Leo VI the Wise (early 10th c.) → successors (Nikephoros Phokas etc.). Contribution: state-army doctrine descending from Hellenistic/Roman models (Aelian, Onasander). Patronage: imperial-state documents addressed to the thematic *strategoi*. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 05 §R):** Greek scholarship foregrounds the **imitation-vs-updating tension** — the genre copies ancient prototypes by convention *and* renews them, so antiquarian copying must be told from live doctrine before the text is used as evidence; the **Latin command vocabulary fossilized in Greek** (e.g. *komēs* < *comes*) is its own philological layer; Leo opens with an explicit **Christian just-war framing**. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Resistant material: ideology (just war) and the antiquarian-copying problem. Classification: **documented-treatise** (with a textual-transmission caution).

### 2.2 Chinese (the Ming military canon)
Chain: Qi Jiguang (*Jixiao Xinshu*, 1560) and Yu Dayou (*Jianjing*) → Cheng Zongyou (Shaolin staff/weapons) → Mao Yuanyi (*Wubei Zhi*, 1621) → Wu Shu (*Shoubi Lu*, the great-spear treatise) → Qing-era inheritors. Contribution: integration of individual weapon-craft with operational drilling and formation. Patronage/substructure: the late-Ming crisis (*wokou* raids, factional strife) drove literati to write on war. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 04 §G):** Chinese sources carry **recension criticism** the English account drops — the *Jixiao Xinshu* exists in a 14-*juan* original (ending at the *Quanjing* chapter, provable from its subtitle) and an 18-*juan* expansion (the *Siku Quanshu* version); full primary texts sit in ctext.org / shuge.org; native sources note the *Wubei Zhi*'s inaccurate firearms plates and the Qing treatment of the lineage as politically dangerous. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Resistant material: the cosmological/qi overlays on the unarmed material. Classification: **documented-treatise.**

### 2.3 Mamluk / Islamic (*furūsiyya*)
Chain: Ibn Akhī Ḥizām → al-Ṭarsūsī → al-Aqsarāʾī → Ottoman successors. Contribution: the integrated equestrian-military science. Patronage: the Mamluk military-slave state and its training establishment. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 04 §I):** the Qatar Digital Library's bilingual catalog of **al-Aqsarāʾī's *Nihāyat al-suʾl*** (Damascus 1348; this copy 1371) gives folio-level detail — lance play, sword-cut trajectories, shield types — and the striking note of **"Euclid's figures" repurposed as cavalry formations**; Bibliotheca Alexandrina's *Dhākirat al-ʿArab* furūsiyya issue is modern Arabic scholarship. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Resistant material: hippology + archery + tactics make this *equestrian-military*, not individual-dueling. Its ethical substructure is *futuwwa* (see 4.2). Classification: **documented-treatise.**

### 2.4 Korean (the *Muyedobotongji* lineage)
Chain: *Muyejebo* (1598, Han Gyo, 6 techniques) → *Muyejebo Beonyeok Sokjip* (1604/10) → *Muyesinbo* (1759, 18 techniques) → *Muyedobotongji* (1790, Yi Deok-mu / Park Je-ga / Baek Dong-su under Jeongjo, 24 techniques). Contribution: a deliberate synthesis drawing on Qi Jiguang and Mao Yuanyi. Patronage: royal commission. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 04 §H):** Korean sources reveal the **factional politics** behind the 1759 *Muyesinbo* — Crown Prince Sado building a military power base against the ruling Noron, which Jeongjo later framed the 1790 work as continuing — and the real **editorial apparatus** (the *byeonggi chongseo* technique-history, the *goi-pyo* variant-readings table). Demystification: Baek Dong-su's popular image owes much to a semi-fictional modern book. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Resistant material: the manual as dynastic-political act. Classification: **documented-treatise.**

### 2.5 Indian (the *Dhanurveda* — textual knowledge-category)
Contribution: the *Dhanurveda* is an *upaveda*-category (weapons, their making/use, *vyūha* arrays, the *chaturanga* army, statecraft), attributed to Bhṛgu/Viśvāmitra/Bharadvāja. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 06 §T) — a category correction:** the surviving witnesses are **embedded and medieval** (the *Agni Purāṇa*'s dhanurveda chapters with the *mukta/amukta/muktāmukta/mantramukta* taxonomy; *Vishnudharmottara*; late *saṃhitā*), not Vedic-age standalone manuals — the "1100–800 BCE" dating is traditional attribution. It is *not* the lineage behind the living arts (kalaripayattu, silambam, *malla-yuddha*), which transmit separately. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Resistant material: the *upaveda* religious framing; the gap between text and practice. Classification: **textual-but-embedded/antiquarian** (a sub-position of documented-treatise, distinct from a practical manual).

## Tier 3 — Lineage kata-and-oral

### 3.1 Japanese (koryū)
Chain (Shinkage line): the *san-dai genryū* (three source-streams) — Nen-ryū (Nen'ami Jion), Shintō-ryū (Iizasa Ienao's Tenshin Shōden Katori Shintō-ryū), Kage-ryū (Aisu Hisatada) → Kamiizumi → Yagyū Munetoshi → Munenori; in parallel Musashi (*Go Rin no Sho*, 1645). Contribution: *ki-ken-tai-ichi*, *suki*, *kuzushi*; systematization via lineage continuity and embodied kata rather than printed treatise. Patronage: Yagyū → Tokugawa. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 04 §F):** Japanese academic *budō-gaku* does the same source-critical demystification the corpus prized — e.g. a 1978 *Journal of Budo* article showing the Yagyū "Katori Shinjūrō" inheritance is undocumented; native sources organize the field by the *san-dai genryū* and document the Edo **sparring-vs-kata enrollment debate** (*shinai* schools grew; kata-only schools did not). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Resistant material: the *densho*/*menkyō* transmission as the unit of systematization. Classification: **lineage/oral (continuous)** — the documented exception among living traditions.

### 3.2 South Indian living arts (kalaripayattu, silambam, varma)
Contribution: kalaripayattu (Kerala) — weapons (*vāḷ*, *kaṭāra*, *kuntam*, *urumi*), the 64 *marmam* vital points, and *kalari chikitsa* medicine; Tamil silambam — a staff art scored by *thodu puḷḷi* point-touches, with *varma/varmakkalai* (Siddha-linked vital points) as the southern substrate. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 05 §O, doc 06 §U):** Malayalam sources state plainly that **no authoritative classical text survives** — transmission is guru-to-disciple oral/embodied — and distinguish the northern (*vaḍakkan*) core from the Tamil-rooted southern (*tekkaṇ*) form; Tamil sources lament cinematic/commercial dilution and note active reconstruction. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Resistant material: the marmam/varma vital-point + medicine integration. Classification: **oral/embodied with reconstruction** (no canon).

### 3.3 Sikh (gatka / shastar vidya)
Contribution: the Khalsa weapon art, orthodox lineage from Guru Hargobind (*mīrī-pīrī*) developed by Guru Gobind Singh; canonical reference Bhai Kahn Singh Nabha's *Mahan Kosh*. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 06 §P):** the Guru-origin narrative is **orthodox but contested** (the term *gatka* is ~19th-c., much present practice is *nagar kīrtan* display); now institutionalized in India's NCERT curriculum (2026). *[Source: medium; Interpretive: medium]*
- Classification: **living-lineage + reconstruction**, strong religious-institutional frame.

## Tier 4 — Religious-civic-institutional (and the chivalric code beneath it)

### 4.1 Persian (the zurkhāneh / pahlavānī)
Contribution: *varzesh-e bāstānī* in the *gowd* (pit), led by the **morshed** (drum + *Shāhnāmeh* chant, as commander) and the **miāndār** (movement-leader); strength implements (*mil*, *charkh*); UNESCO ICH 2010. Founder-legend: Puriyā-ye Valī (semi-legendary). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 05 §L):** Persian gives the native role-structure and the *javānmardī*/Sufi/*ʿayyārī* substructure (the *kashkul* and *tabarzin* dervish emblems). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Resistant material: the practice is codified in **ritual and music**, not a combat treatise. Classification: **oral/embodied + religious-institutional.**

### 4.2 Islamic chivalry (*futuwwa* / *javānmardī*) — the shared substructure
Contribution: the medieval Islamic chivalric code — comparable to chivalry/*virtù* — uniting the *zurkhāneh* (4.1), the *furūsiyya* corpus (2.3), and the Anatolian *Akhi*/Ottoman guilds under one ethic. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 06 §X):** it has a **documented treatise** — Ibn al-Miʿmar al-Baghdadi's *Kitāb al-Futuwwa* (d. 1244; ed. Mustafa Jawad, Baghdad 1958) — and a vivid **patronage instance**: caliph al-Nāṣir (d. 1225) abolished the old *futuwwa* and founded a state "Nāṣirī *futuwwa*" as a transregional loyalty network. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Classification: **documented-treatise** (an ethical-code text underlying several practice traditions).

## Tier 5 — National-cavalry sabre and festival weapon arts

### 5.1 Hungarian sabre (szablavívás) — the continuous case
Chain: medieval *szablya* via Hungary → 19th-c. **József Keresztessy** (the "old Hungarian school," learning under Louis Chappon) → a thick printed-manual corpus (Chappon, Arlow, Leszák, Gerentsér 1944) → Italo Santelli's Italo-Hungarian sport-sabre synthesis → the Gerevich Olympic dynasty. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 05 §M):** Hungarian is a genuinely **continuous, documented** sabre tradition, and is the real referent behind the Polish reconstruction's genealogy (postwar Polish sport sabre revived under the Hungarian master Kevey). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Classification: **documented-treatise (continuous).**

### 5.2 Polish szabla — the reconstructed case
Contribution: the *sztuka krzyżowa* ("cross-cutting art") as national memory. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 04 §E):** Polish sources are openly skeptical — **no period manuals**; the art was declared *lost* by 1864 (Orgelbrand); the modern reconstruction rests on Starzewski's late anecdotal memoirs (describing a *French* sabre) and on the Olympic sabreur Zabłocki, whom Polish critics say back-projected sport fencing. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Classification: **reconstructed-from-indirect-sources** (the corrective demotion from any "continuous" framing).

### 5.3 Russian / Cossack (shashka) — the weapon-without-a-fencing-art case
Contribution: the *shashka* — a guardless, hilt-forward chopping blade. *[Source: high; Interpretive: low]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 05 §N):** Russian sources insist it is **not a fencing weapon** (it replaced the sabre *because* firearms removed the need for sabre fencing; the aim is one decisive cut). The codified "art" lives in *rubka* (target-cutting drill) and in a genuine **Cossack-pike treatise** (the 1843 *Nachertanie*). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Classification: **weapon-and-drill, not a fighting-system** (the limiting case).

### 5.4 Ottoman (matrak, cirit)
Contribution: *matrak* — a sword-and-shield training art using **padded surrogates**, the method for Janissaries and *sipahis*. Primary source: Matrakçı Nasuh's *Tuhfetü'l-guzât* (1530) with rich miniature iconography; connects upstream to the Mamluk milieu (2.3). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 06 §V):** continuity lapsed with the Janissaries (1826) and was reconstructed from 2001 with university backing; the idiom *matrak geçmek* is a language fossil; nationalist "older than kendo" claims are heritage-marketing. *[Source: medium; Interpretive: medium]*
- Classification: **documented-treatise, present practice reconstructed.**

### 5.5 Thai (krabi-krabong)
Contribution: short weapons (*krabi*) + long (*krabong*), trained with **rattan mock-weapons** and hide shields in paired sparring; the armed counterpart of *muay boran*. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 06 §W):** origin undocumented per native sources; now split into display vs. competition, heritage-listed, school-taught; Ramkhamhaeng University scholarship traces the battlefield→recreation→performance arc. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Classification: **oral/embodied + performance + reconstruction.**

## Tier 6 — Reconstructed-from-external-documentation

### 6.1 Mesoamerican (the *macuahuitl* / flower war)
Contribution: the obsidian *macuahuitl* and its polearm cousin *tepoztopilli*; the *telpochcalli*/*calmécac* training schools; the *xōchiyāōyōtl* (flower war). *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- **Multilingual layer (doc 05 §Q):** read directly, the Spanish chronicles (Sahagún, Durán, Bernal Díaz; the CSIC journal *Gladius*) show the combat objective was **capture, not kill** (ceremonial *macuahuitl* deliberately blunted), and tie the flower wars to the 1450–54 famine. The tradition is reconstructed *from precisely these Spanish-language sources*, best read directly. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
- Classification: **reconstructed-from-external-documentation** (the archetype).

### 6.2 Hawaiian (lua) and other reconstructed cases
Contribution: *lua* (joint-locking/bone-breaking) survives substantially through reconstruction and 20th-c. revival from external and oral documentation. *[Source: medium; Interpretive: high]*
- Classification: **reconstructed.** (Carried from the prior corpus; not re-searched in-language — see Part III.)

## Tier 7 — Literary-poetic embedding

### 7.1 Norse (holmgang; the sagas and Hávamál)
Contribution: the *holmgang* formal duel (procedural rules, the hide, three-shield allowance, banned in Iceland 1006) is documented *within* the family sagas and the Hávamál rather than in a combat manual; the modern "Norse martial arts" movement is a HEMA-adjacent reconstruction. Related to the continental *Gerichtskampf* (1.1). *[Source: high; Interpretive: high]*
- Classification: **literary-embedded + reconstructed.** (Norse-language saga scholarship not re-searched in-language — see Part III.)

# Part II — The four-axis re-cut

The practitioner's four axes — **weapon properties, technique, judgment, physical conditioning** — applied across the traditions, with each axis's "resistant material" (what it cannot hold) now expanded by the multilingual findings. The axes never displace the per-tradition view; they run in parallel with an explicit rebuttal, as in the original corpus.

**Axis 1 — Weapon properties.** The geometry, balance, and intended target of the tool. The multilingual rounds sharpen this into a diagnostic: a tradition's *training surrogate* reveals its weapon-theory (Part III). The Russian shashka (guardless, hilt-forward, one-cut) and the blunted ceremonial *macuahuitl* (capture) are cases where weapon properties *determine* that there is little or no "fencing" to analyze. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

**Axis 2 — Technique.** The catalog of cuts, guards, and actions. Resistant material newly surfaced: the *montante*'s task-specific *reglas* (crowd/gangway control, not dueling); the *furūsiyya* sword-cut trajectories defined relative to the horse; silambam's point-touch scoring. Technique is often inseparable from a non-dueling *function*. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

**Axis 3 — Judgment.** Timing, distance, and decision under pressure — *Indes/Fühlen*, *tempo/misura*, *sen*. This is the axis the games-and-design analysis found most universal. Resistant material: the Spanish *matematización* claim that *deductive* judgment can replace tested judgment (Pacheco) is a theory of judgment the axis cannot represent; the Byzantine *Taktika*'s imitation-vs-updating problem is a meta-judgment about the text itself. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

**Axis 4 — Physical conditioning.** Strength, flexibility, endurance. The multilingual rounds enlarge this axis most: the Persian *zurkhāneh* and Indian *akhāṛā* are *primarily* conditioning-and-ritual institutions; the marmam/varma vital-point arts fold conditioning into a medical system (*kalari chikitsa*, Siddha). Conditioning is, in several traditions, the *whole* of the institution, not a support to technique. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

**The cognitive-modes catalog** (Part II of the prior corpus): the seven canonical + three provisional decision-modes the traditions independently arrive at are not culturally arbitrary; the multilingual rounds add no new mode but add new *instances*, and one boundary case — the shashka — where the "mode" is degenerate (a single committed cut), confirming the catalog's range by its edge. *[Source: medium; Interpretive: high]*

# Part III — Consolidated confidence and gap register

### The corrections that most change the corpus (ranked)

1. **Reclassify the Polish szabla** from continuous to reconstructed; **add Hungarian** as the documented-continuous referent behind it. (Part I 5.1–5.2) *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
2. **Restore the missing half of the Iberian corpus** — the *destreza común* track and the montante subgenre alongside the *verdadera destreza* spine. (1.3) *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
3. **Adopt the classification axis** and the **weapon-vs-fighting-system** distinction as standing analytical tools. *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
4. **Recalibrate the language-biased grades** (tighten German/Italian/Japanese where source-language scholarship was unread; lower confidence in some English secondaries). *[Source: medium; Interpretive: medium]*
5. **Relocate the Indian *Dhanurveda*** as a textual knowledge-category, not the lineage of the living arts. (2.5) *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*
6. **Unify the Islamic chivalric substructure** (*futuwwa/javānmardī*) across *furūsiyya*, the *zurkhāneh*, and the Akhi/Ottoman guilds. (4.2) *[Source: high; Interpretive: medium]*

### Coverage to date

Source-language searching completed for: Spanish, Portuguese/Galician, Italian, German, French, Polish, Japanese, Chinese, Korean, Arabic (round 1); Persian, Hungarian, Russian, Malayalam, Punjabi, Spanish-for-Mesoamerican, Byzantine Greek (round 2); Sanskrit/Hindi, Tamil, Ottoman Turkish, Thai, Arabic-*futuwwa* (round 3).

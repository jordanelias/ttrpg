# The Differential Structure of Weapon Typologies
### A cross-tradition reference: every common weapon class as a bundle of trade-offs

---

## 0. Scope, method, and honesty notes

**What this is.** A consolidated, fact-audited treatment of how weapon typologies from the major martial traditions interact, built on one organizing claim: *every optimization is simultaneously a de-optimization.* A weapon and the art around it are tuned to dominate in some set of conditions, and that same tuning de-tunes performance in the complement of those conditions. The treatise that teaches a weapon's strength is, read inversely, the map of its weakness — and the counter usually lives not in a like weapon but in a differently-tuned one whose strength-state coincides with the first's weakness-state.

**Why it is organized by function, not by nation.** The differential is functional. A Japanese katana, an Indian talwar, a Persian shamshir, and a Chinese dao are one typology — the single-edge cutting saber — and they share a weakness-structure regardless of the culture that produced them. Organizing by function (rather than by country) is what makes "every common typology" tractable, and it surfaces the deepest finding in Part IV: independent traditions facing the same physics — armour, reach, the horse, the close, the human hand — converge on the *same* functional weapon classes. That convergence is itself the strongest available answer to "how do typologies relate when they share no common ground."

**Source tiers** (per a strict primary-first hierarchy): **T0** = the thing itself (period treatises, surviving weapons, statutes); **T1** = authoritative synthesis (academic monographs, peer-reviewed journals, forensic/osteoarchaeological studies); **T2** = reputable secondary (named instructor-scholars, ethnographic arms studies); **T3** = tertiary/commercial/enthusiast (encyclopedias, dojo and dealer sites, forums) — used for navigation and uncontested background only, never as the evidentiary basis for a contested claim.

**Four honesty notes that govern everything below.**

1. **Scholarship is wildly asymmetric across traditions.** European HEMA has a corpus of dated technical treatises (Fiore, Liechtenauer's glossators, di Grassi, Silver, Fabris, Capoferro, Thibault) and a peer-reviewed journal (*Acta Periodica Duellatorum*). Most living Asian traditions transmit orally/embodied and have far less peer-reviewed combat-mechanics literature; their popular sources are heavily entangled with performance wushu, lineage marketing, and cinema. So the European entries below rest on T0–T1 evidence; many non-European entries rest on T2–T3 consensus and reasoned mechanics. Confidence is flagged accordingly and is genuinely lower outside Europe — not because those arts are lesser, but because the documentary base is thinner.

2. **Cross-typology outcomes are reconstructed, not attested.** There is essentially no controlled, consequence-bearing data on weapon-versus-weapon fights. The differential is built from doctrine, mechanics, and forensics; it is descriptive, not predictive of a winner.

3. **The framework can slide into the unfalsifiable.** You can always name *some* state in which any weapon loses. The analysis earns its keep only when it specifies *which* states, and *how* an opponent forces them. Entries that merely assert "weak at range X" without a mechanism are doing less work than they appear to.

4. **This privileges the duel and the small-unit fight.** Most of these weapons were *chosen* for contexts — battlefield role, law, what one could carry or afford — in which "who wins a duel" was nearly irrelevant. "Weakness" here is a property of the fight, not a verdict on the weapon's historical fitness.

## Part I — The governing principle

A weapon's properties decide *which* states are strong and weak for it. But two weaknesses are nearly universal, sitting underneath the property-specific ones, and they belong to *states* rather than to features.

**The first universal weakness is commitment and stasis.** Any fully committed action — a delivered cut, an extended thrust, a deep lunge — and any held configuration is, for that instant, readable and unrecoverable, hence punishable, regardless of weapon. The German Liechtenauer-tradition gloss states it flatly: he who lies still in a guard is as good as dead [T0/T1]. The whole art, in every tradition, reduces to *inducing the opponent's commitment* (feint, invitation, provocation, the blade-gain that forces his disengage) while *withholding your own* (keep moving, hold measure, deny him the contact he wants). The property-level differentials below are largely a catalogue of *how* each weapon is forced into commitment.

**The second universal weakness is the hand.** Whatever the weapon, it is held by a hand, and the hand is the nearest, most exposed, and most disabling target — disable it and the weapon is neutralized whatever its typology. Filipino martial arts elevate this to doctrine — *"defanging the snake,"* attacking the weapon-bearing limb rather than trading blows [T2/T3]; George Silver built much of his English method around the undefended hand and the necessity of arming it [T0]. The hand-snipe is the closest thing to a universal counter that crosses every typological boundary, because every typology depends on a hand.

**The method, and its limit.** For each axis and each typology the move is the same: name the property, the state in which it dominates, the complementary state in which it inverts, and the weapon/action class that occupies that complement. The limit (honesty note 3) is that this is only informative when the *forcing mechanism* is specified — how the opponent drags the fight into the weakness-state. Where I cannot specify a mechanism, I say so.

## Part II — The interface axes

These are the dimensions along which any two typologies meet. Each is stated as: strength-state ⇄ shadow weakness-state ⇄ the class that exploits the shadow.

**1. Measure and reach.** The first determinant. Longer reach dominates at distance and collapses past the point; the cliff is sharp. Fiore's measure-grammar (wide play / close play, hinged on the half-sword crossing) is the cleanest model: the shorter weapon's whole task is surviving the wide-play gap to collapse into close play, where handiness and grappling dominate [T0/T2]. A two-handed grip shortens *effective* point-reach even on a longer blade; an over-long blade recovers slowly. Reach is always purchased in time and in close-range helplessness.

**2. Leverage, the bind, and the contact decision** *(refined — this is not a clean binary).* Two hands out-lever one at any crossing; winding exploits sustained contact. But traditions split on whether blade-contact is an *asset* or a *liability*, and they split into *more than two camps*. The German longsword and the Chinese internal weapon arts both treat contact as a sensing organ — German *fühlen* (feeling pressure to act *Indes*) and the CMA "sticking/adhering" (*zhan-nian*) of high-level staff and spear work are both pro-contact sensing systems [T0 / T3]. The Italian dueling rapier treats the same contact as an information leak to be denied: Fabris's reason for finding the opponent's sword *without touching it* is that contact lets the opponent feel your intention and disengage (Capoferro gives the "straddle without touching" instruction; Giganti the "rest lightly on his"; Fabris the full rationale) [T0 via translation]. So the contact-decision is a spectrum with traditions on both poles, and a pro-contact art is starved against an opponent who refuses the bind, while an anti-contact art is read against an opponent who feels pressure faster than line. This axis, more than any other, is the original "no common ground" problem in miniature: the same physical fact (steel on steel) is dominance-enabling in one art and dominance-undermining in another.

**3. Cut geometry — edge vs point, and curve vs straight.** Two sub-axes. *Edge vs point:* the cut is decisive against cloth and flesh and near-useless against plate; the thrust reaches and penetrates but kills by haemorrhage, a process with a time-delay, so it does not reliably *stop* a committed opponent (forensic physiology: the body compensates for blood loss until roughly a third of volume is gone before pressure and consciousness fall — Montgomery & Tipton 2025 [T1]). *Curve vs straight:* a deep curve (shamshir) maximizes the slicing draw-cut, especially mounted where the horse's momentum drives the slice, but sacrifices the thrust; a straight or lightly-curved blade (jian, khanda, talwar's milder curve, arming sword) keeps the point but gives up some slicing authority [T2/T3, ethnographic arms studies]. Point geometry also encodes the *expected armour* of the opponent — broad point for unarmoured (wide wounds, won't stick), slender point for armour (penetrates, but binds and wounds narrowly) (Petri 2019 [T1]).

**4. Mass and percussion vs armour.** Concentrating weight in a head (mace, war-hammer, axe, gada) drives an impact that transmits *through* armour the edge cannot cut — denting helmet and cuirass and incapacitating where a sword glances off — at the cost of precision, speed, and any efficiency against soft, unarmoured targets [T3 consensus; consistent with osteoarchaeology showing axe-clefts as mass-driven damage vs the clean incision of a sword — Giuffra et al. 2013, T1]. Percussion is the armour-defeat answer by force; the slender point (estoc, katar, rondel) is the armour-defeat answer by precision into gaps. Both surrender the cut's versatility against the unarmoured.

**5. Recovery, commitment, and tempo.** Power and reach are bought with slow acceleration and slow redirection. The heavy two-hander, the deep lunge, the cocked guard are all maximal-force/maximal-reach states purchased with a fixed, readable, unrecoverable instant. Every tradition has a grammar of the windows this opens — Silver's "true times" (the hand commits before the body before the foot), the Italian four tempi (*primo, dui, mezzo, contratempo*), the German *Indes* striking the opening the bind reveals [T0/T1]. The recursive counter-ladders (Italian *cavazione / contracavazione / ricavazione*; German *Stück / Bruch*) are the same idea: each committed action opens its own breaker.

**6. Hand exposure and "defanging the snake."** (See Part I.) The complex hilt buys hand security at the cost of mass and point-speed; the simple cross or the bare grip buys speed and reach but offers the nearest opening. The pata (gauntlet-sword) is the limit case — the whole forearm armoured into the weapon, at the price of *all* wrist articulation [T3]. The hand-attack is the near-universal counter precisely because the axis is universal.

**7. Skill floor vs skill ceiling** *(new axis, cross-culturally attested).* A weapon tuned for rapid mass-training has a low skill floor and a low ceiling: it makes a competent soldier fast but offers a master little extra. A high-ceiling weapon rewards a master enormously but is dangerous in untrained hands. The Chinese training-time sayings encode exactly this — the dao (saber) "takes ten days," the qiang (spear) and jian (straight sword) far longer — explicitly as a *mnemonic for difficulty, not a literal claim* [T3, and the sources themselves say it is non-literal]. The European parallel is the messer/dussack as the "common" training weapon versus the longsword and rapier as high-ceiling arts, and Silver's note that the soldier differs not by greater skill but by readiness in every occasion [T0]. The pike, too, is a low-floor weapon — its strength is that it makes a drilled block formidable cheaply, which is the same fact as its uselessness in a skilled individual's hands.

**8. Coordination, formation, and terrain.** A formation weapon dominates in cohesion, on flat ground, in frontal engagement, and collapses in disorder, on the flank, in broken or enclosed terrain. The pike block and the sarissa phalanx are unbreakable from the front and lethal to whatever loses cohesion against them — including themselves the moment cohesion fails. The weakness is never frontal; it is the gap, the flank, the rough ground, the press.

**9. The primary-plus-sidearm law** *(a near-universal consequence of the reach axis).* Because every weapon has a measure outside which it fails, almost every reach or primary weapon presupposes a shorter backup, and the historical record shows this everywhere: the pikeman's sword and dagger, the samurai's *daishō* worn behind the *yari*, the Chinese doctrine of a chain or whip as backup if the primary is lost, the cavalryman's saber drawn after the lance. The corollary is that the close — and ultimately the empty hand — is the terminus to which nearly every weapon's weakness-collapse runs.

## Part III — The functional typologies

For each: function and dominance-state; cross-cultural exemplars; the shadow weakness-state; the counter-class; confidence.

### 1. Two-handed cut-and-thrust sword
*Function:* maximal versatility from a long, two-handed, dual-purpose blade — cut, thrust, half-sword, pommel-strike, and leverage at the bind. *Exemplars:* European longsword; Japanese ōdachi/nodachi; Chinese miaodao/changdao (two-handed saber). *Dominance-state:* the open duel at middle measure, where its length, leverage, and the bind-and-wind game (or its cutting arcs) prevail. *Shadow:* two hands mean no shield and no off-hand for grappling-control; the two-handed grip shortens effective point-reach; in the press and the grapple its length is a liability; against plate the cut is deleted (it must half-sword). *Counter-class:* the point-controller who refuses the bind and snipes the hands; the closer/wrestler; in armour, the thrust-and-grapple. *Confidence:* high (European); medium for the East-Asian exemplars.

### 2. One-handed straight cut-and-thrust sword (the finesse blade)
*Function:* balanced cut-and-thrust in one hand, finesse over power, usually a double edge and a point. *Exemplars:* European arming sword and Bolognese sidesword; Chinese jian ("gentleman of weapons"); Indian khanda (broader, force-leaning). *Dominance-state:* the versatile middle — competent at every range a one-hander reaches, master of none. *Shadow:* the compromise — out-reached by spears and rapiers, out-powered by sabers and two-handers, and demanding a high skill ceiling to extract its versatility (the jian's reputation as the hardest to master). *Counter-class:* the specialist who forces the fight into its own optimized range. Usually paired with a shield (see §13) precisely to cover the defensive gap a single one-hander leaves. *Confidence:* high (Europe); medium (jian, khanda).

### 3. Single-edge cutting saber
*Function:* the cut — decisive, intuitive, fast to train, often curved for slicing. *Exemplars:* Chinese dao ("general of weapons," the foot-soldier's first blade); Japanese katana/uchigatana; Indian talwar; Persian shamshir; Ottoman kilij; European backsword/sabre. *Dominance-state:* the cut against the unarmoured, especially mounted (the draw-cut riding the horse's momentum); a low skill floor makes it the mass cavalry and infantry side-blade across Eurasia. *Shadow:* a deep curve sacrifices the thrust (the shamshir barely thrusts); a secure hilt that enforces the draw-cut can preclude other cuts (the talwar's disc-pommel "presses into the wrist" if used like a conventional saber — strength and constraint are the same feature [T2/T3]); against plate the cut glances off. **Facticity correction:** the katana was a battlefield *sidearm*, not the primary weapon — yari (spear), yumi (bow), and the tanegashima matchlock were the main battlefield arms; the katana's "dominance" is largely a peacetime, dueling, and cinematic construct [T3 consensus, consistent across sources]. *Counter-class:* the thrust (out-reaches and out-times the cut's longer path — Silver's argument); plate (deletes the edge); the hand-snipe. *Confidence:* high on the cut/curve mechanics; the katana de-mythologizing is well-supported.

### 4. Thrust-centric civilian sword
*Function:* reach and speed down a controlled line via the point, in single time. *Exemplars:* European rapier and its descendant the smallsword. *Dominance-state:* the unarmoured one-on-one duel at long measure, controlling the line and thrusting in opposition. *Shadow:* the thrust commits the line and the body; a slim thrust-optimized blade loses every leverage contest at contact and has nothing in the grapple; the thrust kills slowly (haemorrhage), so it may not stop a committed attacker before he reaches you. *Counter-class:* anyone who closes — the grappler, the dagger, the bind-fighter; and the decisive cut. *Armoured cousin:* the **estoc/tuck** — a stiff, often edgeless thrusting sword, "a two-handed spear-point," built to half-sword into armour-gaps; it concedes reach badly and is a poor short sword, but it wins the armoured close the cut cannot. *Confidence:* high.

### 5. Dagger and short blade
*Function:* the close — grappling-range thrust, armour-gap penetration, control and finishing. *Exemplars:* European rondel; Japanese tantō; Indian katar (punch-dagger, fist enclosed in an H-grip channeling body weight into an armour-piercing thrust); Filipino balisong and karambit; Arab/Persian jambiya. *Dominance-state:* contact range and the grapple, where longer weapons are nullified — the bottom rung of the armoured reach-ladder (visor, armpit, groin). *Shadow:* almost no reach; outside grappling measure it is helpless. *Counter-class:* any weapon that keeps the measure open; and the reverse — the dagger is itself the counter to *every* long weapon once the measure collapses, which is why it is the universal sidearm. *Confidence:* high (the katar's specifics, medium).

### 6. Maneuverable spear (reach-thrust, individual)
*Function:* reach plus a fast, simple, powerful thrust, retaining individual agility. *Exemplars:* the one- or two-handed European spear; Japanese yari (the actual Sengoku battlefield backbone — it outnumbered the katana); Chinese qiang ("king of weapons," prized for individual skill, with a deliberately flexible wax-wood shaft whose whip adds thrust deception); Indian bhaala. *Dominance-state:* open ground at thrust-measure, where the first thrust imperils even a more sophisticated weapon and a single thrust can kill (Fiore's spear doctrine: thrust first [T0/T2]). *Shadow:* "very limited control" once its point is passed; the wielder must beat-and-close or be closed upon; no edge for the bind, the haft can be cut. *Counter-class:* the weapon that survives the gap and collapses to grappling pins; the flexible weapon that *traps the shaft* (the kusarigama was developed expressly to ensnare a spear or naginata and close [T3]). *Confidence:* high on the function; medium on exemplar specifics.

### 7. Mass-reach pike (formation weapon)
*Function:* pure reach and frontal density, presented by a drilled block — the extreme of the reach axis, with individual utility sacrificed entirely. *Exemplars:* the European/Swiss/Landsknecht pike; the Macedonian sarissa; the Japanese nagae-yari. *Dominance-state:* the cohesive frontal wall, especially against cavalry, on flat ground. *Shadow:* "useless in close combat" without formation; at "push of pike," when the points pass too close to be useful, men drop them and draw swords [T1, via Smythe and Delbrück]; vulnerable to flank, terrain, and disorder — the same fact as the phalanx undone at Cynoscephalae and Pydna. The shaft is choppable (hence the metal langets near the head, a designed mitigation of a known weakness [T2]). *Counter-class:* the halberd and sword-and-dagger of the press; cavalry on the flank; shot; rough ground. *Confidence:* high (this is the best-documented case of the differential at scale).

### 8. Bladed and hooked polearm
*Function:* reach plus leverage plus versatility — a cutting/thrusting head with the mass and the hook of a pole, usable at close-to-middle range with an adjustable grip. *Exemplars:* European halberd, poleaxe, bill, glaive, partisan; Japanese naginata ("mowing sword," the sweeping-arc glaive, weapon of the sōhei and onna-musha); Chinese guandao/yanyuedao (with a back-spike and sometimes a notch to *catch* weapons) and pudao ("horse-cutter"). *Dominance-state:* the close-to-middle range a pike cannot fight in — the Swiss regarded the halberd as the battle-winner — combining cut, thrust, hook (to pull down a rider or a shield), and the haft as a lever. *Shadow:* weight and slow recovery; the very-close (inside the head) leaves the wielder on the haft; against a faster short weapon that gets inside, it is overcommitted. *Counter-class:* the short, fast weapon that closes inside the head; the bind that controls the heavy lever. *Confidence:* high (Europe); medium (naginata, guandao).

### 9. Staff
*Function:* reach, speed, ambidexterity, and the most forgiving low skill floor of any long weapon — but blunt. *Exemplars:* European quarterstaff; Chinese gun ("grandfather of all weapons," the base of all long-weapon training, a Shaolin specialty); Japanese bō and jō; Indian lathi. *Dominance-state:* the unarmoured civilian fight at middle measure, where its two-ended speed, thrusts, and sweeps and its "sticking" sensitivity at contact (CMA) prevail. *Shadow:* no edge and no point-penetration — limited against armour and slower to incapacitate than a cutting or thrusting weapon; the wooden shaft can be cut or notched by a blade. *Counter-class:* the edged weapon that cuts the shaft or out-wounds the blunt strike; armour. *Confidence:* medium-high.

### 10. Percussion and concussion
*Function:* defeat armour by transmitted impact, and deliver overwhelming force to a small area. *Exemplars:* mace/war-hammer (Europe); Indian gada/gurz (spherical, flanged, or spiked); axe — European one-hand axe and two-handed Dane axe, Chinese fu, Indian tabar/farsa; Japanese kanabō; the flail. *Dominance-state:* against the armoured opponent (denting helmet and cuirass, incapacitating where a sword glances off), and in the post-cavalry-clash melee and at grappling range "where a sword is too constrained" [T3, consistent with osteoarchaeology, T1]. The axe concentrates cleaving power on a small target, "more effective against armour" but "much less precision than a sword." *Shadow:* slow, deeply committed, poor recovery; no edge for efficient wounding of the unarmoured; the haft; the back-swing is a long readable tempo. The flail adds unpredictability at the cost of the wielder's own control. *Counter-class:* the faster, more recoverable weapon that strikes in the back-swing tempo; the point that reaches first. *Confidence:* medium-high on the anti-armour logic.

### 11. Flexible weapon
*Function:* an unpredictable, hard-to-parry trajectory, surprise reach, and entanglement — a moving mass or blade on a chain, rope, or whip. *Exemplars:* Indian urumi (whip-sword); Chinese chain whip / nine-section whip, rope dart, meteor hammer; Japanese kusarigama (sickle-and-chain) and the nunchaku/Filipino tabak-toyok. *Dominance-state:* open space against a single opponent or several, where the wide unpredictable arc denies an area, is hard to read or block, and can *trap a longer weapon's shaft* and drag its wielder into the close (the kusarigama's express purpose against spear and naginata). *Shadow:* a brutal weakness-profile — slow to recover and redirect, dangerous to the wielder and to allies (the urumi was "rarely used in actual battle... you could easily strike your own allies"; it was largely ceremonial and personal-defence [T2/T3]), useless in formation or enclosed space, no real defence, and helpless once the flexible element is grabbed or the measure is closed. *Counter-class:* close inside the arc and grab the chain/blade, or stay in tight terrain where it cannot wind; armour against the lighter strikes. *Confidence:* medium; the de-romanticizing of the urumi is well-supported by the sources themselves.

### 12. Dual-wield / paired weapons
*Function:* two offensive lines, simultaneous offence-and-defence, and the deception of two threats. *Exemplars:* European rapier-and-dagger and sword-and-dagger; the Spanish montante is *not* this (it is a true two-hander); Filipino sinawali (double stick) and espada y daga; Chinese hudiedao (butterfly swords) and shuang dao (double saber). *Dominance-state:* the duel where the off-weapon parries or controls while the main weapon strikes, and the attacker's own shoulders limit him to one line at a time (Thibault's exploit against rapier-and-dagger: advance to the lead-shoulder side so he cannot bring both to bear [T0/T2]). *Shadow:* divided attention; no single committed power; the off-weapon is short, so the pairing still loses the reach contest; it demands high coordination. *Counter-class:* the longer single weapon that stays outside the short off-weapon's range; the overwhelming single committed line that one short parry cannot stop. *Confidence:* medium-high.

### 13. Shield
*Function:* mobile cover that is also a weapon — bind, bash, hook, trap, and vision-management. *Exemplars:* European buckler, targe, heater; Roman scutum; the round shield across Viking, Greek (aspis), and many cultures; the pavise (static). *Dominance-state:* paired with a one-handed weapon, it closes the defensive gap that makes the lone one-hander vulnerable, and the boss/rim is itself offensive at the close. *Shadow:* weight and fatigue; a large shield blinds the bearer's own vision (Silver's specific objection to the buckler-blinded fight [T0]); it can be bypassed by angle, pinned, or rendered moot at half-sword/grappling measure. *Counter-class:* the angle that goes around it; the half-sword or grapple that gets inside it; the two-hander or polearm whose leverage and reach a one-handed weapon-and-shield cannot match. *Confidence:* high (Europe); the cross-cultural shield, high in outline.

### 14. Ranged / projectile
*Function:* reach absolute — strike before the enemy can close. *Exemplars:* bow (yumi, English longbow, composite recurve), crossbow, sling, the matchlock/arquebus/musket, thrown weapons (javelin, francisca, shuriken). *Dominance-state:* distance, where it inflicts casualties before contact — the dominant battlefield arm of the late period (the Mughal matchlock, the pike-and-shot system). *Shadow:* no defence at the close; ammunition and (for firearms and crossbows) a reload window in which the user is "essentially defenceless" — the structural reason musketeers were screened by pikes; the bow's dependence on trained strength and the elements. *Counter-class:* anything that closes the distance or catches the user mid-reload (cavalry on shot-in-the-open); cover; armour against the lighter missiles. *Confidence:* high.

### 15. The empty hand and grappling
*Function:* the universal substrate — the layer to which nearly every weapon's weakness-collapse runs, and the base from which most weapon arts are built (FMA derives the empty hand from weapon mechanics; the European, Japanese, and Chinese traditions all carry wrestling — *ringen*, jūjutsu, *shuai jiao* — as the foundation). *Dominance-state:* the absolute close, inside the measure of every weapon, where leverage, throws, locks, and disarms operate. *Shadow:* no reach and (against a blade) no margin for error; armed and at distance, the empty hand is at maximal disadvantage. *Counter-class:* any weapon, at any range above contact. But the empty hand is the terminus that the differential keeps pointing to: control the measure and you control the weapon; lose the measure to the close and even the empty hand can win. *Confidence:* high.

## Part IV — Synthesis

**Lay the axes and typologies side by side and the original question answers itself structurally.** A weapon's weakness-state is, by definition, where a differently-optimized weapon's strength-state lives. The pike's shadow (the close) is the halberd's and the sword-and-dagger's home. The cut's shadow (armour) is the estoc's, the katar's, and the mace's home. The rapier's shadow (the bind, the close, the grapple) is the German bind-fighter's, the dagger's, and the wrestler's home. The spear's shadow (its passed point) is the kusarigama's and the grappler's home. The bind-feeler's shadow (an opponent who refuses contact) is the point-controller's home. So a typological mismatch is not two systems failing to communicate; it is two weapons *each trying to drag the encounter into its own strength-state* — which is precisely the other's weakness-state — and the fight is decided by who imposes the regime. The techniques are downstream of that imposition.

**The deepest finding is the convergence.** Independent traditions, given the same physics — the reach of a shaft, the leverage of two hands, the curve that trades thrust for slice, the mass that beats armour, the point that pierces a gap, the hand that holds everything, the close that nullifies reach — arrive at the *same functional weapon classes* and, strikingly, at the *same master variables*: reach, tempo, the contact decision, and the hand, with armour as the great re-orderer that can flip every ranking by deleting the edge and forcing the fight down the reach-ladder toward the dagger and the grapple. This is why a Persian saber, a Japanese sword, and a Chinese dao are recognizably one thing, and why a master of one could read another quickly: they are local solutions to a shared problem, not incommensurable worlds. "No common ground" turns out to be the wrong frame — the common ground is the physics, and the traditions are dialects of it.

**And beneath the properties sit the two universal weaknesses of Part I.** Whatever the weapon, commitment and stasis are exploitable, and the hand is the nearest target. Every tradition's highest counsel is therefore some version of *never be caught fixed*, and the one counter that crosses every typological boundary is the attack on the limb that holds the weapon. The weapon's properties set which states are strong and weak for it; the universal game is played on top, in the currency of who commits first and who can reach the other's hand.

**Limits, restated honestly.** This is descriptive, not predictive — it explains the structure of advantage, not the outcome of a fight, which turns on length parity, skill, terrain, armour, and luck. It is built largely from European treatises (T0–T1) and from much thinner, marketing-tinged sourcing for the living Asian traditions (T2–T3); the confidence flags are real and the asymmetry is not a judgment on those arts but on their documentary base. The framework risks unfalsifiability and is only worth its weight where the *forcing mechanism* is named. And the whole analysis is a duel-and-skirmish abstraction from weapons that were mostly chosen for contexts in which winning a duel was beside the point. With those caveats, the differential is the most defensible single structure the combined record — treatises, surviving weapons, biomechanics, forensics, osteoarchaeology, and honestly-labelled reconstruction — will support for how weapon typologies interact.

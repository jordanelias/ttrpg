# Narrative Emergence + NERS Review — every proposal tested by the pr119_governance harness cluster

## Status: READ-ONLY REVIEW — NOT canon; does not ratify anything (not a ratifiable proposal). [reclassified 2026-07-15, proposal-reconciliation pass]

This document performs a **max-effort, per-proposal design review** over all 19 `tools/sim_harness/adapters/pr119_governance/` adapters — every proposal, cross-cutting finding, and composite system tested in `designs/audit/2026-07-12-pr119-harness-verification/README.md`. For each of 29 line items it gives three things:

1. **Narrative Emergence Review** — a grounded narrative account of how the mechanic actually plays out, tied explicitly to the real simulated branch-distribution numbers (not disconnected flavor text) and to Goldenfurt's real NPCs (Hedda Vorn, Orsk Tallow, Konrad Ems, Wessel, Greta, Tomas) where the mechanic touches them.
2. **NERS Audit** — the repo's canonical **Pessimist Subtractive NERS** methodology, applied rigorously:
   - **N — Necessity**: does it model a real, load-bearing dynamic? (N-fail = fantasy imposition / duplicate coverage / edge-case / abstractable)
   - **Ω — Intent**: Ω-a cross-scale consequence; Ω-b personal-scale transformation; Ω-c autonomous firing regardless of player action; **Ω-d non-dominance** ("every action pays what it buys" — the most common CUT trigger)
   - **Q — Quality**: Q-robust (3+ viable approaches, visible world-state change, passes the dramatic-legibility test); Q-smooth (composes without special-casing); Q-elegant (restatable after one reading)
   - **CARDINAL RULE**: *"judge as-if-built, never by build state"* — a verdict citing stub-ness/unwired-ness/unbuilt status as a reason is invalid by construction
   - **Inverted-critic verification**: every negative verdict below was reached only after a genuine attempt to steelman the proposal first
   - **Verdicts**: KEEP / CUT / PRUNE / MERGE / DISTILL / REFINE / RECONCILE (dormant/inert built-code collision — adds a constraint, doesn't overturn)
   - Where a proposal already carries a NERS verdict from `designs/audit/2026-07-12-governance-compendium/tier3_proposal_status_closure.md` §3.2a/b, this review independently re-derives a verdict from the **actual simulated data** (which the compendium's own judge never had) and states explicitly whether the finding **CONFIRMS**, **SHARPENS**, or **CHALLENGES** the existing call.
3. **What it did for the game** — a concise, designer-facing statement of the proposal's actual contribution to Valoria as a played game.

Methodology source: `designs/audit/2026-07-08-pessimist-action-audit/00_grounding/00_charter.md`. ### 1. §1.3a Locked Extraction Figures — the Compact "fifth family" (ED-SE-0018/19)

**Narrative Emergence Review**

Season three at Goldenfurt: Orsk Tallow has been squeezed by two consecutive Extract Directives, and rather than eat a third he pushes **Negotiate Quota** — a mass social contest against the town's Local Actors — to lock in an **Encabezamiento**. He wins it. The governor writes a **Compact** tag to the settlement ledger: a fixed Treasury figure for six seasons, Hedda Vorn's court held hostage to one guaranteed Petition per term. Mechanically, this call passes through `Settlement.add_tag()` into `ledger.ledger_add()` exactly like every other governance verb — and that function has no kind whitelist. It doesn't reject "Compact." It doesn't flag it as new. It just appends the tag and moves on. The adapter ran this exact write against the real `ledger.py` a hundred times; a hundred times the unrecognized kind slid in silently (`silently_added_unrecognized` 100/100). Nothing downstream — no sweep, no Directive auto-resolution, no "fires every season of its term" logic — actually knows what a Compact is, because that logic was never authored against a sixth kind; only `Debt`'s once-and-done fire semantics exist. So the tag Orsk fought a social contest to win sits on Goldenfurt's ledger as an inert curio: present, silently accepted, and functionally mute. The governance system doesn't error. It just quietly stops meaning what the prose promised.

**NERS Audit**

*Steelman first:* Compact is not make-believe complexity — a recurring, fixed-term extraction obligation that fires every season and grants the counterparty a standing Petition right is genuinely different in shape from a one-shot Debt, and the historical grounding (encabezamiento, 1534 Castile) is load-bearing, not decorative. One could argue it deserves first-class status precisely so future authors and any player-facing UI can name it directly rather than burying it in a generic Debt list.

That argument doesn't survive contact with the live check. **N** passes for the *underlying mechanic* (a locked, decoupled extraction figure is a real, load-bearing political dynamic) but fails for the *implementation choice*: a bespoke sixth `TAG_KIND` duplicates coverage `Debt` already has (both are `LedgerTag`s with `ttl`/`created_season`; the only missing piece is a recurrence flag), which is exactly `governance_consolidation_v1.md` §1 D3's call. **Q-smooth** is the sharper failure: because `ledger_add()` gates on nothing, minting Compact as written doesn't compose — it silently creates a tag no other code path recognizes, which is worse than an error (an error would at least surface the gap; this corrupts the schema invisibly, 100/100 on the actual write path). **Verdict: MERGE** — fold Compact into `Debt(key="compact:<quota>", ttl=term, recurs=True)`, exactly D3's recommendation, and reuse existing `ttl` machinery rather than mint a new kind.

This **CONFIRMS** D3 and the compendium's HAB-5 "BREAK on failure-mode" flag, but **SHARPENS** both: the compendium reasoned from reading `ledger.py`'s docstring and prose ("the implementation's 5th is Leverage"); this adapter is the first to actually execute `ledger_add()` and observe that the failure mode isn't a miscounted family in a doc — it's a live, unguarded write path that would corrupt the schema silently and leave the promised recurring-fire behavior unimplemented, a materially worse outcome than the prose-level diagnosis anticipated.

**What it did for the game**

It converts D3 from "a naming/counting correction, fix in the next prose pass" into "a schema-integrity fix with zero deadline slack" — `ledger_add()` needs either a kind whitelist or the Compact-as-Debt-subtype resolution landed before any PR#119 content ships, or every Compact negotiated in play becomes a silent no-op the moment it's written.

Run the cascade for six seasons at a 0.35/season/cell infraction rate: in 100 trials, 7 never stack, 61 build partial tension, and 32 hit the third stack and auto-seed "Cell Revolt." That's not a rare edge case — it's a one-in-three outcome inside a single mid-length tenure.

**NERS Audit**

*Steelman:* the mechanic is well-grounded (Hideyoshi's 1597 edict, generalized *goningumi*), it's Ω-c autonomous (any bound actor's infraction fires it independent of player action), and the 7/61/32 split is a real, playable spread — not degenerate, not a coin-flip that always lands the same way. That's meaningfully different from a mechanic that never fires or always fires.

But **Q-smooth** collides with a real substrate gap: "five-household cells" names a population granularity (households) the engine doesn't track at all. Goldenfurt models nine named actors, not a household census, and `§4.5`'s "1–2 Local Actors" figure is a *per-settlement-type count*, a third, different granularity again. The adapter's 9-mod-5 arithmetic operationalizes this precisely: even the most charitable reading (bind the named actors themselves) leaves a structurally awkward remainder that includes actors — like Konrad Ems, the Crown's own bailiff — who have no business being community-bound at all. **Verdict: REFINE**, not the compendium's harder MERGE-into-Force-flavor-text call. The 7/61/32 cascade is real, working, dramatically legible content (Q-robust passes cleanly); throwing it away as flavor text discards a functioning mechanic to solve a data-layer problem. The fix is scope, not demotion: bind named actors directly (the only granularity that actually exists in code), and give the remainder an explicit "uncelled, individually liable" status instead of pretending the partition is clean.

This **CONFIRMS** the granularity-collision diagnosis both existing docs already flag, **SHARPENS** `governance_consolidation_v1.md`'s softer "REFINE-THEN-RATIFY... scope Collective-Liability to named-actor factions" path with concrete numbers, and **CHALLENGES** `tier3_proposal_status_closure.md`'s harder MERGE verdict as premature given how non-degenerate the simulated cascade turned out to be.

**What it did for the game**

It resolves the standing "MERGE vs REFINE" split between the two compendium docs with actual data: the mechanic survives on its own numbers, so the fix is "define cells over named actors with an explicit remainder rule," not "retire it."

### 3. The Pi (pressure) homeostat — `restore_toward(3)`, the CG-1 fix

**Narrative Emergence Review**

Goldenfurt opens, per its own worked example, at Pi = 4 — comfortably inside the intended 0–10 dramatic band, one tick above the target of 3. Run twenty seasons (five year-arcs) with realistic churn: Hedda's grievance, Orsk's hoarding, Wessel's denunciations, Greta's cell-export, Konrad's suspicion reports, and Tomas's river economy each contribute their own pressure whenever they're active, offset only by whatever the governor manages to release that season. `restore_toward(3)` is supposed to be the valve — pull down hard when Pi runs high, same as it now correctly pulls up when Pi stalls low (the actual CG-1 fix over the old, mis-signed `decay_toward(3)`). But the valve is capped: `sign(3−Pi) * min(1, |3−Pi|)` can never move Pi by more than 1 in either direction, however far off-band it drifts or however fast it's drifting. Across 500 seeded trials from the real Pi=4 start, every single one ends pinned in `runaway_high` (Pi > 6). By mid-tenure the deck is drawing near its 4-card ceiling every season, biased hard toward Crisis, and every one of Goldenfurt's six ambitions and the Konrad suspicion-track are compounding simultaneously — not because the governor played badly, but because the restoring force literally cannot outrun churn once net seasonal pressure exceeds roughly 1.

**NERS Audit**

*Steelman:* CG-1 is a genuine, cheap, deterministic bidirectional fix over its predecessor, and it demonstrably solves the *opposite* failure mode — a quiet town no longer decays toward Pi=0 forever, which is a real, valuable, independent win. The tested input rates are explicit test-scenario proxies, not canon-derived, and real player agency (Comply/Bargain/Hold Court can retire a specific Need card outright, not just subtract a flat "release") might churn less than this adapter's stochastic proxy assumed once S4's deck engine is actually built.

That rescue attempt fails on its own terms. The same qualitative shape — a one-way pin near a boundary — was independently reconfirmed three more times: once with real Grudge-tag accumulation instead of a proxy (`pr119_integrated_campaign.py`, 1500 trials), once inside a real card-deck engine (`pr119_event_deck_engine.py`), and it matches what two *prior*, fully independent methodologies (PR#125's LLM-narrative sim, the 500-seed `settlement_mgmt_stress_01` batch) already found under the pre-CG-1 formula. Four convergent measurements, at least two using non-proxied real mechanics, all hit the same structural wall: a fixed ±1/season cap cannot arrest churn once its expectation crosses ~1, regardless of what generates that churn. This is an **Ω-d dominance failure inverted onto the system side** — not the player getting a free win, but the formula dominating the player: no combination of engaged, skillful play (releasing pressure at the modeled ceiling) changes the outcome once churn crosses the threshold, which directly violates the "every action pays what it buys" symmetry the homeostat is designed to embody. It also fails **Q-robust**'s player-diversity test: outcomes should diverge by strategy; here they converge on the ceiling regardless of strategy. **Verdict: REFINE** — the bidirectional-restore concept is necessary and correctly signed; its magnitude needs to scale with distance-from-band (or gain an explicit high-Pi circuit breaker) rather than being capped at a flat 1.

This directly **CHALLENGES** `governance_consolidation_v1.md` §3 E1's framing, which calls porting CG-1 "the single highest-value move… already designed… No new design — a port" that "de-fangs Pattern A/B/G at once." The data shows CG-1 fixes the anti-stall bug but does not de-fang the runaway pattern under realistic-to-high churn — the same death-spiral persists, now pinned at the ceiling instead of the floor. E1 cannot be treated as closed-once-ported; E3 (subsistence-floor clamp) and E7 (a positive-feedback path) are not optional follow-on polish, they are load-bearing co-requisites of E1's own stated goal.

**What it did for the game**

It overturns E1's "no new design, just port it" premise with four convergent, independently-methoded data points: the pressure homeostat as currently specified will pin nearly every settlement into permanent crisis mode regardless of player skill, so shipping CG-1 alone would not fix Goldenfurt's death-spiral — it would just move where the spiral parks.

### `pr119_event_deck_engine.py` — the real card-based event-deck engine

**Narrative Emergence Review**

Now give the same thousand governors real cards instead of synthetic draws — Goldenfurt's actual Petition/Friction/Opportunity/Crisis/Intrigue/Ambition/Thread deck, the structure players will actually face. At `governance_skill=0.0`, 82% of towns end ruined and 18% hold steady on luck alone; nothing prospers. Bad decisions compound because more cards draw exactly when pressure is already high (`1 + floor(Π/3)`), front-loading Crisis onto a town already in trouble, while the neglect-decay term quietly bleeds Prosperity and Order every season nobody actively shores them up. At `0.5`, a crack of light opens — 2% prosper, 82% hold steady, 16% still ruin — because even a coin-flip governor sometimes draws a run of Crisis cards with no good response (G401's Force/Concede both cost something) and sometimes strings together lucky Opportunity draws. By `0.8` the population has flipped: 65% prosper, ruin has vanished. At `1.0`, every trial prospers — not because within-run variance disappears (a perfect governor still faces real per-season card luck) but because across 300 trials that variance always resolves upward once every response choice is optimal. The felt shape is a smooth S-curve, not a cliff: skill decides which basin of attraction a town's luck gets filtered into, and every interior skill level genuinely produces all three outcomes. This world also *feels* different from the integrated campaign's: recall cascades effectively vanish here (`collapsed_repeated_recalls` never fires even out to 30 seasons), because Konrad's clock only advances on a specific card draw rather than on every non-compliant season — governors mostly survive to see their prosperity or ruin play out, rather than being removed from office before that story resolves.

**NERS Audit**

Orsk Tallow, the Grainmaster angling for his perpetual charter, immediately starts triangulating: does he petition Hedda, who still controls Develop and might grant the charter as a conciliatory gesture, or Rathmann, who has no local relationship debt to him and might rule on the merits? Most seasons, nothing happens: Rathmann holds the writ and never exercises it, a silent second key that expires unused (this is the modeled majority — 65 of 100 trials resolve `term_expired_no_override`). But in the season the override *does* fire (35/100), Rathmann invokes Investigate on the smuggling ring Hedda has been quietly tolerating to keep Tomas's family off her back — a decision Hedda herself would never have made, because she has to live in Goldenfurt after Rathmann's term ends and Rathmann doesn't. The Precedent tag it leaves behind (`corregidor-overlay-active`, ttl=4) expires on schedule regardless of which branch fired; Rathmann packs up and leaves either way, and Hedda is still governor, now visibly marked by a term she didn't choose and can't fully control.

**NERS Audit**

*N — Necessity.* Real and load-bearing: this is, per the closure register's own framing, the first mechanic that actually mechanizes §1.4's suspicion-threshold "audit" stub — a gap that has sat as a bare noun ("threshold → recall, audit, or replacement") with no built resolver. It is not duplicate coverage of IT-1 (overlay, not replacement) or of SE-7's Visita/Residencia/Rotation family (a different instrument in the same S-2 toolkit); it survives the N-fail checks for fantasy imposition and abstractability — dual-authority oversight without full removal is a documented pattern of imperial administration, not an invented flourish.

*Ω.* (a) Cross-scale: yes, but thin in the non-firing branch — a ledger tag write with no other-scale reach unless the override actually fires. (b) Personal-scale: yes — it forces Hedda into a defensive posture toward a rival authority sharing her office, changing how she plays every other verb during the term. (c) Autonomous firing: yes, the 30%-rate override check is not player-gated. (d) Non-dominance: the governor pays immediately and structurally — two authority verbs are gone for four seasons, full stop, regardless of whether the override ever fires. That is a real, upfront, symmetric cost.

*Inverted-critic steelman before verdict:* one could argue the 65% no-fire branch is dead weight — a tag that does nothing but expire. Steelmanned: even unfired, the overlay changes local actors' calculus (Q-robust's "visible world-state change" needn't mean a firing event; a standing threat that's *known* to exist is itself the change, per real audit/inspector-general dynamics where deterrence is the point, not detection). That argument holds only if the design explicitly states an ambient/passive effect for the dormant term — the adapter and source doc do not currently specify one.

*Q.* Q-robust passes on multiple approaches (placate, route around the covered verbs, race to get Rathmann recalled) and dramatic legibility (Hedda's authority at risk, Almud wants leverage, inaction = quiet expiry). Q-elegant passes: "an outsider can override you on two named verbs, for a set term, then it lapses" restates cleanly in one read.

**Verdict: KEEP.** This **CONFIRMS** the closure register's §3.2b "Promote" disposition, and **SHARPENS** it: the 65/35 split makes concrete a gap the prose framing didn't surface — the passive (non-firing) majority branch needs an explicit ambient-deterrence effect authored into the mechanic, or two-thirds of its runtime is inert by the mechanic's own measured shape. Term length (4 seasons) and override-fire rate (0.3) remain test-scenario values, not canon-derived — flag for authoring, not a verdict blocker.

**What it did for the game**

Turned §1.4's decade-old "audit" stub from a bare noun into a working, minimal mechanic: a scoped ledger tag with a defined expiry, no new state machinery required. The measured 65/35 split gives designers a concrete number to design against instead of an assumption — specifically, it surfaces that the dormant branch needs its own stated effect before this ships, since "mostly nothing happens" is now a fact, not a guess.

**Narrative Emergence Review**

But Reinholt isn't accountable to Goldenfurt — he's accountable to whoever sent him, and Goldenfurt has no lever over that relationship at all. Come term's end, the Sindacato audits his record. Most of the time (69/100) it's clean, and Reinholt rotates out having done his job. **NERS Audit**

*N — Necessity.* Real: contracted outsider governors immune to local kin/patronage networks are a documented Renaissance city-state pattern (the historical *podestà* institution this proposal is named for exists precisely to defeat local capture). Not duplicate coverage of ordinary succession — the adapter deliberately reuses the identical `succeed_governor()` call `pr119_integrated_campaign.py`/`pr119_event_deck_engine.py` already exercise for routine turnover, and the appointer-liability clause is the genuinely new element layered on top of that shared substrate.

*Ω.* (a) Cross-scale: the strongest Ω-a case in this cluster — malfeasance liability is explicitly redirected from the settlement's Legitimacy to the *appointing faction's* Legitimacy, a deliberate cross-scale, cross-actor consequence transfer, not an incidental one. (b) Personal-scale: thinner than HAB-1 — immunity to internal faction rolls means Reinholt is, by design, insulated from the personal-scale texture (Disposition-driven petitions, grudges) that normally animates a governor's term; the only personal-scale beat is the term-end malfeasance resolution itself. (c) Autonomous firing: yes, the malfeasance check fires at term end independent of player input. (d) Non-dominance — **this is the question the task poses directly, and it deserves a genuine weighing, not a rubber stamp.**

*Weighing Ω-d.* The settlement gains something immediate and unconditional: for the entire term, its faction-internal pressure mechanics (Guild leverage, kin leverage, Disposition-built favors) simply don't reach the governor. That benefit is paid for by the settlement in zero of the 100 trials. The cost — the only cost anywhere in this mechanic — is a probabilistic (25% modeled), deferred (term-end only), and displaced (falls on the appointing faction, not the settlement enjoying the benefit) Legitimacy hit. *Steelmanned:* this asymmetry may be the correct modeling of a real historical dynamic — the political risk of vouching for an outsider genuinely is retrospective and reputational for the patron, distinct from the immediate operational benefit the client town receives, and that's an accurate, not lazy, asymmetry. *But* the measured 69/31 split means that in the modal case (69%), the settlement receives the full immunity benefit and literally nobody pays anything — which is close kin to the "dominant strategy" failure mode this same charter names (Ω-d, "strictly-best, doesn't pay its cost"), from the appointing faction's chair: appoint a Podestà, get immunity, eat a 31%-chance deferred tax. That is a real, not cosmetic, under-costing risk, separable from the "Fair Magistrate" clause the closure register already flagged for removal — the base rate structure itself, not just that one clause, is where the dominance risk lives.

*Q.* Q-robust passes (multiple viable timings/uses; visible governor-id swap; fires without player action at term end; clean dramatic legibility — Reinholt's tenure and Almud's Legitimacy both visibly at risk). Q-smooth is the standout: it is literally the production replacement call, composing with zero special-casing against the existing succession path. Q-elegant passes cleanly in one sentence.

**Verdict: KEEP, with a REFINE flag on the appointer-liability rate/cost structure before final ratification.** This **CONFIRMS** the core replacement mechanic and appointer-liability *concept* the closure register already calls "Promote," and **SHARPENS** its own "Refine-then-promote" disposition: my finding is that the dominance risk the closure register attributes solely to the "Fair Magistrate" clause is also present, independently, in the base malfeasance-rate/cost asymmetry — the REFINE pass owes an answer on whether 25%/deferred/displaced is calibrated enough, not just a clause strip.

**What it did for the game**

Proved the replacement half of this mechanic needs zero new plumbing — it's the exact `succeed_governor()` path ordinary succession already runs, which is a strong implementation-cost signal for designers weighing this against costlier proposals. The measured 69/31 split converts an abstract "is appointer-liability priced right?" worry into a concrete number designers can now interrogate, and specifically shows the dominance risk isn't confined to the already-flagged Fair Magistrate clause.

### Why pairing mattered

Reviewed separately, both proposals would likely have been taken at their own word — each self-describes as costed and non-dominant, and nothing in either write-up alone forces a reviewer to check that claim against the other. ### HRE-2 — Chapter Capture

**Narrative Emergence Review**

Season 1: the governor of Goldenfurt — reading "Chapter Capture" through its HRE flavor, a seat on a Prince-Bishopric's electoral College — spends nothing but time. No AP-visible cost fires; the design's own text states no fixed number of seats a player can bank, so the ledger simply accrues a Debt-family tag: `college-seats-owed: 3`. Nothing visibly changes in the world. No card fires, no NPC reacts, no Petition surfaces. This is banking as *waiting* — a bet placed on a die the player never rolls.

Across the simulated ten-season tenure, at a 0.15-per-season exogenous vacancy draw, the bet resolves 81 times out of 100. The vignette that actually plays out, in the modal case: somewhere around season 5–6, a Canon dies (off-camera, no player input), the vacancy fires, and the three banked seats convert to real vote-weight in the space of one resolution tick — three votes the governor did nothing that season to earn, landing exactly when the College is voting on something else entirely (a new Bishop, a tithe dispute, a war levy). The dramatic beat is retrospective: the player's earlier patience is redeemed by an event they didn't cause and couldn't see coming. In the other 19 cases, the tenure simply ends — a new governor inherits the seats-owed tag, Ledger-persistence (§1.6) carrying it forward, and the bet passes to someone else's hands.

**NERS Audit**

*Steelman first:* the mechanic is a genuinely distinct causal shape from every other College-adjacent proposal (BYZ-9's reactive override, HAB-1's overlay appointee) — it is the only *proactive, patient, uncertain* patronage-banking verb in the corpus, and it fills a real gap: nothing else lets a player invest now against a payoff the engine, not the player, times. That is Ω-c by construction (fires autonomously) and clean N (a real Renaissance-ecclesiastical dynamic — prebendal expectancy, waiting for a canonry to fall vacant, is well documented). Q-elegant survives one read: "bank seats, they convert on the next vacancy."

*Where the sim earns its keep:* the existing disposition calls this a "clean KEEP… Promote," reasoning from the design's own framing of timing as something "the player doesn't control." The simulated 81/19 split shows that framing is true locally (any single season) but misleading at the tenure scale the game actually runs on. Over 10 seasons at a plausible vacancy rate, "the player doesn't control when" collapses into "the player controls whether, and whether is a near-lock." That is a live Ω-d question the original catalogue entry never had to answer, because nobody had run the numbers: does banking pay what it buys? As pitched, banking costs *nothing stated* — no AP, no Treasury, no Disposition, no exposure. A 81%-favorable uncosted bet is not a bet.

**Verdict: REFINE** (not a clean KEEP). CHALLENGES the closure register's "Promote as pitched" framing specifically on Ω-d — CONFIRMS everything else (N, Ω-a cross-scale, Ω-c autonomy, Q-elegant). The fix is narrow and additive to the existing authoring note: give banking an explicit acquisition cost (an AP spend, a Disposition stake with the sitting Chapter, or a discovery-exposure risk per season banked) so the 81% payoff is earned, not free.

**What it did for the game**

The simulation turned an invisible design gap into a specific, fixable authoring requirement: banking needs a cost clause before it ships, or a patient player gets a near-guaranteed free vote bloc. This is exactly the kind of blind spot pre-build math catches that a prose read-through wouldn't.

### HAB-4 — Overlapping Consulta Arbitration

**Narrative Emergence Review**

Two Habsburg ministries — say, the Council of Finance and the Council of War — collide over the same province's resources in the same season: a mandate for revenue versus a mandate for muster. The arbitration fires, and the engine first picks a procedure: Agenda-Set (one ministry blind-frames the question, the other reacts without knowing the frame) or Ratify (a drafted resolution goes up for a straight accept/veto). Whichever procedure wins, a clock starts — Latency — ticking toward paralysis if the ministries can't converge in time.

Across 100 simulated arbitrations: 30 resolve inside Agenda-Set framing (one council successfully sets the terms and the other accepts them under the frame), 35 resolve inside Ratify (a drafted compromise clears an accept/veto vote), and 35 blow past the second latency tick into outright paralysis — the two ministries deadlock, and by design nobody adjudicates the deadlock for them; it just sits, unresolved, presumably escalating pressure elsewhere in the settlement's Event Deck. That three-way split — no branch dominating, paralysis a real, common outcome rather than a tail case — is a genuinely dramatic council scene: whose frame wins, does the veto hold, or does the whole apparatus seize up.

**NERS Audit**

*Steelman first:* the underlying dynamic is real Habsburg conciliar history — overlapping consulta jurisdictions (Consejo de Hacienda vs. Consejo de Guerra, among others) genuinely produced exactly this kind of procedural gridlock, and "generalize into a domain-agnostic arbitration primitive" (the disposition's own framing) is a legitimately valuable reusable resolver, not a one-off flavor mechanic. The non-degenerate 30/35/35 spread this adapter produced is a good sign under Q-robust: three live approaches, no dominant strategy, dramatic legibility intact (whose position is at risk is always answerable in one sentence: whichever ministry loses the frame or the vote).

*The genuine tension, weighed honestly:* the closure register's flagged gap — "Define 'Ministry priority action' and its action-economy source — Part 7 builds no per-Ministry action capability today" — is not a build-state complaint the cardinal rule would kick back. It's a complaint that the *design itself* doesn't yet specify what a Ministry's priority action even is or where it draws its AP/Competence from; the adapter's own `p_agenda_set`/`p_resolved_before_latency2` params are frank test-scenario stand-ins with "no base rate given," standing in for a mechanism the design hasn't named. Judging as-if-built still requires a *built thing* to project onto, and here the thing the arbitration presupposes (per-Ministry action capability) has no design home yet, not merely no code. **Verdict: REFINE.** CONFIRMS the closure register's "Refine-then-promote" — the sim doesn't overturn it, but it does sharpen *what kind* of refine is owed: not just "specify Latency-1/2 effects" (a numbers gap) but "author the Ministry-action-economy foundation first" (a structural gap one level down), since without it Q-smooth is a projection, not a verified property.

**What it did for the game**

Confirmed the arbitration's dramatic shape is sound (a genuine three-way, non-degenerate procedural contest) while surfacing, with real numbers, that its two headline probabilities are currently placeholders standing in for an unauthored Ministry action-economy — telling the authoring team exactly which foundation to build first before this primitive can be trusted at other domains (§7.1a and beyond).

## Closing synthesis

Eight parallel reviews, one methodology, twenty-nine proposals: not one reached CUT or PRUNE. That is not grade inflation — every review opened with an inverted-critic steelman before any verdict, and several steelmans genuinely could have gone the other way (HRE-2's banking timing, CHN-6's original standalone pitch, the L/PS-reading items' cross-scale claims). What actually happened is closer to a filtering effect upstream: the compendium's own tier-3 closure pass had already routed the weakest 14 proposals to CUT and the most contested 23 to needs-Jordan before this review ever ran, so the 29 items in scope here were pre-selected for structural soundness. What NERS review adds on top of that pre-filtering is not "is this good" but "is this *exactly as strong as the prose claims*, once it has to touch real state" — and there the record is mixed on purpose: 3 items (§1.3b Bind the Cells, the Π pressure homeostat, HRE-2 Chapter Capture) came back with this review's verdict *harder or softer* than the existing compendium call, precisely because running the mechanic against Goldenfurt's actual NPCs and Ledger surfaced something a prose read-through couldn't — a granularity mismatch, a capped-restore ceiling four independent measurements now confirm, an uncosted 81%-favorable bet.

Two structural findings sit above any single proposal and gate a wider set than they name: the L/PS substrate is dormant in running code, so every item that reads Legitimacy/Popular Support (§1.0b/c/d, the §1.8 Mandate formula) is provisionally RECONCILEd, not confirmed, until Stratum-B wiring lands; and the predicate-sweep/card-deck event-architecture fork has no declared authority, so any future settlement authored under either model inherits an unreconciled foundation until Jordan names which one is canonical. Both were already flagged in the corpus at the doc level — what this pass adds is that they are *live, not hypothetical*: every dependent proposal was still being drafted and reviewed against them as if the gap didn't exist.

The single strongest validated claim across all eight clusters is the Ascendancy downfall table (`downfall_exploitation`, 92/100 exploited): the corpus's recurring demand that every rise pay a structurally-matched fall is not just asserted in that proposal, it is what the numbers do when run. The single most consequential open fork is G606's actual wiring — whether the recall clock advances on every non-compliant season (the integrated campaign's reading, which produces near-total recall dominance) or only on a specific card draw (the event-deck engine's reading, where recall cascades barely fire) — because the two readings describe meaningfully different games, and nothing in the corpus has picked one yet.

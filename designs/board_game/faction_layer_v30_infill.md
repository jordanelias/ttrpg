# VALORIA — Faction Layer: Infill
## Historical rationale, examples, and GM guidance for faction_layer_v30.md

## §1-INF — ON STABILITY

### Why PP-403 Was Wrong

The original PP-403 — a Stability cost on any failed external Domain Action — created the right instinct but the wrong instrument. Historically, states did not become unstable because their diplomats were occasionally rebuffed or their merchants lost a contract. They became unstable through sudden, large, structurally visible shocks: losing a city, signing a humiliating peace, being publicly condemned by an alliance of rivals, having an assassination plot exposed, watching their army break on a field.

Venice provides the clearest counter-example. The Serenissima lost battles regularly throughout the fifteenth century — against the Ottomans, against Milan, against the League of Cambrai. Each loss, taken in isolation, would have triggered PP-403 dozens of times per campaign. And yet Venice was one of the most stable polities in European history. Its constitutional structure, its fiscal discipline, its deliberate suppression of dynastic instability through the elected Doge system — all of these made it resilient to the ordinary losses that any active state accrues.

The new trigger system models what actually destabilised states: **specific, categorised shocks** of a kind that undermined the social contract between a leadership and its constituent parts.

### On Institutional Consolidation Recovery

The +1 Stability for a "clean season" (no Trigger 1–5) represents the ordinary stabilising work that functioning states do continuously in quiet times: paying officials, maintaining courts, keeping garrisons fed, processing petitions. This is invisible when nothing goes wrong. It becomes visible only in comparison with what happens when external shocks keep coming — a faction under sustained Occupation, sustained Parliamentary Blockade, and sustained military losses cannot consolidate because there is no breathing room to do so.

## §2-INF — ON OCCUPATION

### The Distinction That Mattered

Every Italian diplomat of the fifteenth century understood the difference between a city that was occupied and a city that was controlled. When the French army marched into Milan in 1499 and Ludovico Sforza fled, France had occupied Milan but not yet controlled it. The Milanese bureaucracy continued to function; the city's guilds continued to operate; the local nobility continued to hold their lands. Control — the ability to tax, to appoint, to levy — came later, and required a separate act of administrative integration.

This is why Occupation in Valoria produces immediate Stability loss (the psychological and military shock of enemy troops in one's territory) but does not immediately strip TCV (the territory is not yet truly in the victor's hands). The three-season conversion window models the administrative and political work required to turn physical presence into genuine governance.

### Fort Levels as Design Signal

The geography deliberately creates chokepoints: Lowenskyst (Fort 3, Ob 5 to occupy) guards the northeastern pass from Altonia; Ehrenfeld (Fort 3, Ob 5) is the military hinge between Crown's heartland and the contested centre. These are not incidentally fortified — they represent the physical infrastructure of the peninsula's defensive architecture. A campaign that successfully breaches Lowenskyst has achieved something historically significant: it has opened the Altonian corridor. The Ob 5 is not punitive game design; it is the mathematical expression of why Charles VIII's invasion of Italy in 1494 via the Alpine passes was considered a near-miracle of logistics.

## §3-INF — ON TREATIES

### Why Negotiation Has Three Phases

Real treaty negotiations in the Italian state system had three identifiable components that correspond directly to the mechanical phases:

**Positioning** (Phase 1): Who came to the table with more leverage? Venice in 1503 had already lost its mainland territories; France had just won Pavia; the Ottoman Empire held Cyprus. These facts determined who set the opening terms before anyone spoke. The Influence roll models this: it is not eloquence, it is demonstrated institutional weight.

**Concession declaration** (Phase 2): No roll because this is the design space — the part where player decisions matter. The historical equivalent was the secret instructions given to ambassadors: what they could give up, in what order, at what price. Giving this to players as a decision rather than a roll preserves the drama. A faction that concedes territory when it didn't need to has made a strategic error that no dice can undo.

**Ratification** (Phase 3): Domestic institutions had to ratify major agreements. The Venetian Senate routinely rejected terms that the Doge's ambassadors had provisionally agreed. The French Parlements resisted royal treaties they found injurious to French commercial interests. The ratification roll models institutional resistance — the reason why a diplomatically successful negotiation can still fail to produce a binding agreement.

### The Guarantor as Design Tension

The guarantor option introduces a third-party risk: the guarantor gains an obligation they cannot easily escape. This models exactly why the Italian peace of the mid-fifteenth century (the Peace of Lodi, 1454, and the subsequent Italian League) held as long as it did — and why it collapsed when Charles VIII invaded. The guarantors (primarily Milan, Florence, Venice, Naples, the Papacy) had agreed to enforce a balance of power. When one of them (Milan under Ludovico Sforza) secretly invited France in to tip that balance, the guarantee structure collapsed and nobody was willing to enforce it against a French army. The guarantor's Mandate cost for failing to enforce models that reputational collapse.

## §4-INF — ON PARLIAMENT

### Why Parliament Matters More Than Individual Actions

The League of Cambrai (1508) was not a military alliance first. It was a political declaration. The assembled powers — Pope Julius II, Emperor Maximilian, France, Spain, Ferrara — did not simply agree to attack Venice. They formally declared Venice a threat to the peace of Christendom, stripped its ambassadors of status, and created a legal framework that made Venice everyone's enemy simultaneously. The military consequences followed from the political declaration, not the other way around.

This is why Parliament in Valoria is more consequential than individual Domain Actions: it is the mechanism by which the faction community formally designates a member as having transgressed acceptable bounds. An Outlawry vote is not just a Mandate cost — it is every faction's standing right to act against you without diplomatic consequence. The Mandate-weighted vote structure ensures that this power cannot be used frivolously: you need the weight of the most powerful factions behind you.

### Sacred Veto as Ecclesiastical Supremacy

The Church controlled the legitimacy of civil deliberation in Catholic Europe. Synods, Diets, and national councils all required episcopal presence and typically papal sanction. Julius II's convening of the Lateran Council (1512) to delegitimise the schismatic Council of Pisa — called by Louis XII without papal approval — demonstrated this power in its clearest form. He did not argue against the Council's decisions; he declared it null by asserting superior ecclesiastical authority. The Sacred Veto is exactly this: Church does not debate the motion's merits; it declares the deliberation theologically invalid.

The self-interest cost (Mandate −1 for using the Veto to protect Church interests) models the historical reality: everyone could see when the Pope was acting from institutional self-interest rather than spiritual authority. When Julius II declared Pisa null, he gained his point but lost some credibility. The Church that too frequently exercises its Veto becomes the Church that everyone knows will only exercise it for itself — which undermines the Veto's legitimacy.

### Hafenmark Parliamentary Manoeuvre

Hafenmark's Parliamentary Manoeuvre (existing Standard Action) is the mechanism by which the Rule of Law framework asserts procedural authority. It is Hafenmark's primary non-military power: the ability to contest another faction's action through institutional channels rather than force. The new Parliament system adds the collective dimension — Hafenmark can now not only block individual actions (Manoeuvre) but call the entire institutional body to formally condemn them (Motion to Censure, Motion to Blockade). This is the difference between a lawyer filing an objection and a court issuing a ruling.

## §5-INF — ON SIGNIFICANT MILITARY LOSS

### The Three Variables

The historical record distinguishes sharply between military reverses that destabilised and those that did not. Three variables explain almost every case:

**Scale of commitment.** A raiding party failing to take a border fort (pool 1–3) is an operational setback, not a strategic crisis. The same fort repelling a major field army (pool 4+) is a different kind of event: it means the faction staked institutional prestige on an outcome and was publicly refused. Condition A (pool ≥ 4) models this threshold.

**Quality of failure.** A fighting retreat that preserves the army's order is recoverable in a way that a rout is not. The French army after Cerignola (1503) withdrew but remained a coherent force. The French army at Bicocca (1522) literally ran away before the battle was finished; the Swiss mercenaries in French service refused to continue serving and walked home to Switzerland. Condition B (Failure, not Partial) and the severity qualifiers (Condition C: net ≤ −2 or pool ≥ 6) model this distinction.

**What was lost.** The death of Gaston de Foix at Ravenna (1512) — the most brilliant French general of his generation, aged 23 — transformed a French victory into a French strategic crisis. He was irreplaceable; the army that had won the battle scattered within weeks of his death. The officer attachment and fate mechanic models this reality: what matters is not just that the battle was lost, but whether the people who understood how to fight it survived.

### On the Maximum Cost of −4

A −4 Stability event (pool ≥ 6, net ≤ −3, officer killed, attacking capital) represents something like Pavia (1525) combined with the loss of the king: the faction's main field army is annihilated beneath the walls of the enemy capital, and its commanding general is dead. This should be a civilisation-threatening moment. Starting Stability values (4–5 for most factions) mean that a single −4 event does not immediately collapse a faction — but it puts it at Stability 0–1, one more shock away from elimination. This seems correctly calibrated for what these events actually did historically.

## §6-INF — GM GUIDANCE

### Running Parliament Without Overwhelming the Session

Parliament is designed to be declarative, not procedural. The Accounting moment where votes resolve should take no more than 5 minutes. Best practice:

1. Before Accounting, any player who declared a Parliamentary Motion in Phase 4 states it aloud.
2. GM states the Mandate totals for each faction (public information).
3. Players and GM quickly calculate majority/supermajority.
4. Targeted faction may immediately declare Rebuttal (counts as a Phase 4 action they already spent or choose to spend retroactively — GM adjudicates based on session flow).
5. Effects applied. Move on.

The detailed historical rationale in this infill exists to ground the design decisions, not to be read at the table.

### Occupation Markers (Physical)

In BG mode, Occupation markers should be visually distinct from control markers. Suggested: a smaller marker in the occupying faction's colour placed OVER the existing control marker. The existing marker remains visible (showing whose territory it legally is), but the occupation marker shows who physically holds it. This visual distinction is important for players understanding TCV calculations.

### Officer Attachment as Player Choice

In Hybrid/TTRPG mode, attaching a named NPC officer to a military_advance action is a meaningful risk decision: it grants +1D to the Military pool but opens the officer to fate consequences. GMs should present this choice explicitly before the roll, never assume attachment. Some players may prefer to keep their valued NPCs out of harm's way even at a pool cost. That is a legitimate strategic decision.

### Treaty Phase 2 (Concession Declaration) at the Table

Phase 2 has no roll because it is player negotiation. In BG mode with experienced players, this can be rich and extended. In TTRPG or Hybrid, with less table focus on faction-layer politics, GMs may want to abbreviate it: each side simply names their top priority (demand) and their maximum concession (offer), and the GM mediates a quick resolution rather than extended negotiation.

The mechanical consequence is the same either way; the texture of the experience differs. This is by design — the system should serve different table styles.

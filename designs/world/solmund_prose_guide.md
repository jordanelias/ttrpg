## 1. PURPOSE

This document is a prose style guide for writing Solmund-related cultural artifacts — scripture, liturgy, hymns, sermons, inscriptions, philosophical treatises, heretical tracts, and folk devotions — within the Valoria setting. It establishes voice, vocabulary, structural patterns, and tonal registers derived from three real-world source traditions:

- **Pier Giorgio Di Cicco and Italian-Canadian confessional poetry** — for the voice of lived institutional faith and its fractures
- **Theological poetry (Hopkins, R.S. Thomas, John of the Cross, Levertov, Eliot)** — for the registers of devotion, doubt, and mystical encounter
- **Renaissance theological prose (Cusanus, Ficino, Pico, Teresa of Ávila, Böhme)** — for the architecture of systematic doctrinal writing

The guide does not define theology. Theology is defined in the philosophical foundations and worldbuilding canon. This guide defines *how that theology sounds when Valorians write it down, sing it, carve it into stone, or argue about it in taverns.*

## 2. THE DI CICCO PRINCIPLE — Faith as Inhabited Architecture

Di Cicco's central contribution to Solmund voice is the concept of faith as *inhabited space* rather than intellectual proposition. His poetry does not argue for God. It describes what it is like to live inside a structure built around God — the rhythms of liturgy as physical habit, the cathedral as something your body knows before your mind consents, the way institutional belonging is simultaneously liberation and confinement.

### 2.1 What Di Cicco Gives Solmund Writing

**The confessional present tense.** Di Cicco writes in the immediate — "I am standing in the church" not "the church stands." Solmund devotional writing should inhabit the moment of devotion. The believer is not reporting belief. The believer is *inside* it. Orthodox Solmund texts read as acts of inhabitation, not argumentation.

**The tension between institution and interiority.** Di Cicco's mid-career silence (ordination, monastic withdrawal, the gap between *Virgin Science* and *Living in Paradise*) maps directly onto the Solmund relationship with the unintelligible ground. The institution provides structure. The interior experience exceeds the structure. Di Cicco returned from silence not by rejecting the institution but by writing from within it about what it could not contain. Solmund heterodox writing — theological dissent that stays inside the Church — uses this voice: not rejection, but the honest report of someone whose experience outgrew the available categories.

**The civic-sacred collapse.** Di Cicco's later work (*Municipal Mind*, the Poet Laureate period) fuses civic imagination with theological language. Himmelenger is a city that IS a cathedral. Solmund municipal writing — legal codes, civic dedications, public inscriptions — carries theological weight without being explicitly devotional. A Himmelenger building permit invokes Solmund not as piety but as the grammar of public life.

**The immigrant's double consciousness.** Di Cicco writes as someone simultaneously inside and outside a tradition — Italian in Canada, Catholic in a secular literary culture. This maps to Valorians in Church territory who are culturally Solmund without being theologically committed. Their relationship to the faith is habitual, embodied, pre-intellectual. They cross themselves at thresholds. They cannot say why.

### 2.2 Voice Rules Derived from Di Cicco

- Orthodox Solmund texts use **first-person plural** ("we who stand in the light") or **second-person address** ("you who enter this house"). The theology is communal.
- Heterodox Solmund texts shift to **first-person singular**. The break from orthodoxy is always a break from "we" into "I." The individual voice is the first sign of theological dissent.
- Solmund devotional poetry is **short-lined, present-tense, concrete.** No abstraction without a physical anchor. "The bell rings and the morning opens" not "the eternal dawn of Solmund's grace."
- Solmund civic writing is **declarative and unlabored.** It does not argue that Solmund matters. It assumes Solmund as the operating condition of public life.

## 3. THEOLOGICAL POETRY TRADITIONS — The Registers of Encounter

Five poets provide the tonal registers for different modes of Solmund writing.

### 3.1 Gerard Manley Hopkins → Orthodox Devotional (Certainty 5-6)

Hopkins' "inscape" — the distinctive inner form of a thing, its thisness — maps to threads as rendered by orthodox consciousness. The Church's account says: the world is woven, and the weaving is Solmund's work. Hopkins' sprung rhythm, his packed compound adjectives ("dapple-dawn-drawn Falcon"), his sense of the divine as materially present in texture and grain — this is the register for Solmund scripture and high liturgy.

**Voice characteristics:**
- Dense, sensory, compressed. Every noun modified. Every verb physical.
- Exclamatory piety — wonder as the default response to existence.
- The world is evidence. Looking closely enough at anything is prayer.
- Thread language rendered as texture: "wound-tight," "drawn-through," "the grain of the given."

**Use for:** Cathedral inscriptions, Confessor's liturgical pronouncements, scriptural passages describing the created world as Solmund's handiwork.

### 3.2 R.S. Thomas → Pastoral Doubt (Certainty 3-4)

Thomas spent a career writing about a God who would not show up. His poems describe empty churches, rural parishes where faith is a leftover habit, the priest who continues the rituals while unsure anyone is listening. This is the register for Valorians in outlying territories — Crown heartland, border settlements — where the Church is present but not overwhelming. Faith as maintenance work. The liturgy performed because the alternative is silence.

**Voice characteristics:**
- Spare, cold, observational. Short sentences. Declarative.
- Landscape as theological argument — the empty field, the stone church, the weather.
- Doubt expressed as description, not confession. "The altar was bare" does more work than "I have lost my faith."
- The absent divine — Solmund invoked but never answering.

**Use for:** Rural parish sermons, marginal Church writings from non-Himmelenger territories, private correspondence of clergy who serve far from the Cathedral city.

### 3.3 John of the Cross → Mystical/Practitioner (Certainty 6+ or Certainty 1-2)

John of the Cross wrote about the *noche oscura* — the dark night of the soul, where all consolation is withdrawn and the mystic is left in pure unknowing. This maps to two Valorian experiences simultaneously: the practitioner who has crossed beyond rendering into direct contact with threads (the Leap), and the Einhir descendant whose ancestral knowledge has been stripped away by institutional suppression. Both occupy the dark night — one by ascent, one by destruction.

**Voice characteristics:**
- Paradoxical, negating. "I knew by not knowing." "In the darkness I saw."
- Erotic vocabulary repurposed for spiritual encounter — union, embrace, wound, burning. The body is the site of metaphysical contact.
- Loss as prerequisite. You cannot find what John describes without first losing everything the institution gave you.
- The beloved is always unnamed. Thread language here becomes non-referential — the practitioner cannot name what they touched.

**Use for:** Practitioner journals, Einhir liturgical fragments (pre-Catastrophe survivals), heterodox devotional writing by Church members who have experienced threadwork firsthand, the interior monologue of characters undergoing Religious Crisis (Stage 4G).

### 3.4 Denise Levertov → Attentive Practice (Certainty 4-5, Thread Sensitivity emerging)

Levertov's theology is attention — looking at something until it opens. Her conversions (agnostic to Catholic) track as gradual accumulations of noticed detail. This is the register for characters at the threshold of thread sensitivity: not yet practitioners, but people whose observation of the world is beginning to exceed the Church's categories without contradicting them.

**Voice characteristics:**
- Patient, accretive, descriptive. Long sentences that build through layered observation.
- The sacred discovered in process, not revelation. Bread rising. Light shifting. Thread-adjacent phenomena noticed without theological framework.
- Pilgrimage as metaphor and literal practice. Walking toward something you cannot yet name.
- Gratitude as the dominant tone — not ecstatic, but sustained.

**Use for:** Monastic journals (Formation 2G), Guild artisan meditations on craft, fieldwork logs from characters who are noticing thread anomalies without understanding them.

### 3.5 T.S. Eliot (Four Quartets) → Institutional Philosophy (Certainty 5, intellectual)

Eliot's *Four Quartets* — time, pattern, the still point of the turning world — provide the register for the Church's *intellectual* tradition. Not devotional warmth but philosophical architecture. The theologian who builds frameworks, who wants the world to cohere, who experiences faith as a structural problem. This is the Confessor's voice when writing doctrine, the Cardinal's voice when composing policy.

**Voice characteristics:**
- Measured, recursive, architectural. Sentences that fold back on themselves.
- Time as the central preoccupation. "The past is present in the future" rendered as institutional certainty about Solmund's continuing presence.
- Pattern-seeking. The world is not random; the theologian's task is to discern the weave.
- Irony held in suspension — the institutional voice that knows its own limits but proceeds anyway.

**Use for:** Doctrinal treatises, Church council minutes, the Confessor's formal theological writings, Cathedral school curricula.

## 4. RENAISSANCE THEOLOGICAL PROSE — Doctrinal Architecture

Renaissance theological writers provide structural patterns for the Church of Solmund's formal literary output — the texts that Church Schooling (Formation 2A) teaches, that Cardinals debate, that the Confessor pronounces.

### 4.1 Nicholas of Cusa — Learned Ignorance

Cusanus argued that the highest form of knowledge is knowing that you cannot know — *docta ignorantia*. The infinite exceeds all finite categories. You approach truth not by accumulating propositions but by recognising the failure of propositions to capture what they point toward.

**Application to Solmund:** The Church of Solmund occupies an impossible position. Its theology claims Solmund's weaving as the foundation of the world. Its institutional survival depends on this claim being received as sufficient — the Church provides the categories through which reality is understood. But the metaphysical truth (Ein Sof as infinite positive being, epistemically inaccessible) means that any theological claim about the ground of being is, by the framework's own logic, a partial rendering at best and a projection at worst.

Cusanus gives the Church an intellectually honest escape: the highest devotion is recognising that Solmund's work exceeds understanding. This is not agnosticism. It is the claim that *the failure to understand is itself evidence of the infinite.* The Church's smartest theologians write in this register — affirming Solmund by affirming the impossibility of fully affirming Solmund.

**Structural pattern:** Cusan Solmund texts build by negation. Each paragraph establishes a claim, then shows the claim's insufficiency, then shows that the insufficiency itself points toward something greater. The rhythm is: *assert — qualify — transcend.* The final position is always higher than the first, but it is higher specifically because it has acknowledged its own limits.

### 4.2 Marsilio Ficino — Emanation and Hierarchy

Ficino's Neoplatonic theology describes a cascade from the One through intellect through soul into matter. Each level participates in the level above it but cannot fully contain what it receives.

**Application to Solmund:** Ficino provides the Church's structural cosmology — the hierarchy by which Solmund's founding act propagates through the world. The Four Cardinals (doctrine, military, wealth, missions) are not arbitrary bureaucratic divisions. They are *emanative orders* — four ways Solmund's original weaving continues to structure reality. Doctrine is closest to the founding act (pure intelligibility). Military is actuality (physical presence in the world). Wealth is temporal depth (accumulated consequence). Missions are the boundary — the Church's edge, where the emanation meets what it has not yet reached.

**Structural pattern:** Ficinian Solmund texts proceed from abstract to concrete, from principle to manifestation. They begin with Solmund, move through the institutional hierarchy, and arrive at the individual believer. The movement is always downward — from source to participation. The formal register is elevated, Latinate, periodic. Sentences are long and subordinated. The prose itself enacts hierarchy.

### 4.3 Pico della Mirandola — Human Dignity and Kabbalistic Synthesis

Pico argued that human beings occupy a unique position — capable of ascending toward the divine or descending toward the animal, self-determining in a way no other creature is. His integration of Kabbalah into Christian theology gives direct precedent for the Ein Sof framework.

**Application to Solmund:** Pico's *Oration* is the template for Solmund's account of human thread sensitivity. Humans are not passive recipients of Solmund's weaving. They are beings capable of *perceiving* the weave — and, in extreme cases, of working it. Thread Sensitivity is the Valorian *dignitas hominis* — the capacity that makes human beings uniquely positioned between the rendered world and the unintelligible ground. The Church's orthodox position is that this capacity is Solmund's gift, meant for reverent perception, not intervention. The heterodox position — which the Church suppresses — is that the capacity is constitutive, not granted. You do not receive thread sensitivity from Solmund. You ARE a being whose consciousness renders threads. The gift framing is institutional, not metaphysical.

**Structural pattern:** Pican Solmund texts are rhetorical, oratorical, addressed. They speak *to* the reader as a being of consequence. They elevate. They challenge. The Church uses this register for ordination addresses, Cathedral school commencement speeches, and — dangerously — for the Restoration Movement's counter-theology. Pico cuts both ways: if human dignity is innate, the Church cannot claim to be its exclusive custodian.

### 4.4 Teresa of Ávila — Interior Architecture

Teresa's *Interior Castle* maps the soul as seven concentric dwelling places. The journey inward is simultaneously a journey toward God. Each mansion has its own temptations, consolations, and modes of prayer.

**Application to Solmund:** Teresa provides the model for how the Church categorises thread sensitivity as a spiritual hierarchy. The Church does not deny that some people perceive more than others. It *domesticates* that perception by framing it as spiritual advancement — grades of holiness, not grades of perception. A TS 30 character is "closer to Solmund" in Church language. A TS 70 character is a living saint. This framing is simultaneously useful (it gives thread-sensitive people a social role) and dangerous (it prevents them from understanding what they actually perceive, because the Church's categories are theological, not phenomenological).

**Structural pattern:** Teresian Solmund texts are intimate, interior, progressive. They describe a journey inward using architectural metaphor — rooms, thresholds, doors. The prose is warm but precise. Each stage has clearly defined characteristics. The reader is always moving deeper. This pattern appears in Monastic Seclusion (Formation 2G) journals, contemplative manuals, and the private writings of thread-sensitive clergy who are trying to reconcile their experience with their doctrine.

### 4.5 Jacob Böhme — The Ungrund

Böhme wrote about the *Ungrund* — the groundless ground, the abyss before and beneath God, from which God himself emerges. This is the most dangerous Renaissance theological idea for the Church of Solmund, because it maps almost exactly onto the Ein Sof as described in the philosophical foundations.

**Application to Solmund:** Böhme's Ungrund IS the unintelligible ground. If the Church ever encounters a text that describes the Ein Sof in Böhmean terms — as a groundless abyss of infinite positive being from which even the divine emerges — it will recognise the text as maximally heretical, because it implies that Solmund himself is a rendered phenomenon, not the renderer. Solmund, in Böhmean framing, is not the source of the weave. Solmund is the weave's most intelligible product. The ground is deeper than Solmund.

**Structural pattern:** Böhmean Solmund texts (which would exist only as suppressed heresies, Einhir fragments, or the private writings of practitioners at TS 50+) are visionary, paradoxical, and structurally chaotic. They do not build arguments. They *erupt.* Images pile on images. Contradictions are not resolved but held. The prose is syntactically strained — sentences that try to say what language cannot contain. This is the opposite of Ficinian order. It is what happens when someone tries to write about what lies below the waterline.

## 5. ARTIFACT TAXONOMY — What Solmund Culture Produces

### 5.1 Orthodox Canon

**Scripture (The Weavings of Solmund):** The central scriptural text. Written in Hopkins-register (dense, sensory, exclamatory). Describes Solmund's foundational act as the weaving of the world. Uses textile and craft metaphor throughout — thread, loom, warp, weft, shuttle, tension, binding. The scripture does not describe threads as the philosophical foundations describe them. It describes threads as *Solmund's tools.* This is the core doctrinal move: threads are instruments, not ontology. The Church's scripture makes threads subordinate to the weaver.

**Liturgy (The Hours of the Loom):** Daily prayer cycle structured around the metaphor of weaving. Morning prayers describe the day as a new thread drawn from Solmund's hand. Evening prayers describe the day's events as woven into the fabric of Solmund's ongoing work. The language is Di Cicco-inhabitation: the believer does not observe the liturgy, the believer is *inside* it. Liturgical Solmund is embodied, rhythmic, seasonal. It follows the agricultural calendar because Solmund's weaving is manifest in growth.

**Hymns:** Short, vertical, Hopkins-inflected. Solmund hymns are not narrative. They are moments of recognition — the singer sees Solmund's work in something specific (a harvest, a birth, a storm weathered) and exclaims. The exclamation is the hymn. Hymns are rarely longer than four stanzas. They use half-rhyme and assonance rather than full rhyme — the sound should feel woven, not clicked into place.

**Cathedral Inscriptions:** Cusan register. Short texts carved above doors, around fonts, beneath windows. Each inscription states a theological claim, then qualifies it, then points beyond the qualification. "Solmund wove the world. The world exceeds the weaving. In the excess, Solmund." These are the most intellectually demanding Solmund texts — concentrated theology in lapidary form.

**Doctrinal Treatises:** Ficinian emanative structure + Eliot's recursive prose. Written by and for trained theologians. Long, hierarchical, architecturally ordered. These texts establish the Church's official positions on thread sensitivity (Teresian framing), the Einhir Catastrophe (divine punishment for pride), the Forgetting (Solmund's mercy — shielding humanity from knowledge it cannot bear), and the Southernmost (forbidden ground, the wound that must heal in Solmund's time, not human intervention).

### 5.2 Heterodox and Marginal

**Practitioner Journals:** John of the Cross register. Written by thread-sensitive clergy who are experiencing phenomena the doctrine cannot account for. These texts are the most literarily complex Solmund artifacts because they are written by people trying to use the Church's language to describe experiences the Church's language was designed to exclude. The result is paradox-dense prose where orthodox vocabulary is strained past its intended meaning. "Solmund's thread was tight and I touched it and it was not Solmund's and it was not a thread and I was not I." The journals are formally devotional — prayers, meditations, confessions — but the content exceeds the form.

**Einhir Fragments:** Böhmean register, but filtered through 245 years of degradation. These are not intact texts. They are scraps — phrases preserved in folk songs, gestures encoded in craft practice, words whose meanings have been forgotten but whose sounds persist. An Einhir fragment might be three words carved into a hearthstone in a language no one living speaks. The prose guide for writing these: use the shortest possible unit. A single image. A single paradox. No context. The fragment should feel like a shard of something vast, recovered from a fire.

**Restoration Movement Counter-Theology:** Pican register — rhetorical, dignitarian, accusatory. The Restoration Movement's theological position is that the Church stole something that belonged to everyone. Their texts argue that thread sensitivity is human birthright, not institutional dispensation. They use the Church's own language against it: if Solmund wove humanity with the capacity to perceive threads, then the Church — by suppressing that perception — is unwinding Solmund's own work. This is a devastating argument precisely because it is orthodox in form and heretical in conclusion. The prose is oratorical, passionate, designed for public delivery. It sounds like Pico's *Oration* weaponised.

**Niflhel Pamphlets:** These are not theological. Niflhel's critique of the Church is material, not metaphysical — the Church hoards wealth, monopolises education, enforces social hierarchy through spiritual coercion. Niflhel pamphlets are written in plain language, short sentences, direct address. They mock the theological registers above. A Niflhel pamphlet might parody Cathedral inscriptions: "Solmund wove the tithe. The tithe exceeds the granary. In the excess, the Confessor's table."

### 5.3 Folk and Vernacular

**Harvest Songs:** Levertov register — attentive, grateful, grounded. Sung during agricultural work. These songs do not mention Solmund by name. They describe the process of growth, harvest, preservation — and the description carries theological weight because in Solmund culture, noticing the world carefully is itself a form of prayer. The songs use simple, repetitive structures. They are designed to be sung while working. Rhythms match physical labour — the swing of a scythe, the pull of a net.

**Threshold Prayers:** Brief formulae spoken when crossing a threshold (entering a house, beginning a journey, starting a new task). These are Di Cicco-habitation at its most compressed — faith as physical reflex. "By the thread I enter. By the weave I am held." The speaker may not believe. The prayer is performed anyway. It is architecture, not argument.

**Sailors' Invocations:** Specific to Schoenland trade routes and Hafenmark coastal communities. These blend Solmund orthodoxy with practical superstition. Sailors invoke Solmund before departure and — significantly — invoke *the sea itself* as a separate entity. The Maritime Forgetting Zone means that southern sea routes are experienced as irrationally dangerous. Sailors' prayers acknowledge this without understanding it: "Solmund keep the thread above water. What is below, we do not name."

**Funerary Rites:** Thomas register — spare, cold, honest. Death in Solmund theology is the thread returning to the loom. Funerary language does not promise resurrection or afterlife. It promises *continuation of the weave* — the dead person's thread is rewoven into the world's ongoing fabric. This is simultaneously comforting (nothing is lost) and terrifying (nothing is preserved as individual). Funerary texts avoid the word "soul." They use "thread" and "pattern."

## 6. VOCABULARY CONSTRAINTS

### 6.1 Orthodox Solmund Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning | Register |
|------|---------|----------|
| The Weaving | Solmund's founding act; creation | Scripture, liturgy |
| The Loom | The world as Solmund's ongoing work | Liturgy, hymns |
| Thread | The substance of reality (domesticated — as Solmund's tool) | All orthodox |
| Pattern | Divine order; the coherence of the world | Doctrinal, inscriptions |
| The Wound | The Southernmost; the Catastrophe | Doctrinal, sermons |
| Solmund's Hand | Providence; divine intervention | Hymns, folk prayer |
| The Unravelling | Sin; disorder; that which works against the weave | Sermons, moral instruction |
| Certainty | Faith (the Church's own term for spiritual commitment) | All registers |
| The Rendering | How the world is experienced (orthodox: Solmund's gift to consciousness) | Doctrinal |

### 6.2 Suppressed / Heterodox Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning | Register |
|------|---------|----------|
| The Ground | Ein Sof; that which is beneath/beyond Solmund | Practitioner journals, heresy |
| Spooling | The continuous drawing-from-ground that constitutes being | Einhir fragments, heterodox theory |
| The Waterline | The boundary between intelligible and unintelligible | Practitioner technical |
| The Oscillation | Thread as co-movement of three axes | Heterodox philosophy |
| The Dark | The unintelligible ground as experienced by practitioners | John of the Cross register |
| The Excess | What the Church's categories cannot contain | Restoration counter-theology |
| The Leap | Crossing from rendering to being-with-threads | Practitioner journals (extremely rare) |

### 6.3 Forbidden Vocabulary

The Church actively suppresses these terms. Using them in Church territory risks heresy investigation.

- **Ein Sof** — the Kabbalistic name for the ground. The Church does not acknowledge the ground as having a name independent of Solmund.
- **Einhir** — used neutrally, this is the name of the pre-Catastrophe civilisation. Used theologically, it implies a pre-Solmund spiritual tradition with independent validity. The Church permits the historical term. It suppresses the theological use.
- **Ungrund** — the groundless ground. If this term appeared in a Valorian text, it would imply that the ground is deeper than Solmund. This is the maximum heresy.

## 7. STRUCTURAL PATTERNS FOR ARTIFACT WRITING

### 7.1 The Cusan Triad (Inscriptions, Aphorisms)

Three movements: assert → qualify → transcend.

> Solmund binds the world in thread.
> No thread binds what it is drawn from.
> In the unbinding, Solmund.

### 7.2 The Ficinian Cascade (Treatises, Doctrine)

Movement from source through hierarchy to individual.

> From Solmund's hand, the first thread. From the first thread, the warp of the world. From the warp, the orders of the faithful — Confessor who speaks the pattern, Sword-Cardinal who holds the weave taut, Keeper who counts the cost of the fabric, Shepherd who carries the loom to those who have not yet been woven. From the orders, the parish. From the parish, the believer. From the believer, the prayer that rises — thread returning to the hand that drew it.

### 7.3 The Di Cicco Inhabitation (Liturgy, Devotional)

Present-tense, first-person plural, physically grounded.

> We stand in the nave and the stone is cold under us.
> The bell has rung. The morning has opened.
> We are the thread that Solmund drew this day
> and we are wound into the hours as the hours are wound into us.

### 7.4 The Thomas Absence (Rural Sermons, Marginal Faith)

Spare, third-person, landscape-dominant.

> The chapel at Feldmark has no bell.
> There is a rope where the bell was.
> The congregation pulls it on feast days
> and the silence that follows is the sermon.

### 7.5 The John of the Cross Paradox (Practitioner Interior)

First-person singular, negating, erotic-spiritual.

> I reached for the thread and my hand passed through.
> What held me was not what I held.
> In the place where Solmund should have been
> there was something older than weaving,
> and it knew me before I was drawn.

### 7.6 The Böhmean Eruption (Suppressed Heresy, Einhir Vision)

Syntactically strained, imagistic, contradictory.

> The ground opens and it is not ground. The light beneath is darker than the dark above. All threads descend into what has no name and every name, and Solmund — beloved, first-drawn — Solmund is also descending, was always descending, was never above the ground but drawn from it as all things are drawn from it, and the ground does not know his name because the ground does not know names because the ground is what names are drawn from.

### 7.7 The Pican Address (Counter-Theology, Restoration Oratory)

Second-person, rhetorical, accusatory-elevating.

> You were woven with eyes that see the thread. Whose hand covered them? You were drawn from the ground with the capacity to perceive the ground. Who told you the ground was forbidden? You are the evidence of what the Church denies — a being made to perceive what the Church says must not be perceived. Your sensitivity is not heresy. Your sensitivity is what Solmund — if Solmund is what the Church claims — intended.

## 8. TONAL CALIBRATION BY CERTAINTY

| Certainty | Dominant Register | Voice Characteristics |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| 1-2 (Heterodox/Secular) | Böhme, Niflhel plain speech | Rejection or indifference to theological framing. Threads are material, not sacred. |
| 3 (Questioning) | Thomas, early Levertov | Faith as habit without conviction. The rituals performed, the meaning uncertain. |
| 4 (Average Valorian) | Di Cicco inhabitation, folk/vernacular | Faith as cultural architecture. Not examined. Not rejected. Lived. |
| 5 (Orthodox) | Hopkins, Ficino, Eliot | Faith as intellectual and sensory commitment. The world is evidence. The Church is correct. |
| 6+ (Devout/Mystic) | John of the Cross, Teresa | Faith as encounter. The doctrine is true but insufficient. What the believer experiences exceeds what the doctrine describes. Paradoxically, the most devout Solmund voices are closest to heresy. |

## 9. IMPLEMENTATION NOTES FOR GODOT

Cultural artifacts appear in-game as discoverable texts, environmental inscriptions, NPC dialogue, and loadscreen fragments. The prose guide governs:

- **Settlement flavour text:** Generated from register appropriate to territory's faction control and Certainty average. Himmelenger text is Hopkins/Ficino. Feldmark text is Thomas. Varfell coastal text is folk/vernacular with Einhir undertones.
- **NPC speech patterns:** Church-faction NPCs at Certainty 5 use Eliot-recursive phrasing. Thread-sensitive Church NPCs at TS 30+ begin introducing John of the Cross paradoxes into otherwise orthodox speech — a signal to the player that this NPC's experience exceeds their doctrine.
- **Discoverable texts:** POI of type Remnant may contain Einhir fragments (Böhmean register). POI of type Secret in Church territories may contain practitioner journals. These texts carry mechanical weight — reading an Einhir fragment may increment Southernmost Awareness.
- **Loadscreen text:** Rotating aphorisms from the Cusan triad pattern. Short, punchy, theologically loaded. These teach the player Solmund cosmology passively.

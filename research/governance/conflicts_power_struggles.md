## Conflicts & Power Struggles — cross-civilizational synthesis

Across all eight civilizations, hierarchy conflict is not a personality accident but the predictable output of specific institutional holes. The recurring conflict classes are remarkably stable: **succession crises** (Ottoman fratricidal war before the kanun; the Roman Year of Four Emperors and the Julio-Claudian and Nerva–Antonine transitions; Carolingian partition; the Milanese Visconti collapse; the Ōnin succession dispute), **usurpations** (Sulla and Caesar; the Severan and barracks emperors; Sforza's seizure of Milan), **civil wars** (the Thirty Years' War, Ōnin, the Sengoku interregnum, Constantine's dismantling of the Tetrarchy), **factional strife** (Florentine scrutiny-bag wars, Han eunuch-vs-*qingyi* proscription, Praetorian king-making), **revolts** (Ciompi, Janissary depositions, Yellow Turbans, Saxon, the Jewish wars), **coups** (Pazzi, Honnō-ji, the Ides of March), and **ruler-vs-assembly collisions** (Gracchi vs. Senate, Emperor vs. Reichstag, shogun vs. *gokenin* vassals).

What makes this theme load-bearing for Valoria is that almost every one of these events is legible as a **two-signal resonance** — an accumulated *material strain* colliding with an *independent interpretive/legitimating trigger*, where neither alone would fire. That is precisely the collision-of-stresses primitive. Crucially, the corpus is dense with clean **non-Mandate-of-Heaven groundings** for it: the Roman/Byzantine usurpation pattern (a frontier defeat or fiscal shock *plus* a legion's acclamation ritual) supplies the identical two-signal structure with no "heaven judges the ruler" theology, and the Ottoman Janissary deposition (military/fiscal strain *plus* a *Şeyhülislam* fatwa) does the same on the juridical side. Wherever this pass touches Han/Three-Kingdoms legitimacy logic, the interpretive signal is grounded in the *Taiping Dao*'s organizational millenarianism or in raw reform impact — never in *zaiyi*/portent framing, which stays historical-only.

For emergent-friction and churn, the decisive observation is that these conflicts leave **residue**: institutional overhangs that outlive their crisis (the Rhenish League surviving the Interregnum's end), suppression debt, venality drift, and quiet sovereignty accretion under long incumbency. Valoria's churn wants accumulators that do *not* reset between crises, relief valves that bleed pressure short of collapse, and blast-radius rules scaling destruction to rank — which is exactly what these histories supply as emergent-arc seeds rather than scripted set-pieces.

## Cross-civilizational synthesis

**What structurally produced the conflicts.** Four generative mechanisms recur. First, the **succession vacuum**: where legitimacy rested on *auctoritas* or acclamation rather than a codified, enforceable succession law, "who rules" collapsed into "which army holds the capital" — Rome (Year of Four Emperors, the *Soldatenkaiser*), Milan (Sforza), the HRE elective monarchy (Great Interregnum). The counter-cases prove the rule by engineering the vacuum shut: the Ottoman kanun converted open fratricidal war into a single sanctioned purge, and the Golden Bull's fixed seven-elector college bounded the contest. Second, the **autonomy-equals-secession identity**: the same grant that makes a delegated seat *functional* makes it able to secede — Carolingian frontier dukes needing foreign-policy authority, Han provincial *mu* holding fused civil-fiscal-military power, personalized Roman legions bonded to a bonus-granting general. Autonomy is never a free variable. Third, **displacement grievance**: elevating one power_base at another's expense (HRE *ministeriales* over free nobility; Ottoman timar reshuffles) primes the displaced type to relitigate on the next unrelated trigger. Fourth, **channel starvation**: where no institutional contest channel exists, elite grievance exits as assassination — the Pazzi (against informal, un-seated Medici dominance), the Ides (no legal recall of a *perpetuo* dictator), Honnō-ji.

**How legitimacy collapsed.** The collapse modes vary but rhyme. Legitimacy resting on a *single* fragile ritual degrades the office to a commodity — the Praetorians literally auctioning the throne to Didius Julianus is the reductio. **Split legitimating bodies** produce parallel, mutually-hostile claims at the same rank: the Papal Schism, contested HRE elections, and the Japanese Northern-and-Southern Courts all fission legitimacy itself, resolvable only by an *extraordinary* higher-tier body distinct from the normal procedure. Venice supplies the clean non-MoH inverse — legitimacy sourced from "was correct procedure followed," so a collapse can read as slow venality/attrition rather than a dramatic portent.

**How power devolved.** The organizing principle is universal: **rank = secession blast-radius**. Capital-embedded high-rank actors blast the top tier (the Janissary Ağa, the Bohemian estates at Kingdom scale, a personalized legion); provincial mid-rank actors stay regional (the Ottoman *ayan*, lesser estates). The inversions sharpen it rather than break it — Honnō-ji shows blast-radius scaling with the *thinness of the layer above* the defector; Sforza shows a *contracted external enforcer* collapsing a polity with a structural hole at its enforcement layer; Venice shows formal rank exceeding real Directive power. And **aggregate low-rank leverage** can force the same governance-mode shift as one high-rank revolt (the Ottoman Sened-i İttifak; the simultaneous maturation of Carolingian magnates at 887–888).

**What varies most.** Three axes. **Fuse length**: fast-fuse (Ōnin, Ciompi, Pazzi) vs. slow-fuse generational erosion (Kamakura's unrewarded Mongol-war service, 1274→1333; the silent Carolingian missi-audit tension) vs. multi-decade cascade with *no relief valve* (Rome's Crisis of the Third Century). **Interpretive-signal source**: juridical code (Ottoman kanun), religious authority (fatwa, excommunication, Taiping Dao), constitutional reading (the Senate reading Gracchan re-election as kingship), or acclamation ritual (the legions). **Presence of a relief valve**: Venice's graduated removal ladder and procurator sinecure, and the Ottoman grand-vizier-as-scapegoat, bled pressure before an all-or-nothing uprising — Rome's third century, lacking any, cascaded. A final divergence: structural fixes only work if they bind the *enforcement* layer — the Tetrarchy and the Go-Tairō both reproduced the disease because army loyalty stayed bonded to a named seat-holder, not an impersonal charter.

## Ottoman Empire — Conflicts & Power Struggles

### Fratricide as designed civil-war prevention (and its cost) — 1451-1595
Because succession was an open contest among all living sons rather than fixed primogeniture, every sultan's death without a codified rule risked multi-year civil war (as happened before codification, e.g., the **Ottoman Interregnum, 1402-1413**, following Bayezid I's defeat and capture at Ankara by Timur — a decade-long war among his four sons Süleyman, Musa, Isa, and eventually victor **Mehmed I**). Mehmed II's 1478 kanunname converted this structural succession-instability into a sanctioned, bounded act: the winning claimant kills his brothers immediately, trading a slow multi-actor civil war for a fast single-transition atrocity. Mehmed III's 1595 execution of 19 brothers (plus pregnant concubines) is the practice's maximal exercise, and also its reputational breaking point.
=> Valoria design hook: exemplar of the **collision-of-stresses primitive** grounded in non-MoH precedent — the *material threshold* is "sultan dies without pre-resolved single heir" and the *interpretive trigger* is "kanun explicitly authorizes fratricide as legitimate, not usurpation"; only when both are present does the succession event resonate into a civil-war-avoiding purge rather than a contested war of succession. Model an equivalent Country-tier succession rule where a codified kanun-equivalent flips a contested-succession event from "civil war" outcome-space into "single sanctioned purge" outcome-space.

### The Kafes system's downstream governance failure
Replacing fratricide with lifetime seclusion (from c. 1603) solved the immediate violence problem but created a chronic one: princes enthroned after decades in the Kafes (e.g., **Mustafa I**, enthroned 1617 and 1622 after prior confinement, widely regarded as unfit to rule and deposed twice) lacked administrative experience, military command exposure, or independent patronage networks — producing a run of weak sultans whose actual governance was captured by Valide Sultans, Grand Viziers, or the Janissary corps.
=> Valoria design hook: models delayed second-order consequence of a succession-safety mechanic — pair any "safe succession" rule (Kafes-equivalent) with a mandatory Legitimacy/competence penalty on the new seat-holder that only manifests several turns into the reign, so players feel the trade-off rather than seeing it as a free civil-war-prevention button.

### Janissary corps revolts — "kazan kaldırma" (overturning the cauldron)
The Janissaries' company cauldrons (kazan) were a corps-identity object; refusing to eat from them, or overturning them in the barracks square, was the corps' formal signal of mutiny/no-confidence, escalating to armed revolt. Landmark events:
- **1622 — deposition and murder of Osman II ("Genç Osman")**: Osman II's plan to replace/dilute the Janissary-devshirme establishment with a loyal provincial army triggered a Janissary-ulema coalition revolt; he was deposed, imprisoned, and strangled — the **first regicide of a reigning Ottoman sultan by his own troops**, shattering the taboo that the dynasty itself was untouchable even as individual sultans could be deposed.
- **1631, 1687 (deposition of Mehmed IV after the failed Second Siege of Vienna, 1683, and the Great Turkish War's reversals), 1703 (the "Edirne Event," deposing Mustafa II)** — each combining military-failure discontent with a Şeyhülislam fatwa legitimating deposition.
- **1730 — Patrona Halil Revolt**: a Janissary/guild coalition (led by an ex-Janissary, Patrona Halil) deposed Ahmed III over war losses (to Persia) and resented Westernizing court extravagance (the "Tulip Era").
- **1807 — Kabakçı Mustafa revolt**: Janissaries deposed reformist Selim III specifically to block his **Nizam-ı Cedid** ("New Order") army, which threatened their military-monopoly power_base.
- **1826 — the Auspicious Incident (Vaka-i Hayriye)**: Mahmud II, having built an alternative loyal artillery force first, provoked the Janissaries into open revolt and then had them bombarded in their barracks, formally abolishing the corps — the mechanism-resolution of two centuries of recurring revolt by eliminating the actor rather than negotiating with it again.
=> Valoria design hook: direct precedent for the **collision-of-stresses primitive**: every successful Janissary deposition combined (a) a *material threshold* — military defeat, fiscal strain on soldier pay, or a reform threatening the corps' monopoly — with (b) an *interpretive trigger* — a Şeyhülislam fatwa or ulema co-signature converting "mutiny" into "legitimate correction." Model a faction-revolt mechanic that requires both an objective strain signal AND an independent legitimating-authority signal before a Standing-ladder actor's rank-appropriate secession/revolt resolves successfully — a bare grievance without the legitimating signal should fizzle, exactly as many lesser 17th-century Janissary grumbles did without ulema backing.

### Rank-scoped secession: the corps as a single high-rank actor, the ayan as many low-rank ones
The Janissary corps, when it revolted, could depose a sitting sultan (Country-tier blast radius) because its rank/power_base was concentrated in a single corps-wide chain of command with capital-city presence. By contrast, 18th-century provincial notables (**ayan**) — local landholding/tax-farming strongmen who filled the vacuum left by timar-system decay — could each only destabilize their own Territory/Duchy, never depose the sultan directly; their collective leverage was eventually formalized (not overthrown) in the **Sened-i İttifak (Deed of Alliance), 1808**, a negotiated pact between Sultan Mahmud II and the assembled ayan limiting central authority in exchange for their military support.
=> Valoria design hook: textbook case for **rank = secession blast-radius** — a rank-7-equivalent, capital-embedded actor (Janissary Ağa/corps) can blast the Country tier; a rank-3-equivalent provincial power_base holder (ayan) can only blast their own Territory/Duchy — and the Sened-i İttifak is a template for a **NEGOTIATED_ESTATES** governance_mode transition forced by a mass of simultaneous mid-rank secession threats rather than one high-rank actor, i.e., aggregate low-rank leverage can force the same governance_mode shift a single high-rank revolt would.

### Celali Revolts and timar-system collapse (c. 1595-1610, recurring to 1650s)
Currency debasement, population pressure, and the crown's growing preference for musket-armed mercenary infantry (**sekban**) over timar cavalry caused mass dispossession of sipahis and unemployed mercenary bands, who coalesced into the **Celali Revolts** — widespread Anatolian brigandage/rebellion that at points controlled large swaths of the interior. This is the fiscal-military system's own advancement/demotion route (timar reversion) generating its own insurgent class when reversion outpaced reassignment.
=> Valoria design hook: models a **suppression/backfire** dynamic on the fiscal/land-grant layer rather than the cultural layer specified in the base primitive — mass, uncompensated demotion on the Standing ladder (timar reversion without redeployment) should generate a roaming, un-enfactioned population that converts a fiscal-policy problem into an open Territory-tier insurgency, i.e., demotion at scale is not consequence-free and should hand the problem to a slower-resolving track rather than resolving cleanly.

### The Şeyhülislam's fatwa as an independent deposition trigger
In 1622, 1687, and 1703 the Şeyhülislam's fatwa functioned as a genuinely independent authority signal — juridically converting a military mutiny into a religiously sanctioned regime change — separate from both the Janissary corps' material force and the sultan's own authority chain.
=> Valoria design hook: exemplar for a standing **ideological/juridical power_base** authority that sits outside the scale hierarchy chain-of-command entirely (not Monarch, not Parliament) but can still gate whether a rank-7 secession event resolves as "successful legitimate correction" or "suppressed illegitimate mutiny" — a third kind of chain-bypass-adjacent authority worth distinguishing from the two ratified bypasses (Monarch, Parliament).

### Recognition-fission and reaggregation precedent: millet boundary shifts
Millet boundaries were not static: the **Bulgarian Exarchate's** split from the Rum millet's Patriarchate (recognized by Ottoman firman in 1870) is a clean historical instance of the Crown formally chartering a diverged confessional-national population out of an existing recognized bloc in response to sustained internal divergence (language, national feeling) that the original millet structure could no longer contain — followed by decades of contested reaggregation/boundary disputes between Exarchist and Patriarchist populations in Macedonia.
=> Valoria design hook: direct historical template for **recognition-fission** as a Crown-negotiated mechanic (not a unilateral secession) — the 1870 firman is the model for the formal Crown act that charters a sub-faction out, and the subsequent Macedonia contest is the model for the follow-on **reaggregation** struggle where allegiance-bloc boundaries stay contested for a long post-fission period rather than resolving instantly.

## 1. The Gracchan rupture (133 BC, 121 BC): assembly vs. Senate as structural collision, not just personality clash

**Tiberius Gracchus** (tribune 133 BC) bypassed the Senate entirely by taking his land-reform bill directly to the concilium plebis, then had his fellow tribune Octavius illegally deposed by mass vote when Octavius vetoed it, then sought unprecedented re-election — and was clubbed to death by a senatorial mob led by the *Pontifex Maximus* Scipio Nasica, the Senate's first resort to lethal force against a domestic political rival. His brother **Gaius Gracchus** (tribune 123–122 BC) built a broader coalition (equestrians, Italian allies, urban plebs) and was killed in 121 BC when the Senate passed its first **senatus consultum ultimum** ("let the consuls see that the state takes no harm") — an emergency decree with no constitutional basis authorizing extrajudicial killing of citizens deemed a threat to the state. **Mechanism**: the tribunate's veto/sacrosanctity was designed as a *check*, but had no corresponding check on a tribune weaponizing the assembly against the Senate's institutional prerogatives — the system had a hole that only raw violence could fill once both sides ran out of legal moves. => Valoria design hook: models the **collision-of-stresses primitive precisely**: a *material threshold* (land/debt crisis, real distributional grievance) plus an *interpretive trigger* (Tiberius's re-election bid, read by the Senate as a bid for kingship) had to co-occur for the assembly-vs-council conflict to go lethal — encode the SCU itself as an emergent, *ad hoc*, non-chartered emergency-bypass action a council can invoke once both signals cross threshold simultaneously, explicitly outside any pre-defined chain-bypass authority.

## 2. Marius vs. Sulla and the militarization of the cursus (107–78 BC)

Marius's recruitment of the *capite censi* (landless proletariat) into the legions (107 BC, the "Marian reforms") created armies personally loyal to their general (who alone could secure their discharge bonus/land) rather than to the state — decoupling military command from civic office's traditional restraint. Sulla marched a Roman army on Rome itself (88 BC, a first), was driven out, marched on it again (82 BC), had himself made **dictator legibus scribendis et rei publicae constituendae** ("dictator for writing laws and reconstituting the state") with no time limit (breaking the Republic's own 6-month dictatorship cap), executed the proscriptions, then — notably — voluntarily resigned the dictatorship and retired (79 BC), restoring the old constitutional forms even as he'd just shown they were hostage to whoever controlled an army. **Mechanism**: once legionary loyalty personalized to a general with land/bonus-granting power, the formal cursus's age-gates and elections became irrelevant to whoever held the actual coercive asset. => Valoria design hook: models the **rank = secession blast-radius principle at maximum scale** — when a rank-holder's power_base flips from "elected office" to "personal army loyalty," their effective secession blast-radius should recompute to include every soldier whose land-grant-bond Ascendancy is tied to that general specifically, not to the state — the entire legion becomes a single collapsing/reaggregating bloc.

## 3. Caesar's civil war (49–45 BC) and the Ides of March (44 BC): the Senate's fear of a permanent chain-bypass

The Senate's demand that Caesar disband his army and return as a private citizen (thereby exposing him to prosecution his enemies had prepared) versus Caesar's demand to stand for consulship *in absentia* while retaining command — an irreconcilable collision because Roman law had no mechanism for a general to safely transition back into civilian rank once he held overwhelming force. Caesar crossed the Rubicon (49 BC — the river marking the legal boundary past which a general could not bring troops into Italy without becoming an enemy of the state), won the civil war, was made **dictator perpetuo** ("dictator in perpetuity," February 44 BC) — explicitly removing the temporal cap that had made Sulla's dictatorship self-limiting — and was assassinated a month later by a senatorial conspiracy (**Brutus, Cassius**, and ~60 others) explicitly because *perpetuo* signaled the chain-bypass had become permanent and unrecallable by any existing institution. **Mechanism**: the conspirators had no legal recall mechanism against a perpetual dictator, so assassination was the only remaining "demotion route" available to the Standing ladder's own senior members. => Valoria design hook: if a Monarch chain-bypass authority ever removes its own *temporal or recall* cap (i.e., converts a scoped/renewable grant into a permanent one), the game should treat that specific state-transition as an automatic trigger check for "the collision-of-stresses primitive" among the highest-Standing OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL members — a permanent-grant transition should be a distinguishable event type from an ordinary power increase, because historically it is what specifically catalyzes assassination-tier conflict rather than mere resentment.

## 4. The Year of Four Emperors (69 AD) and Praetorian/legionary king-making

After Nero's suicide (68 AD) ended the Julio-Claudian line with no designated heir, four men were acclaimed emperor within a single year by different armies: **Galba** (acclaimed by Spanish legions, then murdered by the Praetorian Guard for reneging on a promised donative), **Otho** (bought the Praetorians' support with cash, then lost to Vitellius's Rhine legions at First Bedriacum), **Vitellius** (Rhine legions), and **Vespasian** (Danube/eastern legions, who won at Second Bedriacum/Cremona and founded the Flavian dynasty). **Mechanism**: once the Principate's legitimacy rested on *auctoritas* rather than a codified succession law, and once the legions (not the Senate) held physical coercive capacity, "who is emperor" reduced to "which army/army-bloc currently controls Rome and can pay its own donative" — a pure military-Standing contest with the Senate reduced to ratifying whoever won. Tacitus's verdict (*Histories* 1.4): Galba's fall revealed "the secret of empire, that an emperor could be made elsewhere than at Rome" (*arcanum imperii*). => Valoria design hook: models the **collision-of-stresses primitive** at succession-vacuum scale — the material threshold (no designated heir + treasury able/unable to pay donative) plus the interpretive trigger (a legion's acclamation ritual) must both fire for a legion to attempt king-making; and models **recognition-fission/reaggregation** directly — four regional legion-blocs briefly recognized four different "factions" before violently reaggregating into one, exactly the Crown-adjacent redraw-the-allegiance-blocs mechanic, here executed by force rather than negotiation.

## 5. The Praetorian Guard auctions the throne: Didius Julianus (193 AD)

After Pertinax (successor to the murdered Commodus) was himself killed by mutinous Praetorians after a 3-month reign for trying to enforce discipline and cut their pay, the Praetorian Guard literally put the imperial title up for **public auction outside their camp**, awarding it to the highest bidder, **Didius Julianus** (25,000 sesterces per guardsman) over Pertinax's father-in-law Sulpicianus. Julianus reigned 66 days before being deposed and executed once Septimius Severus's Danube legions marched on Rome, disgusted at the mockery of legitimacy. **Mechanism**: once "recognition by the Praetorian Guard" became the operative legitimation ritual (having replaced Senate/assembly consent in practice), and the Guard's loyalty was purely donative-purchased rather than institutionally bound, the office became a literal commodity — the ultimate reductio of "purchase" as an advancement route colliding with every other legitimation claim simultaneously. => Valoria design hook: models the collapse case for a **"purchased" Ascendancy power_base seat placed at the very top of the ladder** — because a purchased seat carries an inherently weaker L/PS floor (see hierarchy_md §4), installing one at Monarch-tier via bypass should trigger an immediate, compounding L/PS crisis across every OTHER regional power center simultaneously, not a localized penalty — modeling why Julianus's reign collapsed in barely two months against essentially unanimous external non-recognition.

## 6. The Crisis of the Third Century (235–284 AD): sustained collision, no single mechanism

Between Severus Alexander's murder (235 AD) and Diocletian's accession (284 AD), Rome saw roughly 25-30 claimant emperors (the "barracks emperors" / *Soldatenkaiser*), most raised and killed by their own troops within months, compounded by simultaneous external pressure (Sassanid Persia, Gothic incursions) and internal fragmentation into breakaway regimes — the **Gallic Empire** (260–274 AD, Postumus and successors, controlling Gaul/Britain/Spain) and the **Palmyrene Empire** (267–273 AD, Odaenathus then Zenobia, controlling the eastern provinces) — both functioning as fully separate governance structures with their own institutions before being militarily reabsorbed by Aurelian (270–275 AD). **Mechanism**: with imperial legitimacy resting entirely on army loyalty and no reliable succession law, *every* legion-bloc had latent king-making capacity, so any simultaneous external-pressure shock (Persian invasion capturing Emperor Valerian alive, 260 AD) plus a legitimacy-interpretive shock (a rival army's acclamation) reliably produced fission. This is the closest Roman analogue to a genuine "cascading collision-of-stresses" system-collapse, resolved not by any single mechanism but by Diocletian's structural redesign (tetrarchy, decoupling succession from unitary rule). => Valoria design hook: this is the strongest non-Mandate-of-Heaven precedent for a **multi-decade cascading collapse driven by repeated collision-of-stresses events with no single relief valve** — model as a faction-wide "instability accumulator" that doesn't reset between individual succession crises, so each subsequent collision has a lower material threshold to trigger fission, until a structural (not personnel) fix — analogous to the Tetrarchy — is adopted; and Palmyra/the Gallic Empire are clean **recognition-fission** case studies: a diverged, geographically-coherent population under sustained neglect chartering its own parallel governance before being reaggregated by force.

## 7. The Tetrarchy's collapse (306–324 AD): the fix that reproduced the disease

Diocletian's designed four-way power-sharing (two Augusti, two Caesares, meant to regularize succession by seniority and adoption rather than blood) collapsed within two years of his own retirement (305 AD): Constantius Chlorus's son **Constantine** was acclaimed by his father's troops at York (306 AD) in defiance of the tetrarchic succession plan (which favored a different, adopted successor), triggering a two-decade multi-cornered civil war among six simultaneous claimants at points, resolved only when Constantine defeated Maxentius (Battle of the Milvian Bridge, 312 AD) and finally Licinius (324 AD) to reunify the empire under one ruler — precisely undoing the power-sharing structure Diocletian had built. **Mechanism**: a structure explicitly designed to prevent single-army king-making by distributing legitimacy across four co-equal seats still could not survive the *first* instance of a legion acting exactly as legions had for a century — proclaiming their own general regardless of the chartered succession rule — because no enforcement mechanism bound the legions themselves to the tetrarchic charter. => Valoria design hook: a critical cautionary design hook for the "Tetrarchy as anti-secession mechanism" hook proposed in modes_md §4 — a Crown-negotiated multi-seat power-sharing structure only suppresses collision if the *military/enforcement* layer is also bound to the charter, not just the top-level seats; if Valoria implements a tetrarchy-like multi-Monarch power-share, it must gate legion/army-equivalent loyalty to the *structure* itself (not to any one seat-holder) or the mechanic will reliably reproduce exactly the single-claimant collapse it was built to prevent — a strong argument for tying army loyalty checks to an impersonal charter-Key rather than a named seat-holder Key.

## 8. Adoptive vs. dynastic succession: the Nerva-Antonine "best-man" experiment and its Severan/Antonine unwind

Following Domitian's assassination (96 AD) and the resulting legitimacy vacuum, the Senate-installed **Nerva** (96–98 AD) adopted a proven general, **Trajan**, as heir rather than relying on blood descent — inaugurating a run of adoptive successions (Trajan→Hadrian→Antoninus Pius→Marcus Aurelius, 98–161 AD) later idealized by historians (Gibbon's "five good emperors") as the regime choosing competence over inheritance precisely because none of the first four had a surviving natural son, making adoption a low-cost virtue. The run ended the moment **Marcus Aurelius** *did* have a surviving son, **Commodus** (r. 180–192 AD), whom he elevated to co-emperor and successor by blood rather than merit — and Commodus's erratic, increasingly autocratic reign ended in his own assassination (192 AD), triggering the "Year of the Five Emperors" (193 AD) and ultimately the Severan dynasty, whose own succession (Caracalla's murder of his co-emperor brother Geta, 211 AD; the dynasty's terminal collapse into further barracks-emperor chaos by 235 AD) shows dynastic succession reproducing exactly the fratricidal-succession risk that adoptive succession had been praised for avoiding. **Mechanism**: adoptive succession only "worked" as long as it was unforced by a competing blood claimant; the instant a natural heir existed, the *emotional/dynastic* claim reliably out-competed the *institutional/merit* claim for the incumbent's own preference, because the choice was the incumbent's alone with no external check. => Valoria design hook: models a **succession-mode toggle with a hidden fragility condition** — an "adoptive/meritocratic succession" governance rule should be tracked as contingent on "no competing blood-heir currently exists," and the moment a blood-heir is born/comes of age to a sitting Monarch, the game should silently flag succession-mode as at-risk of reverting to dynastic-claim regardless of the nominal rule, forcing the player/AI incumbent to make an explicit, costly (L/PS-priced) choice to *uphold* meritocratic succession against their own heir, rather than treating the succession-mode as a static setting.

## 9. Suppression/backfire: the Jewish revolts as converting a military problem into a permanent one

Judaea's status as a client-then-directly-annexed province (6 AD) under governors insensitive to Jewish monotheistic law (Pilate's introduction of imperial standards into Jerusalem; Caligula's order, 40 AD, to install his statue in the Temple, defused only by his assassination before enforcement) escalated through repeated frictions to the **First Jewish-Roman War (66–73 AD)**, ending in the destruction of the Second Temple (70 AD) and the mass suicide at Masada (73/74 AD) — and, crucially, did *not* resolve the underlying grievance, recurring as the **Kitos War** (115–117 AD, diaspora revolts in Cyrenaica/Egypt/Cyprus) and the **Bar Kokhba Revolt** (132–136 AD), triggered specifically by Hadrian's plan to found a pagan colony (Aelia Capitolina) on Jerusalem's ruins and ban circumcision — after which Rome renamed the province *Syria Palaestina* and barred Jews from Jerusalem outright, a permanent cultural-suppression policy that produced a permanent diaspora-identity problem rather than a solved military one. **Mechanism**: each military "victory" addressed the immediate uprising but the underlying suppression (of religious practice, of physical/political return) regenerated the interpretive trigger for the next uprising a generation later, escalating the material stakes (temple → holy city → circumcision-as-identity-marker) each round. => Valoria design hook: canonical, non-MoH-grounded case for the **suppression/backfire principle**: a cultural-suppression action that "solves" an immediate Fieldwork/Investigation-flagged unrest event should not clear the underlying grievance flag — it should convert it into a dormant, generational-timescale flag that re-arms with a *lower* trigger threshold on recurrence (Bar Kokhba's trigger — a colony + a circumcision ban — was smaller than the First Revolt's, showing escalation in trigger-sensitivity, not just repetition), explicitly modeling suppression as making the problem *harder* to solve militarily each time it recurs, handing it definitionally to the non-military resolution track.

## 3. Conflicts & Power Struggles

### 3.1 Structural driver: partible inheritance vs. imperial unity (the master tension)
Frankish customary practice (rooted in Salic/Merovingian precedent) divided a father's realm among ALL surviving legitimate sons — there was no primogeniture norm. This collided repeatedly with the idea (increasingly asserted rhetorically as "empire," especially after 800) that the Frankish/Roman imperium should be UNITARY and indivisible, or at least hierarchically unified under one senior brother. Every succession from Charlemagne onward re-litigates this collision. The **Divisio Regnorum (806)** shows Charlemagne himself trying to pre-plan a division among his three living sons WITHOUT settling the unity question (no single son was named emperor over the others) — moot when Charles the Younger and Pippin of Italy predeceased him, leaving Louis the Pious sole heir by accident of mortality, not design.
=> Valoria design hook: model **partible inheritance vs. unity** as the archetypal **collision-of-stresses** case for the Duchy tier: the material threshold signal is "multiple viable heirs simultaneously alive at the ruler's death," the interpretive trigger is "does the prevailing succession doctrine at that moment favor partition or primogeniture" — only when BOTH align does a partition-triggered civil war fire, giving designers a lever to make some successions peaceful and others catastrophic from the same family structure.

### 3.2 The *Ordinatio Imperii* (817) and Bernard of Italy's revolt (817–818)
Louis the Pious, attempting to avert future division, issued the *Ordinatio Imperii* at the Assembly of Aachen (817): eldest son Lothar I made co-emperor and effective overlord, younger sons Pippin (Aquitaine) and Louis (Bavaria) given subordinate sub-kingdoms explicitly bound to defer to Lothar, and — critically — Louis the Pious's nephew **Bernard, King of Italy** (son of the deceased Pippin of Italy), was not consulted and found his kingdom's status downgraded to subordinate-to-Lothar without his input. Bernard revolted (817), was betrayed/abandoned by his own supporters when Louis marched south, captured, tried at an assembly, and blinded (818, died of the wound) — see hierarchy_md §2.4. **Mechanism**: an inheritance-settlement issued by fiat at an assembly, without securing prior buy-in from an affected junior title-holder, converted a legal instrument into a casus belli.
=> Valoria design hook: a scripted case for **Consensus resolver failure** — an Ordinatio-type unilateral top-down settlement (AUTOCRATIC_FIAT dressed as assembly ratification) that skips the "assent" step for an affected stakeholder should register a latent holdout flag that can detonate later, rather than resolving cleanly at issuance.

### 3.3 The Rebellions against Louis the Pious (830 and 833) — "Field of Lies"
Louis's REVISION of the 817 settlement (crowning his fourth son by his second wife Judith, **Charles the Bald**, with a new sub-kingdom carved out in 829 — perceived as reneging on the 817 settlement to favor the "new" family) triggered two rebellions by his elder sons Lothar, Pippin of Aquitaine, and Louis the German: a first, limited coup (830, Louis briefly held captive, then restored by a counter-assembly) and the far more serious **Field of Lies / Rotfeld (Colmar, June 833)**, where the sons' combined army, reinforced by **Pope Gregory IV's personal presence** lending unprecedented ecclesiastical cover to a purely dynastic dispute, induced mass desertion from Louis's camp overnight. Louis was captured and subjected to public penance at Compiègne/Soissons (October 833) — effectively deposed — then restored within a year (Thionville, 834) once Lothar's brothers, wary of Lothar's own ambitions, defected back to their father. **Mechanism**: an assembly (placitum) that could legitimate a king could equally be captured and turned into the instrument of his deposition — the same body served both functions, and the deciding factor was which faction controlled the coalition of attendees on the day, not any fixed procedural rule.
=> Valoria design hook: model the **placitum/DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY as a contestable venue, not a fixed-outcome resolver** — the Consensus resolver's outcome should depend on which sub-factions currently hold assent/stand-aside/holdout status THAT SESSION, meaning the same assembly-type event can legitimate OR depose the same ruler in different plays depending on prior faction-relationship state, exactly as 817 assemblies crowned Louis's plan and 833 assemblies broke it.

### 3.4 Fontenoy (25 June 841) and the Treaty/Partition of Verdun (August 843)
Louis the Pious's death (840) triggered full civil war among his three surviving sons: **Lothar I** (claiming the whole per 817's spirit), opposed by younger half-brothers **Charles the Bald** and **Louis the German**, who sealed their alliance with the famous **Oaths of Strasbourg (14 February 842)** — sworn in each other's vernacular (proto-French and proto-German respectively, preserved by Nithard, an eyewitness and Charlemagne's own grandson, in his *Histories*) precisely so each brother's ARMY could understand and verify the oath, an explicit anti-fraud design in the oath-taking technology itself. The decisive **Battle of Fontenoy-en-Puisaye (25 June 841)** inflicted catastrophic Frankish-on-Frankish casualties (contemporary laments, e.g. Angilbert's poem, treat it as a moral catastrophe, "brother against brother"). Exhausted, the three brothers negotiated the **Treaty of Verdun (August 843)**: Charles the Bald took West Francia, Louis the German East Francia, and Lothar I a long, geographically incoherent **Middle Kingdom (Lotharingia + the Italian kingdom + the imperial title)** stretching from Frisia to Rome — chosen specifically to include Aachen and Rome (the coronation sites) so Lothar retained symbolic primacy despite losing practical unity. **Mechanism**: partible inheritance (§3.1) executed via NEGOTIATED_ESTATES-style three-party bargaining after military stalemate, not adjudicated by any standing procedure — the division's borders were drawn by commissioners balancing revenue/population equivalence, not linguistic or "natural" boundaries, planting the seed of a permanently contested Middle Kingdom (Lotharingia) fought over by West/East Francia for the next millennium.
=> Valoria design hook: THE canonical Duchy-tier partition template — model as a **NEGOTIATED_ESTATES governance_mode resolution**: a three-(or n-)way commissioner-balanced split of territory tiers, explicitly decoupled from population/faction-allegiance lines, which should seed a standing, low-boil territorial-claim tension flag on the "orphaned" middle zone (Lotharingia-equivalent) that can be reactivated by either flanking Duchy in future play — a deliberate long-fuse conflict generator distinct from a one-shot succession war.

### 3.5 Further partitions: Meerssen (870) and Ribemont (880)
Lothar I's Middle Kingdom fractured further at his own death (855) into three (Italy/Lotharingia/Provence for his three sons); when his son **Lothar II** died without a legitimate heir (869, his marriage/divorce scandal — hierarchy_md §2.2 — having failed to legitimate Waldrada's children), Charles the Bald and Louis the German simply partitioned Lotharingia between themselves at the **Treaty of Meerssen (870)** without consulting Lothar II's own former vassals; a further adjustment followed at **Ribemont (880)**. **Mechanism**: once the "Middle Kingdom" pattern existed, EVERY subsequent middle-kingdom failure-of-heirs event triggered automatic reabsorption-by-partition from the flanking kingdoms — the 843 template became a recurring script, not a one-off.
=> Valoria design hook: a **reaggregation** case study — model "failure of heirs in a buffer Duchy" as an automatic trigger inviting BOTH neighboring Duchies to file competing partition claims, resolved by a chain-bypass Monarch/Parliament arbitration rather than by the buffer Duchy's own now-absent leadership, mirroring how Lotharingia's fate was decided entirely by outsiders.

### 3.6 Tassilo of Bavaria's deposition (787–788) — center vs. semi-autonomous duchy
Duke Tassilo III of Bavaria, a Carolingian cousin ruling a quasi-autonomous duchy, had sworn fidelity oaths to Charlemagne (757, renewed 781, 787) but maintained an independent foreign policy (Lombard and Avar alliances) that Charlemagne read as treasonous. A three-pronged 787 invasion forced Tassilo's submission without battle; a subsequent 788 Assembly of Ingelheim tried him (with witnesses testifying to secret Avar contacts), convicted him of *harisliz* (desertion from the host) and treason, and Charlemagne converted the death sentence to monastic tonsure, absorbing Bavaria directly under counts. **Mechanism**: a duchy's semi-autonomous military/diplomatic capacity (necessary for frontier defense) was structurally indistinguishable from treasonous independent foreign policy — the same capability that justified the duke's existence was the capability that got him destroyed once the center judged him unreliable.
=> Valoria design hook: model **rank = secession blast-radius** directly here — a Duchy-tier seat with delegated foreign-policy authority (necessary for frontier function) should carry an inherent, visible "independent action" flag that the Monarch chain-bypass authority can escalate into a treason proceeding; the design should make clear that granting a Duchy the AUTONOMY it needs to function is the SAME grant that enables its secession, so players feel Charlemagne's dilemma rather than treating loyalty as a free variable.

### 3.7 The Stellinga revolt (841–842) — a subordinate-population revolt exploiting elite civil war
While Lothar, Louis the German, and Charles the Bald warred over the succession, **Louis the German incited (or at minimum tolerated) the Stellinga**, a movement of Saxon *frilingi* and *lazzi* (freedmen/semi-free commoner strata, recently and forcibly Christianized and subordinated after the 772–804 Saxon Wars) who rose to reclaim pre-conquest customary law and status, attacking the Saxon nobility that had collaborated with Frankish rule. It was crushed once the royal brothers reconciled (842–843) and needed Saxon noble support restored. **Mechanism**: elite civil war opened a structural gap in which a suppressed subordinate population — created by an EARLIER act of conquest/cultural suppression — could mobilize; the revolt was not "caused" by the civil war so much as ENABLED by the momentary withdrawal of unified elite coercive capacity that had been suppressing it all along.
=> Valoria design hook: direct instantiation of the **suppression/backfire** principle — a population subjected to cultural suppression (post-conquest Saxony) should carry a latent, non-decaying grievance Key that a LATER, unrelated elite-level conflict (a succession war) can inadvertently trigger by drawing down the suppressing faction's local military presence — converting Fieldwork/Investigation's "unsolvable problem" framing into a concrete rule: suppression debt comes due whenever the suppressor's attention is forced elsewhere, not on a fixed timer.

### 3.8 The crisis of 887–888: deposition of Charles the Fat and the end of unified Carolingian kingship
**Charles III "the Fat"**, briefly reunifying nearly the whole Carolingian inheritance (884, after a cascade of unexpected deaths among his cousins), proved militarily and politically inadequate — most damagingly, buying off the Viking siege of Paris (886) with tribute and safe passage rather than fighting, which the besieged Parisian defenders (led by Count Odo/Eudes) and the wider East Frankish nobility took as a legitimacy-destroying failure of the ruler's core military function. He was **deposed by an assembly of East Frankish magnates at Tribur (November 887)**, and died months later (888). No single Carolingian claimant commanded assent to succeed him empire-wide: instead, regional magnates crowned LOCAL non-Carolingian (or illegitimate-line Carolingian) kings almost simultaneously — **Odo, Count of Paris**, in West Francia; **Arnulf of Carinthia** (an illegitimate Carolingian) in East Francia; **Guy of Spoleto** and **Berengar of Friuli** competing in Italy; **Rudolph** founding a kingdom in Burgundy. **Mechanism**: the Standing-ladder's own logic turned against the dynasty — regional counts/margraves who had spent a century accumulating hereditary benefices, immunities, and local military followings (hierarchy_md §2.4) now had the independent capacity to simply crown one of their own once the dynasty's central military-legitimation function (the ability to WIN and to reward followers with conquest spoils, per §2.2) visibly failed. This is a legitimacy collapse driven by observable failure of a ruler's core deliverable (military protection), not by portent-doctrine — the Frankish equivalent mechanism to Roman soldier-emperor turnover: performance-based withdrawal of elite consent.
=> Valoria design hook: model as a **non-MoH legitimacy-collapse collision**: the two independently-arriving signals are (1) a material threshold — the ruler's demonstrated failure to deliver the core Directive/Need of military protection (the Paris tribute), and (2) an interpretive trigger — the accumulated, already-near-Rank-cap Standing of multiple regional magnates who have independently reached a threshold where they no longer need the Crown's patronage to hold their own seats. When both align, the result is not one usurper but SIMULTANEOUS regional recognition-fission into multiple new, non-dynastic Duchy/Country-tier seats — a good template for a "central authority fails its core deliverable while peripheral Standing has independently matured" collapse mode, grounded in observable performance rather than any omen-logic.

### 3.9 Ruler-vs-assembly and count-vs-missi friction as chronic (not crisis) conflict
Beneath the dynastic-crisis peaks, capitularies themselves repeatedly complain of counts obstructing missi, nobles skipping the general host-summons (*heriban*) and paying/being fined the *heribannum* penalty instead, and local placita corrupted by the very counts they were meant to check — evidence of standing, low-intensity friction between the center's oversight instruments and the provincial office-holders they audited, never fully resolved and arguably a root cause feeding directly into the long-run drift toward hereditary entrenchment (§2.4) that culminates in 887–888.
=> Valoria design hook: model this as a persistent BACKGROUND tension score (not an event) between the missi-audit channel and comital Standing — let it accumulate silently across many resolution-quality cycles so that even "successful" individual audits can still be building toward an eventual 887-888-style mass recognition-fission if the underlying entrenchment trend isn't separately addressed by the Crown.

**Mechanism:** the Empire's structure required the Emperor to appoint/invest bishops and abbots (who held vast imperial fiefs and were essential to royal administration, the *Reichskirche* system deliberately built by the Ottonians and Salians as a counterweight to hereditary secular dukes) — but the sacramental *content* of that office belonged, in Gregorian reform doctrine, exclusively to the Church. ## 2. The Great Interregnum (1250–1273) — elective monarchy without a tie-breaking rule

**Mechanism:** the death of Frederick II (1250) and collapse of the Hohenstaufen line (his son Conrad IV died 1254, grandson Conradin executed 1268 after his failed Italian bid) left the imperial title contested between weak, largely non-resident claimants (Richard of Cornwall, Alfonso X of Castile) elected by a still-unfixed, bribable set of magnates with no majority-sufficiency rule — precisely the gap the 1356 Golden Bull was engineered to close. With no functioning central authority, princes ruled their territories as de facto sovereigns, private feuding (Fehde) went unchecked, and robber-baron activity along the Rhine flourished, prompting the **Rhenish League of Towns (Rheinischer Städtebund, 1254)**, a self-organized urban military-defensive confederation formed explicitly *because* the imperial peace-keeping function had ceased to exist.
=> Valoria design hook: **NEGOTIATED_ESTATES → self-organized OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL fallback** — when the top-tier chain-bypass authority (Monarch) is vacant or contested for a sustained period, subordinate Settlement/Territory-tier actors should be able to spontaneously form a horizontal defensive league (a bottom-up OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL) that persists even after the vacancy resolves — the Rhenish League didn't dissolve immediately once Rudolf I was elected in 1273, modeling institutional residue that outlives its triggering crisis.

## 3. The Saxon Rebellion (1073–1075) and the 1077 civil war — ruler-vs-magnates collision over centralization

**Mechanism:** Henry IV's attempt to build royal power in Saxony (garrisoning royal castles like the Harzburg, extracting dues, favoring ministeriales over free Saxon nobles for administration) triggered a full-scale regional revolt among Saxon dukes and free peasants alike — an early case of the ministerialis-as-royal-counterweight strategy (modes_md §6) itself becoming the *grievance* that provoked rebellion, because the free nobility resented being administratively bypassed by an unfree service class loyal only to the king. Suppressed militarily at the **Battle of Homburg/Unstrut (1075)**, but the humiliated Saxon nobility then formed the core of the princely coalition that forced Canossa (1077) and elected the antiking Rudolf — the same conflict, re-erupting through the investiture crisis's cover.
=> Valoria design hook: **Ascendancy power_base friction** — deliberately elevating a bureaucratic/ministeriales power_base at the expense of an existing kinship/land power_base (free nobility) is a documented trigger for that displaced power_base's faction to seek an external interpretive pretext (here, the papal-supremacy dispute) to relitigate the displacement militarily — model a Standing-ladder reshuffle that favors one power_base type as generating latent grievance in the displaced type, primed to detonate on the next unrelated collision-of-stresses event.

## 4. The Golden Bull (1356) as conflict-resolution engineering, and its own residual instability

**Mechanism:** the Golden Bull's majority-rule (4-of-7) fix eliminated the *double-election* failure mode but did not eliminate contested succession entirely — it shifted the battlefield to **which house could reliably secure 4 votes**, producing durable elector-bloc politics (the Wittelsbach-Habsburg-Luxembourg triangulation of the 14th–15th centuries) and, later, direct purchase/pledging of electoral seats themselves (Sigismund's 1415/1417 sale of Brandenburg to the Hohenzollerns, hierarchy_md "routes up") as electors used their fixed scarcity value as leverage/collateral.
=> Valoria design hook: **fixed-cap OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL seats become a scarce, tradeable commodity** — once a Standing-ladder council is capped at a fixed seat count (as the Golden Bull capped electors at 7), model seat-holders gaining leverage *from the cap itself* (their vote becomes more valuable precisely because it can't be diluted), enabling purchased-power_base transactions (seat sales, pledges) that wouldn't be possible in an uncapped, expandable council.

## 5. The Bohemian Revolt and Thirty Years' War (1618–1648) — estates rebellion escalating to continental collapse

**Mechanism:** the **Defenestration of Prague (23 May 1618)** — Protestant Bohemian estates throwing two Catholic imperial regents from a window of Prague Castle — was the interpretive/symbolic trigger; the material threshold was the accumulated grievance over Emperor Matthias/Ferdinand II's rollback of Bohemian Protestant privileges (the 1609 Letter of Majesty) and the looming succession of the militantly Catholic Ferdinand II as King of Bohemia, an *elective* crown the Bohemian estates then attempted to exercise by **deposing Ferdinand and electing Frederick V of the Palatinate (the "Winter King") in his place, 1619** — an explicit exercise of an estates' elective-deposition power against a sitting, already-crowned monarch, crushed at the **Battle of White Mountain (1620)**. The Palatinate electorate confiscation (hierarchy_md, "routes down") that followed then dragged in Denmark, Sweden, and France over the next 28 years, ending only at the **Peace of Westphalia (1648)**, which confirmed *territorial sovereignty* (*jus belli ac pacis*, the right of princes to make war, peace, and alliances independently) for all imperial estates — constitutionally gutting the Emperor's residual central authority while leaving the empty imperial shell formally intact until 1806.
=> Valoria design hook: **collision-of-stresses primitive, non-MoH grounded, at maximal scale** — Bohemia is the paradigm two-signal collision (accumulated material grievance over rescinded charter-rights + a single symbolic/interpretive act) triggering a cascade that ultimately restructures the *entire* Scale hierarchy's power balance (Westphalia's territorial-sovereignty grant permanently rebalances Country-vs-Duchy authority) rather than just punishing the immediate rebels — model the largest-scale Valoria collision events as capable of permanently reweighting the L/PS cascade's default authority split between tiers, not just resolving the immediate dispute.
=> Valoria design hook: **rank = secession blast-radius** confirmed at Country scale — Bohemia's estates held Kingdom-tier (near-Country-tier) rank, so their deposition-and-re-election act pulled in the entire electoral college's legitimacy (a rival elector, the Palatine, was the replacement candidate) and triggered continent-wide war; a lower-rank estates' revolt with an identical grievance-plus-trigger pattern should stay regionally contained under this same principle.

## 6. The Reichsreform crisis (1495–1500s) — Emperor vs. Diet over centralizing fiscal-judicial reform

**Mechanism:** Maximilian I's 1495 Diet of Worms package (Perpetual Peace, Reichskammergericht, Common Penny tax) was a genuine centralizing bid, immediately met with estate resistance specifically on the *funding* mechanism (the Common Penny) even while accepting the *institutional* reforms (the court, the peace ban) — because the estates would accept new central adjudicative machinery but not the fiscal capacity that would make the Emperor independently powerful. The 1500 Diet of Augsburg's response, creating the **Reichsregiment** (an imperial governing council dominated by *estate* nominees, not imperial appointees, functioning 1500–1502 and again 1521–1530), shows the estates' countermove: accept centralization only if the estates themselves control its executive machinery.
=> Valoria design hook: **NEGOTIATED_ESTATES as an active check on Monarch chain-bypass authority** — model estates as capable of *conditionally* accepting a Monarch-tier centralizing reform (new courts, new peace bans) while stripping out precisely the fiscal/executive teeth that would let the Monarch act unilaterally afterward, and offering a rival estate-controlled executive council as the price of their assent — a middle ground between full assent and holdout that the binary Consensus resolver should be able to express as a *conditional* assent (accept structure, deny funding/staffing).

## 7. Mediatization and the 1803/1806 collapse — structural exhaustion, not a single battle

**Mechanism:** by 1803, the Empire's central institutions (Reichstag, Reichskammergericht) had atrophied into near-irrelevance for two centuries (post-Westphalia territorial sovereignty had already hollowed the center); the Reichsdeputationshauptschluss's mass mediatization of ecclesiastical territories and free cities (hierarchy_md/modes_md) was not a conflict *resolution* but a compensatory land-grab among the surviving secular princes, brokered under French occupation pressure rather than through any internal imperial procedure — the Empire's own constitutional machinery was bypassed by an external power (Napoleonic France) redrawing the internal hierarchy. The 1806 Confederation of the Rhine princes' formal renunciation of imperial allegiance, followed immediately by Francis II's abdication of the imperial title (6 August 1806), closed the Empire not through defeat in an imperial civil war but through the voluntary secession of its own mediatized/reaggregated upper tier once an external guarantor (Napoleon) offered a more attractive reaggregation.
=> Valoria design hook: **recognition-fission / reaggregation as an externally-brokered event, non-MoH collapse precedent** — this is a clean Roman/Byzantine-style institutional-legitimacy-collapse model (structural hollowing over centuries, then a final externally-triggered reaggregation that the nominal center cannot resist) to use whenever Valoria wants a top-tier polity's dissolution to read as *earned exhaustion* rather than a single dramatic battle — the Country-tier authority can be shown eroding across many prior negotiated-estates events (per §6, §5) before the final secession/reaggregation event merely ratifies an already-hollow center, exactly paralleling how the Rheinbund secession was a formality by 1806, not a surprise.

## The Ciompi Revolt (1378) — guild exclusion + fiscal-military strain

**Mechanism:** Florence's corporatist constitution gave political voice only to guild members; the **Ciompi** (unguilded wool-carders and related laborers, tens of thousands strong in the dominant Arte della Lana's workforce) had none, despite bearing the tax and conscription burden of the ruinous **War of the Eight Saints** against the Papacy (1375–1378), which had drained the treasury and provoked a special war levy. The revolt ignited in **June–July 1378** when a wool-worker leader's arrest under Gonfaloniere Salvestro de' Medici's own reformist agitation became the immediate spark on top of the standing material grievance (exclusion + fiscal squeeze). The wool-carder **Michele di Lando** was raised on shields as Gonfaloniere di Giustizia in a stunning inversion, three new guilds for the previously unguilded trades were chartered, and a broadened Signoria ran the city for four years — until the *popolo grasso* engineered a **Guelf oligarchic restoration in January 1382**, abolished the new guilds, and executed or exiled the Ciompi leadership. => Valoria design hook: this is a strong **non-MoH-grounded collision-of-stresses case**: the material threshold (decades of unrepresented, tax-burdened labor) and the interpretive trigger (a single arrest, amplified by an ongoing reformist Gonfaloniere's own agitation) arrived independently and only *together* produced revolution — model a Territory's labor-population Key emitting a large arithmetic gap on Directive (representation demanded vs. delivered = zero) that stays inert until a *second*, unrelated triggering event (an arrest, a levy) fires in the same cycle, at which point the two signals resonate into an uprising that temporarily promotes a rank-0 population bloc straight onto the Standing ladder — with the historical coda that oligarchic capture of the scrutiny/guild-charter mechanism reverses the gain within four years once the crisis passes, a good template for "revolutionary gains are reversible via list-control capture," not permanent.

## Cosimo de' Medici's exile and recall (1433–1434) — control of the scrutiny bag decides everything

**Mechanism:** The **Albizzi** oligarchy (led by **Rinaldo degli Albizzi**), fearing Cosimo de' Medici's growing bank-funded influence, used their control of the sitting Signoria to have him arrested and, in **September 1433**, exiled to Padua/Venice for ten years on manufactured charges. Because exile did not touch the Medici bank's international correspondent network or its Florentine debtors, Cosimo's faction kept operating from outside; when the **1434 scrutiny** (helped by a sympathetically-packed electoral commission and a favorable Signoria draw in September 1434) produced a Signoria willing to recall him, Cosimo returned in **October 1434** and immediately used an emergency **balìa** to reverse the exile onto his enemies — Rinaldo degli Albizzi and other Albizzi partisans were exiled in turn, and the scrutiny bags were re-packed with Medici loyalists for a generation. **Whoever drew the current Signoria decided who got exiled next** — a completely self-referential, capturable procedure.
=> Valoria design hook: model this as the canonical **"list-control capture reverses the polarity of the demotion weapon" event** — a faction that seizes the Initiation-Gate/eligibility-list control of a rival faction's own demotion mechanism can turn that exact mechanism back on the rival within a single cycle; this is the strongest available precedent for a Valoria mechanic where capturing a settlement's officeholder-eligibility resource doesn't just grant new appointments, it retroactively **weaponizes the existing removal procedure** against the prior controller.

## The Pazzi Conspiracy (26 April 1478) — informal power has no institutional recourse, so rivals go outside the system entirely

**Mechanism:** Because Medici primacy was *informal* (no office, see Modes §1.5), rival elite families like the **Pazzi** had **no constitutional channel** to contest it — there was no vote to win, no office to capture, because the thing they resented (Lorenzo's real power) didn't formally exist. Backed by **Pope Sixtus IV** (furious over Lorenzo's blocking of a Pazzi-financed papal land purchase at Imola) and the Archbishop of Pisa **Francesco Salviati**, the Pazzi and co-conspirators attacked Lorenzo and his brother **Giuliano de' Medici** during Mass in the Duomo on Easter Sunday 1478; Giuliano was killed, Lorenzo survived with a wound. The Signoria and populace rallied to Lorenzo immediately, and reprisals were ferocious and extra-legal: Salviati was hanged from the Palazzo della Signoria's windows still in his archbishop's vestments (triggering a papal interdict and the ensuing Pazzi War, 1478–1480), and Lorenzo used the crisis's political cover to push through the **1480 Council of Seventy** constitutional capture (see Modes §1.5). Martines's *April Blood* is the definitive account.
=> Valoria design hook: this is the strongest precedent for **"an informal (shadow-AUTOCRATIC-under-nominal-OLIGARCHIC) power structure converts elite grievance into assassination rather than institutional contest, because no institutional contest channel exists"** — Valoria should model a captured-council settlement (per §1.5's hook) as having an elevated *assassination/coup* event weight specifically because its normal Consensus-resolver holdout/stand-aside options are unavailable to a disaffected elite actor who holds no real seat to hold out *from*; and the crisis's aftermath (constitutional tightening exploiting the shock) is a clean template for "a survived coup attempt is itself the material+interpretive collision that lets the incumbent ratchet formal power," independent of any MoH-style portent logic.

## The Milanese succession crisis and Sforza's seizure (1447–1454) — a title with no succession law collapses into whoever has an army

**Mechanism:** covered in Modes §1 — Filippo Maria Visconti's death without a legitimate male heir (13 Aug 1447) exposed that the ducal title was a **personal imperial grant**, not a codified hereditary office; the resulting **Golden Ambrosian Republic** had popular legitimacy and even battlefield wins but no independent standing army, so its own hired condottiere, Francesco Sforza, simply starved the city (eight-month siege, surrender 22 March 1450) and took the title by marriage-claim, internationally ratified by the **Treaty of Lodi (1454)**. The structural driver: **outsourcing the coercive-enforcement function of a polity (to a mercenary) while retaining only the legitimating function (republican assembly) is unstable whenever the mercenary's contract lapses.**
=> Valoria design hook: model this via the **"rank = secession blast radius" principle inverted** — here the *contracted enforcer*, not a disloyal internal rank-holder, is the one whose defection collapses the whole polity, because the polity's own Standing ladder had a **structural hole at the enforcement layer** (no native military rank above condottiere-contract); a Territory that hires 100% of its coercive capacity from an external military power_base faction should carry a standing collapse-risk flag proportional to that external faction's rank, independent of the Territory's own internal L/PS score.

## Genoa's chronic doge instability and repeated foreign submission (1339–1528) — a neutral office with no independent force is permanently capturable

**Mechanism:** covered in Modes §3 — the doge, created 1339 explicitly to stand above Fieschi/Grimaldi/Doria/Spinola faction war, had no private force of his own in a city where every great *albergo* fielded its own galleys and armed retinue, so the office was captured, deposed, or the officeholder assassinated in rolling succession, and twice (1421–1435 Visconti, and multiple French submissions) Genoa's oligarchy chose **submission to an external Country-tier power** over continued internal civil war, only reaching stability when Andrea Doria's **1528 reform** flattened eligibility onto a fixed 28-*albergo* roll and bound Genoa into the Habsburg-Spanish orbit.
=> Valoria design hook: model "voluntary submission to an external Monarch-tier authority" as a **legitimate, mechanically-supported de-escalation move available to a collapsing Territory**, not just something that happens to it — i.e., a Territory whose internal factional violence exceeds a threshold should be able to *itself* invoke the chain-bypass Monarch authority to absorb it, trading Territory-tier autonomy for stability, mirroring Genoa's repeated real-historical choice — grounded entirely in Genoese/Milanese precedent, no MoH content involved.

## The Papal Schism (1378–1417) — split electors, dual/triple legitimacy claims, prolonged institutional civil war

**Mechanism:** The 1378 conclave, held under mob pressure demanding an Italian pope, elected **Urban VI**, whose erratic conduct led the same cardinal-electors to declare the election invalid under duress and elect a rival, **Clement VII**, who set up at Avignon — producing the **Western/Great Schism**, eventually a *three*-way split (a Pisan line added 1409) with each line's cardinals denying the others' legitimacy. Because papal legitimacy runs entirely through the electing body's own consensus (conclave, §Modes 4), a split electorate produces **structurally unresolvable dual sovereignty** — no higher authority exists to arbitrate a dispute about who legitimately *is* the highest authority. Resolution required the **Council of Constance (1414–1418)**, which forced all three claimants to resign or be deposed and elected **Martin V** (1417) as sole pope — an extraordinary, one-off supra-conclave mechanism (conciliarism) invoked specifically because the ordinary electoral mechanism had failed to produce unique legitimacy.
=> Valoria design hook: this is strong non-MoH grounding for a **"split-electorate produces parallel, mutually-hostile legitimacy claims at the same rank" failure mode** for the Consensus resolver — if a holdout faction is large enough to convene its *own* rival consensus body rather than merely holding out within the existing one, Valoria should model a fission into two simultaneously "legitimate" claimants at the same Standing rank (a recognition-fission-adjacent case, but triggered by electorate schism rather than population divergence), resolvable only by an extraordinary, higher-tier convened body distinct from the normal appointment procedure — exactly the kind of two-tier "ordinary procedure failed, escalate to an extraordinary one" design Valoria can use without any zaiyi/portent content.

## Savonarola's fall (1497–1498) — charismatic legitimacy stripped by the one authority it never controlled

**Mechanism:** Savonarola's power was purely prophetic/charismatic (Modes §1.6), resting on sustained sermons and popular fervor, not on any civic office. His escalating attacks on papal corruption led **Pope Alexander VI** to excommunicate him (May 1497) — a sanction that, because Savonarola's legitimacy claim was explicitly *religious*, struck at the one pillar he could not route around institutionally. The botched **trial-by-fire** ordeal (7 April 1498, called off by rain/technical dispute) then destroyed his *popular* legitimacy pillar too, in a single afternoon. With both pillars gone simultaneously, the Signoria (a body he had never formally controlled) arrested him within days; he was tortured, tried, and burned 23 May 1498.
=> Valoria design hook: model a charismatic (ideological power_base, no formal rank) actor as vulnerable to a **two-pillar collapse** distinct from an office-holder's demotion: an external authority's sanction (an "ideological disqualification" analogous to excommunication) plus an independent popular-legitimacy failure (a failed public "proof" event) must **both** land to fully zero out a charismatic actor's standing — a second, clean non-MoH collision-of-stresses case (interpretive/institutional signal + a material/observable popular-facing failure) for a leader who was never on the Standing ladder to begin with.

## The Italian Wars watershed (1494) — a Territory-scale polity cannot resist a Country-scale consolidating monarchy

**Mechanism:** French king **Charles VIII**'s invasion of Italy (1494), asserting an Angevin claim to Naples, marched essentially unopposed down the peninsula; Florence's Piero de' Medici's panicked capitulation of Florentine fortresses without Signoria authorization was the proximate trigger for the Medici's expulsion (9 November 1494) and installation of the Great Council republic (§Modes 1.6). The deeper mechanism: none of the peninsula's sophisticated but small-scale governance systems (guild republics, condottiere-principalities, elective ecclesiastical monarchy) had the fiscal-military depth of a consolidating Country-scale dynastic monarchy once that monarchy chose to intervene directly.
=> Valoria design hook: use 1494 as the template event for **an external Country-tier Monarch-bypass actor's unilateral intervention instantly destabilizing every internal governance_mode beneath it, regardless of that tier's own L/PS standing** — the local ruler's individual failure (Piero's capitulation) is what convener/history remembers, but the *scale mismatch* is the real mechanism, a useful non-MoH-grounded "why did the empire collapse" answer set distinct from portent/legitimacy-cycle logic: it's a raw capability-and-scale mismatch, not a moral-legitimacy judgment on the ruler.

## 3. Conflicts & Power Struggles — Republic of Venice

### 3.1 The Candiano usurpation attempt and the 976 revolt
**Doge Pietro IV Candiano** (r. 959–976) attempted to convert the elective dogeship into a hereditary monarchy for his family, alienating the other great houses; a popular uprising in 976 stormed the Doge's Palace, killed Candiano and his infant son, and burned a large part of the city (including the first St Mark's Basilica) in the process. **Mechanism:** an elective office with no term limit and growing personal power, absent a legal removal valve, converts any dynastic ambition into a binary outcome — total success (hereditary monarchy) or violent overthrow — because the constitution supplied no intermediate correction. The long-run response was not a single reform but the beginning of the multi-century pattern of grinding down ducal power piece by piece (culminating in 1172, §1.2).
=> Valoria design hook: use this as the grounding precedent (not MoH) for why a Monarch-tier chain-bypass authority in Valoria should default to *some* removal valve short of total civil collapse — model a graduated Legitimacy/Popular-Support threshold ladder (soft rebuke → forced co-signature requirement → deposition) so that a Monarch drifting toward unilateral rule triggers intermediate consequences before an all-or-nothing uprising resolver fires.

### 3.2 The Tiepolo–Querini Conspiracy, 15 June 1310
Patricians **Bajamonte Tiepolo**, **Marco Querini**, and **Badoero Badoer**, backed by other disaffected nobles, clergy, and commoners, organized a three-column coup meant to converge on the Piazza San Marco, seize the Doge's Palace, and overthrow **Doge Pietro Gradenigo**'s government — the same Gradenigo who thirteen years earlier had sponsored the Serrata (§1.4). The plot collapsed from betrayal, poor coordination, and a sudden storm; Querini and Badoer were killed in the fighting, Tiepolo surrendered and was exiled to Dalmatia under damnatio memoriae (his house demolished, replaced with a memorial "Column of Infamy"). **Mechanism:** the Serrata thirteen years prior had converted an open political order into a closed one, generating a durable pool of excluded or downgraded noble grievance (Tiepolo and Querini were of prominent but disfavored houses) that a charismatic figure could mobilize together with genuinely non-noble discontent. The Republic's institutional answer was to charter the **Council of Ten** as a permanent internal-security tribunal (§1.6) — a standing-body response to a standing structural grievance, rather than addressing the grievance itself.
=> Valoria design hook: model this as the canonical **recognition-fission grievance pipeline**: an elite-closure event (Serrata-analog) creates a durable excluded sub-population inside an existing faction; if unaddressed, it should be eligible to charter itself out as a genuine recognition-fission sub-faction rather than only ever resolving as an internal coup attempt — give the orchestrator a fork where the "excluded bloc" either fissions cleanly (new sub-faction, negotiated) or curdles into conspiracy (suppressed, escalating), so the same structural grievance can resolve two different ways depending on whether a Crown/negotiated recognition path was offered in time.

### 3.3 The Marino Faliero Conspiracy, 1355 — a Doge versus his own oligarchy
**Doge Marino Faliero**, elected 11 September 1354 at roughly age 80 amid public fury over the Republic's naval defeat at Portolungo, was publicly humiliated when patrician **Michele Steno** scrawled an obscene insinuation about the Doge's young wife's fidelity on the ducal throne; the Quarantia's sentence on Steno (a brief prison term) struck Faliero as contemptibly lenient — living proof that even the Doge could not get the oligarchy to discipline one of its own on his behalf. Faliero responded by conspiring with a small circle of Arsenal workers and craftsmen (*arsenalotti*, tailor Bertuccio Isarello, architect Filippo Calendario) to incite a popular rising against the patriciate itself, apparently intending to make himself an absolute prince on the strength of non-noble support. Betrayed before execution, the plot was crushed by the Council of Ten; ten conspirators were hanged from the Palace windows and Faliero himself was beheaded on the same staircase where he had been crowned, on 17 April 1355 — followed immediately (20 July 1355) by the Council of Ten's permanent constitutional entrenchment. **Mechanism:** the very structure that made the Doge a figurehead (§1.3) — real power resting with the collegial nobility, not the office he nominally topped — meant the *one* class of person structurally positioned to try to overthrow the oligarchy on behalf of the excluded orders was the Doge himself, precisely because he alone occupied the capstone rank while genuinely lacking capstone power.
=> Valoria design hook: this is the sharpest available non-MoH illustration of "rank sets secession blast radius but does not equal actual power" — model a standing-ladder edge case where the highest-rank seat (rank 7) has ceremonial Access far exceeding its enforceable Directive-issuing power, so a rank-7 holder who tries to unilaterally exceed their real Access should trigger a special high-blast-radius collision (the whole council apparatus mobilizes at once) rather than a routine single-rank Demotion — the mismatch between formal rank and real power is itself the design payload, not a bug to smooth over.

### 3.4 The War of Chioggia, 1378–81 — existential threat and franchise dilation
The Fourth Genoese War brought a Genoese fleet into the Venetian lagoon itself (the fall of Chioggia, August 1379), the closest the Republic ever came to conquest. To fund the war and reward common defenders, the Great Council admitted roughly 30 new families (the **case nuove**) to the patriciate in 1381 in exchange for war-loan contributions — a deliberate, ratified *expansion* of the closed hierarchy under existential stress, rather than either regime collapse or rigid refusal to adapt. **Mechanism:** a material-threshold crisis (near-conquest) combined with a genuine interpretive question (can the closed 1297 order survive without new resources/legitimacy from below?) resolved through the constitution's own amendment channel (Great Council vote) rather than through revolution — proof that Venice's structure could absorb a two-signal collision without a collapse mechanic, the clearest non-MoH illustration available of "collision of stresses resolved by institutional accommodation, not portent-driven dynastic change."
=> Valoria design hook: ground the collision-of-stresses primitive's non-MoH template here explicitly — the material threshold (near-conquest) and the interpretive trigger (Council debate over whether the closed hierarchy could survive unaided) arrived together and were resolved through an ordinary ratification Key (a Great Council vote widening the standing ladder's rank-0 gate), not a portent/omen resolver — use this as the worked example proving the collision primitive doesn't require any MoH-shaped "heaven judges the ruler" logic to produce a legible two-signal resonance event.

### 3.5 The League of Cambrai and the Battle of Agnadello, 14 May 1509
A coalition of the Papacy, France, Spain, and the Emperor united against Venice's mainland (Terraferma) empire; the catastrophic defeat at **Agnadello** on 14 May 1509 saw nearly the entire mainland empire collapse within weeks as subject cities (Padua, Verona, Vicenza, Brescia) opened their gates to the invaders rather than resist for Venice. **Mechanism — the two-signal collision:** the material threshold was the battlefield defeat itself; the independently-arriving interpretive trigger was the Terraferma cities' own judgment that Venetian rule was no longer worth defending (decades of resented rectorial administration by patrician *Rettori*, §2.3) — the military loss alone would not have caused mass defection without that separate, pre-existing legitimacy deficit resonating with it at the same moment. Venice's institutional response — no regime change, no doge deposed, the same councils that had governed before Agnadello negotiating the mainland's methodical recovery over the following years (Padua retaken within months; the League itself fractured by 1510) — is the clean structural counterpoint: a collegial, non-dynastic system survived a defeat that would likely have toppled a single ruling house.
=> Valoria design hook: this is the flagship non-MoH grounding for the collision-of-stresses primitive at the Country/Duchy scale: model Agnadello as a settlement/territory-scale event requiring both a material Key (military-defeat threshold on a Territory's garrison/L-PS resource) AND an independently-computed interpretive Key (a Province or Settlement's own pre-existing consent deficit toward its parent Duchy) to co-arrive before a mass-defection cascade resolves — a defeat alone, or a legitimacy deficit alone, should each be survivable; only the collision should cascade.

### 3.6 Council of Ten scope-creep and the 1582–83 reform
The Ten's mandate — chartered narrowly in 1310 for a single conspiracy, made permanent in 1355 for "state security" — expanded over two centuries into de facto oversight of finance, morals, and even ordinary civil disputes whenever a case could be framed as security-adjacent, provoking recurring Senate complaints that an emergency organ had swallowed the ordinary legislature's jurisdiction. A significant constitutional correction in **1582–83** (associated with reformist pressure under Doge **Nicolò da Ponte**) formally curtailed the Ten's competence back toward genuine state-security matters and reasserted Senate primacy over ordinary legislation. **Mechanism:** a standing security body chartered under emergency powers has a structural incentive to keep re-classifying ordinary business as security business (the same jurisdictional gravity that produces the modern "national security creep" problem), correctable only by the chartering assembly reasserting itself explicitly — it does not self-correct.
=> Valoria design hook: model any Council-of-Ten-analog security office with an explicit, decaying jurisdiction flag rather than an open-ended "security" tag — require its Directive-issuing Keys to cite a specific, auditable threat-Key it's responding to (mirroring the Avogadori-style legality check, §1.7), so scope-creep becomes mechanically visible (a rising ratio of un-cited security Keys) and triggerable as its own conflict event rather than only being noticed narratively.

### 3.7 The Antonio Foscarini affair, 1622–23 — secrecy apparatus failure and self-correction
Patrician Antonio Foscarini was tried and executed by the Council of Ten in 1622 on charges of leaking state secrets to foreign ambassadors, based on a paid informer's testimony; within a year the informer's testimony was found fabricated, and the Ten formally exonerated Foscarini posthumously, restoring his name and (as far as possible) his family's standing. **Mechanism:** an enforcement apparatus optimized for secrecy and speed (no public trial, no cross-examination of informers) trades away exactly the error-correction that a slower, more transparent system would have — the failure mode is inherent to the design, not an aberration, and the system's only fix was after-the-fact reversal, never prevention.
=> Valoria design hook: this is the direct precedent for building a reversible Dismissal-to-Dishonored state into the standing ladder (§2.6 hook) — but generalize it: any security-office Key that resolves a Demotion based on a single uncorroborated informer-Key should be flagged as provisionally-reversible for a fixed window, so the resolution-quality bridge can retroactively restore standing when a later Key exposes the original evidence as fabricated, modeling institutional fallibility instead of treating every Demotion as final.

### 3.8 Barnaboti venality and the terminal decline, 18th century–1797
By the 18th century a large bloc of impoverished patricians (the *barnaboti*) held full formal Great Council rank but no independent wealth, and survived by selling their votes to wealthier casate or to whichever policy faction would subsidize them — a slow currency-debasement of the standing ladder itself (§2.6) rather than any single dramatic coup. The Republic's institutions never lost a war of succession or suffered a civil war in this period; it simply exhausted its own deliberative capacity, and when Napoleon's forces presented an ultimatum in 1797, **Doge Ludovico Manin** and the Great Council voted their own dissolution rather than resist. **Mechanism:** a closed-hierarchy system with a fixed-size elite and no mechanism for either shrinking the elite or growing the underlying wealth/purpose backing each rank inevitably debases its own standing currency once population-within-rank outpaces the resources that rank was originally built to allocate.
=> Valoria design hook: model long-horizon faction health with an explicit standing-currency-per-capita metric inside a rank tier (not just per-rank thresholds) — a faction whose rank-7/rank-6 population grows faster than its resource base should accrue a slow-burning "venality" pressure that degrades Consensus-resolver outcomes (more holdouts convertible by bribery) well before any single triggering event, giving designers a legible "death by attrition" collapse path distinct from both MoH-style portents and single dramatic conspiracies.

## 3. Conflicts and Power Struggles

### 3.1 The Rebellion of the Seven States (Qi Guo zhi Luan), 154 BCE
- **Mechanism**: the founding-era compromise (kin-kings holding real fiscal/military autonomy inside the commandery-kingdom system, modes_md §1.4) generated an inevitable center-periphery collision once the kings' resources matured — the Kingdom of Wu alone controlled copper mines and salt flats and had built an independent treasury and army over 40 years. Emperor Jing's advisor **Chao Cuo** pushed a direct territorial-reduction policy (*xue fan*, "trimming the feudatories") against the kingdoms in 154 BCE; **Liu Bi, King of Wu**, led a coalition of seven kings (Wu, Chu, Jiaoxi, Jiaodong, Zichuan, Jinan, Zhao) in open revolt rather than accept piecemeal reduction. The court sacrificed Chao Cuo (executed to appease the rebels, per his rivals' urging) but the rebellion continued regardless, was crushed within three months by General **Zhou Yafu**, and its defeat let the center impose direct administrative control over the surviving kingdoms (stripping kings of the right to appoint their own officials) well before Tui'en Ling finished the job by inheritance-partition in 127 BCE.
  => Valoria design hook: a textbook **collision-of-stresses** case with clean two-signal structure — the *material threshold* (Wu's economic/military buildup crossing a viability line for revolt) plus the *interpretive trigger* (Chao Cuo's reduction edict, read as an existential threat rather than routine policy) had to arrive together; model a Duchy-tier revolt threshold requiring both an accumulated-capacity stat crossing a floor AND a triggering Crown Directive read as existential, not either alone.

### 3.2 Wang Mang's usurpation and the Xin interregnum, 9–23 CE
- **Mechanism**: Wang Mang, nephew of an Empress Dowager, rose through the standard consort-clan route (hierarchy_md §2.2) to General-in-Chief and regent for child emperors, then used the zaiyi/portent apparatus (modes_md §1.3) plus a manufactured groundswell of petitions "begging" him to accept the throne to stage a ritually correct *shanrang* abdication in 9 CE, founding the short-lived Xin dynasty. His subsequent radical reforms (land nationalization, currency chaos, restored "well-field" idealism) alienated both landholding gentry and peasantry simultaneously, and the **Red Eyebrows** (Chimei) peasant rebellion combined with a Liu-clan restoration movement to destroy Xin by 23 CE, restoring Han under Emperor Guangwu.
  => historical-only (NOT a Valoria mechanic per design-lead ruling) — but note the *structural* lesson generalizes on non-MoH grounds: Wang Mang's fall is a clean case of a regime alienating landholder and commoner constituencies **simultaneously** via one reform package, i.e. a **collision-of-stresses** where a single policy event supplies both signals at once. => Valoria design hook: model a "radical reform" Directive type that can simultaneously push two independent population-segment thresholds (e.g., landholder Legitimacy and Popular-Support) toward collapse from one action, without needing the portent-legitimacy framing at all — ground the collision purely in material reform impact, not omen interpretation.

### 3.3 The Partisan Prohibitions (Danggu zhi Huo), 166 and 169 CE
- **Mechanism**: Confucian scholar-officials and the Imperial University's student body (organized informal "pure critique," qingyi, opinion networks) increasingly used censorial memorials and public reputation-ranking of officials to attack eunuch faction abuses. In 166 CE, eunuch-aligned prosecutors got Emperor Huan to proscribe (ban from office, "forever") a list of leading critics as an illegal faction (*dang*, "clique") — the first Danggu. In 169 CE, under Emperor Ling, a planned counter-coup by regent General-in-Chief **Dou Wu** and Grand Tutor **Chen Fan** to exterminate the eunuchs preemptively was betrayed and pre-empted; the eunuchs struck first, killing Dou Wu and Chen Fan and executing/proscribing over 100 scholar-officials, with the ban extended to the officials' associates, students, and even fifth-degree relatives, effectively locking the entire "pure critique" faction and its second-generation heirs out of the bureaucracy until 184 CE.
  => Valoria design hook: this is a direct precedent for **suppression/backfire** — banning the qingyi faction from formal office did not eliminate their networks, it *converted a manageable factional-influence problem into an unpoliceable one*: proscribed scholars and their students went underground into private academies and regional gentry networks that resurfaced as the ideological backbone of anti-eunuch warlord coalitions two decades later. Model faction-proscription as removing the faction from the Standing ladder's visible/legible tracking (handing it to Fieldwork/Investigation to even locate) rather than removing its actual influence — proscription should measurably *reduce state visibility* into the faction's people while doing little to its real capacity.

### 3.4 The Yellow Turban Rebellion, 184 CE (formally suppressed 185, residual fighting to 205)
- **Mechanism**: **Zhang Jiao**, founder of the syncretic Taoist "Way of Great Peace" (Taiping Dao) millenarian movement, built a mass religious-mutual-aid network across eight provinces over a decade (explicitly organized into 36 *fang*, military-religious cells of 6,000–10,000 each) exploiting a real material substrate (repeated famine, flooding of the Yellow River, and heavy taxation) *and* an interpretive trigger (Taiping Dao's own portent-reading that the "Azure Heaven" of Han was dying and a new "Yellow Heaven" cycle was due in the *jiazi* year 184 — a deliberate appropriation of dynastic-cycle/zaiyi logic from below rather than from the throne). The revolt was betrayed to the court by an informant before its planned simultaneous multi-province launch date, forcing an improvised early rising that the Han center nonetheless could not suppress with existing standing forces, requiring emergency regional levies that became the seed of independent warlord armies (modes_md §1.7).
  => Valoria design hook: canonical **collision-of-stresses** instance with the two signals cleanly separable and *independently modelable without MoH*: (1) a material threshold — sustained famine/tax-burden crossing a tolerance floor across a population — plus (2) an interpretive trigger — a charismatic organizer's *religious/ideological framing* converting diffuse grievance into coordinated mass action on a specific date. Ground the "interpretive trigger" signal in the Taiping Dao organizational/millenarian precedent (not the throne's own zaiyi logic) to keep the hook clean of the banned MoH framing — implement as: a Territory-tier unrest stat crossing a material floor is necessary but not sufficient; a second, independent "ideological ignition" event (a Fieldwork/Investigation-visible organizer achieving a coordination threshold) must also fire before revolt triggers.

### 3.5 He Jin, the Eunuchs, and Dong Zhuo's seizure of the capital, 189 CE
- **Mechanism**: General-in-Chief **He Jin** (regent for the newly enthroned child Emperor Shao, brother of the Empress Dowager He) plotted with the "pure critique" faction and border general **Yuan Shao** to exterminate the eunuch faction outright, but hesitated to secure Dowager He's sign-off (she, herself dependent on eunuch support against rival consort-clan factions, refused); He Jin then made the fatal structural error of summoning outside frontier troops — most consequentially **Dong Zhuo**'s Liang Province army — to pressure the Dowager, a direct instance of a court faction inviting a chain-bypassing outside military authority into the capital to resolve an internal palace-access deadlock (modes_md §1.8). The Ten Attendants eunuch faction assassinated He Jin first (in the palace, 189 CE); Yuan Shao's troops then massacred the eunuchs wholesale; and Dong Zhuo's army, already marching, arrived in the resulting power vacuum, seized the fleeing child emperor, deposed him for the more pliable Emperor Xian, and became de facto military dictator of the exposed capital.
  => Valoria design hook: this is the paradigm case for restricting **chain-bypass authorities** — He Jin's summons of Dong Zhuo is exactly the failure mode the Monarch/Parliament chain-bypass design must guard against: an internal faction, deadlocked with the Crown itself over palace-access, reaches *outside* the normal chain to an armed third party to break the deadlock, and the third party then captures the prize instead of resolving the dispute. Model chain-bypass invocation as carrying an inherent risk parameter (the invoked outside force may seize the contested seat itself) scaling with how much larger the invoked force's rank/capacity is versus the inviting faction's.

### 3.6 Dong Zhuo's tyranny, the Yuan Shao coalition, and the war-band anacyclosis, 190–192 CE and after
- **Mechanism**: Dong Zhuo's court (191 CE, deposing/poisoning Emperor Shao, ruling through the captive Emperor Xian, later relocating the capital by force to Chang'an in 191 CE and burning Luoyang) provoked a **regional-governor coalition** under Yuan Shao's nominal leadership (190 CE) — precisely the mu/cishi provincial commanders whose fused civil-military authority (modes_md §1.7) let them raise independent armies at will. Dong Zhuo's 192 CE assassination (engineered by his own adopted son-general **Lü Bu** at the instigation of minister **Wang Yun**) did not restore the center; it simply removed the strongest single claimant, and the coalition itself immediately fractured into a decade of inter-warlord war (Yuan Shao vs. Gongsun Zan, Cao Cao vs. Yuan Shu/Lü Bu/Zhang Xiu, Sun Ce's independent southern conquest) with no return to unified command, culminating in Cao Cao's 200 CE defeat of Yuan Shao at Guandu and his eventual (196 CE onward) capture of the puppet emperor as the era's most durable legitimation asset.
  => Valoria design hook: ground the collapse/fragmentation sequence explicitly on **Polybian anacyclosis / Roman-late-imperial soldier-emperor precedent (non-MoH)** rather than dynastic-cycle framing — model a "center seized by a single military strongman" event as inherently *unstable equilibrium*, automatically spawning a counter-coalition among peer-rank regional seat-holders whose rank (province-scale, per §1.7) already gives them the blast-radius to secede; the strongman's assassination should not auto-restore central authority but instead roll the map back to multi-actor Duchy-vs-Duchy war, with "control of the legitimate seat" (the captive emperor/puppet monarch analog) modeled as a fought-over Legitimacy-boosting asset rather than a stable prize.

### 3.7 The scripted end of Han and the reusable usurpation template, 220 CE (and its Jin-dynasty repeat, 265 CE)
- **Mechanism**: Cao Cao (d. 220 CE) never took the throne himself, instead accumulating the ritual escalator of usurpation — Duke of Wei (213 CE), then King of Wei (216 CE), granted the **Nine Bestowments** (*jiuxi*, ceremonial grants of chariot/horses, robes, musical instruments, red gate, guard troops, bow-and-arrows, axe, sacrificial wine — a Han-era-invented ritual sequence signaling "this man now stands functionally outside normal subject hierarchy") — leaving the final *shanrang* abdication step to his son **Cao Pi**, who accepted Emperor Xian's ritually staged abdication in 220 CE, ending Han and founding Wei. The identical script (regional strongman → Nine Bestowments → son completes the abdication) was reused by the **Sima clan** against Wei itself in 265 CE (Sima Zhao took the Nine Bestowments; his son Sima Yan completed the abdication, founding Jin) — a mechanically reproducible institutional *pathway* for legitimate-seeming succession-by-force, not a one-off event.
  => Valoria design hook: model the Nine Bestowments sequence as a **named multi-stage Ascendancy track** ("ritual escalator") distinct from ordinary Standing-ladder promotion — a sufficiently powerful seat-holder (rank 6+, controlling the captive/nominal Monarch chain-bypass seat) can advance through a fixed sequence of formal grants that each incrementally increase their Access and Legitimacy at the *nominal* ruler's expense, with the final stage being a Crown-tier replacement event; keep this pathway grounded purely in the Roman-adoptive-emperor / Ottoman-accession-ritual comparative pattern of institutionalized succession-by-designated-strongman (non-MoH) rather than any portent/abdication-worthiness framing.

### 3.8 The Mu/Cishi governors as ongoing centrifugal structure through the whole Three Kingdoms period, 190s–229 CE
- **Mechanism**: once fused civil-military provincial authority existed as an institutional category (modes_md §1.7), it kept reproducing warlord-scale actors independent of any single crisis: **Liu Biao** in Jing Province ruled as an effectively sovereign mu for two decades (192–208 CE) paying only nominal tribute to whichever center held the puppet emperor; **Liu Yan** and his son **Liu Zhang** in Yi Province (188–214 CE) built an independent Shu-adjacent state later absorbed intact by Liu Bei; **Gongsun Du** in Liaodong (190–204 CE, succeeded by his son and grandson) ran a fully independent statelet on the northeastern frontier for three generations, styling himself effectively a king in all but name, until Cao Wei finally extinguished the line militarily in 238 CE. The formal Three Kingdoms founding dates (Wei 220 CE, Shu-Han 221 CE under Liu Bei, Wu 222/229 CE under Sun Quan) merely *ratified in name* territorial sovereignties that mu-derived governance had already produced in substance a generation earlier.
  => Valoria design hook: model provincial mu-equivalent seats as **capable of accreting a "sovereignty score" that outpaces their nominal Standing-ladder rank over successive turns even absent any single triggering event** — a Territory-tier military governor holding fused civil+fiscal+military authority for N consecutive turns without a Crown recall should automatically accrue Legitimacy/Popular-Support with their own local population at the Crown's expense, formalizing "long incumbency under fused authority" itself as a slow secession-pressure generator, independent of any discrete conflict trigger.

### 3.1 Jōkyū War (1221) — dual authority's first structural test

### 3.2 The Mongol invasions' unpaid bond (1274/1281) as slow-fuse collapse driver
**Mechanism:** see §2.3 — a successfully defended war that nonetheless produced zero shin-on-eligible land converted the vassal bond's reward side into a permanent deficit, papered over by debt-cancellation edicts (tokusei-rei, 1297) that solved the symptom (gokenin insolvency) while worsening the underlying credit/legitimacy environment. ### 3.3 Kemmu Restoration collapse and the Nanboku-chō schism (1333–1392)

=> Valoria design hook: model a **legitimacy-fission event**: a severe enough resolution-quality gap at the very top of the Crown track shouldn't just demote a seat, it should be able to fork the Crown authority itself into two rival Legitimacy tracks simultaneously recognized by different faction blocs, until a later negotiated reunification (Yoshimitsu 1392) — a harder, top-of-stack version of recognition-fission where the *authority itself* fissions, not just a sub-faction beneath it.

### 3.4 The Ōnin War (1467–1477) — the two-signal collision that ended the Muromachi order
**Mechanism:** two independently-arriving stresses collided in Kyoto in 1467. Signal one (material): the great kanrei-adjacent houses Hosokawa (Katsumoto) and Yamana (Sōzen) had rival armies already garrisoned in the capital from an unrelated prior feud. Signal two (interpretive/legitimacy): shogun Ashikaga Yoshimasa's own succession was unresolved — he had adopted his brother Yoshimi as heir, then his wife Hino Tomiko bore a son (Yoshihisa), reopening the succession question Yoshimasa had just closed. Neither factor alone would have produced a decade-long war (garrisoned rival armies without a succession dispute to fight over would likely have stood down; a succession dispute without pre-mobilized armies in the city would have been settled by negotiation as earlier ones had been) — the coincidence is what burned Kyoto and permanently broke shogunal authority, opening the Sengoku period.
=> Valoria design hook: this is the **canonical non-MoH illustration of the collision-of-stresses primitive** as specified — cite Ōnin explicitly in the mechanic's design note as the reference case: require a genuine material threshold (armed forces already positioned/mobilized for unrelated reasons) AND a genuine interpretive trigger (an open, contested succession/legitimacy question) to both be independently true before a settlement-shaking event resonates; either alone should fizzle.

### 3.5 Sengoku gekokujō — named cascade of rank-irrelevant usurpation (c. 1493–1582)
**Mechanism and named cases:** the Ōnin War's destruction of central enforcement meant local military capacity, not inherited rank, determined who actually held power — **Hōjō Sōun** (born a minor samurai of obscure origin) seized Izu province in 1493 and founded the "Later Hōjō" daimyo house from nothing; **Saitō Dōsan** overthrew his own lord's Toki clan in Mino c. 1542, rising from oil-merchant-family origins through the ranks of a retainer band to daimyo; **Matsunaga Hisahide**, a retainer, effectively seized control of his lord's (Miyoshi) domain and in 1565 was implicated in the killing of shogun **Ashikaga Yoshiteru** himself (the Eiroku Incident) — a retainer's retainer striking the shogun; **Akechi Mitsuhide**, one of Oda Nobunaga's own senior generals, betrayed and killed his lord at the **Honnō-ji Incident** (1582), the paradigm case of rank being no protection whatsoever against a subordinate's defection; and **Toyotomi Hideyoshi** himself rose from **ashigaru** (peasant footsoldier) to unifier of Japan and imperial regent (**Kanpaku**, 1585) — the ultimate gekokujō, achieved by seeking Fujiwara-line adoption into the court's regental office when his non-Minamoto lineage barred him from the shogunal title outright, i.e., substituting one legitimation channel for another when the standard cursus was structurally closed to him.
=> Valoria design hook (rank-as-blast-radius): Honnō-ji is the sharpest available case for **rank = secession blast-radius run in reverse** — Akechi's rank was "merely" senior-general, but Nobunaga's own concentration of unchecked personal authority meant a single subordinate's betrayal instantly destroyed the entire unification project's center, i.e., blast radius should scale not just with the defector's rank but with how thin the layer *above* the defector has become.
=> Valoria design hook (power_base substitution): Hideyoshi's Fujiwara-adoption route into Kanpaku rather than shogun is a concrete precedent for letting a seat-holder whose preferred Ascendancy power_base (military merit, in his case) is blocked by a hard eligibility rule (Minamoto lineage requirement) pursue a *different* power_base (patronage-by-adoption into a rival prestige hierarchy) to reach an equivalent top rank — model as an alternate Initiation-Gate a blocked climber can attempt instead of the default one.

### 3.8 Bakumatsu collision (1853–1868) — external shock meets dormant legitimacy claim

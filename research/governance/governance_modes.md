## Modes of Governance — Cross-Civilizational Synthesis

"Modes of governance" names the answer a polity gives, at every tier, to one question: *when a decision must be made, who is entitled to make it, and what must happen before their decision counts?* Across the eight civilizations surveyed — Ottoman, Roman, Carolingian, Holy Roman, Renaissance-Italian, Venetian, Han/Three-Kingdoms, and shogunal Japanese — that answer is never a single dial. It is a stack: a nominal authority, a procedure that binds or constrains that authority, a separate order that confers or withholds legitimacy, and a lived reality that routinely diverges from all three. The comparative lesson is that governance mode is not a costume a ruler wears but an emergent readout of *how* a seat-holder's power_base was granted and *what* must co-sign before their response resolves.

That maps directly onto Valoria's ratified Country→Duchy→Province→Territory→Settlement hierarchy and its proposed eight-value governance_mode taxonomy. The taxonomy's value is not that a polity picks one label, but that the label is a **constraint set on the legal response**: an OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL seat cannot emit a Directive a peer has vetoed; a NEGOTIATED_ESTATES body cannot resolve until its curiae sign in sequence; a LANDHOLDER_FRANCHISE crown must re-legitimate at every succession; a CONSENSUS_UNANIMITY chamber must reach assent or resolve a decaying holdout. History supplies working instances of all eight — and, more usefully, the transitions, drifts, and masks between them.

Two cross-cutting commitments fall out immediately, both load-bearing for the L/PS consent gate. First, legitimacy is a substance separate from formal authority, sourced from procedure-followed (Venice), a population-wide oath reservoir (Carolingian 802), accreted auctoritas (Rome), or dual-track recognition (Japan) — never from any cosmic mandate; the corpus deliberately grounds every collapse-and-collision argument in Contarini/Polybius, the Investiture two-signal, or a Roman Senate reasserting itself after a general's death, and Mandate of Heaven appears as history only. Second, autocracy is never "no rules": every fiat seat in the sample runs inside a second, independently-adjudicated order that can block it.

## Cross-civilizational synthesis

**Autocracy bounded by a parallel legitimacy order** is the most consistent finding. Ottoman AUTOCRATIC_FIAT runs inside kanun-versus-şeriat, the ulema able to issue a binding fatwa that forces a Country-tier legitimacy recalculation without ever sitting in the chain. Rome's Senate held no legislative power, yet its auctoritas throttled what an imperium-holder could execute. The Carolingian Church issued synodal Directive-Keys interlocking with royal capitularies; Han *tingyi* ran a genuine deliberative sub-procedure beneath a pure-fiat crown. What *varies* is only the nature of the bounding order — juridical-religious (Ottoman, Papal, Carolingian, HRE) versus civic-procedural (Roman auctoritas, Venetian procedure) — which Valoria can express as a faction-type whose power_base is ideological/juridical rather than territorial.

**Oligarchic councils split three ways** by how seats are differentiated and filled. *Portfolio-differentiated* councils give each seat its own veto (the Ottoman Divan's military-judicial, fiscal, and chancery seats under a recallable seal-holder). *Co-equal* councils cap and fix membership (the Golden Bull's indivisible electors; Japan's Hyōjōshū under the Shikimoku rulebook). *Estate-allocated* councils pre-assign seats to named blocs and resolve by sequential rather than simultaneous veto — the HRE Reichstag's three curiae, Japan's shogun as first-among-equals bound to named great-houses, Florence's guild-delegate Signoria — the paradigm NEGOTIATED_ESTATES mode, whose *documented representation-lag* (an estate taxed for generations before it wins a vote) Valoria should preserve as texture. Across all three, Rome's consular *intercessio* supplies the sharpest primitive: an internal, peer-blocking veto is categorically distinct from vote-tallying and should gate the Consensus resolver's **holdout** state.

**Selection is an anti-capture engineering problem.** The sample offers age-and-sequence-gated queues (Roman *cursus honorum*), sponsored recommendation with persistent Mentor-bonds (Han *cha ju*), weighted-lottery bags (Florentine *imborsazione*), and — most sophisticated — Venice's multi-round doge pipeline alternating random subsets with scrutiny votes, precisely so that capturing appointment requires controlling *every* round. The Papal conclave's *Ubi periculum* adds the CONSENSUS_UNANIMITY primitive: a holdout imposes an escalating, *decaying* cost (shrinking ration) on the whole body rather than wielding a free veto.

**Meritocratic-bureaucratic recruitment recurs as a gated ladder** (Roman age-minimums and cooldowns; Han sponsor-gated ranks 0→1→2; Carolingian palace office). The HRE *ministeriales* sharpen it: an unfree service class whose bureaucratic power_base makes it *structurally more loyal* — no independent kin/land base to defect to — yet convertible, over successful resolutions, into a knight's kinship/military base. And every merit ladder drifts: Han's examination officialdom silently became a de facto OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL/LANDHOLDER_FRANCHISE aristocracy with no rule changing on paper.

**Governance mode has two failure/change modes of its own.** The *mask* — Cao Cao's Xu court issuing "Han" edicts that were pure warlord fiat; Florence's "informal principate" keeping a republican façade while one patronage network owned every appointment lever — argues for a displayed-mode/substance split, the L/PS gate driven off the *displayed* mode with the gap left exploitable. The *migration* — Venice's Serrata converting DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY into OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL by a single dated charter act; Rome's Principate-mask → Dominate-drop — argues that mode is legislated and observable, L/PS gating whether it holds.

**Chain-bypass authorities are enriched four ways.** Constructed by accretion (Augustus bundling veto + supreme command + precedence via repeated Senate grants) or purchase (Milan's ducal title); negative-only (the Roman tribune's nullify-not-compel *intercessio*); doubled (Japan's bakufu/court as two co-resident L/PS tracks; the HRE's election-plus-ratification and Concordat-of-Worms spiritual/temporal split); and dormant-but-live (a Crown gate that never zeroes under eclipse, snapping back on a two-signal collision — grounded on the Roman Senate, not portent theology). Two whole-polity primitives round it out: **recognition-fission**, both defensive (the Ottoman millet "holding people not territory") and offensive (Han *Tui'en Ling* compelling partition; Sengoku Japan's fragmentation), and **rank = secession blast-radius** (mass-demoting high-immediacy HRE actors in 1803 → the empire-wide Rheinbund; Han's Liu Yan keying blast-radius off *scope of granted authority*).

## Ottoman Empire — Modes of Governance

### 1. Sultanic autocracy bounded by dual law (kanun + şeriat)
The Sultan (Padişah) held theoretically unlimited temporal authority, but exercised it through two parallel legal orders: **şeriat** (Islamic sacred law, interpreted by the ulema) and **kanun** (sultanic/customary secular law, codified in *kanunname*). Mehmed II's **Kanunname-i Al-i Osman** (c. 1477-1481) is the foundational constitutional document of the dynasty — it codified succession, court protocol, taxation, and (infamously) fratricide. Süleyman I ("Kanuni," r. 1520-1566) is named for his kanun codifications precisely because sultanic law-giving, not just conquest, was a legitimating act. Legitimacy was multiply sourced: dynastic descent from Osman I (1299), title of Caliph (asserted forcefully after 1517 conquest of Mamluk Egypt/Hejaz, giving custodianship of Mecca/Medina — "Hâdim ül-Haremeyn"), and Ghazi (holy-warrior) ideology from the frontier-beylik origins.
=> Valoria design hook: AUTOCRATIC_FIAT as governance_mode is not "no rules" — model it as fiat operating inside a second, independently-adjudicated rule layer (kanun vs şeriat), so a Country-tier AUTOCRATIC_FIAT ruler can still be blocked/overruled by a parallel legitimacy authority (ulema-equivalent) without that being a second veto-holder in the chain-bypass sense.

### 2. Imperial Council — Divan-ı Hümayun
The central deliberative-executive body, meeting (originally 4x/week, later reduced) at Topkapı Palace. Composed of the Grand Vizier (Sadrazam, presiding after Mehmed II stopped attending in person c. 1475), other vezir-i azams/kubbealtı vezirleri ("dome viziers"), the two kadıaskers (chief military judges of Rumelia and Anatolia), the defterdar (chief treasurer), and the nişancı (keeper of the imperial seal/tuğra, drafter of kanunname). Decisions were procedurally collective but the Grand Vizier held the sultan's seal (mühr-i hümayun) and could act with near-sultanic authority when it was delegated to him.
=> Valoria design hook: direct model for OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL at Duchy/Country tier — a fixed slate of named seats (military-judicial, fiscal, chancery) each with a distinct portfolio-veto, chaired by a delegate-executive who holds a physical instrument-of-authority (the seal) that can be recalled by the Monarch chain-bypass authority at will.

### 3. Sublime Porte (Bâb-ı Âli) as a rival executive center
From the mid-17th century, especially the **Köprülü era** (1656-1683: Köprülü Mehmed Pasha, then his son Fazıl Ahmed Pasha, then Kara Mustafa Pasha), the Grand Vizier's own office/household — the Sublime Porte — increasingly ran state business independent of the sultan's physical presence, as sultans (post-Kafes, see below) grew less capable/willing to rule directly. By the 18th-19th centuries "the Porte" is effectively metonymic for the Ottoman government itself in foreign diplomacy.
=> Valoria design hook: models institutional "regency drift" — an OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL or bureaucratic apparatus can accrete de facto Country-tier authority away from a nominal AUTOCRATIC_FIAT seat without a formal governance_mode change being declared; Legitimacy/Popular-Support should be able to decouple from the nominal seat-holder and attach to the de facto executive, creating a lagging-indicator problem the player must notice.

### 4. Millet system — confessional self-governance
Non-Muslim religious communities (millets) were granted structured autonomy over personal-status law, education, and internal taxation, administered through their own religious hierarchy, which was in turn accountable to the Sultan. The **Rum millet** (Orthodox Christians) was headed by the Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople (formalized functionally after Mehmed II's 1454 restoration/appointment of Patriarch Gennadios II Scholarios), the **Armenian millet** by the Armenian Patriarch (est. 1461), and the **Jewish millet** by the Chief Rabbi (Hahambaşı). These heads were simultaneously religious authorities and state-recognized civil administrators/tax farmers for their community — a delegated-sovereignty model, not toleration as absence of governance.
=> Valoria design hook: exact template for **recognition-fission** — the Crown charters a diverged confessional/cultural population into a formally bounded sub-faction with its own internal Standing ladder and a single Crown-facing liaison office; this is how a faction can "hold people not territory" while still being governed through a named, auditable hierarchy rather than being ungoverned.

### 5. Ulema hierarchy and the kadı courts
Islamic legal-scholarly authority ran through the medrese system to the qadi (kadı) courts, capped by the **Şeyhülislam** (Grand Mufti, office institutionalized as head of the ilmiye by the 16th century, formal cabinet rank from 1836). The Şeyhülislam issued fatwas that could religiously authorize or de-authorize state acts — including, on at least three occasions, the deposition of a reigning sultan (1622, 1687, 1703 — see conflicts). Kadıs administered şeriat and kanun jointly in local courts and doubled as municipal administrators (assessing taxes, registering property, supervising guilds) — judiciary and local executive were not separated.
=> Valoria design hook: models a **second, non-territorial Legitimacy-adjudication authority** parallel to the normal Country->Settlement chain — a religious/juridical office that can issue a binding ruling (a "fatwa Key") which forces a Legitimacy recalculation at the Country tier without itself sitting in the scale hierarchy; useful as a template for a Valoria faction type whose power_base is purely ideological/juridical rather than territorial.

### 6. Harem politics and the "Sultanate of Women" (1533-1656)
The Valide Sultan (Queen Mother) held a formal court office with budget, staff, and — under figures like **Hürrem Sultan** (favorite/later wife of Süleyman I, d. 1558), **Kösem Sultan** (Valide/regent across multiple reigns, effectively ruling 1623-1651, executed 1651 in a palace coup), and **Turhan Hatice Sultan** (regent for Mehmed IV from 1651) — exercised sustained regency-level executive authority during sultans' minorities or incapacity. This was procedurally irregular (no kanun seat for it) but operationally durable for over a century.
=> Valoria design hook: models an **unratified but persistent power_base** (kinship-proximity to the Monarch chain-bypass authority) that can override the nominal governance_mode for extended periods — useful for scripting a Country-tier "shadow regent" state that behaves as AUTOCRATIC_FIAT-by-proxy without any formal mode flip, and which the Standing ladder should be able to represent as an off-ladder rank rather than forcing it into rank 0-7.

### 7. Provincial administration — beylerbeyi and sanjak beys
The empire was tiered: **eyalet** (province, headed by a beylerbeyi) subdivided into **sanjaks** (headed by a sanjakbeyi), the base administrative/military unit tied directly to timar assignment (below). This is the direct precedent for a Country -> Duchy -> Territory cascade, with military-fief officers doubling as the local governance layer rather than a separate civil bureaucracy at that tier.
=> Valoria design hook: direct precedent for the Duchy/Territory tiers defaulting to LANDHOLDER_FRANCHISE or ROYAL_COURT_APPOINTMENT governance_mode when the officer's authority derives from a land-service bond (timar) rather than election or council — the mode should be an emergent readout of *how* the local officer's power_base was granted, not a free player choice.

## 1. The Republic's dispersed, colliding sovereignties (to 27 BC)

Rome never had one seat of decision; it had at least four bodies each claiming a piece of legitimacy, deliberately arranged so none could act alone.

- **The magistracies (imperium-holders).** Two annually elected **consuls** held *imperium* (supreme civil/military command) and could each veto the other (*intercessio*, collegiality). Below them: **praetors** (judicial/military imperium, from 8 under Sulla), **aediles** (civic administration, games), **quaestors** (finance) — all annual, all collegial. **Censors**, elected every ~5 years for an 18-month term, held the *lectio senatus* (enrolling/expelling senators) and the property census that set voting class. => Valoria design hook: OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL governance_mode should model *internal veto power*, not just majority vote — a council seat able to unilaterally block a peer's action (consular intercessio) is distinct from vote-tallying and should gate the Consensus resolver's "holdout" state.
- **The Senate** (c. 300 members, 600 post-Sulla 81 BC, 900 under Caesar) had no formal legislative power, yet its *senatus consulta* and its intangible **auctoritas** (weight of collective standing opinion) governed provincial assignments, war/peace, and finance in practice. Soft power constraining hard power. => Valoria design hook: the clearest real-world precedent for **Legitimacy/Popular-Support (L/PS) as a consent mechanic separate from formal authority** — an OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL whose votes are non-binding but whose L/PS-weighted opinion throttles what a chain-bypass Monarch authority can execute without paying an L/PS cost.
- **The popular assemblies.** *Comitia centuriata* (organized into 193 timocratic "centuries" weighted toward the wealthy) elected consuls/praetors/censors and declared war. *Comitia tributa* (35 geographic tribes) elected lower magistrates and passed *leges*. The plebeian-only *concilium plebis* elected the ten **tribunes of the plebs** and passed *plebiscita* — made binding on the whole populace by the **lex Hortensia, 287 BC**, ending the centuries-long "Struggle of the Orders." => Valoria design hook: this is the archetype for **DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY as a distinct governance_mode from OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL operating at the same tier simultaneously** — Rome ran both concurrently with different electorates and different binding scope; Valoria should allow a Settlement/Territory to carry two co-resident governance_mode bodies whose jurisdictions overlap and periodically collide rather than forcing one mode per tier.
- **Tribunician sacrosanctity and veto.** A tribune's person was legally inviolable, and a tribune could veto (*intercessio*) any magistrate's act, any senatus consultum, even a fellow tribune's veto — an institutionalized, unaccountable "stop everything" lever answerable only to plebeian recall. Tiberius Gracchus's deposition of his fellow tribune Octavius (133 BC) by mass vote of the plebs — itself constitutionally dubious — is the paradigm case of this lever being fought over rather than merely used. => Valoria design hook: a tribune-style veto is a **chain-bypass authority scoped to blocking rather than commanding** — distinct from the Monarch/Parliament bypass pair in the ratified scale hierarchy; model it as a third bypass *type* (negative-only, cannot compel, can only nullify) available to a designated "champion of the base" seat, itself removable only by the assembly that created it.
- **The censorial *nota* and *regimen morum*.** Censors could strike a man from the senatorial rolls for financial or moral turpitude without trial, and assign *aerarii* (disenfranchised tax-only status) — an extra-judicial standing sanction resting purely on two men's discretion, subject to fierce backlash (e.g., Appius Claudius Caecus's defiantly independent censorship, 312 BC). => Valoria design hook: models a **non-judicial demotion channel** for the Standing ladder — a designated "arbiter" seat can apply a Demotion Trigger outside the normal resolution-quality bridge, at the cost of provoking L/PS backlash against the arbiter's own faction.

## 2. Cursus honorum as a chartered career ladder with hard age-gates

The **lex Villia Annalis (180 BC)** and Sulla's reforms (81 BC) fixed minimum ages and mandatory sequencing: quaestor (age 30, automatic Senate entry), aedile or tribune of the plebs (optional), praetor (age 39), consul (age 42), with a mandatory **biennium** (2-year gap) between offices and (post-Sulla) a **decennium** before repeating the consulship. This converted "who governs" into a scheduled, examinable, publicly visible queue rather than an open contest. => Valoria design hook: direct template for **MERITOCRATIC_BUREAUCRATIC governance_mode as an age/sequence-gated ladder** rather than a pure merit-score — implement hard minimum-rank-duration and cooldown timers on Standing-ladder promotion, not just a threshold check, so a faction cannot rush a favored client through every rank in one cycle.

## 3. The Principate settlement (27 BC and 23 BC): republican forms, monarchic substance

Octavian's "restoration of the Republic" in **January 27 BC** returned formal control of provinces and the treasury to the Senate and people while he retained, via Senate grant, *imperium proconsulare* over the frontier provinces housing the legions — the **First Settlement**. The **Second Settlement (23 BC)** refined this into the durable template: Augustus resigned the consulship itself but was granted **tribunicia potestas** (the tribune's sacrosanctity and veto power, without ever holding the office — because a patrician legally could not) for life, and **imperium proconsulare maius et infinitum** (proconsular command senior to and overriding any other proconsul's, valid even inside Rome's sacred boundary). Legitimacy was framed not as a title but as **auctoritas** — Augustus's own boast in the *Res Gestae Divi Augusti* (ch. 34) that he "excelled all in auctoritas" while holding "no more official power than my colleagues in each magistracy." => Valoria design hook: this is the purest historical case of a **chain-bypass authority constructed by grant-of-powers rather than by conquest of an office** — the Monarch bypass tier should be reachable by a faction *accreting* a bundle of nominally-separate legal powers (veto + supreme command + precedence) onto one seat via repeated OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL grants, rather than only by military seizure; each grant should be a loggable Key event so the accretion is auditable and reversible in principle (as the Senate periodically re-voted Augustus's powers) even if never actually reversed in practice.
- **The "shame" of naked autocracy avoided.** Augustus and his successors retained consulships, censuses, Senate meetings, and elections as theater — the Senate still formally "elected" and "ratified" — because Roman political culture could not legitimate a king (*rex* was a toxic word since the expulsion of Tarquinius Superbus, 509 BC). => Valoria design hook: models **L/PS as path-dependent on institutional vocabulary, not just raw power** — a Monarch-tier authority operating inside a polity with strong prior DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY/OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL memory should pay ongoing L/PS upkeep to preserve those bodies as ceremonial shells, and suffer an L/PS penalty spike (not just a one-time cost) the moment it dispenses with the shell entirely.

## 4. The Dominate (284–305 AD): the mask drops

**Diocletian** (r. 284–305), securing power after the fourth-century "barracks emperor" chaos, abandoned the Principate's republican fiction outright: adopted **dominus** ("master/lord," previously an embarrassing term), instituted **adoratio purpurae** (ritual prostration before the emperor), removed the imperial court from Rome to mobile capitals (Nicomedia, etc.), and in **293 AD** created the **Tetrarchy** — two senior *Augusti* and two junior *Caesares*, bound by adoption and marriage alliance, each with a defined territorial command, explicitly designed to regularize succession and end simultaneous civil war on multiple fronts. The Senate was reduced to a ceremonial, Rome-local body with no provincial or military role. => Valoria design hook: direct precedent for a scripted **governance_mode transition AUTOCRATIC_FIAT replacing an eroded ROYAL_COURT_APPOINTMENT/OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL hybrid** after a sustained collision-of-stresses episode (the Crisis of the Third Century) — and the Tetrarchy itself is a template for **deliberate multi-seat power-sharing installed as an anti-civil-war mechanism**, i.e., a designed alternative to single-heir succession that Valoria's Duchy/Country tier could offer as a player-chosen mitigation against secession risk, at the cost of creating exactly the four-way collision it was meant to prevent (see conflicts_md, Tetrarchy collapse 306–324 AD).

## 5. Provincial governorship as devolved, semi-autonomous sub-sovereignty

## 1. Modes of Governance

### 1.1 Dynastic sanction as founding legitimation act (751, 754, 800)
The Carolingian mode of rule begins with an act of **institutional replacement legitimated by external religious sanction**, not conquest or election in the classical sense. In 751 Pippin III (the Short), already *maior domus* (Mayor of the Palace) and de facto ruler, deposed the last Merovingian king Childeric III (tonsured, sent to a monastery — Fulda, per the *Royal Frankish Annals* / *Annales regni Francorum*) after securing a prior ruling from **Pope Zachary** that "he who held the power should have the title of king" (reported in the ARF and Einhard's *Vita Karoli*, retrospectively). Pippin was elected/acclaimed by Frankish magnates at an assembly (Soissons, 751) and anointed — the first Frankish royal anointing, borrowed from Old Testament/Visigothic precedent — by Archbishop Boniface. Pope Stephen II personally traveled to Francia and re-anointed Pippin and his sons at Saint-Denis in 754, adding the title *patricius Romanorum* and binding the dynasty to the papacy (the *Donation of Pippin*, promising the Papal States). This created a durable governance template: **legitimacy is a composite of acclamation-by-magnates + sacral anointing + external supreme-authority ratification**, not any single mechanism.
=> Valoria design hook: model this as the founding case for **recognition-fission / reaggregation** — a Crown mechanic where a rising power-base (patronage/military) petitions an external chain-bypass authority (Monarch or Parliament) for a charter that converts de facto control into de jure title, redrawing allegiance blocs without a battle.

### 1.2 Coronation as Emperor, 25 December 800 (Rome, St. Peter's)
Charlemagne's coronation by Pope Leo III created a second, higher-order legitimation layer (imperial vs. merely royal), producing an enduring ambiguity over whether emperorship was a papal grant, a Frankish/Roman-restoration act, or (per Einhard's claim that Charlemagne would not have entered the church that day had he known Leo's intent) something closer to an unwanted fait accompli imposed on him. The Byzantine reaction (a rival, "legitimate" Roman emperor already existing in Constantinople) forced protracted diplomacy, resolved only by Byzantine recognition of Charlemagne's imperial title (Treaty of Aachen, 812) in exchange for Frankish renunciation of Venice/Dalmatia claims.
=> Valoria design hook: a **governance_mode transition trigger** — AUTOCRATIC_FIAT elevated to a higher-legitimacy tier only via an external chain-bypass authority's ratification (Monarch-equivalent), with a documented lag/dispute period (799–812) before Legitimacy fully stabilizes — i.e., L/PS gain from a title-grant should have a cooldown/contest window, not instant effect.

### 1.3 The palace (*palatium*) and itinerant kingship, fixed at Aachen from ca. 794
Government was personal and peripatetic before Charlemagne built a fixed capital at Aachen (Aix-la-Chapelle) from the 790s, with its palace chapel, archive, and treasury. **Hincmar of Rheims's *De Ordine Palatii* (ca. 882, itself reworking a lost earlier Adalhard-of-Corbie memorandum from Louis the Pious's reign)** describes the palace offices that constituted the executive: the *camerarius* (chamberlain, treasury), *senescalcus* (seneschal, household provisioning), *comes stabuli* (constable, horses/military transport), *maior domus* (now subordinated, a vestige of the office Pippin himself had once held), the *cancellarius*/notaries (chancery, charter production), and the *comes palatii* (Count of the Palace, effectively the chief justice hearing appeals referred from provincial courts). This was a **household-as-state bureaucracy**: offices with defined portfolios but staffed by aristocrats who owed personal fidelity to the ruler, rotating between palace service and provincial command.
=> Valoria design hook: **MERITOCRATIC_BUREAUCRATIC governance_mode**, but explicitly hybridized with kinship/patronage power_base on the Standing ladder — palace office should function as a Rank-advancement Initiation-Gate whose Mentor field is the ruler personally, distinct from provincial command tracks.

### 1.4 Capitularies as the primary legislative/administrative instrument
Carolingian rulers governed less through standing law codes than through **capitularies** (*capitula* — enumerated clauses), issued after deliberation with magnates and promulgated to counts/missi for local reading-aloud and enforcement. The **Admonitio Generalis (789)** is the paradigm case: 82 clauses on ecclesiastical reform, administrative correction, and moral governance, issued in Charlemagne's name but citing conciliar/canonical authority, explicitly instructing missi to verify compliance. Other landmark capitularies: the **Capitulare de Villis** (ca. 792–800, estate-management ordinance for royal domains), the **Capitulare Missorum Generale (802)**, and later the **Capitulary of Quierzy (877)**, issued by Charles the Bald before departing on campaign, which regularized (rather than invented) heritability of counties and honores for absent counts' heirs — a watershed conceding hereditary succession to office. Capitularies required **assembly deliberation** for legitimacy but issued as royal command — a hybrid of DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY input and AUTOCRATIC_FIAT output.
=> Valoria design hook: capitularies map directly to the **Key substrate's Directive/Need arithmetic** — a capitulary is an IN-Key (royal directive) whose delivered-vs-demanded gap against provincial Need determines whether counts/missi advance or are flagged for corrective missi intervention next cycle (the resolution-quality → standing bridge).

### 1.5 The *missi dominici* — paired itinerant oversight (formalized 789x802)
The missi dominici ("those sent by the lord [king]") were **ad hoc, then regularized (802 Capitulare Missorum Generale) paired inspectors** — canonically one lay magnate (often a count from elsewhere) and one bishop/abbot together, deliberately drawn from OUTSIDE the missaticum (inspection circuit) they were assigned, to prevent collusion with local counts. Circuits (missatica) covered several counties; missi held their own assize-like hearings (*placita*), reviewed counts' judicial conduct, heard appeals against local officials, administered the general oath of fidelity (802, see below), inspected royal estates and church institutions, and reported back to the palace. This is the clearest Carolingian instance of **built-in structural auditing**: an oversight pair independent of the audited hierarchy, empowered to depose or refer for deposition.
=> Valoria design hook: a direct template for the **collision-of-stresses primitive's independent-signal requirement** — model missi as a standing dual-channel audit (lay + ecclesiastical) whose paired, cross-checking reports constitute the "material threshold" signal feeding into faction Standing-ladder Demotion-Trigger evaluation, structurally separate from the "interpretive trigger" (e.g., a scandal/rumor Key) so that a demotion needs BOTH channels to concur — mirroring the historical anti-collusion design.

### 1.6 Placita — general assemblies (Marchfield/Mayfield and beyond)
Annual general assemblies (*placitum generale*), traditionally held in March under the Merovingians ("Marchfield," *campus Martius*) and moved to May under Pippin/Charlemagne ("Mayfield," *campus Madius*, to coincide with the campaigning season's fodder availability), gathered magnates (lay and ecclesiastical), sometimes with wider free-man attendance, to deliberate capitularies, approve campaigns, receive envoys, and adjudicate high disputes. Attendance and precedence at the assembly WAS a standing signal — exclusion or relegation was itself a political demotion. Louis the Pious's and his sons' assemblies (Compiègne 833, Soissons 833) became sites where the king's own deposition was staged (see conflicts_md). Regional/local placita, held by counts or missi, handled lower justice.
=> Valoria design hook: canonical seed for **DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY governance_mode** at Duchy/Province tier — model placitum attendance/precedence as a visible Standing signal (not just a resolver mechanic): exclusion from the assembly should itself trigger an L/PS penalty cascade, and the Consensus resolver's "holdout" state should be able to escalate a placitum into a deposition proceeding, per §1.7/§3 below.

### 1.7 The Church as parallel administrative arm
Bishops and abbots were simultaneously spiritual officers and secular administrators: they held immunities (freedom from count's judicial entry), hosted royal missi functions, administered charity/hospital infrastructure, ran chanceries (near-monopoly on literacy), and — crucially — many were **royal appointees functioning as a loyalist counterweight to hereditary lay aristocracy** ("imperial church system," reaching full form under the Ottonians but rooted in Carolingian practice: e.g., Charlemagne's use of Alcuin, Angilbert, and later Louis the Pious's reliance on Benedict of Aniane for monastic reform via the Council/synod of Aachen 816–817, which issued a unified monastic rule (*Institutio canonicorum* and reformed Benedictine customary) binding all monasteries empire-wide — an administrative standardization act functioning like a capitulary but issued through synodal machinery). Councils (Frankfurt 794 — condemning Adoptionism and implicitly rejecting the Second Council of Nicaea's icon doctrine — Aachen 809 on the *filioque*, Paris 829) functioned as legislative-administrative sessions nearly interchangeable with royal placita, often co-located and co-attended by the same magnates.
=> Valoria design hook: model Church institutions as a **separate faction-type whose power_base is bureaucratic/ideological rather than military**, capable of issuing its own Directive-Keys (synodal canons) that interlock with royal capitularies — giving the Monarch chain-bypass authority a second, ecclesiastical audit/legitimation channel independent of the count/duke hierarchy, usable to counterbalance an overmighty duchy without direct military confrontation.

### 1.8 Oaths of fidelity as the connective tissue of the whole system
Beyond individual vassalic commendation, Charlemagne extracted a **general oath of fidelity from all free adult male subjects in 789 and again, more elaborately, in 802** (Capitulare de fide/oath capitulary), administered BY THE MISSI on circuit — not just "obey the king" but a specified list of obligations (military service, no harboring of the king's enemies, no obstruction of justice, no desertion). This converted personal lordship into something closer to sovereign allegiance, blurring vassalage and citizenship — a distinctively Carolingian institutional move (contrast pure feudal commendation, which bound only the vassal class).
=> Valoria design hook: the 802 universal oath is the historical anchor for treating **fidelity-oaths as a Key-type distinct from vassal Standing-ladder initiation** — a population-wide consent Key that feeds the Legitimacy/Popular-Support gate directly (bypassing the faction Standing ladder entirely), letting Monarch-tier chain-bypass actions draw on a "general oath" Legitimacy reservoir separate from noble patronage networks.

## Overview: a polycentric elective empire, not a state

The HRE was never a unitary monarchy. It was a composite, elective, negotiated order in which the Emperor was one power-holder among many constitutionally entrenched estates (Reichsstände), each with its own courts, coinage, armies, and diplomacy. Max Weber's and later Peter Moraw's characterization — "not a state but a *Personenverbandsstaat*," a association-of-persons rather than a territorial administration until very late — is the load-bearing frame for everything below. Three modes coexist and shift in relative weight across the Empire's ~900-year life: **elective monarchy** (Kaiser/König), **negotiated-estates deliberation** (the Reichstag and its constituent colleges), and, briefly and consequentially, **theocratic dual-sovereignty conflict** (the Investiture Controversy) that permanently fixed the limits of imperial sacral claims.

## 1. Elective monarchy: the King of the Romans and the Imperial election

- **The elective principle predates any fixed procedure.** From Henry I's election at Fritzlar in 919 (the first Ottonian, chosen by the Frankish and Saxon dukes after Conrad I's dying recommendation) through the 12th century, "election" meant acclamation by an unfixed, fluid body of dukes, archbishops, and magnates — a *Wahlkönigtum* legitimated by consent of the realm's great men (the *Fürsten*), not primogeniture. This is structurally opposed to hereditary succession and is the single most consequential design fact of the polity: no line, however powerful (Ottonian, Salian, Hohenstaufen, Luxembourg, Habsburg), ever converted the crown into unconditional hereditary property, even when the same family won election after election for centuries (Habsburgs held it near-continuously 1438–1740, 1745–1806, but by *repeated election*, not by right).
  => Valoria design hook: **governance_mode taxonomy** — this is the textbook case for distinguishing `LANDHOLDER_FRANCHISE` (a bounded electoral college of magnates choosing a head-of-state office-holder) from `AUTOCRATIC_FIAT`. A Duchy/Country-tier polity flagged `LANDHOLDER_FRANCHISE` should model succession as a *recurring* election event even where one house wins repeatedly, so the L/PS consent gate is re-rolled/re-legitimated at every succession rather than silently hardening into de facto inheritance.
- **The King of the Romans** (*Rex Romanorum*) was the title of the elected ruler prior to imperial coronation; **Emperor** (*Imperator Romanorum*) required a further, separate legitimating act — originally papal coronation in Rome (Charlemagne 800, Otto I 962), later (from Maximilian I, 1508) the self-assumed style "Elected Roman Emperor" (*Erwählter Römischer Kaiser*) taken at Trent without a Rome coronation, because war with Venice blocked the journey — a deliberate rupture that decoupled imperial legitimacy from papal ratification for good.
  => Valoria design hook: models a **two-stage legitimation pipeline** — election confers office, but a *second*, independent ratification act (historically papal, structurally analogous to a Parliament/Monarch chain-bypass authority signing off) can be withheld, delayed, or unilaterally dispensed with. Useful for the Monarch/Parliament chain-bypass pair: one authority can grant *procedural* legitimacy (the electors' vote) while the other withholds *substantive* legitimacy (coronation/ratification), producing a title-holder who is functionally weaker than L/PS alone would suggest until the second signature lands.
- **The Golden Bull of 1356** (Charles IV, issued at diets in Nuremberg and Metz) is the Empire's closest approach to a written constitution before 1806. It did not invent the electoral college but **fixed its membership at seven**: three ecclesiastical (Archbishop-Electors of Mainz, Trier, Cologne) and four secular (King of Bohemia, Count Palatine of the Rhine, Duke of Saxony [Saxe-Wittenberg line], Margrave of Brandenburg). It specified: majority vote (4 of 7) sufficient and binding even without unanimity; Frankfurt am Main as the mandatory election site; Aachen as the mandatory coronation site; Mainz's Archbishop as convening officer and tie-breaker/president; explicit primogeniture and **indivisibility** of the secular electorates (to prevent an electoral vote from fragmenting across heirs); electors granted regalian rights (mining, coinage, jurisdiction) making the electorate itself a bundle of sovereign privilege, not just a vote.
  => Valoria design hook: a **Standing ladder** top-rung case study — the Golden Bull is a real-world "charter a fixed council" event: it converts a previously fluid, contestable Ascendancy pool (any great magnate might claim electoral weight) into a hard-capped, named, indivisible seat-set. Model this as an `OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL` governance_mode with a **fixed rank-7 seat cap** and an explicit rule (indivisibility) preventing seat-splitting on succession — directly usable wherever the design wants a faction's council rank-0/rank-1 seats to be non-fragmentable across an NPC's heirs.
- **Disputed elections were endemic before 1356 and recurred after.** The double election of 1198 (Philip of Swabia vs. Otto IV, both crowned by rival factions) produced parallel rival kings and a de facto civil war resolved only by Otto IV's 1208 sole recognition (then his own 1215 deposition after Bouvines). The double election of 1257 (Richard of Cornwall vs. Alfonso X of Castile, neither effectively ruling in Germany) opened the **Great Interregnum (1250/1257–1273)** — no effectively functioning king, princes ruling as de facto sovereigns, widespread private warfare — ended only by the 1273 election of Rudolf I of Habsburg, the first of that house, chosen explicitly *because* he was judged too weak to threaten the electors' autonomy. The double election of 1314 (Louis IV of Bavaria vs. Frederick the Fair of Austria, both crowned, war until Mühldorf 1322) shows the same failure mode recurring even with an electoral body in place but no fixed majority rule — precisely the gap the Golden Bull closed 34 years later.

## 2. The Imperial Diet (Reichstag) and the estates — deliberative-assembly governance

- The Reichstag evolved from the informal *Hoftag* (royal court assembly, no fixed membership or venue) into a tri-curial deliberative body. By the late 15th century it formalized into **three colleges** voting separately, with agreement across all three required for a binding *Reichsabschied* (imperial recess/resolution): the **Council of Electors** (Kurfürstenrat, the same seven/eight/nine as the Golden Bull roster, expanded to 8 in 1648 with Bavaria and 9 in 1692 with Hanover); the **Council of Princes** (Reichsfürstenrat — secular and ecclesiastical imperial princes, counts voting collectively in four "curiate" blocs by 1653/54); and the **Council of Imperial Cities** (Reichsstädtekollegium), which only won a full, equal, binding vote at the **1489 Diet of Frankfurt** — before that, free cities could be consulted but their assent was not constitutionally required, a 500-year gap between fiscal/military obligation and formal voice.
  => Valoria design hook: this is the paradigm **NEGOTIATED_ESTATES** governance_mode — three separately-convened curiae with sequential, not simultaneous, veto power, and a *documented historical lag* between an estate bearing obligations (cities funding the Reichsarmee, paying the Common Penny) and that estate winning a formal vote. Model NEGOTIATED_ESTATES settlements so that a subordinate estate can be taxed/levied for generations before a recognition-fission/reaggregation event finally seats it in the deliberative body — the lag itself is the design-relevant texture, not an oversight to smooth away.
- **The Reichskammergericht (Imperial Chamber Court)**, established at the same **1495 Diet of Worms** that issued the Perpetual Peace, and the older **Reichshofrat** (Vienna, under direct imperial control) formed a dual judicial system: the Chamber Court staffed and funded by the estates (judges nominated by territorial rulers, a deliberate check on unilateral imperial justice), the Aulic Council staffed by imperial appointees.   => Valoria design hook: models a **dual-track resolver** for the resolution-quality→standing bridge — an event Key can be adjudicated through either an Estates-appointed or a Monarch-appointed court depending on who is the aggrieved/accusing party, with the arithmetic gap on Directive/Need computed differently (and possibly divergently) depending on which track resolved it, generating built-in forum-shopping and legitimacy disputes as emergent content.
- **The Perpetual Diet at Regensburg (from 1663)** converted the Reichstag from an episodic assembly (convened and dissolved per session, as at Worms 1495 or Augsburg 1500/1530/1555) into a standing, permanently-sitting congress of ambassadors representing their princes rather than the princes attending in person — a structural shift from personal presence-based legitimacy to representative/proxy-based legitimacy.

## 3. The Investiture Controversy (1075/76–1122): the limit-case theocratic collision

- **Gregory VII's Dictatus Papae (1075)** asserted, among 27 propositions, that only the Pope could depose or reinstate bishops, that the Pope could depose emperors, and that the Roman church had never erred — a maximal claim of spiritual supremacy over temporal investiture (the ceremonial granting of ring and staff, the symbols of episcopal office, historically performed by kings/emperors as de facto appointment power over their own bishops and abbots).
- **Henry IV's response**: the **Diet/Synod of Worms (January 1076)**, where Henry and German bishops loyal to him declared Gregory deposed, addressing him as "Hildebrand, no longer Pope but false monk." Gregory retaliated by excommunicating Henry and **releasing his subjects from their oath of fealty** — the constitutionally devastating move, because it converted a theological dispute into a live license for the German princes (Rudolf of Rheinfelden foremost among them) to rebel without violating their feudal oaths.
- **The Walk to Canossa (January 1077)**: Henry, facing the Saxon rebellion and a princely diet at Tribur threatening to elect an antiking (which happened anyway — **Rudolf of Rheinfelden elected antiking March 1077**, killed at the Battle of the Elster 1080), crossed the Alps in winter and stood as a penitent outside Gregory's refuge at Canossa for three days until absolved — a humiliation that nonetheless *worked*: it stripped the princes of their moral pretext, restored Henry's fealty claims, and let him fight the civil war of 1077–1085 to eventual victory (sacking Rome 1084, installing antipope Clement III).
- **The Concordat of Worms (1122)**, negotiated under Henry V, formally split investiture in two: the **spiritual symbols (ring and staff)** conferred by the Church alone; the **temporal symbols (the scepter, representing regalian fiefs and secular authority)** conferred by the Emperor — with German elections held in the Emperor's presence (retaining practical influence) but Italian/Burgundian elections free of it. This is the durable settlement: it does not resolve who is "supreme," it **partitions the office** into separable spiritual and temporal grants that require two different authorities' sign-off to be complete.
  => Valoria design hook: **collision-of-stresses primitive**, explicitly non-MoH-grounded. The controversy needed (a) a *material* threshold — a live vacancy/appointment dispute over a specific bishopric (Milan, 1075, was the proximate trigger) — plus (b) an *interpretive* trigger — Gregory's universalizing doctrinal claim (Dictatus Papae) reframing an ordinary appointment fight as a supremacy question — to resonate into empire-wide civil war. Use this, not Mandate of Heaven, as the grounding precedent whenever a Valoria event needs two independently-arriving signals to escalate a local personnel dispute into a polity-shaking collision.
  => Valoria design hook: the **Concordat of Worms' bifurcated grant** (spiritual vs. temporal investiture, two separate authorities each competent over half the office) is a clean non-MoH model for a **dual sign-off requirement** on high offices — e.g., a Settlement/Territory governor position requiring both a religious/cultural-legitimacy grant and a Crown/Parliament temporal grant before the office-holder counts as fully seated, with each grantor able to withhold independently.

## 4. Landfrieden — the peace-of-the-land movement and its culmination

- **Landfrieden** (territorial/public peaces) were sworn or decreed suspensions of the right of private feud (*Fehderecht*), issued repeatedly and regionally from the 11th century onward (e.g., the Peace of God movements imported from France, regional Landfrieden sworn among Rhenish and Swabian lords through the 12th–13th centuries) precisely because feuding — private, legally sanctioned armed dispute settlement between nobles — was a *constitutionally recognized right* in the Empire, not a crime, until abolished.
- **The Perpetual Peace of the Land (Ewiger Landfrieden), Diet of Worms, 1495**: Maximilian I's reform diet permanently banned the Fehde empire-wide, established the Reichskammergericht as the mandatory adjudicative alternative, and (attempted to) fund enforcement via the **Common Penny (Gemeiner Pfennig)**, a universal imperial tax that met heavy estate resistance and was never reliably collected — the single clearest case in Empire history of a governance reform that **succeeds procedurally (the law passes, the court is founded) but fails operationally (the funding mechanism the estates must consent to is starved)**.
  => Valoria design hook: **suppression/backfire** — banning the Fehde without adequately funding its replacement enforcement/adjudication apparatus did not eliminate noble self-help violence, it just removed its *legal* channel while leaving the material grievances unaddressed, pushing conflict into extralegal channels (continued feuding under other names, brigandage, private garrisons) that the Reichskammergericht structurally could not reach. Model a Landfrieden-style reform as converting a formerly "militarily/legally solvable" dispute-resolution problem into one that only surfaces via Fieldwork/Investigation once its legal venue is defunded or overloaded — directly the suppression/backfire principle, applied to *judicial* rather than *cultural* suppression.
  => Valoria design hook: **resolution-quality → standing bridge** — the Common Penny's failure is a case where the *demanded* Directive (universal imperial taxation to fund peace enforcement) vastly exceeded the *delivered* amount (patchy, resisted, under-collected), and that gap itself became visible, ongoing evidence used by both reform and anti-reform factions in later Diets — model the arithmetic gap as persisting and being re-litigated across multiple subsequent event resolutions, not resolved once.

## 5. Mediatization and Imperial Immediacy (Reichsunmittelbarkeit)

- **Imperial immediacy** meant direct legal subordination to the Emperor/Empire with no intermediate lord — the status held by imperial princes, imperial counts and knights (Reichsritterschaft), free imperial cities, and prince-bishoprics/abbeys. **Mediatization** was the process (and its final catastrophic acceleration) of stripping that immediacy, subordinating a formerly-immediate polity to a territorial prince instead.
- The **1803 Reichsdeputationshauptschluss** (Imperial Recess), negotiated under French and Russian pressure to compensate princes who lost Rhineland territory to France (1801 Peace of Lunéville), mediatized **essentially all ecclesiastical territories and the vast majority of free imperial cities** (of ~51 free cities, only 6 survived; virtually every prince-bishopric was secularized and absorbed) in a single stroke — the largest one-time redistribution of sovereign status in the Empire's history, and the direct structural precondition for the Empire's 1806 dissolution (Francis II's abdication of the imperial title under Napoleonic pressure, the Confederation of the Rhine already having seceded).
  => Valoria design hook: **Scale hierarchy / mediatization as a demotion mechanic** — model an explicit "immediacy" flag on Territory/Settlement-tier polities (whether they report directly to Country/Monarch or are routed through an intervening Duchy), and let a Reichsdeputationshauptschluss-style event mass-flip immediate polities to mediated status in one negotiated act as compensation currency in a larger settlement — i.e., immediacy itself becomes a fungible chip traded away in a Crown-brokered deal between *other* parties, exactly as the 1803 Recess compensated secular princes with ecclesiastical/city territory that had no say in the negotiation.
  => Valoria design hook: **rank = secession blast-radius** — because 1803 stripped so many *high-immediacy* actors (Reichsstädte, prince-bishoprics) simultaneously, the resulting defections (the 1806 Rheinbund states seceding en masse from the Empire) had empire-wide scope precisely because the mediatized/demoted actors had previously held top-tier immediate rank; model the blast-radius scaling so that mass-demoting a cluster of high-rank immediate actors in one move creates a correspondingly empire-wide secession event rather than isolated local ones.

## 6. The ministeriales — an unfree bureaucratic-service class as a governance mode in miniature

- **Ministeriales** were legally unfree (servile) household knights and administrators bound to a lord (initially the Crown, then also bishops and great princes) from the 11th century, used precisely *because* their unfreedom made them unable to inherit office as an independent noble right — they served at the lord's pleasure, giving the lord a **personally loyal administrative and military corps outside the free-noble feudal hierarchy** that could not use its offices as leverage for its own dynastic claims. Salian and early Hohenstaufen emperors (esp. Frederick Barbarossa) staffed castellanies, provincial administration (Reichsvogtei), and military households with ministeriales as a deliberate counterweight to over-mighty free princes.
- Over the 12th–13th centuries, ministeriales **rose** — through administrative indispensability, marriage, land-grants (Lehen) attached to office, and eventual formal manumission — into the lower nobility, ultimately merging into the free imperial knighthood (Reichsritterschaft) by the 13th–14th century; this is one of the clearest medieval European examples of an entire **service class ascending an entire rank tier** via bureaucratic function rather than birth, war, or purchase.
  => Valoria design hook: **MERITOCRATIC_BUREAUCRATIC governance_mode + Standing ladder power_base = "bureaucratic."** The ministeriales are the textbook case for a rank-0/1 seat whose Ascendancy power_base is purely bureaucratic/administrative competence rather than kinship or purchase, explicitly *because* the seat-holder starts with no independent standing (legal unfreedom) to leverage against the appointing lord — model a bureaucratic-power-base seat-holder as structurally *more* loyal to the appointing authority (lower downfall liability from disloyalty, since they have no independent kin/land base) but also capable, over successive successful resolutions, of accumulating enough land-grant/patronage capital to convert into a different, more independent power_base (kinship/military) — precisely the medieval ministerialis-to-knight arc.

## 1. Florence — the communal-republic-into-oligarchy-into-signoria arc

### 1.1 The commune, the *popolo*, and the Ordinances of Justice (1250–1293)
Florence's constitutional bedrock was laid in two waves. The **Primo Popolo** (1250–1260) first organized the merchant/artisan class (the *popolo*) into an armed political bloc with its own *Capitano del Popolo*, standing against the noble *magnati*. After Ghibelline/Guelf whiplash, the **Secondo Popolo** consolidated Guelf, guild-based rule, culminating in the **Ordinances of Justice**, promulgated 18 January 1293 under the reforming prior **Giano della Bella**. The Ordinances named **152 magnate families** (later expanded) and barred them categorically from communal office, subjected them to collective penal liability for any magnate's violent crime against a popolano, and created a new seventh (later senior) prior, the **Gonfaloniere di Giustizia** ("Standard-Bearer of Justice"; first holder Baldo Ruffoli, Feb. 1293), whose job was to muster the guild militia and enforce sentences against magnates. This is the founding act of Florentine republicanism: legitimacy flows from **guild membership**, not blood, and the constitution is explicitly anti-aristocratic by design.
=> Valoria design hook: model this as a **governance_mode = OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL** civil-rights carve-out — a settlement-tier rule that a named social bloc (a "magnate" analog) is formally excluded from Standing-ladder ranks 3+ regardless of wealth, with a companion enforcement office (Gonfaloniere-analog) whose OUT includes coercive Directives against that bloc specifically.

### 1.2 The Signoria, priorate, and guild rotation
Executive authority sat with the **Signoria**: eight **Priori delle Arti** (priors of the guilds) plus the Gonfaloniere di Giustizia, nine men total, serving staggered **two-month terms**, sequestered in the Palazzo della Signoria (Palazzo Vecchio) for their term, forbidden to leave or receive visitors alone. Eligibility was gated by guild membership: only members of the **7 Arti Maggiori** (Calimala/cloth finishers, Cambio/bankers, Lana/wool manufacturers, Por Santa Maria–Seta/silk, Medici e Speziali/physicians-apothecaries [Dante's guild], Giudici e Notai/judges-notaries, Vaiai e Pellicciai/furriers) and the **14 Arti Minori** (butchers, shoemakers, blacksmiths, etc.) could stand. This is **corporatist republicanism**: the state is literally an assembly of guild delegates, not a franchise of individuals. Two advisory colleges flanked the Signoria — the **Dodici Buonuomini** (Twelve Good Men) and the **Sedici Gonfalonieri delle Compagnie** (Sixteen Standard-Bearers of the militia companies) — and ad hoc bodies called *Consulte e Pratiche* were convened to canvass elite opinion before major decisions (a deliberative, non-binding sounding-board, richly documented by Najemy).
=> Valoria design hook: the Signoria's guild-delegate structure is a clean template for **OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL with NEGOTIATED_ESTATES flavor** — a settlement council whose seats are pre-allocated to named factions/guilds rather than elected at-large, with a short, forced-rotation term (a real "musical chairs" anti-entrenchment device) that Valoria could use to keep a single faction from permanently owning a council seat.

### 1.3 Scrutiny and sortition: *squittinio* and *imborsazione*
The single most distinctive Florentine mechanism: eligible citizens' names, after being vetted in a **scrutiny (squittinio)** every few years by convened electors, were written on slips and placed into **leather bags (borse)**, one per office/quarter; when a term expired, a blindfolded child (or by the 15th century, a controlled official) drew a name by lot — the **tratta**. This is **sortition legitimated by prior oligarchic vetting**, not democracy: the real power was in *who got scrutinized in* and *whose names were binned or purged*. From the 1430s the Medici regime controlled this by installing loyal **accoppiatori** ("couplers/pairers"), officials empowered to hand-pick the scrutiny councils and even to override the *tratta* by direct appointment (*a mano*) in security emergencies — turning nominal sortition into a rigged lottery. Rubinstein's *The Government of Florence Under the Medici* is the definitive account of exactly how this capture worked, office by office, decade by decade.
=> Valoria design hook: **imborsazione is the perfect concrete implementation of the "resolution-quality → standing bridge" plus Ascendancy power_base=bureaucratic** — model a settlement's eligible-officeholder pool as a literal weighted lottery bag whose composition (who's "in the bag") is itself a resource that a faction's `bureaucratic` power_base can quietly pack over multiple in-game cycles, turning apparent random/procedural legitimacy into slow-motion capture that players can audit but rarely reverse quickly.

### 1.4 Parte Guelfa and the *ammonizione*
The **Parte Guelfa** ("Guelf Party"), originally the confiscated-property trust of exiled Ghibellines, became by the 14th century a semi-official parallel institution empowered to "**admonish**" (*ammonire*) citizens suspected of Ghibelline sympathies or magnate blood — an **ammonizione** barred the target from all communal office for a period without trial, on suspicion alone. This is a naked instance of an unaccountable body wielding a quasi-judicial disqualification weapon for factional ends (used heavily against the *popolo grasso*'s rivals in the 1360s–70s).
=> Valoria design hook: a **NEGOTIATED_ESTATES governance_mode variant** where a chartered faction body (Parte Guelfa-analog) holds a standing "disqualify without trial" Directive against named rival Standing-ladder holders — i.e., a demotion trigger that bypasses the normal resolution-quality bridge entirely, modeling suspicion-based purges as a distinct, more abusable lever than performance-based demotion.

### 1.5 The Medici informal principate (1434–1494)
**Cosimo de' Medici** returned from exile in October 1434 (see Conflicts §) and built a **de facto, title-less principate** resting on four pillars: (1) control of the *accoppiatori* and scrutiny bags (see 1.3); (2) an unmatched patronage/credit network run through the **Medici Bank**, which financed allies' dowries, debts, and tax (*catasto*) burdens, buying loyalty without any formal office; (3) periodic **balìe** — emergency plenipotentiary commissions voted by a specially-convened parlamento, nominally temporary, that suspended normal constitutional process and let the regime re-pack the scrutiny lists; and (4) marriage/faction alliances. Cosimo held ordinary office only rarely and briefly; his grandson **Lorenzo "il Magnifico"** (r. de facto 1469–1492) formalized the capture constitutionally in **1480** (in the wake of the Pazzi Conspiracy, see Conflicts) by creating the **Consiglio dei Settanta** (Council of Seventy) — 70 citizens co-opted for **life terms**, to which the Signoria and all other councils became subordinate — which in turn delegated foreign/military affairs to the **Otto di Pratica** (Eight of Practice) and domestic finance to the **Dodici Procuratori** (Twelve Procurators). This is the endpoint of the republic's slow conversion into an oligarchic, patronage-legitimated crypto-monarchy — power without a throne.
=> Valoria design hook: this is the canonical case for **"informal principate" as a governance_mode overlay, not a new taxonomy value** — keep the settlement's nominal mode flagged OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL / DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY on paper, but let a single faction's Ascendancy `patronage` power_base own every appointment lever (accoppiatori-analog) underneath, so the L/PS cascade reads "legitimate republic" while the actual decision authority is a shadow AUTOCRATIC_FIAT. Model the 1480 Council of Seventy as a legal ratification event that converts an informal capture into a chartered one — a good template for a "regime consolidation" narrative beat.

### 1.6 Interregna: the Ciompi commune (1378–1382), the Great Council republic (1494–1512), Savonarola's theocracy (1494–1498)
Twice the Medici/oligarchic mode was interrupted. After the **Ciompi Revolt** (see Conflicts) a genuinely popular government briefly ran three new guilds and a more inclusive Signoria (1378–1382) before oligarchic reaction dismantled it. After the Medici were expelled in **November 1494** (Charles VIII's invasion, Piero "the Unfortunate"'s capitulation), Florence adopted, on the advice of the friar **Girolamo Savonarola**, a **Great Council (Consiglio Maggiore)**, explicitly modeled on Venice's Maggior Consiglio — some 3,000+ eligible male citizens (heads of household meeting a tax/lineage threshold) sitting as a mass sortition-and-election body, a genuine **DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY** unprecedented in Florentine history. Savonarola's own authority was purely charismatic/theocratic (prophetic sermons, no office), and collapsed when Pope Alexander VI excommunicated him (1497) and the failed **trial by fire** (April 1498) discredited him with the populace; he was arrested, tortured, and burned 23 May 1498. The Great Council survived him under **Piero Soderini**, elected **Gonfaloniere di Giustizia for life** in 1502 (an explicit, Venetian-dogal-style tenure innovation) — Machiavelli served as this republic's second chancellor — until Spanish arms restored the Medici in 1512 (Battle of Prato).
=> Valoria design hook: model Savonarola as a **charismatic legitimacy track with no Standing-ladder rank at all** — a "prophet" actor who can spike a settlement's Popular-Support number directly without ever occupying an office, making him high-leverage and catastrophically fragile (no institutional seat to fall back on when PS collapses) — a distinct L/PS interaction pattern from office-based Standing.

### 1.7 The podestà and the Capitano del Popolo
Nearly every Italian commune (Florence, Milan, Genoa, and dozens more) imported a **podestà**: a foreign nobleman/jurist, hired for a fixed term (usually six months to a year), bringing his own retinue (*familia* — judges, notaries, police) at communal expense, explicitly barred from local marriage/property/faction ties, whose function was impartial criminal/civil justice and militia command **precisely because he was an outsider with no local faction stake**. The office is documented from the 1150s (imposed on Lombard cities by Emperor Frederick Barbarossa as an instrument of imperial control) and was rapidly indigenized as a neutral-arbiter device. From the mid-13th century communes paired the podestà with a **Capitano del Popolo**, a second foreign officer representing specifically the guild *popolo* against the magnates.
=> Valoria design hook: the podestà is a ready-made **"imported neutral adjudicator" office type** for the Standing ladder — a rank-holder who is *structurally* barred from local patronage/kinship power_bases (a design-enforced Ascendancy restriction, not just flavor) and whose entire legitimacy is procedural/meritocratic (MERITOCRATIC_BUREAUCRATIC), giving Valoria a clean "import a judge to defuse local factional standing disputes" mechanic — with the historical twist that podestà were themselves sometimes captured by whichever local faction hired them, providing a built-in corruption-of-neutrality failure state.

## 2. Milan — signoria by seizure, ducal title by purchase, condottiere principate

Milan's arc runs from communal commune → archiepiscopal *signoria* → imperial-vicariate *signoria* → purchased hereditary duchy → condottiere-usurped duchy. The **Visconti** family entered power when Archbishop **Ottone Visconti** defeated della Torre forces at the **Battle of Desio (1277)**, founding a *signoria* legitimated by ecclesiastical office plus military victory, not election. **Matteo Visconti** and successors ruled as **imperial vicars** (a delegated-Emperor legitimation, renewed/purchased repeatedly from the Holy Roman Emperor) until **Gian Galeazzo Visconti**, already holding effectively sovereign territorial power, had Emperor **Wenceslaus** sell him the title **Duke of Milan** outright for roughly **100,000 florins**, diploma signed at Prague **11 May 1395**, ratified at Milan **5 September 1395** — governance mode converted overnight from personal *signoria* to a hereditary, dynastically-transmissible principality purely by cash transaction with the nominal imperial suzerain.
=> Valoria design hook: this is a textbook **ROYAL_COURT_APPOINTMENT-from-a-chain-bypass-authority** case — Valoria's Monarch bypass authority selling/granting a Duchy-tier title directly to a lower-tier power-holder for a lump payment, instantly re-tagging that holder's governance_mode and unlocking hereditary transmission rules that a mere *signore* lacked; a clean "purchased legitimacy" alternate route up the hierarchy distinct from military conquest or election.

When **Filippo Maria Visconti** died without a legitimate male heir (13 August 1447), the ducal title's purely personal/imperial-grant character meant there was **no settled succession law** — the vacuum produced the short-lived **Golden Ambrosian Republic** (1447–1450), a restored commune run from the University of Pavia with real popular backing (it won field battles at Bosco Marengo 1447 and Caravaggio 1448) but no standing army of its own, forcing it to hire the condottiere **Francesco Sforza** — who promptly turned his contracted army against his employer, besieged Milan for eight months, starved the city into surrender, entered 22 March 1450, had himself acclaimed **Capitano del Popolo** and then, by right of his marriage to Filippo Maria's illegitimate daughter **Bianca Maria Visconti**, **Duke of Milan** — ratified internationally by the **Treaty of Lodi (9 April 1454)**.
=> Valoria design hook: model this as the archetypal **military-merit route up the Standing ladder converting to full governance_mode capture** — a condottiere's `military` power_base contract, if the employer polity lacks its own independent armed enforcement, can flip from hired defender to owner of the whole settlement/duchy tier; Valoria should let a Territory hire a mercenary-faction Key whose unresolved gap (unpaid wages, unclear post-war status) is the material threshold that converts "contractor" into "usurper."

## 3. Genoa — endemic factional oligarchy and the doge as failed neutral arbiter

Genoa's republic was governed by a rotating, faction-riven aristocracy long before it had a single head of state; the office of **Doge** was created only in **1339**, when the popolo, exhausted by unending Guelf/Ghibelline noble strife, acclaimed the plebeian **Simone Boccanegra** as the first **Doge of Genoa** by public acclamation (a DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY-flavored crisis election, not a scrutiny). Genoa's structural instability came from its **alberghi** — federated noble clan-consortia (the great rival houses **Fieschi** and **Grimaldi** on the Guelf side, **Doria** and **Spinola** on the Ghibelline side) — each with private fleets, fortified compounds, and armed retinues, meaning the doge (intended, like the podestà, as a neutral arbiter above faction) had no independent coercive base of his own and was chronically deposed, exiled, or assassinated by whichever *alberghi* coalition currently held the edge (Boccanegra himself was forced to resign in 1345, returned only via a 1356 popular uprising, and died suddenly, probably poisoned, in 1363). Repeatedly, rather than resolve this internally, Genoa **submitted its sovereignty to a foreign protector** as an escape valve — to the **Visconti of Milan** (1421–1435), to the **French crown** (1396–1409, and again 1458–1461) — trading independence for stability. The cycle only broke with **Andrea Doria's constitutional reform of 1528**, which fixed a closed, alphabetically-rotated list of **28 alberghi** eligible for office, allied Genoa to Habsburg Spain, and finally produced two centuries of oligarchic stability.
=> Valoria design hook: Genoa is the model for **"chartered neutral office with no independent enforcement = capturable by faction militia by construction"** — a Standing-ladder rank whose demotion trigger is simply "whichever faction currently has more armed people nearby," and for the **chain-bypass Monarch mechanic as an explicit escape valve a Territory can invoke on itself** (voluntarily subordinating to an external Duchy/Monarch tier to buy stability it cannot produce internally) — a design-legal way for a collapsing Territory-tier polity to hand its own crisis upward rather than resolving it locally.

## 4. Papal States — elective ecclesiastical monarchy over a temporal principality

The Papal States were a unique hybrid: an **elective monarchy** (the pope, chosen for life by the **College of Cardinals** in **conclave**) ruling a temporal Italian principality through **legates** and **vicariates** in the Romagna, Umbria, and Marche. Conclave procedure itself was a governance fix: after the **1268–1271 vacancy** (nearly three years without a pope), Pope **Gregory X**'s bull ***Ubi periculum*** (1274) locked cardinal-electors in seclusion with shrinking food rations until they produced a two-thirds majority — a deliberately coercive consensus-forcing procedure, precisely because the office had **no hereditary or default successor** and needed one engineered. Popes routinely practiced **nepotism** — elevating nephews to the cardinalate and to secular fiefs (the "cardinal-nephew" institution) — as the papacy's substitute for dynastic continuity, since a pope's own biological children (where they existed, as with Alexander VI's Borgia children) could not inherit the office itself, only whatever temporal territory could be carved out and defended during the pontificate. **Cesare Borgia**'s conquest of the Romagna (1499–1503) under his father Pope **Alexander VI**, using condottiere contracts and vicariate deposition, is the fullest expression of the papacy operating as an ordinary temporal signoria with an elective-monarchic legitimating apex.
=> Valoria design hook: conclave's ***Ubi periculum*** coercive-consensus procedure is strong precedent for Valoria's **Consensus resolver (assent/stand-aside/holdout)** — model a holdout elector as imposing an escalating cost (shrinking resource ration) on the whole body until they flip to assent, giving holdout a real but decaying leverage curve rather than a free veto; and the cardinal-nephew pattern is a clean **`kinship` Ascendancy power_base note**: an office with no hereditary transmission of its own can still generate a *shadow* hereditary track by granting land/rank to kin during the tenure — useful for any Valoria office (e.g., an elected Warden) that a player wants to let "found a dynasty" sideways rather than directly.

## 5. Consolidating-monarchy backdrop and the Italian Wars (1494–1559)

Italy's fragmented city-state/signoria/papal patchwork existed inside a widening gap with the **consolidating dynastic monarchies** of France (Valois), Aragon/Spain, and the Empire — France's **Charles VIII** invasion of 1494 (the trigger for the Medici's fall, see Conflicts) opened sixty-five years of **Italian Wars** in which Milan, Naples, and the smaller states became a battleground and ultimately client-territories of larger consolidating crowns (French occupation of Milan 1499–1512 and again 1515–1521 under Louis XII/Francis I; Spanish/Imperial dominance confirmed at the **Battle of Pavia, 1525**, and the **Treaty of Cateau-Cambrésis, 1559**). The mechanism is structural: city-state/signoria-scale polities, however sophisticated their internal governance, lacked the fiscal-military scale to resist Country-tier consolidating monarchies once those monarchies turned outward.
=> Valoria design hook: this is the historical grounding for Valoria's **scale hierarchy itself** — a Territory/Settlement-tier polity (however elaborate its internal governance_mode) is structurally out-classed by a Country-tier actor exercising the **Monarch chain-bypass authority**; use 1494–1559 as the template for a "great-power intervention" event class where an external Country-tier actor's arrival resets the internal L/PS calculus of every lower tier simultaneously, regardless of local legitimacy.

## 1. Modes of Governance — Republic of Venice

### 1.1 Origins: from acclamation-assembly to closed constitutional machine
Venice's earliest "doges" (from *dux*) were reputedly acclaimed by a general assembly of citizens, the **Concio** (or *Arengo*), meeting in the Piazza San Marco — a loose, plebiscitary legitimation resembling a Germanic/Byzantine provincial acclamation more than a procedural election. Through the 11th–12th centuries this open, crowd-based mode was progressively displaced by nested, self-selecting councils. This is the master trajectory of Venetian constitutional history: **legitimacy migrates from an unbounded crowd to an increasingly bounded, procedurally-defended elite body**, at every step justified as *protecting the commune from any one man's ambition* (a Doge's, a great family's, or the mob's).
=> Valoria design hook: model this as a governance_mode transition path DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY → OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL triggered by an elite-closure event (see Serrata, §1.3) rather than a single fixed mode per polity — the taxonomy should support a settlement/territory's mode *migrating* under an in-fiction ratified event, with L/PS consent gating whether the migration holds or triggers unrest.

### 1.2 The crisis-response of 1172: creation of the Great Council and collegial constraint on the Doge
Doge **Vitale II Michiel** was assassinated by an enraged crowd in 1172 after a catastrophic, plague-ravaged naval campaign against Byzantium — the last time an unconstrained Doge's personal failure was answered by lynching rather than legal process. The constitutional response (traditionally dated to the same year) was twofold and became the template for everything after: (a) the **Great Council (Maggior Consiglio)** was created/enlarged as an indirect electoral body so no Doge again held power by raw acclamation, and (b) the Doge was thereafter always flanked by a college of **Ducal Councillors** (six by the mature constitution, one per *sestiere*) whose co-signature was required on ducal acts. From this point the Doge is never again a sole executive — every subsequent restriction on the office (§1.3) is an accretion on this 1172 template.
=> Valoria design hook: this is the historical prototype for constitutionally *neutering* a chain-bypass authority — model the Monarch bypass tier with an optional "leashed monarch" configuration (mandatory co-signature key from an attached council office before a Monarch-tier directive resolves) that a settlement can adopt via an L/PS-gated charter event, so the bypass authority still exists in the scale hierarchy but cannot act unilaterally without a companion Key.

### 1.3 The Doge: elected head of state, constitutionally a figurehead
By the high medieval constitution the Doge was **elected for life** (not hereditary — dynastic ambition had already been tried and violently repudiated: **Doge Pietro IV Candiano** attempted to found a hereditary dogeship and was killed in a 976 popular revolt that burned much of the city, including St Mark's and the Doge's Palace). Once elected, the Doge:
- swore the **Promissione Ducale**, a written oath enumerating specific restrictions, re-drafted and *lengthened* at every single accession by a standing magistracy, the **Correttori alla Promissione Ducale** ("correctors of the ducal promise"), whose explicit job was to write into the incoming Doge's oath a new clause closing off whatever abuse the *previous* Doge had gotten away with;
- could not open diplomatic correspondence, receive foreign envoys, or leave the Doge's Palace unescorted without Signoria permission;
- could not correspond privately with foreign powers or his own relatives abroad;
- had no vote-breaking or veto power in any council — he presided, but decisions were made by the collegial bodies below;
- upon death, was subject to a **posthumous audit**: the *Inquisitori sopra il Doge defunto* ("Inquisitors over the deceased Doge") reviewed the dead Doge's entire term for oath violations and could levy fines against his estate before his heirs could inherit.
This oath-then-audit pairing — a written mandate issued at accession, and a formal reconciliation of conduct against that mandate at exit — is the purest expression of Venice's **elect-inward / account-back layering**: the Doge is elected inward by an ever-narrower filtered electorate (§1.6) and accounted back against a written promise the moment he is out of a position to resist the audit.
=> Valoria design hook: this is the direct model for the resolution-quality → standing bridge. Bind the Correttori-alla-Promissione pattern to the Key arithmetic: an office-holder's **appointment Key** should carry an explicit Directive/Need "promise" payload set at investiture, and the **exit Key** (retirement, term end, death, deposition) computes the delivered-vs-demanded gap against that same payload — a bad gap should be able to dock the *successor's* effective standing-ladder authority (e.g. narrower initial Access) exactly as a new Doge's oath was narrowed by a predecessor's failures, making institutional memory mechanical rather than flavor text.

### 1.4 The Great Council (Maggior Consiglio) and the Serrata of 1297
The Maggior Consiglio was originally the broad-based electoral/legislative assembly (in principle open to any adult male citizen meeting a property/lineage bar) that elected the Doge, the Senate, and virtually every magistracy. On **28 February 1297** (28 Feb 1296 Venetian-style dating), under the sponsorship of Doge **Pietro Gradenigo**, the Council passed the **Serrata del Maggior Consiglio** ("Lockout of the Great Council"): membership was frozen to men who had sat on the Council in the preceding four years, or their patrilineal descendants, with new entrants added only by an annual lottery of ~40 candidate names. This single ordinance converted an elective, formally open commune into a **closed hereditary caste** — the Venetian patriciate — and is the hinge event of the entire hierarchy in §2. It was reinforced by later measures (the 1315–18 *Libro d'Oro*/registration process, formalized as the **Libro d'Oro** proper in 1506) that made noble birth a matter of documentary proof rather than council memory.
=> Valoria design hook: model the Serrata as the canonical **recognition-fission-in-reverse** event for the governance_mode taxonomy — a DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY that ossifies into OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL via a single ratified charter act (not gradual drift), with an immediate, mechanical side effect: the standing ladder's rank-0 entry gate switches from open application to closed-lineage + lottery. This is a clean, dated, non-MoH template for "a polity's governance_mode is legislated, not merely observed."

### 1.5 Senate, Signoria, and the Full College (Collegio) — the executive-legislative core
- **Senate (Consiglio dei Pregadi)** — literally "the invited/prayed-for council," reflecting its origin as an ad hoc body of trusted advisors summoned by the Doge, formalized as a standing body from roughly 1229 and growing from ~60 seats to some 300 by the 16th century (through repeated *Zonta*, "augmentation," additions). The Senate held the effective legislative sovereignty over foreign policy, war, finance, and maritime/mainland administration, subordinate only to Great Council ratification of major constitutional change.
- **Signoria** — the ten-person collegial "head of state": the Doge, his six Ducal Councillors, and the three heads (*Capi*) of the Quarantia Criminale. This body, not the Doge alone, held ceremonial and emergency sovereignty and had to act collectively — no member (including the Doge) could act unilaterally.
- **Collegio (Full College / Pien Collegio)** — 26 members: the 10-person Signoria plus 16 **Savi** ("wise men" / sages) split into three colleges — 6 *Savi Grandi* (grand sages, senior policy), 5 *Savi di Terraferma* (mainland affairs), 5 *Savi agli Ordini* (naval/maritime affairs). The Collegio set the Senate's agenda, received ambassadors, and functioned as the inner cabinet/steering committee that filtered virtually everything the Senate voted on.
This nested structure (Collegio drafts → Senate deliberates and votes → Great Council ratifies constitutional matters and elects personnel) is a textbook layered-consensus system where no single chamber both proposes and disposes.
=> Valoria design hook: this is a direct template for the DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY / OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL layering and for the Consensus resolver — model an agenda-setting inner Collegio-analog whose output is a proposal Key that a larger Senate-analog body then resolves via assent/stand-aside/holdout, with escalation to a Great-Council-analog only for charter-tier (governance_mode-changing) decisions.

### 1.6 The Council of Ten and the State Inquisitors — the security/enforcement layer
Created in 1310 as a **temporary emergency tribunal** to investigate the **Tiepolo–Querini conspiracy** (see conflicts_md §3.2), the **Council of Ten (Consiglio dei Dieci)** was renewed for successive terms (days → two months → five years) before being made **permanent on 20 July 1355** — deliberately days after the Marino Falier affair underscored the need for a standing internal-security organ. It comprised 10 elected members serving one-year non-renewable terms, sitting with the Doge and his six councillors ex officio (17 total when full), and after 1529 was regularly convened "with the *Zonta*" — a temporary augmentation of 15–20 additional senators — for grave state-security cases. In 1539 it spun off the **Inquisitori di Stato** (three State Inquisitors, two drawn from the Ten and one Ducal Councillor), Venice's famous secret police, who ran paid informer networks and the **Bocche di Leone** ("Lions' Mouths") — carved stone denunciation slots into which anonymous accusations of treason, tax fraud, or corruption could be dropped. The Ten's proceedings were secret, its verdicts (exile, execution, damnatio memoriae) were final, and it deliberately bypassed the ordinary judiciary (Quarantia) for anything touching state security.
=> Valoria design hook: the Ten + Zonta + Inquisitori structure is the model for a standing security/enforcement office whose **rank sets secession blast-radius** (ROTK principle): a Ten member (elite standing-ladder rank) who turns disloyal can pull an entire faction network and force a Zonta-scale response, while a low-rank informer's betrayal is locally contained — calibrate Demotion Magnitude and blast-radius scope directly off which rank tier holds Council-of-Ten-equivalent Access.

### 1.7 Legality-checking bodies: Avogadori di Comun and the Quarantia
The **Avogadori di Comun** (three magistrates, "advocates of the commune") were the Republic's guardians of legality: they could suspend or annul *any* act of *any* body, including the Great Council itself, if procedurally improper, and they were the office responsible for verifying patrician birth registrations against the Libro d'Oro. The **Quarantia** ("Council of Forty") began as Venice's supreme court and secondary legislative chamber before the Senate eclipsed it in that latter role; caseload growth split it into **Quarantia Criminale**, **Quarantia Civil Vecchia**, and **Quarantia Civil Nuova** by the 14th century. Its three presiding officers (*Capi di Quaranta*) sat in the Signoria, linking judiciary to executive.
=> Valoria design hook: the Avogadori are a clean model for a standing **legality/legitimacy auditor office** independent of the chamber it audits — implement as a permanent watcher Key-emitter that can null a resolution-quality Key retroactively (voiding an improperly-obtained standing gain) rather than merely scoring it, closing a loophole the arithmetic-gap bridge alone would miss (gaming the Directive/Need delta through procedural fraud rather than genuine delivery).

### 1.8 Doge selection: the 1268 sortition-and-election protocol
After a deadlocked election in 1229, Venice adopted (1268) the most elaborate electoral security protocol of the medieval world, in force unchanged until 1797. A child (the *Ballottino*, an orphan boy chosen to be above suspicion of bribery) drew lots at each stage. The nine-stage alternation of sortition (lot) and election (vote) ran: 30 Great Council members chosen **by lot** → reduced **by lot** to 9 → the 9 **elect** 40 → the 40 reduced **by lot** to 12 → the 12 **elect** 25 → the 25 reduced **by lot** to 9 → the 9 **elect** 45 → the 45 reduced **by lot** to 11 → the 11 **elect** the final **41 electors**, who then elected the Doge, requiring a supermajority of **at least 25 of 41 votes**. The purpose, per the Republic's own framing and modern game-theoretic analysis (Mowbray & Gollmann's protocol study), was to make it computationally and socially infeasible for any family or faction to pack the final electorate — sortition breaks pre-arranged coalitions between stages, election lets the body still exercise judgment.
=> Valoria design hook: this is the canonical non-arbitrary precedent for a hybrid sortition+election resolver — implement doge-analog high-office selection (e.g., a settlement's top OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL seat) as a multi-round Consensus-resolver pipeline alternating random Key-holder subsets with assent/stand-aside/holdout votes, rather than a single election Key, specifically to make faction-capture require controlling *every* round rather than one — a mechanically legible defense against a single Ascendancy power_base (patronage) dominating appointment.

### 1.9 Legitimation ideology: the "myth of Venice" and the mixed constitution
Venetian self-legitimation rested on the **civic religion of St Mark** (the Evangelist's relics "translated" to Venice in 828, underwriting the city's sacred founding claim) and its calendar rituals — most famously the **Sposalizio del Mare** ("Marriage of the Sea"), the Doge's annual ring-casting ceremony asserting Venice's dominion over the Adriatic, first performed from roughly 1000 and formalized after 1177. Politically, the Republic's constitution was explicitly theorized — most influentially in **Gasparo Contarini's *De Magistratibus et Republica Venetorum* (1543)** — as a **mixed constitution** combining monarchy (the Doge), aristocracy (Senate/Ten), and democracy (Great Council), consciously invoking the Polybian *anacyclosis* tradition (Polybius's theory that pure constitutional forms degenerate in a fixed cycle and that only a mixed constitution resists decay) to explain the Republic's uniquely long institutional stability compared to Rome's civil wars or the Greek poleis' revolutions.
=> Valoria design hook: use the Contarini/Polybius framing — **not** Mandate of Heaven — as the grounded historical citation anywhere a design note wants to justify why a mixed OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL/DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY hybrid mode is unusually collapse-resistant versus a pure AUTOCRATIC_FIAT or pure CONSENSUS_UNANIMITY mode; this satisfies the "ground collapse/collision reasoning in non-MoH precedent" constraint directly.

## 1. Modes of Governance

### 1.1 The imperial center: Three Excellencies and Nine Ministers (Sangong Jiuqing)
- Formalized under Emperor Wu (r. 141–87 BCE) and stable through Later Han (25–220 CE), the top of the civil bureaucracy comprised the **Three Excellencies** (Sangong): the **Grand Commandant** (Taiwei, military affairs), the **Excellency over the Masses** (Situ, civil administration/education), and the **Excellency of Works** (Sikong, public works/law). Each held nominal salary rank "Fully Two Thousand Bushels" (Zhong Erqian Shi) and ran an independent personal secretariat that vetted recommendations and drafted memorials.
- Subordinate to them, the **Nine Ministers** (Jiuqing) — e.g. the Superintendent of Ceremonial (Taichang), the Minister of the Guard (Weiwei), the Minister of Justice (Tingwei, the central court of last appeal for capital cases), the Grand Herald (Dahonglu, foreign/tributary relations), the Minister of Finance (Dasinong) — each ran a "Court" (Si), a proto-ministry with its own clerks and sub-bureaus.
- Real decision procedure: the emperor convened **court conferences** (tingyi) where Excellencies, Ministers, and invited specialists debated a policy question and recorded opinions; the emperor then ruled, typically adopting the majority view or a synthesis, but was never procedurally bound. This is consultative autocracy, not constitutional deliberation — the body could be, and often was, overruled or bypassed entirely by direct rescript.
  => Valoria design hook: model tingyi as a **DELIBERATIVE_ASSEMBLY** governance_mode nested *inside* an **AUTOCRATIC_FIAT** crown tier — a settlement/territory can run a genuine deliberative sub-procedure for advisory input while the ratifying authority above it remains pure fiat; expose this as a distinct governance_mode flag on the advisory body, separate from the tier that actually ratifies.

### 1.2 The Imperial Secretariat's rise — the real locus of power migrates (Later Han, esp. from Emperor He, r. 88–105 CE onward)
- Structurally, the **Shangshu Tai** (Imperial Secretariat / Masters of Writing), nominally a minor palace clerical office under the Privy Treasurer, became the emperor's actual decision-processing organ: memorials were routed through it, drafts issued through it, and by mid-Later Han its **Director** (Shangshu Ling) and **Vice Director** (Shangshu Puye) had eclipsed the Three Excellencies in real influence while remaining junior in nominal rank (salary grade only "One Thousand Bushels").
- This is the textbook "inner court supersedes outer court" dynamic: formal hierarchy (Excellencies/Ministers, high salary-rank) diverged sharply from functional hierarchy (Secretariat clerks with the emperor's ear). Real authority tracked *proximity to the person of the ruler*, not table-of-organization rank.
  => Valoria design hook: this is a concrete precedent for **Standing ladder power_base = "bureaucratic" outranking nominal rank via patronage-proximity** — implement an NPC's *effective* influence on Directive resolution as a function of (patronage/access to the seat-holder) independent of their nominal rank on the 0–7 ladder, so a rank-3 "inner secretary" can out-leverage a rank-5 "outer minister" exactly as Shangshu officials did to the Sangong.

### 1.3 Legitimation of the throne — Heaven's Mandate as ideology (historical background only, per binding constraint)
- Han legitimation rested on **Tianming** (Mandate of Heaven): the emperor as **Son of Heaven** (Tianzi) ruled by a conditional cosmic commission, made empirically legible through **zaiyi** (portents-and-anomalies) theory — eclipses, comets, floods, earthquakes, five-colored vapors, and "reproving" (zaiyi) memorials from officials (esp. the Chunqiu/Gongyang and Yijing-based cosmology of scholars like Dong Zhongshu, d. c. 104 BCE) read as Heaven's commentary on the ruler's conduct. Court astrologers/Grand Clerks (Taishi Ling) and censors invoked portents to force policy reversals, minister dismissals, or self-blame edicts (zui ji zhao) from the throne.
- The doctrine cut both ways: it disciplined bad rule (a portent could force an emperor to issue self-censure and reshuffle ministers) but also **legitimated dynastic replacement** — Wang Mang's usurpation (9–23 CE) and, later, Cao Pi's acceptance of Han Xiandi's abdication (220 CE) were both staged as Heaven *transferring* its mandate through ritualized abdication (*shanrang*), supported by a dense apparatus of engineered omens, "discovered" prophetic texts (chenwei apocrypha), and court petitions "begging" the new ruler to accept Heaven's will.
  => historical-only (NOT a Valoria mechanic per design-lead ruling)

### 1.4 Territorial administration — the dual commandery/kingdom system (junguo, 郡國制)
- Emperor Gaozu (r. 202–195 BCE), needing allies at the founding, re-enfeoffed the eastern half of the realm as semi-autonomous **kingdoms** (guo) under kinsmen kings (wang) with their own courts, coinage, and tax collection, while administering the western core directly through **commanderies** (jun) run by centrally-appointed **Governors** (Taishou) reporting through the Excellencies. This is a genuine dual governance_mode empire: ROYAL_COURT_APPOINTMENT commanderies coexisting with quasi-LANDHOLDER_FRANCHISE kingdoms answering only loosely to the center.
- The center steadily converted kingdom autonomy into commandery uniformity: Emperor Jing's suppression of the **Rebellion of the Seven States** (154 BCE, see conflicts_md) let him strip kings of administrative staff appointment rights; Emperor Wu's **Tui'en Ling** ("Decree of Grace," 127 BCE) mandated partible inheritance of kingdoms among *all* a king's sons (not just the heir), fragmenting each kingdom into shrinking marquisates within a generation or two — a bloodless structural dissolution of the rival power base.
  => Valoria design hook: Tui'en Ling is a direct precedent for **recognition-fission as a Crown-side offensive tool, not just a diverged-population response** — the Crown can *compel* an over-large landholder faction to fission its holdings among heirs/claimants, deliberately using inheritance-partition rules to shrink a Duchy-tier rival's territorial base over several turns without a single battle; model this as a Monarch-tier chain-bypass directive that rewrites a landholding faction's territory-to-heir mapping.

### 1.5 Recruitment before examinations matured — recommendation, not testing
- Han staffed officialdom chiefly through **cha ju** ("investigate and recommend"): commandery Governors and kingdom Chancellors nominated candidates annually under fixed categories — most importantly **Filial and Incorrupt** (Xiaolian, quota-based by commandery population from 134 BCE) and, for higher/irregular talent, **Abundant Talent** (Maocai). Nominees were interviewed/tested at court (sometimes via written policy-question essays, *duice*) but the *gate* was social recommendation by an incumbent official, not open competitive examination — the imperial examination proper (keju) is a Sui-Tang (581 CE+) institution, centuries later.
- This produced a **de facto local-gentry veto on entry to officialdom**: recommenders (governors, chancellors, and later the "pure critique" (qingyi) opinion-makers of the capital and academies) functioned as informal gatekeepers whose personal networks of obligation (the recommended owed lifelong quasi-filial loyalty to the recommender, a bond called *guli*, "old lord") created vertical patron-client chains cross-cutting the formal chain of command.
  => Valoria design hook: model cha ju as the **MERITOCRATIC_BUREAUCRATIC** governance_mode's actual Initiation-Gate at Standing ladder ranks 0→1 and 1→2 — advancement requires a *sponsor* (an existing rank ≥3 seat-holder) to spend patronage capital nominating the candidate, creating a permanent Mentor-link (per the Standing ladder's Mentor field) whose obligations persist and can be called in later, exactly as historical *guli* bonds were.

### 1.6 The Nine-Rank/Rectifier system — Cao Wei's replacement for cha ju (instituted 220 CE)
- With Han recommendation infrastructure collapsed by civil war, **Chen Qun** (d. 237 CE), a senior minister under Cao Pi (Emperor Wen of Wei, r. 220–226), proposed the **Nine-Rank Arbiter System** (Jiupin Zhongzheng Zhi): each commandery/province got a **Rectifier** (Zhongzheng, "impartial arbiter," typically a locally prominent official serving concurrently at court) who graded resident gentry candidates on a nine-tier scale (Upper-Upper through Lower-Lower) by *pin* (moral repute) and *xingzhuang* (character summary), triennially, feeding the Ministry of Personnel's appointment pool.
- Intended as meritocratic triage amid wartime chaos, it structurally re-entrenched aristocracy within two generations: Rectifiers were themselves drawn from and beholden to the great established clans, so "upper" grades clustered on pedigree ("no commoners in the upper ranks, no gentry in the lower ranks" — the proverbial critique that crystallized by the Western Jin, 265+ CE, under the reformed system's drift into the "Rectifier Nine-Rank" default of the great clans).
  => Valoria design hook: this is the canonical real-world case of a **MERITOCRATIC_BUREAUCRATIC governance_mode drifting into de facto OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL / LANDHOLDER_FRANCHISE without any rule changing on paper** — implement a slow-drift mechanic where a faction's stated governance_mode can silently diverge from its *effective* power_base distribution (patronage/kinship creeping into a nominally merit-gated ladder) unless an explicit periodic audit event (a "Rectifier probity check") resets grading toward the merit criteria; tie the drift rate to how concentrated the Mentor-graph already is.

### 1.7 Regional inspectors becoming regional rulers — the Inspector-to-Shepherd (Cishi → Mu) conversion
- Emperor Wu created 13 **Regional Inspectors** (Cishi, 106 BCE) as low-rank (600-shi) roving overseers auditing commandery governors — deliberately kept junior in rank to *prevent* them accumulating independent standing (an inspector outranked in salary by the governors he audited).
- Crisis reversed this by design choice, not drift: in 188 CE, amid the post-Yellow-Turban emergency, the minister **Liu Yan** petitioned Emperor Ling to upgrade selected Inspectors into **Regional Shepherds** (Zhou Mu) with full civil, fiscal, *and* military command over their entire province, explicitly to concentrate suppression authority. The reform worked operationally and failed politically: shepherds (Liu Yan himself in Yi Province/Sichuan, Liu Biao in Jing Province, Yuan Shao in Ji Province, Gongsun Du in Liaodong) converted delegated emergency authority into permanent, hereditary-trending regional sovereignty within a decade, answering to Luoyang/Chang'an in name only.
  => Valoria design hook: this is the precise real-world template for the **rank = secession blast-radius principle** — a Territory-tier military delegation that is *upgraded in scope* (civil+fiscal+military fused into one seat, matching a Province-tier grant) rather than merely in nominal rank produces a secession blast-radius equal to the full delegated scope, not the seat-holder's prior rank; the mechanic should key blast-radius off *scope of granted authority*, not off standing-ladder position alone, precisely because Liu Yan's shepherds seceded at province-scale despite modest prior personal rank.

### 1.8 Court factional government under child emperors — Regents, Empresses Dowager, and the Eunuch counterweight
- Later Han's structural weak point: child emperors succeeded repeatedly (Emperor Shang enthroned at 100 days in 105 CE; Emperor Chong at 1; Emperor Zhi at 7; Emperor Huan at 14; Emperor Ling at 12), forcing **regency by the Empress Dowager**, exercised in practice through her natal family — the "consort clan" (waiqi) — who staffed the Grand Marshal/General-in-Chief (Da Jiangjun) office and the inner court. Notable regental clans: the Deng (Empress Dowager Deng Sui, regent 106–121), the Liang (General-in-Chief Liang Ji, effectively dictator 141–159, responsible for the murder of Emperor Zhi in 146 CE by poisoned cake after the boy-emperor called him a "domineering general" to his face), the He (General-in-Chief He Jin, regent for Emperor Shao, 189).
- The throne's structural counter-lever against an over-mighty consort clan was the **palace eunuch corps** (huanguan), who alone had unsupervised physical access to the adult emperor once he came of age and could execute a coup *inside the palace walls* on his behalf: Emperor Huan, at 14 effectively a prisoner of the Liang clan, used five eunuchs (Shan Chao, Xu Huang, Ju Yuan, Zuo Guan, Tang Heng) to destroy Liang Ji in a single palace action in 159 CE, ending the Liang regency — and was rewarded by enfeoffing all five as marquises the same day. This created the recurring three-body oscillation of Later Han politics: **Emperor ↔ consort-clan regent ↔ eunuch faction**, with the regular Confucian bureaucracy (the "pure critique" scholar-officials) a fourth party perpetually shut out of the other three's palace-proximity advantage.
  => Valoria design hook: model this as a standing **three-seat OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL with an asymmetric access channel** — implement a "palace access" resource distinct from formal rank that only the eunuch-analog faction and the regent-analog faction can hold, and let a chain-bypass (Monarch-tier) action become available specifically to whichever faction currently holds palace access, regardless of Standing-ladder rank, so the counter-coup dynamic (junior-rank faction unseats senior-rank faction via access, not standing) is mechanically reachable.

### 1.9 Legitimation of a broken center — provincial governors ruling in the emperor's name (190s–220 CE)
- After Dong Zhuo's 189–192 CE seizure of the child Emperor Xian (r. 189–220) and subsequent warlord fragmentation, no governance_mode label cleanly fits the interregnum: warlords like Cao Cao (who took physical custody of Emperor Xian in 196 CE, moving the court to Xu) ruled their territories as **AUTOCRATIC_FIAT** in substance while performing **ROYAL_COURT_APPOINTMENT** in form — issuing edicts *in the emperor's name*, appointing rivals to hollow imperial titles to buy them off (e.g., Cao Cao's repeated grants of Excellency/General titles to Yuan Shao before their war), and using the captive court's ratification machinery purely as legitimation theater for decisions already made by the war-band's own command structure.
  => Valoria design hook: implement a **governance_mode "mask"** distinct from governance_mode "substance" — a Territory/Duchy can display one governance_mode (what its paperwork and public Legitimacy calculations show) while its actual decision procedure runs a different mode entirely; drive the Legitimacy/Popular-Support gate off the *displayed* mode and let sophisticated players/NPCs exploit the gap, mirroring how Cao Cao's Xu-based court kept issuing "Han" edicts that were pure warlord fiat.

### 1.1 The founding dual-authority settlement (Kamakura, 1180–1221)
Minamoto no Yoritomo built the bakufu ("tent government") as a parallel military-administrative apparatus that never abolished the imperial court (**kuge**) in Kyoto — it grew a second, competing structure alongside it. Institutionally: the **Samurai-dokoro** (Board of Retainers, est. 1180) administered discipline and muster among Yoritomo's vassal band; the **Kumonjo** (1184, renamed **Mandokoro** in 1191) handled civil administration and documents; the **Monchūjo** (1184) was the judicial board adjudicating vassal disputes. ### 1.2 Oligarchic council government under the Hōjō regency (1203–1333)
After Yoritomo's death the shogunal line itself was hollowed out: his sons Yoriie and Sanetomo were murdered (Sanetomo assassinated 1219 by his own nephew Kugyō), extinguishing the Minamoto line in the third generation. Power passed to the **Hōjō** family, who held the office of **shikken** ("regent to the shogun," 1203–1333, beginning with Hōjō Tokimasa then Hōjō Yoshitoki) — regents to a shogun who was thereafter a legitimating figurehead only: first infant Fujiwara/sekke ("regental house") nominees, later imperial princes ("miya shōgun"). Real decision-making sat with the **Hyōjōshū** (Council of Retainers/State, established 1225 by Hōjō Yasutoki) — a standing council of senior gokenin and Hōjō kin that deliberated and voted on law, land disputes, and war. In 1232 Yasutoki promulgated the **Goseibai Shikimoku**, the first warrior-authored law code, explicitly grounding bakufu justice in precedent and council deliberation rather than shogunal fiat.
=> Valoria design hook: the Hyōjōshū is a clean historical anchor for **OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL** as a governance_mode — a body of co-equal seated members (not one autocrat) issuing binding rulings by internal procedure, with a named seat-count and a codified rulebook (Shikimoku) functioning as the settlement's governance charter.

### 1.3 The Jōkyū collision and permanent court subordination (1221)
Retired Emperor Go-Toba, exploiting the succession vacuum after Sanetomo's murder, tried to reclaim direct control over jitō-administered estates and called the country to arms against Hōjō Yoshitoki. The bakufu crushed the revolt within weeks; three sovereigns (Go-Toba, and the reigning/retired Juntoku and Tsuchimikado) were exiled to remote islands (Oki, Sado, Tosa); huge tracts of court/rebel estates were confiscated and redistributed to loyal gokenin. The bakufu then installed the **Rokuhara Tandai** (Kyoto military deputyship, 1221–1333) as a permanent supervisory garrison over the court — the moment the "dual authority" stopped being a partnership and became bakufu suzerainty with court legitimacy retained only as a formal fiction.
=> Valoria design hook: model this as the point where a Legitimacy-holding authority (Crown) loses the material capacity to enforce its consent-gate, while the administering authority (a chain-bypass or Parliament-equivalent) keeps the fiction running for procedural cover — a template for a "hollow Crown" state where L/PS still nominally gates governance-type cascades but a garrisoned overseer office actually enforces compliance.

### 1.4 The Kemmu Restoration and the Nanboku-chō schism (1333–1392)
Emperor Go-Daigo, allied with turncoat bakufu general Ashikaga Takauji and loyalist Nitta Yoshisada, destroyed the Kamakura bakufu in 1333 and attempted genuine **AUTOCRATIC_FIAT** direct imperial rule (the Kemmu Restoration) — rewarding court nobles over the warriors who had actually done the fighting. This reward-mismatch alienated the warrior class within three years: Takauji rebelled (1335–36), installed a rival emperor (Kōmyō, the Northern Court, backed by his own shogunate), and Go-Daigo fled to Yoshino to found a rival Southern Court. Japan then had **two simultaneously legitimate emperors each issuing conflicting investitures** for 56 years (1336–1392), until Ashikaga Yoshimitsu brokered reunification in 1392 on a promise of alternating succession that the Ashikaga then reneged on.
=> Valoria design hook: a direct, non-MoH template for the **resolution-quality → standing bridge**: Go-Daigo's response Key to the warrior faction's service-Directive (reward with land/office) delivered a large negative gap versus what was demanded, and that arithmetic gap is what drove the demotion of his regime's standing among its own military backers — not portent theology, a literal under-delivery on an obligation.

### 1.5 Ashikaga/Muromachi: negotiated oligarchy of shugo daimyo (1338–1573)
The Ashikaga shogunate governed through the **kanrei** (deputy shogun), an office rotated among three houses — Shiba, Hosokawa, Hatakeyama ("Sankanrei") — who, together with the resident **shugo daimyo** required to keep mansions in Kyoto, formed a standing consultative council around the shogun. This is meaningfully different from Kamakura's council: it is not one clan's regency but a genuine multi-house power-sharing arrangement the shogun needed to broker continuously. Ashikaga Yoshimitsu (3rd shogun, r. 1368–1394) additionally sought external legitimation by accepting Ming China's investiture as "King of Japan" (**Nihon Kokuō**) for tributary trade purposes in 1401 — a foreign-sourced legitimacy layer stacked on top of the domestic emperor-derived one, controversial precisely because it bypassed the court.
=> Valoria design hook: model as **NEGOTIATED_ESTATES** — the shogun is first-among-equals bound to standing consultation with named great-house delegates, and a faction can additionally seek an external chain-bypass legitimation (a foreign Crown-equivalent recognition) that domestic rivals treat as illegitimate cover; useful for a faction seeking Parliament-adjacent recognition it hasn't earned domestically.

### 1.6 Sengoku fragmentation: governance devolved to the domain (1467–1590s)
The Ōnin War (1467–1477) gutted shogunal authority; for over a century governance effectively relocated to individual **han**/domains under **Sengoku daimyo**, each running an autonomous military-fiscal state with its own house law (**bunkoku-hō**, e.g. the Imagawa Kana Mokuroku 1526, the Date's Jinkaishū 1536) — governance modes fragmented into as many local experiments (some near-autocratic, some council-of-retainers, some proto-meritocratic) as there were surviving houses (~250 by 1600).
=> Valoria design hook: this is the historical case for **recognition-fission at scale** — a central Country-tier authority's collapse doesn't produce anarchy, it produces dozens of self-legitimating Territory/Duchy-tier governance experiments simultaneously, each needing its own governance_mode roll rather than inheriting the parent's.

### 1.7 Toyotomi interlude: cadastral fiat and collective regency (1582–1600)
Toyotomi Hideyoshi (himself a gekokujō case, below) unified Japan via **AUTOCRATIC_FIAT**, executed nationwide via two structural instruments: the **Taikō kenchi** (cadastral surveys, 1582–1598) that fixed the kokudaka assessment underpinning all later Japanese governance, and the **katanagari** ("sword hunt," 1588) that froze social status by disarming the peasantry and separating warrior from farmer permanently. On his deathbed (1598) Hideyoshi, lacking confidence in his five-year-old heir Hideyori, formally chartered the **Go-Tairō** (Council of Five Elders: Tokugawa Ieyasu, Maeda Toshiie, Mōri Terumoto, Uesugi Kagekatsu, Ukita Hideie) to rule collectively, checked by the **Go-Bugyō** (Five Commissioners, administrators) — a deliberate oligarchic council substituted for a minority regency.
=> Valoria design hook: the kenchi is the historical origin of the **kokudaka standing-currency** mechanic below; the Go-Tairō is a second, harder OLIGARCHIC_COUNCIL case — explicitly designed as a check-and-balance rather than an organic development, useful for a scripted "regency council" governance_mode transition triggered by an heir being below an age/rank threshold.

### 1.8 The bakuhan taisei: dual-track sovereignty formalized (1603–1868)
Tokugawa Ieyasu's shogunate (est. 1603) formalized what Kamakura and Ashikaga had improvised: the **bakuhan system** — a central bakufu (shogun; **rōjū**, Council of Elders, rotating monthly duty, drawn from mid-rank fudai daimyo deliberately capped below ~50,000 koku so none could rival the shogun; **wakadoshiyori**, junior elders overseeing hatamoto; **ō-metsuke**, inspectors-general; **machi-bugyō**, city magistrates; **kanjō-bugyō**, finance magistrates) governing directly only the shogun's own ~25% of assessed national kokudaka plus major cities, while roughly 260 **han** retained internal self-government under their daimyo (own retainer bands, own taxation, own law) so long as they met bakufu-imposed obligations. The emperor's court in Kyoto was preserved as the sole source of the shogun's title (**sei-i taishōgun**, re-conferred at each succession) but was placed under total supervision by the **Kyoto Shoshidai** office and bound by the **Kinchū narabini kuge shohatto** (1615), which regulated even the emperor's scholarly and succession activities. The **Buke shohatto** ("Laws for the Military Houses," first issued 1615, revised and made binding 1635 under Iemitsu) codified daimyo obligations including **sankin-kōtai**.
=> Valoria design hook: bakuhan is the cleanest real-world model for **Country → Duchy** bidirectional cascade with a gating consent mechanic: the han (Duchy-analogue) is self-governing by default (a LANDHOLDER_FRANCHISE/MERITOCRATIC_BUREAUCRATIC blend internally) but every daimyo's continued tenure is gated on a running Legitimacy/compliance score the Country-tier authority (bakufu) can zero out at will (kaieki, below) — model the shogun as able to reach into any Duchy and revoke its governance charter without going through the normal cascade, i.e., a second chain-bypass authority layered under the Monarch-equivalent Crown fiction.

### 1.9 Sankin-kōtai: hostage-kin as a continuous consent-enforcement mechanic (codified 1635, in force to 1862)
Iemitsu's 1635 revision of the Buke shohatto made **sankin-kōtai** ("alternate attendance") compulsory: daimyo alternated residence between their domain and Edo (roughly annually, cycle varying by house category and distance), while wives and heirs were required to reside in Edo **permanently** as de facto hostages. This was reinforced by ruinous processional and dual-residence costs (scaled to a house's kokudaka and status tier) that kept daimyo treasuries — and thus their capacity to raise independent armies — chronically drained. It functioned continuously for 227 years, relaxed only in 1862 as the regime's authority visibly failed, and disappeared with the bakufu in 1867.
=> Valoria design hook: model sankin-kōtai as a standing **Ascendancy power_base tax** — a faction seat-holder's kinship power_base is directly leverageable by the suzerain authority (mandatory hostage-kin at the capital), continuously suppressing the "military" and "kinship" power_base levers available to a rank-holder without ever triggering an active Demotion event; a clean mechanical precedent for a passive, always-on standing-suppression modifier tied to rank rather than a one-time punishment.

### 1.10 Bakumatsu: the dormant Crown reactivates (1853–1868)
Commodore Perry's 1853 arrival forced the bakufu, for the first time in centuries, to consult daimyo opinion and even solicit the court's endorsement of a foreign treaty — an admission that the emperor's nominal sovereignty had never actually been extinguished, only suspended. This reopened the **sonnō jōi** ("revere the emperor, expel the barbarians") legitimacy claim among tozama-domain lower samurai (chiefly Satsuma and Chōshū), culminating in Tokugawa Yoshinobu's **Taisei Hōkan** (return of governing authority to the emperor, November 1868), which dissolved the bakufu by restoring, in form, the very authority the 1185/1192 settlement had displaced 683 years earlier.
=> Valoria design hook: the Crown-analogue chain-bypass authority should be able to sit dormant/nominal for centuries of AUTOCRATIC/OLIGARCHIC eclipse and still be mechanically "live" — a design invariant that a Monarch-tier consent gate never actually goes to zero even under total practical eclipse, so a sufficiently large two-signal collision (external material shock + internal legitimacy reactivation) can snap governance-type back to a Crown-appointment cascade overnight. Non-MoH grounding: this is legitimacy-fiction dormancy/reactivation, structurally closer to a Roman Senate reasserting itself after a general's death than to any zaiyi/portent logic.

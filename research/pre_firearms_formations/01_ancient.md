# Chunk A — Ancient Formations (~3000–500 BC)

**Scope:** From earliest attested organized warfare through the eve of the Greco-Persian Wars. Covers Sumer, Akkad, Egypt, Assyria, Hittites, early Greece (archaic-to-early-classical hoplite), Persian Achaemenid system, and the Eurasian steppe (Scythian/Cimmerian).

This is the substrate for everything that follows. Many "Renaissance" patterns (combined arms, shock-and-missile coordination, flank security, the recurring tension between depth and frontage) are visible here in primitive form.

---

## 1. Sumerian Phalanx (~2500 BC, southern Mesopotamia)

**Primary source:** Stele of the Vultures (Lagash, reign of Eannatum, c. 2450 BC). The earliest pictorial evidence of an organized close-order formation in human history.

- **Composition:** Heavy spearmen with rectangular body shields (probably leather over wood frame), bronze helmets, copper-headed spears. Supported by battle wagons (4-wheeled, onager-drawn) carrying javelin-armed warriors — proto-chariots, not yet horse-drawn.
- **Geometry:** Six visible ranks on the stele, shields overlapping in the front rank. Likely deeper than the carving shows (artistic compression). Frontage uncertain; the unit on the stele appears to be a "block" rather than a long line.
- **Equipment dependency:** Required matched equipment (shield + helmet + spear) — i.e., a state capable of equipping a class of professional/semi-professional infantry. This is the formation's strategic prerequisite.
- **Tactical role:** Shock impact via massed spear-points and an immovable shield front. Defensive against missiles by virtue of shield coverage, offensive by mass forward motion.
- **Strategic context:** City-state warfare in flat alluvial terrain (lower Mesopotamia). Disputes over canal water and border-fields. Ground favored close-order infantry; no significant cavalry yet (horse not yet domesticated for riding in this region in this period).
- **Vulnerabilities:** Flanks; rear; uneven ground; mobile missile harassment (limited in the era).
- **Throughline flag:** *The earliest grand-formation principle visible in history is "concentrate spearheads behind a shield-line, and march forward." Every shock-infantry formation through the Swiss pike square is a variation on this primitive.*

## 2. Akkadian Combined-Arms Levy (~2300 BC)

Sargon of Akkad's empire moves beyond pure phalanx. Compositions described in administrative tablets include:

- Spear infantry (continuation of Sumerian tradition).
- **Bowmen** as a distinct arm — Akkadian composite-bow technology is the first widespread use of a missile arm that can threaten armored opponents at range.
- Light skirmishers (javelin, sling).

**Geometry / role:** Unknown in detail; texts emphasize bowmen as battle-deciders ("the bow ate the spear"). The tactical implication is that pure spear-phalanx ceases to be sufficient — missile screening, missile counter-fire, or rapid closing becomes necessary.

**Throughline flag:** *The phalanx-vs-bow tension is the first attested "rock-paper-scissors" relationship in military history. It recurs at Cannae (Roman vs. Numidian skirmishers), Carrhae (legion vs. Parthian horse archers), Crécy (French men-at-arms vs. English longbow), and arguably in every era thereafter until firearms collapse the bow leg of the triangle.*

## 3. Egyptian New Kingdom Army (~1550–1100 BC)

After the Hyksos expulsion, Egypt reorganizes into a professional standing force structured around chariot corps.

- **Divisions:** ~5,000 men each, named after gods (Amun, Ra, Ptah, Set). Ramses II at Kadesh (c. 1274 BC) fields four such divisions.
- **Components per division:**
  - **Chariotry** — elite arm, 2-man chariots (driver + composite-bow archer; sometimes a third shield-bearer). Used in massed charges and flanking sweeps.
  - **Infantry** — spearmen with smaller shields than Sumerian models, plus axemen with the **khopesh** (sickle-sword).
  - **Archers** — long-range missile arm, composite bows.
  - **Mercenary auxiliaries** — Sherden, Libyans, Nubians (Medjay light troops, often used as scouts and skirmishers).
- **Geometry:** Infantry deployed in ranks; chariotry on flanks or as reserve striking force. Order-of-march separated from order-of-battle: Egyptian divisions are documented marching in column, then forming line on contact.
- **Tactical role of chariotry:** Mobile missile platform (the archer shoots from the moving car) AND shock arm (running infantry down once they break). Two roles, one unit — a recurring pattern.
- **Battle of Kadesh case study:** Hittite chariots ambush the Egyptian Re division on the march before it can form. Ramses's own Amun division is also caught half-deployed. The battle is "won" (or drawn) by Egyptian counter-charge from the Ne'arin reinforcement column hitting the Hittite chariots in flank while they loot the Egyptian camp.
- **Throughline flag:** *Battles are often decided before the main lines meet, by which side's secondary force (reserve, flank column, late-arriving allies) reaches the field in usable order. This is structurally invariant from Kadesh through Marengo.*

## 4. Hittite Heavy Chariot Doctrine (~1400–1200 BC)

- **3-man chariots** (driver, shield-bearer, spear/javelin warrior) — heavier than the Egyptian 2-man version.
- **Tactical role:** Pure shock arm — they close to javelin or spear range and deliver mass against opposing chariotry or unformed infantry. Less missile-centric than Egyptian doctrine.
- **Implication:** Heavy chariot doctrine privileges the charge; light chariot doctrine privileges the missile platform. The same vehicle class hosts two distinct unit-formation philosophies. (Pattern recurs in heavy vs. light cavalry through every cavalry era.)

## 5. Assyrian Combined Arms (~900–612 BC) — the first true integrated system

The Neo-Assyrian Empire fields what is arguably the first standing army with permanent, integrated combined arms. Innovations:

- **Cavalry as a permanent arm** — replacing chariots gradually through the 8th century. Riders initially in pairs (one holding both horses while the other shoots), maturing into single riders by Sennacherib's era. The cavalry arm becomes the primary mobile striker.
- **Heavy spear infantry** with large shields, conical helmets, scale armor. Deployed in close order, used for the main battle line.
- **Archers** — both foot and (later) mounted. Often paired in front of the spear line, shooting in support before melee contact.
- **Sappers, engineers, siege specialists** — Assyrian siege capability (battering rams, siege towers, mining) is unmatched until Rome.
- **Light troops** — slingers, javelin men, often drawn from subject peoples.
- **Logistics corps** — supply trains, road-building, communications by signal-fire.

**Order of battle:** Spear infantry in center, archer detachments interspersed or screening front, cavalry on wings, chariots (still used) as elite shock reserve. Light troops as skirmish line in front of the main body.

**Strategic context:** Imperial frontier warfare against highly varied enemies (Aramaean tribes, Urartu mountain kingdoms, Babylonian heavy infantry, Elamite cavalry, Egyptian chariotry). A diverse threat menu drives a diverse force structure.

**Throughline flag:** *Combined-arms force structure is an empire-level institution. Mono-cultural armies (all-pike, all-cavalry, all-missile) emerge from polities with narrower tax bases or narrower threat models. The Assyrian rule is: as your enemy menu diversifies, your force structure must too.*

## 6. Greek Hoplite Phalanx (archaic, ~700–490 BC)

The classical Greek heavy-infantry formation in its early form.

- **Composition:** Free citizen-soldiers (hoplites — from *hoplon*, the large round shield).
  - **Aspis** (also called *hoplon*): 80–100 cm diameter, wood faced with bronze, weight ~6.5–8 kg. Held by central armband (porpax) and rim grip (antilabe), allowing the shield to cover the bearer's left side AND the right side of the man to his left.
  - **Dory**: 2–3 m thrusting spear, butt-spiked (sauroter) for use if the head broke off.
  - **Xiphos** / **kopis**: short sword and slashing sword as backup.
  - **Bronze cuirass / linothorax** (linen composite), greaves, Corinthian helmet.
- **Geometry:** Standard depth 8 ranks. Frontage as wide as the field allowed — typical phalanx is 8 deep regardless of how wide. Some Greek city-states (Thebes) experimented with deeper formations; Sparta sometimes fought 12 deep.
- **Spacing:** ~1 m per file in march order; closes to ~0.5 m per file (*synaspismos*, "shield-locking") on contact. The shield-locking is the formation's defining moment — it converts the line from a marching column into an interlocked wall.
- **Tactical role:** Decisive shock combat. The phalanxes close, push spear-to-spear at the front, and the rear ranks press forward (*othismos*, "the shoving") to break the opposing line.
- **The right-flank drift:** Each hoplite's shield covers the man on his left. Men instinctively shade right to seek the cover of their neighbor's shield. The whole phalanx drifts rightward in advance, and the right wing tends to overlap and envelop the enemy's left while suffering envelopment on its own left. This is observable at Mantinea (418 BC, Thucydides describes it explicitly), Delium (424 BC), and probably present in every archaic phalanx battle.
- **Strategic context:** Greek polis warfare — disputes between agriculturally-based city-states over border land. Ground typically chosen by mutual consent for set-piece battles. The hoplite is a citizen-farmer, not a professional; the phalanx model only works if the polis can field a few thousand of these men, which constrains the population base.
- **Vulnerabilities:**
  - Flanks (especially left, due to shield orientation).
  - Rear.
  - Uneven / rough ground (breaks shield-locking).
  - Light troops in numbers when the phalanx cannot close — at Sphacteria (425 BC) and Lechaeum (390 BC), Spartan hoplites are worn down by missile harassment they cannot reach.
  - Cavalry once it becomes effective (Macedonian period).
- **Notable engagements:** Marathon (490 BC) — Greek phalanx defeats Persian sparabara; Thermopylae (480 BC) — phalanx exploits terrain to negate Persian numbers; Plataea (479 BC) — phalanx breaks Persian infantry on open ground.

**Throughline flag:** *The right-flank drift is one of the cleanest examples of "unit-level mechanics producing emergent grand-formation behavior." No general orders the drift; it emerges from individual shield orientation. The principle — that bottom-up mechanics produce top-down patterns — is a meta-throughline worth elevating.*

## 7. Achaemenid Persian System (~550–330 BC)

The largest empire of its era fields a doctrinally and ethnically heterogeneous army centered on a missile-heavy infantry core supported by cavalry.

- **Sparabara** — the Persian infantry standard. Front rank carries a large rectangular wicker-and-leather shield (*spara*) and a short spear; the nine ranks behind are archers. The spara forms a portable wall; archers shoot over and through it.
- **Immortals** — elite 10,000-man corps (Herodotus's name; Persian name probably *Anūšiya*, "companions"). Always maintained at full strength by immediate replacement. Functioned as guard infantry and the spear-and-bow core of major engagements.
- **Cavalry** — Median heavy cavalry, Persian noble cavalry, plus subject contingents (Saka horse archers, Bactrian, Indian).
- **Subject infantry** — extremely varied: Babylonian, Egyptian, Greek mercenary hoplites, Anatolian peltasts, etc.
- **Geometry:** Sparabara deployed in a wall — line of pavise-bearers in front, 9 archer ranks behind. Sometimes described as a "ten-deep" formation. Cavalry on flanks.
- **Tactical role:** Wear the enemy down with missile fire from a protected base. Cavalry exploits any opening or screens flanks. The Persian model treats infantry primarily as a missile platform, not a shock arm.
- **Vulnerability against hoplites:** The spara-wall stops arrows and javelins, but Greek hoplite phalanxes closed too fast for arrow attrition to matter and then crushed the spara-wall in melee. Marathon and Plataea both show this dynamic. The Persian system worked well against other missile-centric or unarmored opponents; it failed against fully armored heavy shock infantry.
- **Strategic context:** A multi-ethnic empire fielding mass conscript armies needs a force structure that can absorb diverse contingents. The sparabara model is "scalable across populations" in a way that pure hoplite is not — you can issue wicker shields and bows to large numbers quickly.

**Throughline flag:** *Force structure is downstream of state structure. The kind of army a polity can field is constrained by who it can recruit, how it can equip them, and what its supply economy supports. This is invariant from Achaemenid Persia through Renaissance Italian condottieri.*

## 8. Eurasian Steppe Horse-Archery (Scythian, Cimmerian, ~700 BC onward)

The first attested mounted-archer doctrine in the historical record. Sets the template for every steppe people that follows (Sarmatians, Huns, Avars, Magyars, Pechenegs, Cumans, Mongols, Turks).

- **Composition:** Almost entirely cavalry, predominantly mounted archers with composite bows. Light by Iranian/Greek standards — minimal armor on rider and horse — but extreme mobility.
- **Geometry:** No formation in the sedentary sense. Operates in loose swarms, often spread across miles of frontage. Concentrates briefly to deliver missile volleys; disperses before the enemy can close.
- **Tactical role:** Attrition through missile fire while refusing close combat. Engagement on the steppe horse-archer's terms: their tempo, their distance, their direction of withdrawal.
- **Key tactical primitive — feigned retreat:** Engage, withdraw under apparent rout, shoot backward over the horse's rump (the "Parthian shot"), draw the pursuer onto fresh forces or rough ground.
- **Strategic context:** Pastoral nomadism produces a male population that is, from childhood, an archer-cavalryman. The state's military capability is identical to its civilian skill base. No supply train is needed in the steppe-economy sense — herds move with the warriors.
- **Vulnerabilities:** Fortified positions; rough or wooded terrain; enemies who can match their mobility (other steppe peoples); long campaigns against well-supplied sedentary defenders.
- **Historic engagement:** Darius I's invasion of Scythia (~513 BC) — the Scythians refuse battle, withdraw, and exhaust the Persians, who eventually retreat. The campaign is a textbook example of attrition-by-mobility against a sedentary force.

**Throughline flag:** *Steppe horse-archery is the single most asymmetric military system in pre-modern history. It dominates the Eurasian land mass from ~700 BC to ~1700 AD against virtually every sedentary opponent that cannot field comparable cavalry or impose terrain. The only reliable counters are: (a) one's own steppe cavalry (Byzantine, Chinese, Mamluk approaches), (b) fortified positions and dense terrain, (c) firearms that ultimately collapse the bow's advantage. This is the second meta-throughline candidate.*

## Chunk A — tentative throughlines

1. **Shock + missile = the durable combination.** Pure shock (Sumerian phalanx, Hittite chariot) or pure missile (sparabara, steppe horse-archer) is beaten by the integrated form (Assyrian combined arms, mature Egyptian doctrine). The integration is what makes a force "complete."

2. **Force structure is downstream of state structure.** What a polity fields reflects who it can recruit and equip. Citizen-hoplite, pastoralist horse-archer, imperial mercenary auxiliary — each is the army its society can produce.

3. **The phalanx-vs-bow rock-paper-scissors emerges immediately.** Spear-shield blocks bow only when it can close; bow eats shield-block when range is preserved. Cavalry enters as the third leg (mobile, can dictate engagement). This three-cornered relationship is visible in Akkadian sources and never fully resolves until firearms.

4. **Right-flank drift / asymmetry-from-individual-mechanics.** Hoplite drift is the cleanest example, but emergent behavior from unit-level rules is everywhere — chariot lines, shield-wall geometry. *Meta-throughline candidate: grand-formation behavior is rarely commanded; it's emergent from unit-formation behavior, which is emergent from individual equipment and training.*

5. **Battles decided by the secondary force.** Kadesh's Ne'arin column, Plataea's Spartan late-arrival, the second wave at Marathon — across the era, the decisive moment is often when reinforcement, reserve, or flanking force arrives, not when the main lines first meet. *Meta-throughline candidate: timing of the second commit matters more than placement of the first.*

6. **The steppe asymmetry.** Mounted-archer doctrine is so durable across millennia that it deserves its own analytical track. (Will recur in chunks C, D, and E2 especially.)

7. **Combined-arms is an institutional achievement, not a tactical choice.** Assyrian integration represents centuries of state-building. Polities that cannot afford or organize multiple arms field mono-cultures and lose to integrated opponents — unless their mono-culture is itself the meta-dominant one (steppe horse-archery).

# Chunk B — Classical Antiquity (~500 BC–500 AD)

**Scope:** From the mature Greek hoplite (Peloponnesian War era) through Macedonian and Hellenistic successor states, Roman manipular and cohort systems, Carthaginian doctrine, Parthian and Sassanid Iran, and parallel developments in Han China and Mauryan/Gupta India.

This is the era where most of the durable "doctrinal primitives" of pre-firearms warfare get established. The Roman material is dense; everything after deals in some way with the legacy.

---

## 1. Mature Greek Hoplite & Theban Innovations (~431–362 BC)

The classical phalanx (Chunk A §6) reaches doctrinal maturity during the Peloponnesian War and is then deliberately re-engineered by Thebes in the early 4th century.

- **Standard phalanx** unchanged in equipment, but command begins to manipulate depth and frontage deliberately rather than reflexively.
- **Pelopidas's Sacred Band** (Thebes, founded ~378 BC): 300-man elite unit, 150 pairs of (allegedly) lovers, fights in close formation as the spearhead of the Theban left.
- **Epaminondas at Leuctra (371 BC):** the first attested deliberate weaponization of the right-flank drift problem.

### The oblique order

Standard phalanxes meet in parallel lines. Epaminondas does this:

- **Stacks his left wing 50 ranks deep** instead of the conventional 8–12. The Sacred Band fronts this deep wedge.
- **Refuses his right and center** — they advance slowly or hold position, deliberately delayed so they do not engage at the same moment as the deep left.
- **Aims the deep left at the enemy's strongest wing** (Spartan right, where the kings traditionally stood).

Result: 50-deep Theban left hits the Spartan right with overwhelming local mass, breaks it before the rest of the lines engage, and rolls up the now-leaderless Spartan center. The Spartan right is destroyed and King Cleombrotus is killed. The Spartan center and left, still advancing into their own undamaged opposite numbers, are unable to react in time.

This is the **oblique order** — refuse one wing, mass the other, defeat the enemy in detail. It will be reproduced (consciously or not) by Frederick the Great, Napoleon, and arguably von Manstein. It is the first attested example of a general winning by deliberately *not* presenting a uniform line.

**Throughline flag:** *Local concentration beats uniform engagement. The "uniform line" is a special case; serious commanders weight one sector and let others time their engagement against it.*

## 2. Macedonian Sarissa Phalanx (Philip II, ~359 BC onward)

Philip II of Macedon redesigns the phalanx around a much longer pike (the **sarissa**, 4.5–6 m, two-handed) and integrates it with cavalry, light infantry, and engineers as a true combined-arms force.

- **The sarissa:** two-handed pike requiring both arms; shield (small *pelte*) hangs from a neck-strap and covers the left shoulder. The pike is too long for one-handed use.
- **Geometry:** 16 ranks deep standard. First 5 ranks' pikes project beyond the front; ranks 6–16 hold pikes vertical or angled, providing replacement and weight. This gives the formation **5 layers of pike-points** at the front — far denser than the hoplite's single rank of dory.
- **Spacing:** typically 0.5 m per file (close order); some sources suggest even tighter on contact (*synaspismos*).
- **Tactical role:** Hold the enemy line in place and grind it down by pike pressure. The phalanx is the **anvil**.
- **Companion cavalry (hetairoi):** Heavy shock cavalry, the **hammer**. Wedge formation, used to charge enemy cavalry off the field and then strike the held enemy infantry in flank or rear.
- **Hypaspists:** elite infantry — better armored, more flexible than the phalanx, used on the hinge between phalanx and cavalry, and for special operations (siege storms, river crossings, pursuit).
- **Light troops:** Agrianian javelin men, Cretan archers, Thracian peltasts — screening, skirmishing, broken terrain.
- **Hammer-and-anvil:** the doctrine Alexander uses throughout his campaigns. Phalanx fixes the enemy in place; cavalry maneuvers to deliver the killing blow on flank or rear.

### Granular battle-phase choreography — Gaugamela (331 BC), per Arrian / Diodorus

Phases observable in source accounts:

1. **Approach / deployment.** Alexander angles his army oblique to Darius's longer line, refusing his left and weighting his right. The Persian line overlaps both Macedonian flanks.
2. **Skirmish / cavalry screening (~30 min).** Persian scythed chariots are loosed at the Macedonian center; Agrianian javelin men and Macedonian foot open lanes between phalanx files, the chariots pass through and are destroyed. Persian and Macedonian light cavalry on the right skirmish, with Alexander gradually drawing his Companions farther right, dragging the Persian left along.
3. **Gap-opening.** As the Persian left cavalry stretches to keep up with Alexander's rightward shift, a gap opens in the Persian line near Darius's center-right.
4. **Companion charge (the killing moment).** Alexander wheels the Companions left into a wedge and charges *through* the gap, aimed directly at Darius. The wedge penetrates; Darius's bodyguard collapses; Darius flees.
5. **Phalanx exploitation.** The Macedonian phalanx, which has been advancing into the Persian center, exploits the panic; meanwhile the Macedonian left (Parmenion) is in serious trouble against Persian cavalry but holds long enough for Alexander to disengage from pursuit and ride to Parmenion's relief.
6. **Pursuit.** Companions and Thessalian cavalry run down the routed Persian army for miles.

**Timing observations:**
- The decisive moment is phase 3–4, when the cavalry punch lands. Total elapsed time from contact to Persian collapse: probably under 90 minutes for the main decisive sector, with hours of pursuit after.
- The phalanx never "decides" the battle — it fixes the enemy so the cavalry can.
- Parmenion's left wing **must hold without winning** for the right-wing victory to be exploitable. Refused-wing doctrine requires a wing willing to suffer.

**Throughline flag:** *Hammer-and-anvil = the dominant pattern of decisive shock-arm doctrine for the next ~2000 years. The "fixing force" and the "deciding force" become a permanent doctrinal pairing — visible in Roman legion + auxiliary cavalry, medieval levy + knights, Swiss pike + arquebusiers (eventually), Mongol nerge tactic, Zulu chest + horns.*

## 3. Hellenistic Successor Armies (~323–146 BC)

After Alexander's death, his generals (Diadochi) carve up the empire. Each successor state inherits the Macedonian system but evolves it differently — and in the process exposes the phalanx's limits.

- **Pike phalanx becomes brittle.** Successor commanders make the phalanx longer-piked (up to 7 m sarissa) and deeper (sometimes 32 ranks). It becomes nearly immovable frontally but completely dependent on flat ground and on its cavalry winning the flank fight.
- **War elephants** introduced from India after Alexander's campaigns. Used as shock weapons, as a screen against opposing cavalry (horses unfamiliar with elephant scent panic), and as mobile platforms. Counters develop: caltrops, fire, leaving lanes for charging elephants to pass through and be killed from behind (the Roman approach at Zama).
- **Scythed chariots** used by Seleucids — generally ineffective against trained infantry that can open lanes.
- **Cataphracts** — fully armored heavy cavalry on armored horses — emerge in the Seleucid east and especially Parthia. Heavier than the Macedonian companion, used for crushing impact.

### Key Hellenistic engagements

- **Battle of Raphia (217 BC):** Ptolemy IV vs. Antiochus III. Both sides field full Macedonian-style armies with pike phalanxes, elephants, and cavalry. Phalanxes grind in the center; the battle is decided by cavalry on the wings. Demonstrates that two Macedonian-style armies fighting each other produces a long, indecisive central grind plus a cavalry-decided flank.
- **Battle of Magnesia (190 BC):** Roman legions vs. Seleucid Antiochus III. Antiochus's cavalry breaks the Roman left but pursues too far. The Roman center exploits the now-unsupported Seleucid phalanx, which is unable to maneuver. The phalanx's flanks are hit and it disintegrates.
- **Battle of Pydna (168 BC):** Roman manipular legion vs. Macedonian sarissa phalanx of Perseus. The phalanx advances onto broken ground; gaps open between phalanx blocks; Romans pour into gaps and attack the phalanx flanks where pikes cannot pivot. Phalanx destroyed; end of Macedonian independence.

**Throughline flag — the phalanx's lethal weakness:** *The sarissa phalanx cannot maneuver after it is committed. Once it advances, it must keep advancing in a straight line on flat ground. The instant it encounters broken terrain, an oblique angle, or any need to reorient, gaps open between blocks; pikes cannot be brought to bear sideways. The system is a one-way irresistible force when its conditions are met, and a coffin when they're not.*

## 4. Roman Manipular System (~390–107 BC)

The Romans, after early military disasters (Allia, 387 BC; Caudine Forks, 321 BC), develop a flexible heavy-infantry system that addresses exactly the phalanx's failure modes.

### Composition — the *triplex acies* (triple battle line)

By the Polybian period (~150 BC, after the Punic Wars), a legion of 4,200–5,000 men is organized as:

- **Velites** (light infantry skirmishers, 1,200 per legion). Javelins, sword, small round shield, sometimes a wolf's-head helmet. Open-order screen in front of the heavy infantry.
- **Hastati** (front line heavy infantry, 1,200 per legion). Young men, *scutum* (large semi-cylindrical shield), *gladius* (short stabbing sword), two **pila** (heavy javelins), helmet, mail or scale armor for those who could afford it. 10 maniples of 120 men.
- **Principes** (second line, 1,200 per legion). Veterans, similar equipment, often better armored. 10 maniples of 120.
- **Triarii** (third line, 600 per legion). Oldest, most experienced. **Spear** (not gladius) — the only line still fighting in something like phalanx style. 10 maniples of 60.
- **Equites** (cavalry, 300 per legion). Roman citizen cavalry, weak; supplemented by allied (*socii*) cavalry which is the real Roman mounted arm.

### Geometry — the famous quincunx

Each line is deployed in 10 maniples. **Gaps between maniples equal to a maniple's frontage.** The principes maniples cover the gaps of the hastati; the triarii cover the gaps of the principes. Viewed from above, the deployment looks like the five-spot face of a die — the **quincunx**.

```
[H] _ [H] _ [H] _ [H] _ [H] _ [H] _ [H] _ [H] _ [H] _ [H]    (hastati)
  _ [P] _ [P] _ [P] _ [P] _ [P] _ [P] _ [P] _ [P] _ [P] _    (principes)
[T] _ [T] _ [T] _ [T] _ [T] _ [T] _ [T] _ [T] _ [T] _ [T]    (triarii)
```

### Tactical role / phase structure

1. **Velites engage first.** Skirmish, throw javelins, fall back through the gaps in the hastati line.
2. **Hastati throw pila at ~15–20 m.** Two pila per man, in volleys. The pilum is designed to bend on impact so it cannot be thrown back, and to weight or pierce enemy shields, fouling them.
3. **Hastati close with gladius and scutum.** Short, brutal stabbing combat. Maniples rotate fresh men forward at intervals.
4. **If hastati break or tire, principes pass through.** The maniple structure allows the rear line to advance into the gaps; tired hastati fall back through the new gaps that open behind the principes. This is the system's defining innovation — **line relief mid-battle without breaking formation.**
5. **If principes also fail, the triarii commit** — "*res ad triarios rediit*," "it has come to the triarii," meaning desperate straits. Triarii fight in spear-phalanx style with their pila planted, and the hastati and principes shelter behind them or rally.
6. **Cavalry on the wings** — usually allied. Often a weakness; Roman armies frequently lose the cavalry fight on the flanks (Cannae being the catastrophic example).

### Why it works

- **Modular.** The maniple is large enough to be effective alone, small enough to maneuver around obstacles. A maniple can wheel, face about, change frontage independently. The Macedonian phalanx as a whole cannot do any of these things.
- **Self-relieving.** No other classical formation can swap fresh troops in mid-battle. The Macedonian phalanx fights with the men it deploys; once the front rank is exhausted or dead, the phalanx degrades. The Roman legion brings up the next line.
- **Modular against terrain.** On broken ground the maniples cluster around defensible features; on the flat they form continuous lines. The phalanx has only one mode.

### The Cannae problem

The system is **not** invulnerable. At Cannae (216 BC), Hannibal exploits Roman over-confidence and the Roman habit of fielding extremely deep formations under unified consular command:

- Roman line drawn extremely deep (probably 50+ ranks in the center) — a single mass intended to bull through Hannibal's center.
- Hannibal's center (Spanish and Gallic infantry) is deliberately weak and is pulled into a **convex bulge that becomes concave** as the Romans press in.
- Hannibal's heavy African infantry, in column on the flanks, *face inward* as the Roman mass passes between them — the line is now a U-shape with the Romans inside.
- Numidian and Spanish heavy cavalry win the cavalry fight on the wings, then circle behind the Roman rear.
- The Roman mass is enveloped on all four sides and slaughtered. ~50,000+ Romans killed in a single afternoon.

**Cannae is the textbook double envelopment.** It is still studied at staff colleges. The key insight: a deeply massed center can be *consumed* rather than resisted — let it advance, then close around it.

**Throughline flag:** *Depth without flexibility is a vulnerability, not a strength. Mass alone does not decide; mass plus the ability to maneuver decides. This pattern reappears in every century — Crécy, Agincourt, Bicocca, Cerignola — where a deep, committed enemy line is consumed by a flexible opponent.*

## 5. Roman Cohort System (~107 BC onward, post-Marian reforms)

Gaius Marius's reforms abolish the property qualification, regularize equipment, and replace the maniple with the **cohort** as the legion's tactical building block.

- **Cohort:** 480 men (6 centuries of 80). Ten cohorts per legion (the first cohort is double-strength in the Imperial era, ~800 men).
- **No more distinction** between hastati / principes / triarii — all legionaries are equipped uniformly with gladius, pilum, scutum, mail.
- **Standardized equipment** — state-issued. The legionary becomes a long-service professional (16–25 years).
- **Deployment** — typically 4-3-3 or 3-4-3 in cohorts across three lines, with the same quincunx-style staggering. The cohort is larger than the maniple, allowing it to fight in deeper local order while preserving inter-cohort gaps.

The cohort system trades some maniple-level granularity for cohort-level robustness. It scales better — Imperial Rome routinely fields legions across vast distances under varied commanders, and the standardized cohort survives officer quality variance better than the more complex Polybian system.

**Auxiliary integration.** Imperial legions are paired roughly 1:1 with **auxiliary cohorts** of non-citizen troops: light infantry, archers, slingers, and especially **auxiliary cavalry** (Gaulish, Thracian, Numidian, Syrian, eventually cataphract units). Auxiliaries provide the missile, light, and mounted arms the legion lacks. **Combined arms** is the imperial form; the legion is the heavy-infantry core, not the whole army.

**Throughline flag:** *The legion's superpower is institutional: long-service professionals, standardized equipment, training pipeline, retirement system, engineering corps. Other classical armies have better individual warriors but worse institutions. Rome wins by force-generation, not by tactical novelty.*

## 6. Carthaginian System & Hannibal's Doctrine

Carthage's army is a confederation: Punic citizen heavy infantry (small in number), Libyan-Phoenician spearmen (the African heavy infantry — disciplined and well-equipped), Iberian and Celtic infantry mercenaries, Numidian light cavalry (the finest light cavalry of the era), Iberian heavy cavalry, Balearic slingers, and elephants.

Hannibal's genius is in **integration**. At Cannae (covered above), at Trebia (218 BC), at Lake Trasimene (217 BC — an ambush, not a set-piece), he combines:

- A central infantry line whose specific purpose is to **absorb without breaking** (often Spanish and Gallic, deliberately placed there as expendable cohesion).
- Heavy African infantry on the wings as the **maneuver force** (the inward-turning jaws of the envelopment).
- Numidian and Spanish cavalry as the **deciders** — they must win the flank cavalry fight quickly so they can wheel into the enemy rear.

The doctrine requires **perfect timing** across multiple unit types: the center must hold long enough for the wings to develop the envelopment; the cavalry must win the flank fight in time to close the rear; the infantry wings must pivot at the right moment.

**Throughline flag:** *Carthaginian doctrine under Hannibal is the high point of pre-Roman tactical coordination — a single commander synchronizing units of radically different speed, weapon, and morale across a 2 km front. The skill set required is exactly what later imperial commanders (Belisarius, Subutai, Jan Žižka, Gonzalo de Córdoba) will rediscover. Set-piece envelopment is a permanent template.*

## 7. Parthian Doctrine (~247 BC – 224 AD)

Parthia inherits the Iranian and steppe cavalry traditions and produces a doctrine specifically lethal to Roman legions.

- **Cataphracts** — fully armored heavy cavalry (rider in scale or mail head-to-knee; horse in scale or mail trapper). Armed with **kontos** (heavy two-handed lance) and mace. Shock arm.
- **Horse archers** — light, mobile, composite-bow armed. Mass missile fire from beyond legion pilum range. Cretan-bow Roman auxiliaries cannot match Parthian bow rate-of-fire or accuracy from horseback.
- **No infantry to speak of.** Parthian armies on campaign are nearly all cavalry.

### Carrhae case study (53 BC)

Crassus marches a Roman army of ~7 legions into Mesopotamia and is met by Surena with ~9,000 horse archers and ~1,000 cataphracts.

Phase-by-phase:

1. **Roman approach.** Crassus forms his legions into a hollow square (the *agmen quadratum*) to protect against surround. Heavy infantry on all four faces; cavalry and baggage in the center.
2. **Parthian envelopment from range.** Horse archers ring the Roman square at ~50–100 m and shoot in massed volleys. Roman scuta and mail provide some protection, but at long sustained engagement, wounds accumulate.
3. **Resupply.** Surena has prepared a camel-train of replacement arrow bundles — Romans expect Parthian archers to exhaust their quivers and stop. They do not. This is a logistical innovation that breaks Roman doctrinal assumptions.
4. **Cataphract charge against weakened sectors.** When a face of the Roman square is sufficiently weakened by missile attrition, cataphracts charge it. Roman infantry, having spent hours in defensive posture and absorbing missile fire, cannot effectively resist a heavy cavalry charge from a single direction.
5. **Roman cavalry counter-charge** by Crassus's son Publius — destroyed by feigned retreat onto fresh horse archers.
6. **Final collapse.** Crassus killed; ~20,000 Romans killed, ~10,000 captured. One of Rome's worst defeats.

**The key principle Carrhae illustrates:** *if your enemy can shoot you and you cannot reach him, you lose. Period. Roman doctrine had no answer to sustained horse-archer attrition on open ground, and the auxiliary system had not yet integrated enough horse-archer or counter-cavalry capability.*

**Throughline flag — steppe-archer asymmetry reconfirmed.** This is the same dynamic the Scythians used against Darius, the Parthians against Crassus, the Huns against the late Romans, the Mongols against everyone. **The "shoot and don't be reached" doctrine is a permanent fixture of the meta until firearms.**

## 8. Sassanid Doctrine (~224–651 AD)

The Sassanid successor to Parthia adds:

- **Heavier cataphracts** — the *clibanarii*, even more armored than Parthian counterparts.
- **War elephants** in significant numbers.
- **Foot infantry** — relatively poor quality but in larger numbers than Parthia.
- **Persian archers** — foot archers, in massed formation, complement the cavalry.

Sassanid armies fight Rome (and Byzantium) to a strategic standstill for four centuries. The combined-arms structure is more balanced than Parthia's pure-cavalry approach, and it is what Byzantium ultimately learns from and adapts (see Chunk C).

## 9. Han Chinese Military System (~206 BC – 220 AD)

Parallel development on the eastern end of Eurasia. Han China fields the largest professional armies of the era — campaigns of 100,000+ men are normal.

- **Heavy infantry** — disciplined conscript and professional spearmen, halberd-armed (*ji*, *ge*), with composite armor.
- **Crossbow** — the Han military innovation that most differentiates them. The Chinese crossbow has a complex bronze trigger mechanism enabling sustained accurate fire; crossbow corps form a major fraction of Han armies. Effective at greater range than the composite bow of mounted opponents.
- **Heavy cavalry** — develops in response to the Xiongnu (steppe) threat.
- **Steppe-style horse archery** — Han adopts the steppe pattern in mirror form by recruiting nomadic auxiliaries.
- **Massive logistics base** — Han state capacity supports armies at distances and durations no other classical state can match.

### The Han answer to steppe horse-archery

Where the Romans never solve the Carrhae problem, the Han partially do:

- **Crossbow over horseback bow** — at sustained range, massed crossbow volleys defeat horse-archer skirmish. The crossbow is slower per-shot but harder-hitting and can be drawn by men who are not lifelong archers (training base is wider).
- **Mirror-cavalry recruitment** — adopt the steppe mode by paying or co-opting steppe auxiliaries.
- **Logistics-as-weapon** — Han garrisons in the Tarim Basin pre-position supplies, so armies do not have to forage. Steppe opponents who try to outlast Han forces find Han forces don't run out.

**Throughline flag:** *Crossbow + state-logistics is the most successful anti-horse-archery doctrine before firearms. Variants of it appear in late Roman / Byzantine systems and (most directly) in medieval European city-state militias armed with crossbows (Genoese, Flemish, Italian). It works because it shifts the rock-paper-scissors: now there is a missile weapon that can be wielded by non-elite troops, can defeat armor at range, and is cheaper to train into a population than horse-archery.*

## 10. Mauryan / Gupta India (~322 BC – 550 AD)

Indian military doctrine is structured around the **chaturanga** — the "four-fold army" (the same word as chess in Sanskrit). The four divisions are:

- **Patti / infantry** — the bulk; spear and shield, sometimes bow.
- **Ashva / cavalry** — chariot-derived and pure cavalry; quality varies by region.
- **Hasti / elephant corps** — the prestige arm. Indian war elephants are larger and more aggressively used than Hellenistic-imported ones.
- **Ratha / chariots** — persist longer in India than in the West.

### Elephant doctrine

- Used as **mobile fortresses** — a war elephant carries a *howdah* with 2–4 archers or javelin men plus a mahout. The combination is a moving missile platform with crushing close-combat capability.
- Used as **shock weapon** — mass elephant charges can break unprepared infantry; horses panic in front of elephants whose scent they don't know.
- **Vulnerabilities** — elephants panic under heavy missile fire, and panicked elephants are as dangerous to their own side as to the enemy. Indian doctrine includes the mahout's *ankusha* (hook) and sometimes a sharp blade to kill the elephant if it goes rogue.

**Battle of the Hydaspes (326 BC):** Alexander vs. Porus. Porus fields ~200 elephants in a line in front of his infantry. Alexander's phalanx cannot advance against the elephants; his cavalry maneuvers them out of position; Macedonian light infantry harass the elephants with javelins until they panic and trample their own side.

The fundamental tactical primitive of Indian warfare — **infantry-elephant-cavalry-chariot in a four-arm combined formation** — predates Hellenistic combined arms by centuries but does not develop the institutional command-and-control to use the four arms in tight coordination. Indian armies are often defeated by smaller, more tightly integrated forces (Alexander, later the Ghaznavids).

**Throughline flag — institutional command vs. force composition.** *Having the right unit types is necessary but not sufficient. You also need command-and-control institutions that can coordinate them. Indian doctrine has the composition; it lacks the institutional integration that Hellenistic and Roman systems develop. This will recur when Ottoman, Mongol, and European systems meet Indian states in later eras.*

## Chunk B — tentative throughlines

1. **Oblique order / local concentration.** Epaminondas weaponizes asymmetry deliberately. The principle that "uniform line is a special case" runs through Hellenistic, Roman, and every subsequent era of skilled command.

2. **Hammer-and-anvil = the dominant decisive pattern.** Fix the enemy with infantry, deliver the killing blow with cavalry (or with a maneuver force of any kind). Macedonian phalanx + Companions is the prototype; the structure recurs through medieval knights + infantry, Mongol heavy + light cavalry, Zulu chest + horns.

3. **Pike phalanx is brittle.** Long pikes are irresistible frontally on flat ground and useless in any other condition. Trade-off: maximum frontal force vs. zero flexibility.

4. **Roman maniple/cohort = the durable answer.** Modular formation that can swap fresh troops mid-battle, fight on broken ground, and react to flanking pressure. This is the gold-standard heavy-infantry system of classical antiquity, and its descendants (drilled cohort-style infantry under unified command) reappear in Byzantine doctrine and Renaissance Spanish reforms.

5. **Cannae = the envelopment template.** Hold center weak-but-flexible, let the enemy advance, swing the wings inward, close cavalry behind. Studied for 2,200 years and counting.

6. **Steppe horse-archer asymmetry persists.** Carrhae confirms what Darius found against Scythians: heavy infantry cannot reach mounted archers on open ground. Counters require either (a) one's own steppe cavalry, (b) terrain, (c) a missile weapon usable by mass infantry (Han crossbow), or (d) eventually firearms.

7. **Force-generation > tactical brilliance over the long run.** Rome wins by being able to replace four legions after Cannae and field them next year. Carthage cannot replace Hannibal-grade armies; nor can Hellenistic kingdoms; nor can Parthia after Carrhae. Institutional depth — recruitment, training, logistics, supply, replacement — outlasts tactical genius.

8. **Combined arms requires command integration.** Having the units isn't enough; you need command-and-control institutions to synchronize them. Indian chaturanga has the composition; Hellenistic and Roman systems have the integration. Composition without integration loses.

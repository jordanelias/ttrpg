# Chunk E1 — Late Medieval & Renaissance Pre-Firearms Europe (~1300–1525)

**Scope:** The European theater from the early-14th-century "infantry revolution" through the late-Renaissance gunpowder transition. This is the most heavily weighted chunk in the project. It covers:

- The original Western infantry victories over feudal cavalry (Courtrai 1302, Bannockburn 1314).
- The English longbow system at maturity (Crécy 1346, Poitiers 1356, Agincourt 1415).
- The Hussite wagenburg revolution (1420s–30s).
- The Swiss pike system at peak (Laupen, Sempach, and especially Grandson / Murten / Nancy 1476–77).
- Burgundian combined-arms reforms under Charles the Bold (the failure case that defines what late-medieval combined arms can and cannot do).
- Landsknechte as the German mirror of Swiss doctrine.
- Italian condottieri and the "battle-avoidance" mercenary tradition.
- French gendarme cavalry — the *compagnies d'ordonnance* as one of the first true standing armies.
- Gonzalo de Córdoba and the Spanish reforms (the *coronelía* and proto-tercio).
- The Italian Wars transition battles where pike begins meeting arquebus at scale (Cerignola 1503, Ravenna 1512, Marignano 1515, Bicocca 1522, Pavia 1525).

The dominant pattern of the era: **the feudal-cavalry-dominant model that ruled Western Europe for two centuries breaks down across multiple independent fronts** — pike, longbow, wagon-fortress, and ultimately gunpowder — and a new set of doctrinal primitives emerges that will define warfare for the next 200 years.

---

## 1. The Infantry Revolution Begins — Courtrai (Battle of the Golden Spurs), 11 July 1302

The Flemish cities, in revolt against French Crown control, deploy urban militias against a French royal army under Robert of Artois. The battle is the **first major Western European victory of formation infantry over heavy feudal cavalry in centuries** and is the doctrinal opening of the era.

### Flemish composition

- **Urban militia** — guild-organized townsmen from Bruges, Ypres, Ghent, and surrounding towns. ~9,000 men.
- **Geldermalsen heavy infantry** — better-equipped levy.
- **Equipment:** the iconic weapon is the ***goedendag*** ("good day" — a heavy ironshod club ~1.5–2 m, with a steel spike at the head). The goedendag is a hybrid: clubbing weapon when swung, spear when held with the spike forward. Cheap, devastating against armored opponents at close range. Many militia also carry pikes (4–5 m), spears, crossbows.
- **No cavalry of consequence.** The Flemish are functionally a pure infantry army.

### French composition

- ~2,500 mounted men-at-arms (heavy cavalry) — the elite striking force.
- ~5,000 mercenary crossbowmen and pikemen (mostly Italian and Spanish).
- Light infantry.

### The decisive geography

The Flemish deploy in front of the town of Kortrijk (Courtrai) behind two converging brooks — the Groeningebeek and the Grote Beek — and several marshy ditches. The ground is **deliberately chosen to obstruct cavalry**:

- Brooks ~3 m wide, with marshy banks.
- Ditches running across the French approach.
- Soft ground softened further by Flemish overnight preparation (some accounts: they actively muddied the field).
- Open ground beyond for the militia to deploy in a line ~2 km long.

### Phase choreography

**Phase 1 — French opening missile fire.** Genoese and other crossbowmen advance, exchange fire with Flemish crossbowmen and militia. The French missile arm has the advantage of training; the Flemish absorb losses but hold position behind the brooks.

**Phase 2 — French command failure.** Robert of Artois, seeing the missile fire degrading the Flemish line, orders his crossbowmen to fall back so the cavalry can charge. *This is the critical command decision.* The crossbowmen withdraw through the cavalry, disrupting the cavalry's formation.

**Phase 3 — the cavalry charge across obstacles.** French heavy cavalry charges across the brooks. Several outcomes simultaneously:

- Many horses balk at the brooks; some fall.
- Those that cross arrive disorganized, with broken cohesion, on uphill soft ground.
- The charge **fragments** as it crosses — it is no longer a unified mass.

**Phase 4 — Flemish counter at contact.** The Flemish line, with goedendags and pikes presented, meets the disordered cavalry at contact. Goedendags are particularly effective against armored riders — the heavy ironshod head crushes plate and mail; the spike penetrates. Knights pulled from horses are killed where they fall.

**Phase 5 — collapse.** The French cavalry's commitment to the charge cannot be reversed. Subsequent waves are funneled into the same kill zone. Robert of Artois is killed (his identity confirmed by his coat of arms; legend says he was offered surrender and refused, calling his attackers "weavers and fullers"). The bulk of the French aristocracy on the field dies in roughly 45–60 minutes of combat.

**Aftermath:** the field is stripped of gilded spurs (the *éperons dorés* — gold-plated spurs were a sign of knighthood). The Flemish hang ~500 pairs in the Church of Our Lady at Kortrijk. The battle is known thereafter as the **Battle of the Golden Spurs**.

### Doctrinal lessons of Courtrai

1. **Heavy cavalry on bad ground is not heavy cavalry.** The mass and speed advantage that makes the charge work assumes flat, firm terrain. Choose ground that negates these and the charge is just a slow-moving target.
2. **Command-and-control failure compounds.** Pulling crossbowmen back through cavalry destroys cavalry order. The French could have continued missile attrition; they didn't, and lost the battle in the next 60 minutes.
3. **Infantry with the right weapon can defeat dismounted or disordered knights.** The goedendag is a class-of-weapon that did not previously exist in Western European doctrine and that changes what unarmored or lightly-armored urban militia can do against armored opponents at close range.

**Throughline flag — the "prepared ground + appropriate weapon" pattern.** *Courtrai is the first of a long line of late-medieval battles where infantry — typically not the prestige arm of the era — defeats heavy cavalry by combining (a) deliberately chosen restrictive terrain, (b) a weapon system that beats armor at close range, and (c) iron discipline against pursuit. This template recurs at Bannockburn (1314), Crécy (1346), Sempach (1386), Agincourt (1415), and Grandson (1476).*

## 2. Scottish Schiltron — Stirling Bridge 1297, Falkirk 1298, Bannockburn 1314

The schiltron (Scottish: *sgilltrum* — "shield-troop") is a Scottish pike formation contemporary with the Flemish development, fought against the heavy English cavalry-and-longbow combination.

### Composition and geometry

- **Heavy spearmen / pikemen** — 4–5 m spear (later 4–5 m proper pike).
- Tightly packed in a **circular or roughly circular formation** — the "hedgehog." Pikes project outward from all sides, ~4 ranks deep around the circumference.
- 1,000–2,000 men per schiltron; multiple schiltrons in army deployment.

### Falkirk (22 July 1298)

William Wallace deploys four schiltrons in a defensive line, with archers and a small cavalry contingent. Edward I of England commands a combined-arms force with heavy cavalry and (importantly) Welsh longbowmen.

**Mechanics of the Scottish defeat:**

1. Scottish cavalry — small and outclassed — withdraws or is driven off early.
2. English heavy cavalry charges. Schiltrons hold against the charge; horses cannot penetrate the hedgehog.
3. Scottish archers, exposed in gaps between schiltrons, are destroyed by English cavalry.
4. **English longbowmen are now uncontested.** They shoot into the stationary schiltrons at sustained massed-volley rate.
5. The schiltrons cannot maneuver — they are circular static formations. They cannot pursue archers; they cannot disperse without losing the pike-hedge.
6. **Attrition by archery alone decides the battle.** Gaps open in the schiltrons; English cavalry exploits the gaps; the formations dissolve.

**Doctrinal lesson:** *the static schiltron is excellent against cavalry alone but is a sitting target for unopposed missile fire. Without missile counter-fire or mobility, it can be eaten by archery at leisure.*

### Bannockburn (23–24 June 1314) — Robert the Bruce's innovation

Bruce learns from Falkirk and produces the **mobile schiltron**:

- Schiltrons trained to **advance while holding the pike-hedge**.
- Coordinated multi-schiltron advance — three or four formations moving in line abreast.
- Deliberate engagement in terrain that limits English archery and cavalry use.

**Strategic setup:** Edward II marches a large army (~15,000–20,000) to relieve the besieged Stirling Castle. Bruce (~6,000–8,000) occupies the Bannock Burn area south of Stirling, with carefully chosen ground:

- Bog and small streams to the south and east.
- A narrow front between woods and marsh.
- "Pots" — small concealed pits dug to trap cavalry charges, prepared by Bruce's army overnight.

**Phase 1 (Day 1, 23 June) — preliminary cavalry skirmish.** An English vanguard cavalry detachment under Henry de Bohun encounters Bruce personally in single combat (Bruce kills de Bohun with an axe-blow to the head — a moral victory). Bruce's schiltrons engage the vanguard; English cavalry, attacking piecemeal on broken ground, is driven off.

**Phase 2 (Day 2, 24 June) — main engagement.**

1. Edward II's army camps overnight in cramped wet ground between the Bannock Burn and the Pelstream Burn — a position Bruce has effectively confined them to.
2. Morning: Scottish schiltrons advance in coordinated line, pike-hedges projecting.
3. English van moves to meet them, but the cramped ground prevents the deployment of more than a fraction of the English army.
4. **English longbowmen attempt to deploy on the Scottish flank**. Bruce commits his **small cavalry reserve** — perhaps 500 horse — under Sir Robert Keith. They scatter the longbowmen, who have not yet established a fixed firing position.
5. Without longbow attrition, the schiltrons grind forward into the compressed English mass. English heavy cavalry, unable to maneuver in the cramped ground, cannot deliver an effective charge.
6. English morale collapses; the rear ranks try to retreat across the Bannock Burn, and the panic compounds; many drown in the bog and stream.
7. Edward II flees the field.

### Doctrinal lessons of Bannockburn

1. **Mobile pike infantry beats heavy cavalry in restricted ground.** Bruce's innovation — schiltrons that can advance — converts the formation from a defensive porcupine into an offensive grinding mass.
2. **Cavalry-against-longbow is the answer to longbow-against-pike.** The small Scottish cavalry's destruction of the English archers is the decisive moment. Without missile counter-fire, archery dominates static pike; without archery, pike dominates cavalry. The three-cornered rock-paper-scissors is fully in play.
3. **Ground selection wins battles.** Bruce engineers a battlefield where the English army cannot deploy its strengths. The English are defeated by the geometry of the field before contact.

**Throughline flag — the rock-paper-scissors of late-medieval Western warfare.** *Pike beats cavalry. Cavalry beats archers. Archers beat pike. No single arm dominates; an army needs all three plus the command to time their use against the enemy's commitments. The Bannockburn / Falkirk comparison is the cleanest demonstration of the principle in pre-firearm history.*

## 3. English Longbow System — Crécy 1346, Poitiers 1356, Agincourt 1415

The English longbow military system, from roughly 1330 to 1450, is the most distinctive Western European military institution of the late medieval period. It produces three of the most-studied battles in military history and shapes Anglo-French warfare for over a century.

### The weapon

- **English / Welsh longbow** — yew (preferred), 1.8–2.0 m long, draw weight 70–80 kg (some war-bow archaeology suggests up to 90 kg).
- **Arrow** — 70–80 cm, bodkin or broadhead point depending on target (bodkin for armor penetration, broadhead for unarmored or horse).
- **Range** — effective volley fire 200–250 m; aimed fire 100–150 m; armor-piercing close range 30–50 m.
- **Rate of fire** — 10–12 arrows per minute for a trained archer for short bursts; sustainable 6–8 per minute.
- **Lethality** — at close range, can penetrate mail and lighter plate; at distance, kills unarmored men and horses, suppresses armored ones by wound-rate and morale effect.

### The institutional base

The longbow only works because England builds the institution to support it:

- **Practice law.** Acts in 1252 (Assize of Arms) and progressively through 1363 require every Englishman between 15 and 60 to practice archery on Sundays after church. The Statute of 1363 (Edward III) explicitly bans other sports on practice days. This produces a population of trained archers in numbers no other Western European state achieves.
- **Indenture and contract.** English military expansion abroad relies on indentured archer service — captains contract with the Crown to supply a specified number of trained archers for specified service, paid at a specified rate. This is one of the earliest large-scale military-contract systems in Europe.
- **Standardized arrow production.** State and town stockpiles, contracted bowyers and fletchers, regulated arrow specifications. The supply system is institutional.

**This is the same logic as the Mongol decimal system or the Han crossbow corps.** The weapon is not the institution; the institution is the weapon. The longbow is useless without the population trained to draw it.

### Tactical deployment — the "longbow formation"

Standard English deployment from Edward III through Henry V:

- **Dismounted men-at-arms in the center.** Heavy infantry — knights and squires fighting on foot, with pole-arms, swords, and shields. The fixing force.
- **Longbow wings (the "herses").** Archer formations on both flanks, often deployed in **wedge or chevron** (the "herse" — Old French for "harrow", referring to the tooth-like projection of files of archers). These wings are sometimes refused (angled back) so they can shoot into the front of an approaching enemy from oblique angles.
- **Stakes.** Sharpened wooden stakes ~1.5 m driven into the ground in front of the archer positions, angled outward, creating a portable anti-cavalry obstacle. (This is an Agincourt-era innovation; earlier battles use natural terrain instead.)
- **Mounted reserve.** Some men-at-arms remain mounted as a tactical reserve / pursuit force.

### Crécy (26 August 1346) — phase choreography

Edward III with ~8,000–14,000 (perhaps 5,000–7,000 archers, 2,500–4,000 men-at-arms, plus Welsh spearmen and Irish hobelars) vs. Philip VI of France with perhaps 25,000–30,000 (including ~6,000 Genoese crossbowmen and 8,000–12,000 mounted men-at-arms).

**Phase 1 — English deployment.** Edward selects defensive ground on a slope between Crécy and Wadicourt. Three "battles" deployed: vanguard under the Black Prince on the right, second division under Northampton on the left, the king's reserve behind the center. Archer wings on the flanks of each division. Wagons formed into a defensive park behind the line.

**Phase 2 — Genoese crossbowmen advance.** The French missile arm advances first. The Genoese are tired from a long march, and a brief afternoon rain damages their crossbow strings (which they leave on the bow when not in use, unlike the longbow which is unstrung when not drawn — a small but lethal logistical detail). They open fire at extreme range.

**Phase 3 — longbow vs. crossbow.** English archers, with bows kept strung properly and with much higher rate of fire (10:1 advantage over crossbow), unleash mass volley fire. Genoese take heavy casualties almost immediately. They begin to withdraw.

**Phase 4 — the French command catastrophe.** Philip VI, seeing the Genoese withdrawing, believes them to be traitors or cowards and orders his cavalry: "*Tuez-moi cette canaille!*" — "Kill me this rabble!" French heavy cavalry charges *through* the retreating Genoese, killing many, and continues toward the English line.

**Phase 5 — cavalry into the killing zone.** The French heavy cavalry charges uphill into the killing arc of the English longbows. Sustained volley fire kills horses, wounds riders, breaks the cohesion of the charge. Knights who reach the English line do so in disorganized small groups against fresh dismounted men-at-arms. They are cut down or driven back.

**Phase 6 — repeated charges.** Sources count 15 or 16 separate French cavalry charges through the late afternoon and into the night. Each suffers the same fate. The French cannot disengage; troops continuing to arrive on the field commit themselves piecemeal to the failing assault.

**Phase 7 — collapse.** By dawn, French aristocratic casualties include the King of Bohemia (charging blind with his horse tied to those of his attendants — symbolic), the Count of Flanders, the Duke of Lorraine, and over 1,500 knights and squires. Philip VI escapes wounded. English casualties: perhaps 200–300 total.

**Throughline flag — the longbow ambush.** *Crécy works because the English impose a battle the French cannot win and the French cannot avoid. The French cannot disengage politically — to retreat without engaging would be cowardice — and once they commit, they cannot reverse. The longbow doesn't defeat the cavalry on equal terms; the longbow defeats the cavalry on terms the cavalry is forced to accept.*

### Poitiers (19 September 1356) — phase choreography

The Black Prince with ~6,000 (2,000 men-at-arms, 3,000 archers, 1,000 light) vs. John II of France with ~11,000 (8,000 of which men-at-arms and crossbowmen).

**Strategic setup:** the Black Prince, on a raid through southern France, is intercepted near Poitiers and forced to fight a much larger force. He chooses defensive ground behind a thick hedge with a single narrow gap, surrounded by vineyards and woods.

**French doctrinal adaptation.** Having absorbed Crécy's lesson, the French now dismount most of their men-at-arms — recognizing that mounted charges into longbow fire are suicidal. The doctrine becomes: heavy infantry assault, with reduced cavalry component for pursuit.

**Phase 1 — small mounted vanguard charge.** The French send a small cavalry detachment forward against the hedge gap, hoping to break the English archers. Volley fire devastates it.

**Phase 2 — dismounted main attack.** The bulk of French men-at-arms advances on foot toward the hedge gap. The narrow approach forces them into a compressed column. Archer fire from both flanks devastates the column.

**Phase 3 — sustained grinding combat.** The fighting at and around the hedge gap continues for hours. English men-at-arms hold the line at the gap; archers exhaust their arrow supply. As archers run out of arrows, they pick up swords, daggers, and discarded weapons and join the close fighting.

**Phase 4 — the captal de Buch flanking maneuver.** The Black Prince commits a small reserve cavalry force (perhaps 400 horse) under the Captal de Buch on a wide flanking sweep. This force, having ridden around the French left, emerges on the French rear and charges.

**Phase 5 — French collapse.** The flank attack, combined with continued frontal attrition, breaks French morale. King John II fights on foot in the third French division and is captured (a vast political event — the King of France a prisoner of war, ransomed later for an enormous sum).

**Doctrinal lesson:** dismounting the men-at-arms reduces the absolute scale of the disaster relative to Crécy, but does not solve the underlying problem. The English position attrites them; the small flanking force breaks them. **A flanking commit of a few hundred horses, properly timed, decides a battle of ~17,000.**

### Agincourt (25 October 1415) — phase choreography

Henry V with perhaps 6,000 (5,000 archers, 1,000 men-at-arms) vs. Constable Charles d'Albret with ~20,000 (most dismounted men-at-arms after Crécy/Poitiers lessons, plus crossbow and a few thousand mounted men-at-arms).

**Strategic setup:** Henry's army, on a march from Harfleur to Calais, is intercepted by a much larger French force near Agincourt in northern France. The English are exhausted, hungry, and significantly outnumbered. Ground: a narrow plowed field between two woods (Agincourt to the south, Tramecourt to the north), recently rain-soaked.

**Phase 1 — English deployment and advance.** Henry deploys three battles dismounted (his own in the center, Duke of York on the right, Lord Camoys on the left), with archer wings on each flank and additional archers in two refused wedges in the center. The English **advance** — pulling up their stakes, walking forward 200 m, replanting stakes, and then assuming a defensive posture in shooting range. This forces the French to either engage or stand under sustained volley fire.

**Phase 2 — opening longbow volleys.** English archers open fire at maximum range. The French response is mostly stoic — heavily armored men-at-arms can stand at long range without prohibitive casualties — but the French command commits to attack rather than absorb volleys indefinitely.

**Phase 3 — French cavalry charge.** A small mounted French force charges the English archer wings. The stakes break the charge; archers shoot horses at close range; horses panic and stampede back through their own lines, disordering the advancing French main body.

**Phase 4 — French dismounted main attack.** The bulk of French men-at-arms, dismounted, advances on foot across the rain-soaked plowed field. Heavy armor and deep mud combine: the French sink calf-deep in mud, exhausted before contact, unable to maintain formation. The narrow field between woods compresses them into a dense column.

**Phase 5 — contact.** The French front line reaches the English position, exhausted and disordered. English men-at-arms meet them at the line. Archers shoot at point-blank into the flanks of the French column. Men-at-arms in mud-stuck plate armor who fall cannot rise without help; subsequent ranks tread on the fallen; piles of armored men accumulate.

**Phase 6 — the archer melee commit.** As the French press deeper into the English position, English archers — having shot their arrows or about to run out — draw mallets, swords, and daggers and attack the flanks of the densely-packed French column. The archers are unarmored (or lightly armored) and agile; the French are armored, exhausted, and trapped in mud. The killing is one-sided.

**Phase 7 — the prisoner massacre.** Late in the battle, Henry orders the killing of French prisoners (already gathered behind English lines), fearing a renewed French attack from a fresh second-line force visible in the distance. This is the morally fraught element of Agincourt; thousands of captured aristocrats are killed. The threatened second attack does not materialize.

**Aftermath:** the French aristocracy is gutted. Charles d'Albret, the Constable of France, dies on the field. The Dukes of Brabant, Bar, and Alençon die. Total French aristocratic casualties exceed 5,000; English losses are a few hundred at most.

### Doctrinal lessons of the longbow battles

1. **The longbow battles are not "longbow vs. cavalry" — they are combined arms with the longbow as the missile arm.** Dismounted men-at-arms fix the enemy; archers attrite; the synthesis decides. Without the men-at-arms, the longbow cannot stop a sustained infantry assault (Patay 1429 will show this — French heavy cavalry catches English archers without formed men-at-arms support and slaughters them).
2. **Battle is imposed.** All three battles are fought because the English have selected a position the French cannot ignore or maneuver around. The longbow doctrine requires a defensive position the enemy must attack; it cannot create those positions in an offensive campaign without parallel operational doctrine (which the English don't fully develop).
3. **Institutional decline.** By the mid-15th century, the longbow institution is degrading. Practice laws weaken, archer recruitment becomes harder, the social and economic base for the system thins. The 1429 reverses at Patay and Orléans, and the gradual French recovery through the rest of the Hundred Years' War, reflect institutional decay as much as French tactical adaptation.

**Throughline flag — institutional decline as tactical reversal.** *No military system is permanently dominant. The longbow rules from ~1330 to ~1420 and is significantly degraded by ~1460. The institution that sustained it cannot be reconstructed quickly when it has decayed. This pattern recurs: Swiss pike supremacy decays by 1525; Spanish tercio supremacy decays by 1640; etc. Doctrinal decline is institutional, not tactical.*

## 4. Hussite Wagenburg — Žižka's Wagon Fortress (1419–1434)

The Hussite Wars in Bohemia produce one of the most distinctive late-medieval European military systems: the **wagenburg** (wagon-fortress / wagon-laager), under the one-eyed (later totally blind) military genius Jan Žižka.

### Composition

- **War wagons (*vozy bojné*)** — purpose-built fighting wagons, ~3 m long, with reinforced sides, often armored boards, mounting:
  - Wall-shields with loopholes for crossbow / handgun fire.
  - Sometimes light cannon (the **houfnice** — origin of the word "howitzer").
  - Drop-doors for sortie.
- **Crew per wagon:** typically 16–22 men:
  - 4–6 crossbowmen / handgunners.
  - 2 cannoneers (if the wagon has artillery).
  - 6–8 flailmen — peasants with heavy iron-spiked flails (the Hussite signature weapon; converted threshing flail, with chains and metal heads).
  - 2–4 pikemen / halberdiers.
  - 2 horsemen (for moving the wagon and for sortie use).
- **Cavalry** — small, supporting; not the prestige arm.
- **Foot artillery** — primitive but present; field guns mounted on carriages.

### Geometry — the wagon-fortress

The wagenburg is **a fortified position assembled from wagons** wherever the army stops. Procedure:

1. Wagons drawn into a circle, square, oval, or other enclosed shape, depending on terrain.
2. Wagons chained together at the wheels and tongues to prevent enemy displacement.
3. Earthworks and stakes added where time permits.
4. Gaps left for sortie at predetermined points, blocked by removable wagons or chains.
5. Internal lanes for movement of reserves.
6. Reserve cavalry and pike infantry inside the wagon-fortress.

A typical Hussite wagenburg has 50–300 wagons; some campaigns assemble formations of 500+.

### Tactical doctrine

The wagenburg is **defensive in posture but designed for offensive sortie**:

1. The wagon line absorbs the enemy attack with combined missile fire from inside (crossbow, handgun, light cannon) and pike-and-flail close defense from the wagons.
2. The attack is allowed to break against the line; enemy cavalry, in particular, cannot displace chained wagons.
3. **When the enemy attack culminates and begins to falter, the wagenburg launches a counter-sortie.** Cavalry and flail-infantry exit through prepared gaps, attack the now-exhausted and disordered attacker, and complete the destruction.

This is **conceptually identical to Byzantine Phokas's hollow square** (Chunk C) — infantry as the defensive base, mobile striking force inside, sortie when the enemy is degraded — but executed at much larger scale and with gunpowder weapons.

### Key engagements

**Sudoměř (25 March 1420):** Žižka's first major engagement. Outnumbered ~400 Hussites vs. ~2,000 royal cavalry. Žižka deploys his wagons across a fishpond dam, with women supporting the line by throwing veils and clothes onto the soft ground to make it harder for cavalry to advance (a folk-legend element, but possibly partially true). Royal cavalry charge is broken at the wagons. Hussites win.

**Vítkov Hill (14 July 1420):** Žižka, with perhaps 9,000 men, defeats King Sigismund's massive crusader army (~30,000+) outside Prague. Hussite wagons fortify the slopes of Vítkov; repeated crusader assaults break against the wagon-line; Žižka's sortie reverses the situation; Sigismund withdraws.

**Domažlice (14 August 1431):** The fifth anti-Hussite Crusade. ~90,000 crusaders led by Cardinal Cesarini approach the Bohemian frontier. Hussite forces (much smaller) advance toward them. According to legend, the crusaders hear the Hussite hymn ("*Ktož sú boží bojovníci*" — "Ye Who Are God's Warriors") in the distance and panic before contact, fleeing without engagement. While the legendary version is exaggerated, the crusader army did dissolve into rout before serious contact. The wagenburg's reputation had outpaced its physical presence.

### Doctrinal innovations

1. **Mobile fortification.** The wagon-fortress can be assembled wherever the army stops — converting any field into a defensive position. This solves the basic problem of European armies on campaign: they're vulnerable on the march, and once they stop they need to fortify. The wagenburg makes fortification a routine operation.
2. **Combined arms in a small package.** Every wagon is a miniature combined-arms unit (missile + pike + cavalry). The wagenburg is a fractal of combined-arms doctrine — every node has every arm.
3. **Mass-recruited armies with appropriate doctrine.** The Hussite forces are largely religiously-motivated peasants, not trained men-at-arms. The wagenburg system makes mass-recruited peasant armies capable of defeating professional feudal forces — a key political fact that resonates through the Reformation and the German Peasants' War.
4. **Gunpowder integration earlier than most Western forces.** Hussite light cannon and handguns are integrated into the wagon-fortress system in ways that anticipate later tercio-style integration of pike and shot.

**Throughline flag — the wagenburg as the bridge formation.** *The wagenburg is conceptually halfway between medieval shield-wall and early-modern pike-and-shot. It is the late-medieval Western formation that most explicitly anticipates how gunpowder will be integrated into infantry doctrine. The principle — combined missile + close-defense + sortie — recurs in the Spanish coronelía and tercio, the Hussites' direct doctrinal legacy.*

## 5. The Swiss Pike System (~1315–1525)

The Swiss pike formation is, at peak, **the most feared infantry in Europe**. From the early 14th century through the Italian Wars, Swiss mercenary contingents are the prestige hire for any European army that can afford them, and the Swiss home cantons are functionally untouchable in their own terrain.

### Composition and equipment

- **Pike (the *Spiess*)** — long pike, 4.5–6 m by the mature period, two-handed. The dominant Swiss arm.
- **Halberd** — pole-arm ~2 m with axe-head, spike, and back-hook; supplements the pike, used for closer combat.
- **Two-handed sword (*Zweihänder*)** — used by elite *Doppelsöldner* ("double-paid men") at the corners and front of formations; reaches over the pikes to hack at enemy pike points.
- **Crossbow** initially; **arquebus** from ~1480 onward (Swiss adopt firearms cautiously and later than some neighbors).
- **Light armor** — Swiss are generally less armored than Landsknechte or Italian condottieri infantry; mobility is prized.
- **No significant cavalry.** Swiss are functionally a pure heavy-infantry army.

### Geometry — the Gewalthaufen ("force-heap")

The Swiss deploy in massive square formations:

- **Front rank — pikes leveled horizontal.**
- **Second rank — pikes leveled, projecting between the front rank.**
- **Third and fourth ranks — pikes leveled, projecting overhead and between.**
- **Fifth rank and beyond — pikes upright** (transported in upright position, dropped to horizontal as the front ranks fall).
- **Halberdiers and *Doppelsöldner*** at the formation corners and front, ready for close-quarters work after pike-clash.
- **Crossbowmen / arquebusiers** in detached forlorn-hope screens in front, or in supporting groups on the flanks.

Squares typically 40–80 men per side, so 1,600–6,400 men per square. Multiple squares per army — the Swiss army of the late 15th century deploys in **three squares — vorhut, gewalthaufen, nachhut** (vanguard, main, rearguard) — staggered en echelon so the second can support the first and the third can act as reserve.

### Tactical doctrine — *aggressive* offense

The crucial Swiss innovation: the pike is used **offensively**, not defensively.

Macedonian phalanx, Scottish schiltron, Flemish urban militia — earlier pike doctrines are defensive. The pike holds; the enemy comes; the pike kills them at range.

**Swiss pike marches.** The Swiss square advances rapidly — sources describe the pace as a quick walk, almost a trot — directly toward the enemy. The square does not stop to receive a charge; it **crashes into the enemy** with the full mass of the formation behind 4 leveled pikes per file.

This is shock attack with a 5-ton bus instead of a battering ram. The pike heads reach the enemy at the moment the rear ranks deliver maximum momentum. The enemy must either:

- Hold formation and absorb the pike-points (almost always fatal).
- Receive the impact while their own formation is disordered (catastrophic).
- Break ground and retreat fast enough to disengage (possible only with disciplined cavalry or pike of their own).

**Tactical philosophy: the Swiss don't fight on the enemy's terms.** They impose tempo. The advance is fast enough that enemies cannot complete maneuver before contact. Cavalry trying to charge a Swiss square at the run finds the square already at the cavalry; halted cavalry presents stationary horses to pike-points.

### Key historical engagements

**Morgarten (15 November 1315) — the original Swiss victory.** Habsburg Duke Leopold I leads a force into the Schwyz canton along a defile beside Lake Ägeri. Swiss confederates (perhaps 1,500) wait above the road. As the Habsburg column passes, the Swiss launch logs, rocks, and themselves down the slope; the Habsburg cavalry, trapped between cliff and lake, cannot deploy. Massacre. ~1,500 Habsburg dead, very few Swiss losses. This is the foundation legend of Swiss military reputation; tactically it is more a successful ambush than a pike-formation battle.

**Laupen (21 June 1339).** Bernese and confederate Swiss field a more conventional pike force against a Habsburg combined-arms army. Pike square holds against cavalry charges; counterattack breaks the Habsburg line. Establishes that Swiss pike works in open battle, not just terrain ambush.

**Sempach (9 July 1386).** Duke Leopold III of Austria (Leopold I's grandson) attempts to crush the Swiss confederation. The Habsburgs dismount and advance in a deep column; the Swiss respond with their own square formation. Hours of pike-and-halberd combat. The legend of **Arnold von Winkelried** — a Swiss who grasps a sheaf of Habsburg pikes to his chest, opening a gap his comrades exploit — is from this battle. Whether literally true or symbolic, the gap is exploited; Habsburg formation breaks; Leopold dies on the field.

**Grandson, Murten, Nancy — 1476–1477.** The destruction of Burgundian power by Swiss pike. Covered in Section 6 below as case studies in Swiss pike vs. combined-arms.

### Strategic role — Swiss mercenary supremacy

By the mid-15th century, the Swiss have become **Europe's preferred mercenary infantry**. The major powers — France, the Papal States, Milan, Venice, the Holy Roman Empire — all hire Swiss contingents at high rates. Swiss mercenary service produces:

- A constant outflow of trained men from the Swiss cantons (which the cantons regulate and tax).
- The Swiss "right of capitulation" — Swiss contracts specify they cannot be ordered to fight other Swiss; they have specific terms of service, rights of plunder, etc.
- The Swiss Guard at the Vatican (founded 1506, still extant) — the long tail of the institution.

**Throughline flag — Swiss pike as the "shock infantry breakthrough."** *The Swiss demonstrate that disciplined heavy infantry, used offensively, can defeat heavy cavalry in open battle. This is the doctrinal opening for the entire pike-and-shot era. Cavalry remains important but is no longer the deciding arm of Western European warfare. From ~1480 to ~1640, infantry rules; cavalry is supporting.*

## 6. Burgundian Combined Arms and the Wars of Charles the Bold (1474–1477)

Duke Charles the Bold of Burgundy attempts to build a fully professional combined-arms standing army — one of the most ambitious military reforms of the late medieval period — and is destroyed by the Swiss in three set-piece battles in 14 months. The case is doctrinally critical: it shows both the *potential* and the *limits* of late-medieval combined-arms reform.

### Burgundian composition

Charles's "Ordonnance Companies" (formal organization codified 1471–1473) consist of:

- **Men-at-arms (heavy cavalry)** — armored, lance, sword, etc. The traditional prestige arm.
- **Coustilliers** (light cavalry skirmishers).
- **Mounted archers / crossbowmen** (mounted missile arm).
- **Foot pikemen.**
- **Foot archers / crossbowmen / handgunners.**
- **Field artillery** — Burgundian artillery is the most advanced in Europe in the 1470s. Light field guns, falconets, ribauldequins (multi-barrel volley guns).
- Each ordonnance company has a **fixed composition** by Charles's regulations — every company has the same ratio of cavalry, missile, pike, artillery. **Standing army with standardized composition.**

This is approximately the structure that Spain will perfect 30 years later in the coronelía / tercio. Charles is correct doctrinally — and ahead of his time.

### What goes wrong

Three battles, three Swiss victories, ending in Charles's death.

#### Grandson (2 March 1476) — phase choreography

Charles besieges Grandson Castle, executes the garrison after surrender. A Swiss relief army (~18,000) marches against him. Charles (~20,000) deploys on a long narrow plain between Lake Neuchâtel and a steep wooded ridge.

**Phase 1 — Burgundian deployment.** Charles deploys with artillery in the center, cavalry on the wings, missile infantry forward. He plans to attrite the Swiss column with artillery fire as they emerge from the defile, then break them with cavalry.

**Phase 2 — Swiss approach.** Swiss vanguard emerges from the defile in pike formation, advances *directly* toward the Burgundian line at a rapid pace. Artillery fire and missile fire kill some, but the pike block moves too fast for sustained attrition to matter.

**Phase 3 — the unplanned Swiss arrival.** The Swiss main body and rearguard emerge from forest paths on the Burgundian flanks — not part of Charles's expected geometry. Three Swiss squares are now visible from three directions simultaneously.

**Phase 4 — Burgundian command error.** Charles orders his cavalry to fall back to a better defensive line. The cavalry begins moving backward; foot troops see the cavalry "retreating" and panic. **The Burgundian line dissolves without effective contact ever occurring.** Most of the Burgundian army flees the field. The Swiss capture Charles's baggage train — including the famous Burgundian crown jewels and tapestries, looted by the cantons.

Casualties are light on both sides — the battle is decided by maneuver and morale before close engagement. But Charles's army is *psychologically* broken. Reputation matters.

#### Murten / Morat (22 June 1476) — phase choreography

Charles reassembles, marches against Bern, besieges the town of Murten. A Swiss relief army (~25,000) marches to relieve it.

**Phase 1 — Burgundian deployment.** Charles fortifies a long defensive line: ditch, palisade, artillery emplacements. He has prepared positions and his army intact. This time he plans to defeat the Swiss by absorbing their charge in fortifications.

**Phase 2 — the rain delay.** Rain on the morning of 22 June causes Charles to dismiss most of his troops from the lines temporarily — he believes the Swiss will not attack in poor weather.

**Phase 3 — Swiss surprise attack.** The Swiss attack into the rain. The vanguard breaks the Burgundian outposts; the main body crashes into the under-manned Burgundian line; the rearguard rolls up the flank.

**Phase 4 — collapse.** Burgundian troops in their tents and quarters cannot reach their positions in time. The pike squares roll through the encampment. Many Burgundians flee toward Lake Murten and drown. ~8,000–10,000 Burgundians killed; Swiss losses light.

#### Nancy (5 January 1477) — phase choreography

Charles, now with a reconstructed but smaller army (~10,000), besieges Nancy in Lorraine. René II of Lorraine assembles a relief force (~20,000, including a substantial Swiss contingent).

**Phase 1 — Burgundian deployment.** Charles deploys in a frozen plain south of Nancy with woods on his flanks. He places artillery centrally and prepares for a Swiss frontal attack.

**Phase 2 — the Swiss flanking march.** The Swiss column, guided by local peasants, marches through woods around Charles's flank. They appear on his right wing, unseen until the last moment.

**Phase 3 — pike attack from the side.** Swiss pike crashes into the Burgundian right flank. Charles's artillery, oriented for a frontal attack, cannot redirect. His cavalry attempts to respond and is broken.

**Phase 4 — total Burgundian destruction.** The Burgundian army is encircled and slaughtered. Charles is killed in the rout — his body found stripped and partially eaten by wolves days later, identifiable only by old scars and missing teeth.

### Doctrinal lessons of the Burgundian wars

1. **Standardized combined-arms force composition is not sufficient.** Charles's army is structurally correct; the failures are at the level of command, deployment, ground selection, and reaction speed. **Doctrinal correctness on paper is necessary but not sufficient; execution under fluid conditions matters more.**
2. **Swiss pike disrupts the timing assumptions of combined-arms doctrine.** Combined arms assumes a missile attrition phase, then a cavalry-or-shock decisive phase. Swiss pike compresses the battle's timeline: by the time missile attrition would matter, the pike is already at contact. Charles's artillery and missile arms never effectively engage in any of the three battles.
3. **Morale cascade is a tactical force.** Grandson is lost without significant fighting because the Burgundian line believes itself to be losing. Reputation effects compound: after Grandson, the Burgundian army is functionally degraded in any future Swiss engagement.
4. **The expensive standing army can still die in a single battle.** Charles spent over a decade building his ordonnance system. It dies in 14 months. **Force-generation depth matters: a state that loses its entire army should be able to field another. Burgundy could not.** This pattern is one of the most important lessons of the late-medieval era — the institutional depth that allows replacement after disaster matters more than the original quality of the army.

**Throughline flag — execution beats composition.** *Burgundian force composition was correct by Spanish-tercio-era standards. Burgundian execution was inadequate. The Spanish, 30 years later, will have the same composition AND the institutional culture to execute it (and the force-generation depth to replace losses). Composition without execution is wreckage.*

## 7. Landsknechte — the German Mirror (~1487–1560s)

Emperor Maximilian I, observing the Swiss success and unable to hire enough Swiss for his own wars, creates the **Landsknecht** (literally "servant of the land") — German pike infantry deliberately modeled on Swiss doctrine.

### Composition and doctrine

- **Pike, halberd, two-handed sword** — same kit as the Swiss.
- *Doppelsöldner* with *Zweihänder* — sometimes more prominently featured than in Swiss formations.
- *Gewalthaufen* square formation — same geometry.
- More heavily armored than Swiss on average (Landsknechte often display heavier armor; the famous slashed-and-puffed costume is partly armor-substitution and partly status display).
- More integrated firearms component by the early 16th century — Landsknechte adopt arquebus faster than Swiss.

### Differences from Swiss

- **Mercenary career, not cantonal levy.** Landsknechte are professional mercenaries serving any paying employer; Swiss are nominally cantonal forces (though heavily contracted out).
- **Lower institutional discipline.** Pay disputes are common; mutinies frequent (the most famous: the 1527 Sack of Rome by mutinying Landsknechte who hadn't been paid).
- **The "Bad War"** — Swiss and Landsknechte view each other as institutional rivals. When they meet on opposite sides (frequently in the Italian Wars), they fight without quarter — neither side takes prisoners. The "Bad War" between them is one of the few late-medieval combat contexts where total annihilation is mutual policy.

### Key engagement: Bicocca (27 April 1522) — phase choreography

A combined Swiss-French force vs. a Spanish-Imperial force defending a sunken road and fortified position near Milan. **This battle ends Swiss pike supremacy.**

**Spanish-Imperial composition:** Spanish coronelía under Prospero Colonna, with arquebusiers behind a sunken road and earthworks; Landsknechte in support; Spanish men-at-arms in reserve.

**Swiss-French composition:** ~8,000 Swiss pike in their traditional offensive role; French gendarmes (heavy cavalry); some French artillery and infantry.

**Phase 1 — Swiss insist on attack.** French commanders, including Lautrec, prefer to maneuver around the fortified position. Swiss commanders demand immediate attack — the Swiss are owed back-pay, refuse to continue without combat, threaten to march home. The French agree under duress.

**Phase 2 — Swiss assault on the sunken road.** Two Swiss squares advance against the fortified line. They cross the open ground at full Swiss attack-pace. They reach the sunken road and discover it is deeper than expected — they must descend into it and climb the far side under fire.

**Phase 3 — arquebus fire at point-blank.** Spanish arquebusiers, deployed in four ranks behind the breastwork at the far side of the sunken road, fire rotating volleys into the densely-packed Swiss pike block descending into the road. The Swiss cannot effectively return fire (their crossbow / arquebus support has been left behind in the rapid advance). The pike-points cannot reach the arquebusiers behind the breastwork.

**Phase 4 — Swiss carnage.** The Swiss take massive losses standing in the sunken road. Repeated attempts to climb the far slope are shot down. ~3,000 Swiss die in roughly 30 minutes — including most of the elite *Doppelsöldner* in the front ranks. Surviving Swiss withdraw.

**Phase 5 — Landsknecht counter-charge.** Imperial Landsknechte, on the Spanish right, advance against the Swiss flank as the withdrawal begins. Combined Landsknecht pressure and continued arquebus fire shatter the Swiss formations.

**Aftermath:** the Swiss army withdraws, their reputation broken. **Swiss pike is no longer the dominant offensive infantry of Europe.** Spanish arquebus + fortification + integrated pike is.

### Doctrinal lessons of Bicocca

1. **The pike charge is not invincible.** Against unprepared opponents it remains lethal; against prepared positions with massed arquebus fire it is suicide.
2. **The Swiss doctrine's *aggressiveness* — the same trait that made it dominant — becomes its undoing.** Refusing to maneuver, insisting on attack, refusing to wait: these features beat opponents who would not have lost slowly, but they hand quick decisive victories to opponents who can prepare a kill zone.
3. **Pike + shot integration is the next doctrinal step.** The Spanish coronelía has both arms in proper proportion. After Bicocca, the Italian Wars proceed under tercio-style pike-and-shot doctrine.

**Throughline flag — the "perfect-system" trap.** *A military doctrine refined to maximum efficiency in its sweet spot is brittle against doctrinal counters. Swiss pike at peak (Grandson/Murten/Nancy) is the best mass-shock infantry in Europe. The same doctrine 45 years later (Bicocca) is destroyed by an opponent who has built specifically to exploit the doctrine's blind spots. This pattern recurs at Patay (longbow → cavalry without men-at-arms), at Rocroi 1643 (Spanish tercio → French line), and elsewhere. Doctrinal supremacy carries the seeds of its own counter.*

## 8. Italian Condottieri (~1300–1530)

The Italian city-states fight much of their warfare through hired professional captains (*condottieri*) and their companies. This produces a distinctive doctrinal culture.

### Composition and equipment

- **Heavy cavalry (*lance*)** — the basic Italian tactical unit is the "*lancia*" — typically a 3- to 5-man group: one fully-armored man-at-arms plus 2–4 squires / coustilliers / mounted servants. Multiple lances combine into companies; companies into companies-of-companies.
- **Heavy armor** — Italian condottieri men-at-arms are among the most heavily armored troops in Europe, often head-to-toe plate by the late 15th century.
- **Foot infantry** — typically less developed; Italian cities prefer to spend on cavalry. Infantry is often Genoese crossbowmen (rented from Genoa) or various foot-mercenary types.

### Doctrine and strategic culture

The classic stereotype — that condottieri fought "bloodless" battles to preserve their assets — is exaggerated but not wrong. Several features of Italian warfare reinforce battle-avoidance:

- **Mercenary self-interest.** A condottiere's company is his livelihood. Losing it to a battle is professional suicide; capturing the other side's company (then ransoming or hiring them) is profitable. Battle is therefore expensive to win badly and risky to win at all.
- **Limited political stakes.** Italian city-state wars are often over territorial adjustments or commercial advantage, not over existence. A negotiated outcome is usually acceptable.
- **Sophisticated diplomatic embedding.** Condottieri are deeply linked to the diplomatic-political system. A condottiere on one side this year may be on the other side next year.
- **Reputation matters.** Captains build careers over decades; cultivating a reputation for skill and prudence is more valuable than a single decisive victory.

### Battles and doctrinal style

Italian battles of the era are often **maneuver-dominated, with long campaigns of march-and-countermarch resolved by political agreement or by relatively short engagements**. Casualties are sometimes low (the chronicler Machiavelli mocks the Battle of Anghiari 1440 as having one casualty — falsely, but the propaganda point is "Italian battles don't kill many"). At other times, Italian battles are bloody enough — but the systemic disposition is toward preservation.

The **decline of condottieri** in the Italian Wars after 1494 (France invades Italy, beginning 60 years of foreign warfare on Italian soil) exposes the doctrine's limits: condottieri systems are not built for the high-attrition, high-stakes warfare of inter-state conflict between major powers. The French gendarme system, Swiss pike, and Spanish tercio all run over Italian condottieri-style forces relatively easily.

**Throughline flag — the "preservation doctrine" as institutional adaptation.** *Italian condottieri are not "cowardly"; they are doctrinally adapted to a specific political-economic environment. When that environment changes — major-power warfare arrives in Italy — the doctrine cannot adapt fast enough. This is parallel to the Byzantine *Strategikon*'s "preserve the force" doctrine (Chunk C) but exposed by a doctrinal shift that Byzantium also struggled to manage.*

## 9. French Gendarme Cavalry — the *Compagnies d'ordonnance* (1445 onward)

Charles VII of France, having barely survived the late phase of the Hundred Years' War, creates one of the first true standing armies in late-medieval Europe.

### The *compagnies d'ordonnance*

Established by ordinance in 1445. Composition:

- **15 compagnies** initially, each of **100 lances**.
- Each lance: 1 gendarme (heavy cavalry man-at-arms), 2 mounted archers, 1 coutilier (light cavalry), 1 page, 1 valet. **Six men per lance.**
- 100 lances × 15 companies = 1,500 lances = 9,000 men in total standing army.
- Pay is by the Crown directly, not through captains. Soldiers serve continuously, not for specific campaigns.

This is **the first European standing army of the early modern type** — paid, permanent, organized centrally, equipped to a standard.

### Doctrine

The gendarme is heavy shock cavalry at the peak of plate-armor development:

- **Full plate armor** for both rider and horse (the horse barding sometimes nearly as elaborate as the rider's harness).
- **Lance** as primary weapon; sword, mace, dagger as backup.
- **Tactical role:** decisive shock against pike formations, missile troops, or enemy cavalry.

The doctrine is fundamentally **the conroi at maturity** — same single-charge logic, but with much better armor and institutional standardization. The gendarme retains the Western European "single shot" character of the heavy cavalry charge.

### Performance in the Italian Wars

The French gendarme is initially extremely effective. At Fornovo (1495), against Italian condottieri-style opposition, the gendarmes punch through and recover the French king's army from a perilous tactical situation. Through the early Italian Wars, the gendarme is the prestige arm.

But by Bicocca (1522) and especially Pavia (1525), the gendarme is exposed: against integrated pike + shot, the heavy cavalry charge cannot finish the engagement. Heavy cavalry survives as an arm but is no longer decisive.

**Throughline flag — institutional standing forces matter, but doctrine matters too.** *The *compagnies d'ordonnance* solve the force-generation problem (paid, standing, replaceable). They don't solve the doctrinal problem: heavy cavalry alone is no longer decisive in the late-Renaissance environment. Spain solves both — standing force AND new doctrine.*

## 10. Gonzalo de Córdoba and the Spanish Reforms (1495–1525)

Gonzalo de Córdoba, "*El Gran Capitán*", commands Spanish forces in Italy from the late 1490s through 1504 and produces the doctrinal synthesis that will define European warfare for the next 150 years.

### The coronelía (proto-tercio)

A *coronelía* is a regiment of approximately 1,000–1,500 men, organized around an integrated combination of:

- **Pikemen** — the shock and anti-cavalry core.
- **Sword-and-buckler men (*rodeleros*)** — armored swordsmen with small shields, used for close-quarters work, sortieing through pike-lines to attack disordered enemy ranks, and fighting in broken terrain. Distinctively Spanish.
- **Arquebusiers** — the missile and firearm component. Increasing fraction through the period; by 1525 perhaps 1/3 of the coronelía.
- **Light cavalry (*jinetes*)** — drawn from the Reconquista frontier tradition; light, mobile, javelin/lance armed.

The composition is the early version of what will mature into the **tercio** in the 1530s–40s, fully institutionalized by Charles V in 1534.

### Doctrinal innovations

1. **Field fortification.** Gonzalo systematically uses prepared positions — ditches, breastworks, sunken roads — to defeat opponents who otherwise have tactical advantages. **Cerignola (1503) is the canonical case** (see below).
2. **Integrated pike + shot + sword.** The three foot arms function as a system. Pike holds; arquebus shoots; rodeleros sortie. This is the answer to the "Swiss pike vs. Spanish arquebus" question that Bicocca will demonstrate spectacularly.
3. **Discipline and standing service.** Spanish infantry is paid (irregularly, but conceptually) standing service. The Spanish *tercio viejos* (oldest tercios) become a permanent institution.
4. **Reconquista cultural substrate.** Spain has 700 years of frontier warfare against Muslim states, with constant small-unit engagement, raid, ambush, fortification. The Spanish military culture is more "professional small-unit" than the chivalric culture of France or Italy. This produces a force more adapted to the realities of integrated combined-arms warfare.

### Cerignola (28 April 1503) — phase choreography

The first battle in European history decided primarily by firearm fire.

**Strategic setup:** Gonzalo (~6,300 Spanish, including coronelías) vs. Louis d'Armagnac (~10,000 French, including ~700 gendarmes, Swiss mercenary pike, and Italian crossbowmen). Gonzalo deploys on a slight slope at Cerignola, in front of a vineyard. He **digs a ditch and constructs a small earthwork** across his front overnight — perhaps an hour or two of work for thousands of men.

**Phase 1 — French approach.** Louis arrives at Cerignola in the late afternoon. His council suggests waiting for morning; he insists on immediate attack to maintain momentum. The French deploy hurriedly.

**Phase 2 — French cavalry charge.** Gendarmes lead the French attack, charging up the slope toward the Spanish line. They encounter the ditch — *which they don't see until the last moment.* The lead horses fall into the ditch; subsequent horses pile up; the charge fragments before reaching the breastwork.

**Phase 3 — arquebus fire at point-blank.** Spanish arquebusiers, in formed ranks behind the earthwork, deliver sustained fire into the disorganized gendarmes. Louis d'Armagnac is killed by an arquebus ball early in the engagement. The French cavalry attack disintegrates.

**Phase 4 — Swiss pike attack.** French-allied Swiss pike advances up the slope. Same problem: the ditch breaks their formation. Arquebus fire shreds them as they cross.

**Phase 5 — Spanish counterattack.** As the French/Swiss assault collapses, Gonzalo orders a counter-advance. Spanish pike, sword-and-buckler, and surviving gendarmes (cavalry) pursue. The French army disintegrates.

**Total time of decisive engagement: probably under 90 minutes. Spanish losses: very low (~100). French/Swiss losses: ~3,000–4,000.**

### Doctrinal lessons of Cerignola

1. **Field fortification + firearms + pike = the decisive system.** Each component supports the others. The ditch breaks the charge; the arquebus exploits the disorder; the pike provides anti-cavalry insurance and offensive option.
2. **Firearm-killed king-equivalents.** The Duke of Nemours (Louis d'Armagnac) is the first commander of major rank killed by a firearm in European history. The political-symbolic weight of "the king can be shot by a peasant" is enormous.
3. **The era of pure shock-arm dominance is ending.** From Cerignola onward, no major European power will field a doctrine without integrated firearms.

**Throughline flag — the gunpowder transition is doctrinal, not just technical.** *Firearms have existed in European warfare for over a century before Cerignola. What changes at Cerignola is that firearms are integrated into a doctrinal system that can win battles at the strategic scale. The lesson is not "guns appeared"; it is "a system appeared that used guns properly." Technology requires doctrine.*

## 11. The Italian Wars Transition — Marignano, Bicocca, Pavia (1515–1525)

The final battles of pre-firearm Europe blur into the early gunpowder era. Three engagements illustrate the transition.

### Marignano (13–14 September 1515)

Francis I of France vs. Swiss mercenary forces hired by the Duchy of Milan. Two-day battle near Milan. The Swiss attack the French position — gendarmes + artillery + Landsknecht infantry — on the first day; the French line holds through the night; on the second day, Venetian reinforcements arrive and the Swiss are driven from the field after enormous casualties (~10,000 Swiss dead, including most of the senior officers).

**Doctrinal point:** even at Marignano, **Swiss pike comes close to winning a battle against a doctrinally-modern combined-arms army.** The Swiss attack the night before the Venetians arrive and nearly break the French line; only superior French artillery and the resilience of the gendarmes' position prevents collapse. This is the Swiss doctrine's last great-power offensive battle.

### Bicocca (1522) — covered in §7.

### Pavia (24 February 1525) — phase choreography

The decisive battle of the Italian Wars. Francis I (~28,000) besieging Pavia is relieved-and-attacked by Imperial forces under Charles de Lannoy and Fernando d'Avalos (~23,000).

**Strategic setup:** the Imperial army has been gathering for weeks; the French army has been worn down by the siege. Both sides have substantial firearms components, pike, cavalry, and artillery. The Imperial force breaches the wall of the Mirabello park (the hunting preserve outside Pavia) overnight; the engagement begins inside the park at dawn.

**Phase 1 — French initial response.** Francis sees the Imperial breakthrough, leads his gendarmes in a charge against the Imperial vanguard. The charge is initially successful — the gendarmes punch through and rout part of the Imperial advance.

**Phase 2 — French masking their own artillery.** The gendarme charge is so aggressive that the gendarmes themselves obstruct the French artillery's field of fire. The French guns, which had been doing significant damage to the Imperial advance, must cease fire. This is a catastrophic command-and-control error.

**Phase 3 — Spanish arquebusier deployment.** Spanish arquebusiers under d'Avalos disperse into the woods of the park (broken terrain favorable to skirmishing firearms), shooting at the now-disorganized French cavalry from concealment.

**Phase 4 — French cavalry shot to pieces.** The gendarmes, in heavy plate armor on tired horses, are shot down individually by arquebusiers in the trees. The doctrine that worked at Fornovo three decades earlier is incapable of dealing with skirmishing arquebus fire in broken ground. **Francis I's horse is shot, then he himself is captured.**

**Phase 5 — French infantry disintegration.** Without the gendarmes, the French infantry — including Swiss pike — has lost cohesion. Imperial pike + arquebus + cavalry roll up the French line.

**Aftermath:** Francis is captured (a king of France, prisoner of war — the political shock is enormous). The Treaty of Madrid follows (terms favorable to Charles V). **The era of pre-firearm Europe is decisively over.** Subsequent European battles will be pike-and-shot battles, then musket-and-pike, then linear musket. The doctrines covered in this chunk are now archaeology.

### Doctrinal lessons of the transition

1. **Heavy cavalry is no longer decisive.** A gendarme is still effective in certain engagements but cannot win a battle against integrated pike + shot.
2. **Broken ground favors firearms.** The Mirabello park's woods are catastrophic for cavalry, perfect for arquebusiers. Doctrinal implication: the firearm-equipped army wants broken or restrictive ground; the cavalry-equipped army wants open ground. The ground-selection competition becomes central.
3. **Combined arms with firearm integration is the new dominant system.** Spain has the lead at Pavia; France, the Empire, the Italian states all follow within a generation.

## Chunk E1 — tentative throughlines

1. **The infantry revolution of the 14th century** opens with Courtrai (1302) and Bannockburn (1314): infantry with appropriate weapons on prepared ground can defeat feudal heavy cavalry. The doctrinal monopoly of the mounted aristocracy on the Western European battlefield is broken.

2. **The "prepared ground + appropriate weapon + iron discipline" pattern.** Courtrai (goedendag + brooks + Flemish militia), Bannockburn (mobile schiltron + pots + Bruce), Crécy (longbow + slope + dismounted men-at-arms), Agincourt (longbow + mud + woods + stakes), Grandson/Murten/Nancy (Swiss pike + cantonal discipline + ground selection). The pattern is invariant: choose the ground, equip for the threat, discipline the troops, and the prestige-arm of the opponent breaks itself.

3. **The longbow institutional case.** A weapon system depends on the institution that supports it. England's archery practice laws, indenture system, and arrow production capability are the actual military system; the bow is the weapon the system produces. Institutional decline produces tactical reversal even when the weapon itself remains nominally available.

4. **The "discipline timing" axis is fully operational** (echoing Chunk D). At Crécy, Poitiers, Agincourt, the longbow + men-at-arms combination must hold while the French commit. Premature pursuit at Patay 1429 (reverse engagement) loses the longbow battle. The same axis runs through Hattin/Arsuf and Bannockburn.

5. **Hussite wagenburg as the bridge.** Combines defensive infantry doctrine with integrated firearms, anticipates pike-and-shot. Žižka's system is the bridge between medieval shield-wall and Renaissance combined arms.

6. **Swiss pike = offensive shock infantry.** The historical opposite of the Scottish schiltron — the pike used aggressively, with formation tempo as the weapon. Swiss doctrine dominates 1480s–1510s and ends Western European cavalry monopoly.

7. **The "perfect-system trap."** Doctrinal supremacy carries its counter. Swiss pike at peak is invincible by 1480s; the same doctrine breaks at Bicocca in 1522 against an opponent built specifically to exploit its blind spots. The faster a doctrine matures, the faster a counter develops.

8. **Burgundian failure case: composition is not execution.** Charles the Bold's ordonnance system is doctrinally correct on paper. It dies because execution under fluid conditions is inadequate. The same composition + Spanish institutional discipline (a generation later) produces the dominant European force.

9. **Italian condottieri "preservation doctrine."** Like Byzantine *Strategikon* doctrine in Chunk C, this is institutionally adapted to a specific political environment and is exposed when the environment changes. Doctrine cannot be evaluated separate from the political-economic context that produced it.

10. **French *compagnies d'ordonnance* = the first standing army.** Force-generation depth (paid permanent troops, central organization, standardized equipment) emerges as a recognizable institution. Spain perfects the model 30 years later. The standing-army logic of the next 200 years is established here.

11. **Gonzalo de Córdoba's integrated pike + shot + sword = the doctrinal synthesis.** Cerignola 1503 is the first firearm-decided battle. Bicocca 1522 and Pavia 1525 complete the transition. The era of pre-firearm Europe ends.

12. **The gunpowder transition is doctrinal, not technical.** Firearms have existed for over a century before they become decisive. What changes is the doctrine that integrates them. Technology requires doctrine to be decisive.

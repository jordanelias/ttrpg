# Chunk E4 — Pre-Columbian Americas (~900–1525)

**Scope:** The independent military traditions of the Americas before sustained European contact. Covers:

- **Aztec (Mexica) Triple Alliance** (~1428–1521) — the dominant Mesoamerican military system at European contact.
- **Inca Empire** (~1438–1533) — the largest Andean state, with decimal military organization independently developed.
- **Maya post-classic** — fragmented city-state warfare with continuity from classic-period traditions.
- **Mississippian chiefdoms** (~900–1500) — Cahokia, Moundville, and successor polities.
- **The collision events** (Spanish-Aztec 1519–1521; Spanish-Inca 1532–1533) — treated briefly as a terminus, since detailed Conquest history is a major topic in its own right.

The dominant pattern of this chunk: **pre-Columbian military traditions developed within a fundamentally different technological matrix from the Old World.** No metallurgy of arms beyond limited bronze in the Andes; no draft animals; no wheel for military application; no gunpowder; no horse; no high-tensile composite bow. Within this matrix, sophisticated institutional military systems nevertheless emerged — but the **doctrine assumed the threat menu of the local opponent landscape**, and proved catastrophically unable to adapt to the asymmetric Old World menu introduced in 1519. The Spanish conquest catastrophes are not "doctrinal vs. doctrineless" engagements; they are **doctrines optimized for one threat menu meeting a fundamentally different one**, compounded by epidemic disease and timely civil war.

This chunk also serves as the **clearest single case** in the entire project of why **doctrine cannot be evaluated independent of the opponent menu and technological substrate**. Throughline candidate flagged accordingly.

## 1. Aztec (Mexica) Triple Alliance — the Dominant Mesoamerican Military System

The Aztec Triple Alliance (Mexica of Tenochtitlán + Acolhua of Texcoco + Tepanec of Tlacopán), founded 1428, ruled most of central Mexico by 1500. Its military system is the most documented pre-Columbian institutional military of the Americas.

### The threat menu and strategic doctrine

The Aztec strategic environment consists of:

- **Other Nahua city-states** (Tlaxcala, Cholula, Huexotzinco) with similar military culture, similar weapons, similar doctrine. The Aztec system is calibrated against this opponent menu.
- **Tarascan (Purépecha) state** to the west — a more centralized and metallurgically more advanced opponent. Aztec military campaigns against the Tarascans repeatedly failed; the Aztecs never conquered them.
- **Various Mixtec and Zapotec polities** to the south.
- **Maya** further south — too distant for direct conquest in most periods.

Aztec strategic doctrine is **tribute-extraction through threat and selective campaigning**, not unified-empire territorial occupation. Conquered polities retained their local elites and laws in exchange for tribute and military levies. **The Triple Alliance is a tribute network, not a territorial empire** in the Roman/Han sense, and its military doctrine reflects this — armies project force outward in campaign seasons, then withdraw.

### Composition

**Tlatoani** ("speaker", supreme ruler-commander) — directs strategic decisions. The current Tlatoani at the time of Spanish contact is Motēuczōma II (Montezuma).

**Cuauhpilli / military aristocracy** — nobles, professional warriors, the command class. Promotion within the warrior aristocracy is partly hereditary, partly merit-based (captured-opponent counts matter).

**Warrior societies (elite units):**

- **Eagle warriors (*cuāuhtli*)** and **Jaguar warriors (*ocēlōtl*)** — elite warrior orders, distinguished by their distinctive feathered/jaguar-pelt uniforms and helmet animal forms. Required minimum number of personal captures in battle for admission (typically 4+). The most prestigious units in the army.
- **Otontin (*otomī*)** — distinctive "shaved" or "tonsured" hairstyle; required 6+ captures.
- ***Cuachicqueh*** ("shorn ones") — the most elite warriors; required exceptional combat records. Vowed to never retreat. Function as forlorn-hope and shock troops.

**Mass infantry levy from *calpulli*** — urban wards or kin-based local units providing the bulk of the army. Trained from boyhood in the local *telpōchcalli* ("youth house") — military training is universal for young men of the Mexica.

### Equipment

- **Macuahuitl** — flat hardwood club ~1 m long with rectangular obsidian blades inset along both edges. Cuts through flesh and bone at melee range; obsidian edges are dramatically sharper than steel but more brittle. Combination cutting-and-clubbing weapon. **The signature Aztec weapon.**
- **Tepoztopilli** — long obsidian-edged thrusting spear (2–2.5 m), used for keeping enemies at distance and for thrust-and-cut wounds.
- **Atlatl** — spear-thrower extending throwing range and force; used by some warriors to deliver short darts (*tlacochtli*).
- **Bow and arrow (*tlahuitolli*, *mitl*)** — less prestigious than melee weapons; used by lower-class warriors and as supporting fire.
- **Sling (*tematlatl*)** — auxiliary missile weapon, used at long range.
- **Shield (*chimalli*)** — round, ~70 cm diameter, wood or woven cane covered with cotton, hide, and decorative feathers.
- ***Ichcahuipilli*** — quilted cotton armor, layered tightly enough to stop obsidian arrows and reduce blade penetration. The Spanish later adopt this armor for their own troops in Mesoamerica — it works against indigenous weapons better than European mail.
- **Elite war suits (*tlahuiztli*)** — full-body warrior costumes representing the warrior's order (eagle, jaguar, etc.), constructed of feathers, hides, and quilted cotton. Display function as well as protective.

### Geometry and tactics

Aztec battle deployment is **less rigidly geometric than Old World pike-based formations** but is far from disorganized. Key features:

- **Skirmish opening.** Long-range missile exchange (bow, atlatl, sling) opens engagement. Both sides take losses; the missile phase is sometimes prolonged.
- **Massed advance with elite formations forward.** Eagle, jaguar, otomi, and cuachic warrior society units lead the advance. The macuahuitl + tepoztopilli combination is used in close combat — pikes thrust, swords cut.
- **Calpulli levy units** advance behind the elites, exploiting any opening, defending against any breakthrough.
- **Tactical role of the warrior societies — to penetrate and capture.** Elite warriors target opposing elites for capture in personal combat. **Capture, not killing, is the primary tactical objective.**

### The capture-priority and the Flower Wars

This is the most discussed and most misunderstood feature of Aztec doctrine.

**Capture-priority:**

- Aztec religious doctrine requires regular sacrificial offerings to maintain cosmic order. Captives — particularly warrior captives, particularly captives taken in single combat — are the primary sacrificial resource.
- Aztec warriors are rewarded for **captures**, not kills. Promotion within warrior societies depends on number and quality of personal captures.
- This produces a distinctive battlefield behavior: when a warrior has subdued an opponent in close combat, his goal is to drag the prisoner back to friendly lines rather than kill him outright. Friends may help (or compete) with the capture.

**Flower Wars (*xōchiyāōyōtl*):** prearranged ritualized warfare between the Triple Alliance and certain rival polities (especially Tlaxcala). Features:

- Set times and places, often festival-aligned.
- Both sides commit warriors; battle is fought; captives are exchanged or kept (largely for sacrifice).
- Limited territorial objective; no city-capture; no civilian targets.
- Functions as: warrior training, captive supply, political-religious display, and possibly as a form of attritional pressure on the rival polity that does not provoke total war.

**Doctrinal implications:**

1. **Capture-priority is not "naïve" or "ceremonial."** It is institutionally rational within the Aztec religious-political economy. Warriors who kill rather than capture lose promotion opportunities; the religious system that legitimizes the elite class needs the captives.
2. **It produces a specific tactical pathology against opponents with different doctrines.** Against another Aztec-style force, capture-priority is a shared rule and not a disadvantage. Against an opponent who fights to kill — including Tarascans (who appear to have fought more lethally) and especially the Spanish (who killed without hesitation) — capture-priority is a unilateral handicap.
3. **The doctrine "works" within its threat menu and fails outside it.** This is not unique to the Aztecs — Italian condottieri "preservation doctrine" (Chunk E1) has the same property. Doctrine evolved against a specific opponent menu may fail against an opponent not in that menu.

### Strategic-logistical structure

- **Tribute-supported campaigns.** The Triple Alliance demands tribute in food, cotton, jade, feathers, and labor from subject polities. This supports campaign armies in the field — soldiers eat tribute supplies, not their own provisions.
- **No standing field army in the Eurasian standing-army sense**, but the warrior aristocracy and warrior societies form a permanent military caste, and campaign mobilization is rapid (days, not weeks).
- **Roads and causeways** — well-developed within the Valley of Mexico, but not the empire-scale network the Inca have.
- **No draft animals; no wheeled transport.** All military logistics is human portage. This constrains both campaign distance and army size: typical Aztec campaign armies are 10,000–25,000; massing 100,000+ for short-distance operations is reported but probably exaggerated.

### Tarascan exception

The Tarascan / Purépecha state of Michoacán defeated multiple Aztec invasion attempts (1469, 1480s, 1500s). Tarascan doctrine differs in:

- **Bronze and copper weapons** (the Tarascans had functional metallurgy that the Aztecs lacked at scale).
- **Possibly different tactical priorities** (some evidence suggests Tarascans fought more to kill than to capture).
- **Defensive geography** — the western mountains favor the defender.

The Tarascan exception is the **clearest pre-contact case** of an Aztec doctrinal limitation — they could not defeat an opponent fighting them on different doctrinal terms.

**Throughline flag — the doctrine-opponent-menu match.** *The Aztec military system is well-calibrated against other Mesoamerican states with similar weapons, similar doctrine, and similar political-religious assumptions. It is poorly calibrated against the Tarascans (different weapons + apparently different doctrine) and catastrophically miscalibrated against the Spanish (radically different weapons + radically different doctrine + epidemic disease). **A doctrine is only as good as its match to the opponent menu it was developed against.***

## 2. Inca Empire — Decimal Military Organization in the Andes

The Inca Empire (*Tahuantinsuyu*, "Land of the Four Quarters"), expanding from Cuzco from c. 1438 under Pachacuti and his successors, controlled by 1525 the largest territory of any pre-Columbian American state — roughly 2 million km², stretching from southern Colombia to central Chile along the Andes.

### Organization — Inca decimal hierarchy

The Inca developed, independently of the Old World, a **decimal military organization** that bears structural resemblance to (but no historical connection with) Mongol and Han Chinese systems:

- ***Chunca*** — 10 men.
- ***Pachaca*** — 100 men.
- ***Huaranca*** — 1,000 men.
- ***Hunu*** — 10,000 men.

Each level has a named commander. Multiple *hunu* form an army.

**The Inca decimal system is one of the most striking parallel-evolution cases in pre-modern military organization.** Decimal hierarchy emerges independently in multiple polities (Sumerian, Han Chinese, Mongol, Inca) because it is **a natural organizational solution to the command-and-control problem at large scale**. Counting in tens is universal among complex polities; nesting tens recursively gives a logarithmic command-depth (10,000 men under 4 levels of command). Other systems exist (Roman cohort+centuria isn't strictly decimal; Ottoman *bölük* organization varies) but the decimal pattern recurs.

### Composition

- **Sapa Inca** — supreme ruler and commander.
- **Standing professional army.** Unlike the Aztec mostly-levy system, the Inca maintains a substantial standing field force, especially during the expansion era under Pachacuti, Topa Inca Yupanqui, and Huayna Capac. Some estimates suggest 200,000+ men under arms at peak.
- **Ethnic auxiliary forces.** Conquered peoples (Chimu, Cañari, various coastal and highland groups) contribute auxiliary units retaining their own equipment and tactical style. The Inca army at peak is **multi-ethnic combined arms** at empire scale.
- **Officer class** — drawn from Inca nobility and from loyal conquered elites; both Cuzco-based and provincial.

### Equipment

- ***Macana / champi*** — wooden or bronze-headed war clubs, often with star-shaped or spiked heads. Crushing weapon.
- ***Chuqui*** — long thrusting spear, often with a bronze or copper point.
- ***Huaraca*** — sling. **The Inca sling is extraordinarily effective** — Andean shepherds spend their lives using slings to drive llamas and herd them; military sling use produces heavy bruising and lethal head injuries at 100+ meters. Stones weighing 50–100 grams thrown at ~30 m/s deliver substantial kinetic energy.
- **Bola** — three weighted cords used to entangle legs of animals or warriors.
- **Bows** — used primarily by Amazonian-frontier auxiliaries; less central to Inca highland tactics than to Mesoamerican.
- **Bronze axes** — copper and bronze metallurgy is more developed in the Andes than in Mesoamerica.
- ***Cotton armor*** quilted, similar to Aztec *ichcahuipilli*.
- ***Wari*chs / helmets*** — wooden, bronze-edged in some elite cases.
- **Shields** — small, round, often wooden with decorative metal.

### Strategic infrastructure — the road system

The Inca infrastructure is the single most distinctive feature of their military capability:

- **~40,000 km of roads** — paved or graded, with bridges (rope, wooden, or stone), tunnels, stairways through Andean grades.
- ***Tambo*** — way-station and supply-depot system every ~20 km along major routes. Tambos stockpile food, clothing, weapons, and serve as troop-rest points.
- ***Chasqui*** runner-relay system — message couriers operating in relay along the road system. Communication speeds of 150+ km/day claimed.
- ***Storehouses (qollqa)*** — strategic-reserve granaries, with stockpiles supporting army campaigns and famine relief.

**This is the most developed strategic infrastructure in the pre-Columbian Americas, and arguably more comprehensive than any contemporary Old World equivalent except Roman Empire and Persian / Achaemenid Royal Road systems.**

### Doctrinal characteristics

- **Mass infantry with sling-and-club combined arms.** Slings open at long range; clubs and spears decide at close range. Bronze weaponry gives a marginal edge over Mesoamerican obsidian (less brittle, more durable, but less sharp — different trade-offs).
- **Less capture-oriented than Aztec doctrine.** Inca religious practice includes some sacrifice but is less centered on warrior-captive sacrifice; military doctrine appears to fight more to defeat than to capture.
- **Engineering and logistics as strategic doctrine.** Inca campaigns are decided as much by which side controls supply, roads, and reinforcements as by tactical engagement. The state's institutional capacity to project force at distance is its defining military advantage.

### The civil war (1529–1532)

The Inca state's catastrophic vulnerability to Spanish intervention is partly produced by an internal disaster. After the death of Sapa Inca Huayna Capac (~1525–1527, probably of smallpox spreading ahead of direct Spanish contact), his sons Atahualpa (Quito-based, controlling northern armies) and Huascar (Cuzco-based, controlling traditional Inca heartland) fight a civil war for succession.

By 1532, when Pizarro arrives, the war has just concluded with Atahualpa's victory. The state is exhausted; rival factions are deeply hostile; provinces are uncertain; and the northern army (loyal to Atahualpa) is geographically separated from the southern (Cuzco-based) population.

This timing is **the single most critical strategic factor** in the Spanish-Inca collision, more than weapon asymmetry or tactical doctrine.

**Throughline flag — the Inca decimal system as parallel evolution of the same organizational primitive seen in Mongol and Han Chinese systems.** *Three independent inventions of decimal military hierarchy across three civilizations with no historical contact strongly suggest decimal organization is a natural answer to the large-scale command problem. The same applies to **roads + waystations + couriers** as strategic infrastructure (Roman/Persian/Inca independent invention) and to **standing army + agricultural tax base** (independently emerging in many polities). **Some military-institutional solutions are doctrinal optima that recur whenever the problem is the same.***

## 3. Maya Post-Classic Warfare (~900–1500)

The Classic Maya collapse (~800–900 AD) ends the era of major city-state warfare in the Petén; subsequent Maya political life is fragmented into city-states across the Yucatán Peninsula (and southern highlands).

### Composition and equipment

Similar in many respects to Aztec systems (shared Mesoamerican cultural matrix):

- **Macuahuitl-equivalent** (similar obsidian-edged clubs/swords).
- **Atlatl and bow.**
- **Cotton armor.**
- **Round shields.**
- **Warrior castes** with similar status and promotion systems.

### Distinctive features

- **Smaller scale.** Maya post-classic city-states are smaller than the Triple Alliance; armies of a few thousand are typical.
- **Coastal trade-warfare.** The Putún Maya of the Gulf Coast develop substantial canoe-borne warfare and trade, including amphibious raid operations.
- **Less institutional centralization.** The Maya post-classic period has no equivalent to the Triple Alliance — multiple competing polities with shifting alliances.
- **Itzá and other late polities** maintain Maya martial culture through the 15th and into the 16th centuries; the last independent Maya polity (the Itzá kingdom at Nojpetén) survives until 1697.

### Doctrinal characteristics

Maya warfare emphasizes:

- Raiding and small-unit operations as much as set-piece battle.
- Captive-taking for sacrifice and political display (similar to but not identical to Aztec practice).
- Coastal/riverine operations more developed than in highland Mesoamerica.

The doctrinal contrast with Aztec / Triple Alliance practice is mainly one of **scale and centralization**, not of tactical fundamentals.

**Throughline flag:** *Smaller polities operating in the same cultural matrix as larger ones produce doctrines that are recognizably the same in tactical fundamentals but adjusted for institutional scale.*

## 4. Mississippian Chiefdom Warfare (~900–1500)

The Mississippian cultural complex of the eastern North American woodlands produces the most institutionally complex pre-Columbian societies north of Mexico. Cahokia (modern East St. Louis area), at peak in the 12th century, was a city of perhaps 15,000–20,000 — comparable in scale to large medieval European towns.

### Composition

- **Warrior elite caste** — drawn from chiefly lineages; receives specialized training and equipment.
- **Common warriors** — male adults of the community, generally trained from youth in war and hunting.

### Equipment

- **Bow** — the primary missile arm; Eastern Woodlands archery is highly developed.
- **War club** — wood, often with stone or bone head; the close-combat weapon.
- **Atlatl** in earlier periods (largely displaced by bow by the late Mississippian).
- **Knife** — flint or bone.
- **Shield** — wooden, hide-covered.
- **Wood/hide armor** in some contexts; less developed than Mesoamerican cotton.

### Settlement and defensive doctrine

The distinctive Mississippian feature is the **palisaded town**:

- Major Mississippian centers (Cahokia, Moundville, Etowah, Spiro) are fortified with wooden palisades, often with bastions or guard towers.
- Earthworks supplement the palisades.
- Towns serve as refugia in case of attack — and as launching points for raids.

### Doctrinal characteristics

Mississippian warfare is primarily:

- **Raid-based** between rival chiefdoms.
- **Personal-prestige-oriented** — warrior status, scalping, captive-taking for adoption or sacrifice.
- **Seasonally constrained** — major operations in summer when food is more available.

The doctrinal scale is smaller than Mesoamerican / Andean systems — Mississippian polities lack the population base or institutional depth to field armies of tens of thousands. But the institutional sophistication (fortified centers, warrior elite, ranged hierarchy) is real.

The Mississippian system **declines through the 14th and 15th centuries** for reasons still debated (climate change, internal conflict, possibly early disease from continental contact); by the 16th century, most major mound centers are abandoned, replaced by the smaller polities the early European explorers encounter.

## 5. The Collision Events — Spanish Conquest as Doctrinal Failure Case (1519–1533)

The Spanish defeat the Triple Alliance (1519–1521) and the Inca state (1532–1533) with very small Spanish forces and large indigenous allied contributions. The "few hundred Spaniards defeat empires" framing is misleading — the actual mechanism is **doctrinal asymmetry compounded by epidemic disease and political fracture**.

### Cortés and the Aztec collision (1519–1521)

**Spanish force:** initially ~600 men, growing through reinforcement to ~2,000 at peak. Equipment: steel armor and swords, arquebus, small artillery, crossbows, **horses** (functionally a new doctrinal arm Mesoamericans had never faced), and war dogs.

**Allied indigenous force:** ~200,000+ Tlaxcalan, Texcocan (defectors), and other anti-Aztec forces by the siege of Tenochtitlán. **The Aztec Triple Alliance is destroyed primarily by an indigenous coalition, with Spanish forces as catalyst, command core, and technological multiplier.**

**Mechanisms of Aztec doctrinal failure:**

1. **The capture-priority pathology.** Aztec warriors fighting Spaniards continued to attempt capture rather than kill. Spaniards killed without restraint. The doctrinal asymmetry produces a casualty ratio that is unsustainable for the Aztecs.
2. **Cavalry was a new arm.** Aztec doctrine had no answer to mounted warriors. Horses themselves were terrifying (no large draft animal existed in Mesoamerica), the speed differential was radical, and the mounted lance / arquebus combination was unprecedented.
3. **Steel weaponry asymmetry.** Obsidian weapons are sharper than steel but more brittle. Cotton armor is effective against obsidian but inadequate against steel sword strokes and arquebus balls. Spanish steel armor is largely impervious to obsidian. The exchange ratio in close combat is heavily Spanish-favored.
4. **Smallpox.** Reaching Tenochtitlán by ~1520, smallpox kills perhaps 30–50% of the Aztec population over the next year, including the second Tlatoani after Moctezuma (Cuitláhuac, who died of smallpox while organizing resistance). The military command class is depleted at the moment of greatest strategic need.
5. **Political fracture.** The Tlaxcalans had been Aztec rivals for generations; Cortés exploited this masterfully. Many subject polities also defected. The Triple Alliance had relied on tribute coercion to maintain control; once an effective alternative appeared, it lost legitimacy across its periphery.

### Pizarro and the Inca collision (1532–1533)

**Spanish force:** ~170 men initially.

**Mechanisms of Inca doctrinal failure:**

1. **Civil war timing.** As discussed above, the Inca state was just emerging from civil war when Pizarro arrived. Atahualpa's victory was recent and fragile.
2. **Atahualpa's capture at Cajamarca (16 November 1532).** Pizarro tricks Atahualpa into a meeting unarmed, then attacks his entourage and captures him in person. The Sapa Inca is the institutional pivot of the entire state; with him captured, the army defers rather than acting independently.
3. **Cavalry shock.** Same as in Mesoamerica — the Inca had no doctrinal preparation for mounted warriors.
4. **Smallpox.** As noted, the disease reached the Inca state before sustained direct Spanish contact. Huayna Capac's death from probable smallpox is what produced the civil war in the first place.
5. **The ransom and execution.** Atahualpa offered an enormous gold-and-silver ransom for his release. The Spanish accepted it, then executed him anyway. By this point the Inca state was politically paralyzed; the rapid Spanish march to Cuzco met fragmented resistance.

### Why these collisions are not a fair test of doctrine

The Spanish-Inca and Spanish-Aztec engagements feature:

- Radical weapon asymmetry (steel, firearms, horses, war dogs vs. obsidian, atlatl, sling, club).
- A catastrophic disease pandemic killing 30–50% of indigenous populations.
- Indigenous political fracture exploited deliberately by the Spanish.
- A new opponent menu the indigenous doctrines were never developed against.
- Spanish doctrinal preparation drawing on Reconquista (700 years of frontier warfare against well-armed Muslims), recent Italian Wars experience, and contemporary European military culture.

**These are extreme doctrinal-mismatch cases, not engagements between comparable systems.** The lesson is not "Aztec/Inca doctrines were primitive" but "**doctrine evaluated against the wrong opponent menu, with the wrong technological substrate, against a foe carrying epidemic disease, is helpless regardless of its internal sophistication.**"

**Throughline flag — doctrine-substrate-opponent triangle.** *Every military doctrine in this entire project is the product of three things: (1) the institutional / technological / economic substrate that allows the doctrine to exist; (2) the opponent menu it was developed against; (3) the cultural-political values that shape what it considers victory. **A doctrine evaluated outside its triangle is a doctrine in free-fall.** The Spanish conquests are the most extreme case in the project of all three legs of the triangle going wrong simultaneously.*

## Chunk E4 — tentative throughlines

1. **Pre-Columbian military development was real and substantial.** Aztec warrior society institutions, Inca decimal organization with 200,000+ standing forces, Mississippian palisaded centers — these are the products of mature military cultures within their technological matrices.

2. **The technological matrix shapes the doctrinal space.** No iron metallurgy of arms, no draft animals, no wheels for military use, no gunpowder, no horse, no composite recurve bow at full Asian effectiveness — within this matrix, certain doctrines (mass pike phalanx, heavy cavalry charge, integrated firearms, wagon-fortress at large scale) are simply impossible. Indigenous doctrines optimize within the matrix they have.

3. **The Aztec capture-priority doctrine = institutionally rational within its opponent menu, catastrophic outside it.** Within Triple Alliance / Tlaxcalan / Mesoamerican warfare, capture-priority is shared and works. Against opponents fighting to kill, it is a unilateral handicap.

4. **Tarascan exception** is the pre-contact demonstration that Aztec doctrine was already vulnerable to differently-doctrinated opponents. The Spanish collision is the extreme version of the same vulnerability.

5. **Inca decimal organization** as a parallel-evolution case alongside Mongol, Han Chinese, Sumerian decimal systems. The Old World decimal-hierarchy primitive is independently rediscovered in the Andes. **Some institutional solutions are doctrinal optima that recur whenever the problem is the same** (echoing wagon-fortress finding from Chunk E3).

6. **Inca strategic infrastructure** — 40,000 km of roads, *tambo* supply waystations, *chasqui* couriers, *qollqa* state stockpiles. The most developed pre-Columbian strategic logistical system, parallel in concept (and possibly exceeding in some dimensions) Roman and Persian Royal Road systems.

7. **Mississippian palisaded chiefdoms** show that fortification-and-warrior-elite organization emerges even at chiefdom scale below state-level institutionalization. Scale-of-polity affects doctrinal depth but not doctrinal fundamentals.

8. **The Spanish-conquest collisions are doctrine-substrate-opponent failures, not pure doctrinal failures.** Indigenous doctrines optimized for one threat menu meet a fundamentally different menu, compounded by epidemic disease and political fracture. **The lesson is not "the indigenous systems were primitive" — it is that doctrine is inseparable from its substrate, its opponent menu, and its cultural assumptions about victory.**

9. **Disease as an unintended strategic factor.** Smallpox kills 30–50% of indigenous populations in the conquest era — including, critically, military command personnel and political leaders. The Spanish invasions ride a wave of demographic catastrophe they did not engineer but ruthlessly exploited.

# Chunk F1 — Throughlines, Meta-Throughlines, and the Matchup Matrix

**Scope:** The synthesis of the eight catalogue chunks. This document distills the recurring patterns identified across ~2,750 lines of regional / era coverage into:

1. **Throughlines** — the recurring patterns visible across multiple eras and regions.
2. **Meta-throughlines** — the higher-order structural principles that organize the throughlines themselves.
3. **The matchup matrix (Task 3 deliverable)** — formation-vs-formation efficacy with conditions, asymmetries, and generic conclusions.

The companion document **F2** will handle the **Task 4 deliverable** — granular unit-position / trajectory / timing / speed analysis through battle phases.

---

## Part I — Throughlines

These are the recurring patterns identified during the catalogue chunks. They are organized into thematic clusters, with the chunks they were first or most prominently flagged in.

### Cluster A — Combined arms and force composition

- **T-1: Shock + missile = the durable combination.** Pure shock (Sumerian phalanx, Hittite chariot, medieval heavy cavalry mono-culture) or pure missile (sparabara, steppe horse-archer, Mamluk *furusiyya*) loses to the integrated form. First visible in Akkadian sources (Chunk A); confirmed across every era thereafter. **No mono-cultural army ever defeated a comparable-quality combined-arms opponent for sustained periods.**

- **T-2: Combined arms requires command integration, not just unit composition.** Having the right unit types is necessary but not sufficient. Indian chaturanga (Chunk B) has the composition; Macedonian, Roman, Byzantine, Ottoman systems have the integration. Burgundian ordonnance (Chunk E1) has the composition; Spanish coronelía has the integration. **Composition without integration is wreckage.**

- **T-3: The rock-paper-scissors persists.** Pike beats cavalry; cavalry beats missile; missile beats pike. Visible in Akkadian sources, confirmed at Bannockburn / Falkirk (Chunk E1), persistent through every pre-firearm era. **Only the gunpowder transition collapses the bow leg of the triangle and forces a new equilibrium.**

- **T-4: Force structure is downstream of state structure.** Hoplite-citizen, pastoralist horse-archer, slave-soldier mamluk, mercenary condottiere, feudal levy, peasant ashigaru — each is the army its society can produce. Force-generation institutions are inseparable from the political economy supporting them.

### Cluster B — Institutional substrate

- **T-5: Force-generation depth outlasts tactical brilliance.** Rome replaces post-Cannae losses; Carthage cannot replace Hannibal-quality armies; Timur's army dies with him; Burgundy's ordonnance system dies in 14 months with Charles the Bold; Mongol decimal organization outlasts Genghis by generations; Ottoman devshirme is multi-generational. **Institutional depth = sustained capability.**

- **T-6: Weapons depend on institutions.** English longbow without practice laws + indenture + arrow-supply = a pile of wood (Chunk E1). Mongol horse-archery without remount strings + decimal hierarchy + the *yam* communication system = good individual fighters (Chunk D). Crossbow effectiveness depends on state production capacity (Han/Song/Genoese, Chunks B/C/E1). **The weapon is not the system; the institution that produces and supports the weapon is the system.**

- **T-7: Slave-soldier institutions as a dominant force-generation pattern across Islamic civilizations.** Ghulam (Abbasid, Chunk C) → Mamluk (Ayyubid + Sultanate, Chunks C/D/E2) → Janissary / *kapıkulu* (Ottoman, Chunk E2). The institutional logic — "import boys, raise them in barracks, produce an elite professional army without depending on hereditary nobility" — produces some of the most effective troops of the medieval and Renaissance eras.

- **T-8: Doctrine is institutionally portable across cultures.** Mongol pattern → Mamluks at Ain Jalut (Chunk D); Hussite wagenburg → Ottoman tabur cengi → Spanish coronelía → Babur's araba at Panipat (Chunks E1/E2/E3); Ottoman/Anatolian firearm doctrine → Mughal Panipat. **Doctrine is not culture-bound at the institutional level — any state willing to import specialists and integrate them can adopt the dominant system.**

### Cluster C — Command and timing

- **T-9: The "discipline-timing" axis is the central command skill across all doctrines with a decisive commit.** Heavy cavalry charge (single-shot), Mongol *mangudai* recall, Swiss pike attack pace, Hussite wagon-sortie, Crusader fighting-march holding under harassment — each requires holding the commit until the right moment AND releasing it correctly. Premature commit fails; absent commit also fails. **Hattin vs. Arsuf is the canonical comparison** (Chunk D).

- **T-10: Battles are decided by secondary commits, not primary clash.** Kadesh's Ne'arin column (Chunk A); Gaugamela's Companion wedge through the gap (B); Liegnitz's Mongol flank repositioning (D); Bannockburn's small Scottish cavalry breaking the English archers (E1); Poitiers's Captal de Buch flanking sweep (E1); Ankara's Anatolian Turkic defection (E2). **The first lines meeting is rarely the decisive moment; timing of the second commit is.**

- **T-11: Local concentration / oblique order beats uniform engagement.** Epaminondas at Leuctra (B); Macedonian doctrine at Gaugamela (B); Hannibal at Cannae (B); arguably all the longbow battles where the English massed flanking missile fire (E1); Swiss pike's massing into single squares against extended lines (E1). **The uniform line is a special case; serious commanders weight one sector and time engagement against the others.**

### Cluster D — Doctrine vs. environment

- **T-12: Context dependence of doctrinal efficacy.** Japanese individualized doctrine fails on the Hungarian plain, succeeds on a Kyushu beach (Chunks C/D). Mongol horse-archery dominates on open steppe, struggles in Vietnamese mountains or Vienna's siege walls (D). Western fighting-march works at Arsuf, fails at Hattin (D). **No doctrine is universally good or bad — only well-matched or poorly-matched to specific environments.**

- **T-13: Strategic mobility can substitute for tactical superiority.** Vikings dominate the era's strategic level via amphibious mobility, often without tactical superiority (Chunk C). Mongol operational tempo (50–100 km/day) wins campaigns the Mongols couldn't necessarily win in symmetric set-pieces (D). Crimean Tatar raid-economy sustains a state for three centuries (E2). **Picking when and where to fight is itself a doctrinal lever.**

- **T-14: Steppe horse-archer asymmetry persists for 2,500 years.** From Scythians vs. Darius (~513 BC) through Mongols vs. Hungary (1241) through Crimean Tatars vs. Russia (well into the 17th century). The doctrine that "shoot and don't be reached" defeats sedentary infantry without effective answer is one of the most durable asymmetries in pre-firearm military history. Counters: one's own steppe cavalry (Byzantine, Han, Mamluk), terrain (Vietnamese, Caucasian, alpine), or — eventually — firearms.

### Cluster E — The fortification / mobility axis

- **T-15: Fortification doctrine as legitimate substitute for field doctrine.** Southern Song's 40-year defensive holdout against the Mongols (Chunk D); Mamluk fortification of Egypt as backstop against Mongol incursion; Byzantine Theodosian walls; *trace italienne* response to artillery; Hussite wagenburg as mobile fortification. **If you cannot beat them in the field, beat them at the walls.** Older than gunpowder.

- **T-16: Wagon-fortress as doctrinal optimum found multiple times.** Hussite (1420s), Ottoman (1440s+), Ming (independent, possibly pre-Hussite), Spanish coronelía field fortification (1500s), Babur's *araba* (1526). **Independent invention or rapid transmission of the same structural primitive** strongly suggests wagon-fortress is the natural answer to "how does mass infantry with missile weapons defeat heavy cavalry on open ground."

### Cluster F — Cultural / value constraints

- **T-17: The cultural-doctrinal compatibility filter.** Mamluk caste rejection of firearms on social-prestige grounds (E2); Qizilbash religious-chivalric refusal to attack a deploying army (E2); French knights at Crécy charging through their own crossbowmen for class reasons (E1); Aztec capture-priority refusing to kill outright (E4). **Cultures unable to absorb new doctrine lose to cultures that can. Cultural attitudes constrain the adaptive range of any doctrine.**

- **T-18: Victory conditions are culturally specified.** Aztec capture-priority (E4); Italian condottieri "preservation doctrine" (E1); Byzantine *Strategikon*'s "avoid battle unless certain of victory" (C); Crusader chivalric obligation to engage; Mongol terror-as-policy. **What counts as "winning" varies by culture and shapes the doctrine that emerges.** A doctrine optimized for one definition of victory fails when the opponent uses a different definition.

### Cluster G — The gunpowder transition

- **T-19: The gunpowder transition is doctrinal, not technical.** Firearms existed in European warfare for ~150 years (and in Chinese warfare for ~300+ years) before they became battlefield-decisive. Cerignola 1503 doesn't introduce gunpowder; it introduces a *system* that uses gunpowder properly. **Technology requires doctrine to be strategically decisive.**

- **T-20: The global firearm-transition window 1503–1526 is a single transcontinental event.** Cerignola (Italy 1503), Marj Dabiq (Syria 1516), Chaldiran (Anatolia/Iran 1514), Mohács (Hungary 1526), Panipat (India 1526). Five battles, five different cultural-political contexts, the same doctrinal lesson: **integrated firearms + field fortification + combined arms defeats pre-firearm doctrines, regardless of whether those doctrines are pure-cavalry, pre-firearm combined arms, or anything between.** This is **the** doctrinal hinge of the project.

- **T-21: Mass cavalry doctrines fail catastrophically and rapidly in this window.** Mamluk *furusiyya* dies in two battles in six months. Safavid Qizilbash humbled in one battle. Hungarian heavy cavalry destroyed in under two hours at Mohács. **The technology is gradual; the institutional collapse is sudden** — once the doctrine fails publicly, the political-legitimacy infrastructure built on it follows.

### Cluster H — Geography and technology substrate

- **T-22: The technological matrix shapes the doctrinal space.** Pre-Columbian Americas (E4) without iron metallurgy of arms, draft animals, wheels, gunpowder, or horse — pike phalanx, integrated firearms, large-scale wagon-fortress, heavy cavalry charge are all unavailable. **Within the matrix, sophisticated doctrines emerge anyway** (Aztec warrior societies, Inca decimal organization). **Outside the matrix, none of these doctrines port.**

- **T-23: Operational geography is part of doctrine.** Vietnamese terrain-doctrine (E3); Scottish highland adaptation; Caucasian resistance; Alpine Swiss defense; Khazar / Crimean steppe-economy; Inca road network constraining Andean campaigns. **The map is part of the recipe.** A doctrine without its geography is an abstraction.

### Cluster I — Specific doctrinal primitives

- **T-24: Hammer-and-anvil = the dominant decisive pattern.** Macedonian Companion + phalanx (B); Roman legion + cavalry auxiliary; medieval knight + foot levy; Mongol heavy + light cavalry; Ottoman Janissary + sipahi; Zulu chest + horns. **One arm fixes; one arm decides.** Recurs from the 4th century BC through every subsequent era of skilled command.

- **T-25: The Cannae template (double envelopment).** Hold center weak-but-flexible, let the enemy advance, swing the wings inward, close cavalry behind (B). Reproduced or attempted at multiple points across the 2,200 years since. The template is recognizably present in Hattin (D), partially in Liegnitz (D), partially in Bicocca (E1, against the Swiss this time).

- **T-26: The single-shot character of the heavy-cavalry charge** is the defining structural constraint of Western European medieval doctrine (D, E1). The charge spends itself on contact and cannot easily re-form mid-battle. This single fact organizes much of late-medieval Western warfare, including the strategic vulnerability that longbow + pike + wagenburg exploit.

- **T-27: Modular line-relief as Roman maniple/cohort breakthrough.** The Macedonian phalanx fights with the men it deploys; the Roman legion swaps fresh troops mid-battle (B). The modular system is the durable answer to the brittleness of monolithic shock formations. Byzantine *kontaria*, Ottoman *bölük* organization, Renaissance pike-and-shot all carry forward modular line-relief logic.

### Cluster J — Emergent behavior

- **T-28: Grand-formation behavior emerges from unit-formation mechanics.** Hoplite right-flank drift (A): no general orders it; it emerges from individual shield orientation. Capture-priority pathology in Aztec engagements with Spanish: no commander orders sub-optimal behavior; it emerges from institutional reward structures. **Bottom-up mechanics produce top-down patterns. Doctrine has to account for what units actually do, not what they're told to do.**

- **T-29: Morale cascade as a tactical force.** Grandson is lost without significant fighting because the Burgundian line believes itself to be losing (E1). Roman armies after Cannae remain functional units only because of institutional discipline that re-anchors morale faster than panic propagates. Mongol *mangudai* deliberately weaponizes morale cascade — the appearance of withdrawal triggers the enemy's commitment to pursue. **Battlefield morale is a propagating field, not a property of individual men.**

## Part II — Meta-Throughlines

The throughlines above are organized into thematic clusters; the meta-throughlines are the higher-order structural principles that organize the throughlines themselves. These are the "throughlines of throughlines" — the patterns visible only after the patterns are catalogued.

### M-1: The doctrine-substrate-opponent triangle

Every military doctrine in this entire project is the product of three things, all of which must be specified for the doctrine to be evaluated:

1. **Substrate** — the institutional, technological, and economic base that makes the doctrine possible. Hoplite phalanx requires citizen-farmer polities + bronze metallurgy + agricultural surplus + civic religion. Mongol doctrine requires pastoralist economy + composite-bow technology + decimal organization + the *yam* infrastructure. Spanish coronelía requires Reconquista military culture + iron arquebus production + standing-army finance + tercio-precursor training.

2. **Opponent menu** — what the doctrine is designed against. Aztec doctrine is calibrated against other Nahua / Mesoamerican opponents. English longbow is calibrated against French heavy cavalry + crossbow. Mamluk *furusiyya* is calibrated against Mongol horse-archery and Crusader heavy cavalry.

3. **Cultural victory conditions** — what counts as winning. Aztec capture-priority, Italian condottieri preservation, Byzantine "preserve the force," Roman strategic patience, Mongol terror-as-policy. **What the doctrine is *for* shapes the doctrine.**

**A doctrine evaluated outside its triangle is in free fall.** Most of the catastrophic failures in the catalogue (Mamluks at Marj Dabiq, Qizilbash at Chaldiran, Aztecs against Spanish, French knights at Crécy) are not "the doctrine was wrong" — they are "the doctrine was optimized for a triangle that no longer applied."

This is **the most powerful organizing principle of the entire project**. Every other meta-throughline can be located inside this triangle.

### M-2: Institution > tactics > weapons over time

Three nested layers of military effectiveness, ordered by their persistence and decisive influence:

- **Weapons** are the surface layer. Visible, often dramatic, but most easily copied or substituted. The longbow can be hired or imitated; the arquebus spreads continent-wide in a generation; horse barding crosses the Eurasian land mass repeatedly.

- **Tactics** are the recipe layer. The specific procedures by which weapons are used in combination: line-relief, oblique order, feigned retreat, hammer-and-anvil. Tactics transfer faster than institutions but slower than weapons; tactical innovations like Mu Ying's volley fire (Chinese, 1380s) take ~200 years to reappear in European practice.

- **Institutions** are the substrate layer. The recruitment, training, supply, command-and-control, and replacement systems that produce and sustain forces. Institutions transfer slowest of all: the Mongol decimal hierarchy, Roman cohort discipline, Ottoman devshirme, Spanish tercio drill — each took generations to build, decades to replicate, sometimes never replicated successfully in other contexts.

**The order of importance for long-term effectiveness is institutions → tactics → weapons.** Weapons are the kitchen tools; tactics are the recipes; institutions are the cooks and the kitchen and the supply chain. **A polity can win once with new weapons; it wins consistently with new tactics; it wins for centuries with new institutions.**

### M-3: Parallel evolution of doctrinal optima

Some institutional and tactical solutions recur independently across cultures with no contact:

- **Decimal hierarchy.** Sumerian (~3000 BC), Han Chinese (Tang fubing), Mongol (1206), Inca (15th c.) — four independent inventions of the same nested-tens command structure. This is convergent evolution at institutional scale.

- **Wagon-fortress.** Hussite (1420s), Ottoman (via Hussite), Ming (independent), Spanish coronelía (1500s), Babur's *araba* (1526). At least two independent invention paths, possibly more.

- **Shield-wall** as the universal "no-cavalry default." Sumerian, Greek hoplite, Roman, Byzantine skoutatoi, Frankish dismount, Anglo-Saxon, Viking, Flemish urban militia — recurring whenever a culture lacks effective cavalry. Convergent answer to the same problem.

- **Hammer-and-anvil** as the universal decisive pattern. Independently visible in Macedonian, Roman + auxiliary, medieval knight + levy, Mongol, Ottoman, Zulu doctrine.

- **Mounted-archer doctrine** as the recurring steppe answer. From Scythian through Hun, Avar, Magyar, Pecheneg, Cuman, Mongol, Turkic, Ottoman akıncı, Crimean Tatar. The same answer to the same ecological conditions over 2,500 years.

**The pattern suggests that doctrine is, partly, the discovery of locally-optimal solutions to a finite set of structural problems.** When the problem recurs, the same solution recurs — even across thousands of years and continents.

### M-4: The discipline-timing axis as the universal command skill

The single skill that distinguishes great commanders from competent ones, across every era of the catalogue, is **timing the decisive commit**.

The pattern:
- The army has a high-investment decisive arm (heavy cavalry charge, Mongol main-body commit, Swiss pike attack pace, Hussite wagon-sortie, Ottoman Janissary firing line opening, Aztec elite warrior society breakthrough).
- The decisive commit can be made too early (the charge is wasted on uncommitted ground; the pike attack reaches the enemy before the enemy is fixed; the sortie is delivered before the attacker is exhausted).
- The decisive commit can be made too late (the army has degraded; the enemy has reinforced; the opportunity has passed).
- **The window of effective commit is often minutes, sometimes seconds, and depends on real-time judgment of conditions the commander cannot fully see.**

This holds across cultures, eras, weapon systems, and tactical primitives. Great commanders — Alexander, Hannibal, Belisarius, Subutai, Saladin, Žižka, Gonzalo de Córdoba, Babur — are great primarily because of timing judgment, not because of unique tactical innovations or superior force compositions.

**Battlefield commander effectiveness is concentrated in a small number of timing decisions made under uncertainty with incomplete information.** This is **the** human-skill bottleneck of pre-firearm warfare and accounts for much of the variance in battle outcomes between armies of similar composition.

### M-5: The perfect-system trap

Every doctrinal supremacy carries its own counter. The faster a doctrine matures to peak efficiency in its sweet spot, the faster a counter develops to exploit its blind spots.

Cases:
- Swiss pike at peak (1480s) → exploited at Bicocca (1522) by Spanish-Imperial arquebus + fortification.
- Mamluk *furusiyya* at peak (1450s) → exploited at Marj Dabiq (1516) by Ottoman firearm + artillery.
- English longbow at peak (1410s) → degraded at Patay (1429) by French heavy cavalry catching archers without men-at-arms support.
- Hellenistic sarissa phalanx at peak (270 BC) → exploited at Pydna (168 BC) by Roman maniples exploiting terrain gaps.
- Mongol horse-archer at peak (1240s) → countered at Ain Jalut (1260) by Mamluks using Mongol-pattern tactics with heavier armor.

**The mechanism:** A doctrine refined to peak efficiency in its sweet spot is optimized for a specific set of conditions. The conditions and assumptions on which the doctrine depends become known to opponents and observable in the doctrine's successes. **The very visibility of doctrinal supremacy enables doctrinal counters.**

**Implication for game design (and military theory):** doctrinal supremacy is temporary by nature. The system that wins today has by its winning made itself a target. Defensive doctrinal flexibility — the ability to adapt the system before its peak is exploited — is more valuable than maximum peak efficiency.

### M-6: The cultural-doctrinal compatibility filter

Some doctrinal transitions are blocked at the cultural level, not the technological or institutional level. **Cultures filter the doctrines they can adopt.**

Cases:
- Mamluks could acquire firearms; they could not absorb arquebusier-as-infantry as a status-acceptable role for the mamluk caste (E2).
- Safavid Qizilbash could acquire firearms; their tribal-religious self-conception did not permit fighting alongside or as foot soldiers (E2). Shah Abbas I had to dismantle Qizilbash dominance and build a slave-soldier infantry corps from scratch a century later.
- French chivalric culture in the 14th century could acquire crossbows (they did) but could not permit common crossbowmen to dictate cavalry timing (Crécy 1346, E1).
- Aztec religious-economic system required captures; killing in battle was less institutionally rewarded; the doctrine couldn't shift to lethal combat against the Spanish even when survival demanded it (E4).
- Roman late-republic culture could not absorb professional standing armies as institutionally normal until Marius's reforms broke the property-qualified citizen tradition (B).

**The pattern:** A culture's self-conception — who counts as a warrior, what counts as honorable fighting, what reward structures motivate soldiers, what social hierarchies are reflected in unit composition — constrains the doctrines the culture can absorb. **Cultures unable to absorb the new doctrine lose to cultures that can, or face institutional transformations of the kind that destroy the original culture.**

### M-7: The strategic-political dimension of doctrine

Doctrine is not separable from politics. The military system operates within a strategic-political envelope that determines:
- What armies can be raised (force-generation).
- What campaigns can be sustained (logistics, alliances).
- Whether opponents will defect, ally, or stand neutral (Timur at Ankara, E2; Cortés's Tlaxcalan alliance, E4).
- Whether the army's loyalty holds (Tang at Talas, C; civil war effects on Inca defense, E4).

**Strategic-political preparation can decide tactical outcomes** without a tactical innovation being involved. Timur peeling the Anatolian beys from Bayezid; Cortés exploiting Mexica subject-polity grievances; Mamluk political faction-fighting at Marj Dabiq; the Korean naval-Confucian-officer culture limiting Imjin-era army responsiveness. **A doctrinally adequate force led by a politically failed leadership loses to a doctrinally adequate force led by a politically prepared leadership.**

### M-8: Compound failures explain catastrophic outcomes

Examination of the project's catastrophic-outcome battles (Cannae, Carrhae, Manzikert, Hattin, Liegnitz, Mohács, Marj Dabiq, Pavia, the Spanish conquests) shows that **disasters of this scale are almost never single-cause events**. The failure pattern is typically:

- A doctrinal mismatch (the loser's system is unsuited to the opponent's system); compounded by
- A command failure (premature commit, refusal to retreat, factional non-cooperation); compounded by
- A logistical / environmental factor (waterless ground, broken terrain, weather, supply failure); compounded by
- A political-strategic vulnerability (subject defections, succession crisis, civil war, disease).

**Single-cause failures of this magnitude don't exist in the catalogue.** Even the most one-sided battles involve at least 2–3 of these factors compounding. **Disaster requires a stack.**

Implication: armies survive routine doctrinal mismatches if their command and politics are sound. They survive command failures if their doctrine and politics are sound. They survive political crises if their doctrine and command are sound. **Only when multiple layers fail simultaneously do you get Cannae or Mohács.**

## Part III — The Matchup Matrix (Task 3)

This section addresses Task 3: formation-vs-formation efficacy with generic conclusions.

### Methodology and limitations

Matchups are analyzed on the assumption of **comparable institutional quality, comparable scale, and "fair fight" conditions** (no decisive terrain advantage, no surprise, no command failure). These are approximations. **In reality, ground, command, and timing dominate matchup outcomes more than the formation-class confrontation does.** The matrix below should be read as "what each formation does to each other formation *in isolation*, before terrain / command / timing modifiers" — useful for game-design abstraction but not a substitute for the full doctrine-substrate-opponent triangle.

### Formation classes (rows / columns of the matrix)

For tractability, I collapse the catalogue into 13 formation classes:

| # | Class | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Heavy cavalry shock | Conroi/knight, gendarme, kataphraktos lancer, sipahi heavy, Mongol heavy, Mamluk furusiyya lancer |
| 2 | Light cavalry / horse-archer | Steppe, Mongol light, akıncı, Tatar, Numidian, Parthian/Sassanid skirmisher |
| 3 | Mixed cavalry (heavy + bow) | Byzantine kavallarioi, Sassanid clibanarii, Ottoman timar sipahi, Mamluk (full mode) |
| 4 | Heavy pike infantry | Macedonian sarissa, Swiss Gewalthaufen, Landsknecht, late ashigaru yari, Spanish coronelía pike |
| 5 | Shield-wall heavy infantry | Hoplite phalanx, Roman legion (fixing role), Viking/Anglo-Saxon, Frankish, Byzantine skoutatoi |
| 6 | Sword-and-shield modular infantry | Roman maniple/cohort, Spanish rodeleros |
| 7 | Defensive pike circle | Scottish schiltron (static), Flemish urban militia |
| 8 | Mass missile infantry | English longbow, Han/Song crossbow corps, Janissary bow/arquebus, Byzantine toxotai |
| 9 | Skirmisher light infantry | Velites, peltasts, slingers, azab, Aztec atlatl-men |
| 10 | Wagon-fortress / field fortification | Hussite wagenburg, Ottoman tabur cengi, Babur araba, Mongol/Ming chained-cart laager |
| 11 | War elephants | Hellenistic, Indian chaturanga, Vietnamese, Khmer, Lodi |
| 12 | War chariots | Sumerian, Hittite, Egyptian, Mycenaean, late Achaemenid scythed |
| 13 | Pre-Columbian elite infantry | Aztec eagle/jaguar/cuachic, Inca lancer-club |

### Matrix conventions

For each pairing I give:
- **Winner by default** — the formation expected to prevail in fair-fight conditions.
- **Why** — the doctrinal mechanism.
- **Conditions where the loser can win** — what reverses the matchup.
- **Notable engagements** — historical examples.

I exclude trivial / impossible matchups (chariots vs. wagenburg — they're not contemporaries; pre-Columbian vs. heavy cavalry — covered separately).

### Tier 1: The classical rock-paper-scissors

**Heavy cavalry shock (1) vs. Heavy pike infantry (4)** → **Pike wins by default.**
- Why: Pike-points project at 4 m+; cavalry cannot penetrate without breaking the formation; horses balk at projecting points.
- Reversal conditions: pike formation in motion / disordered / broken terrain breaking ranks / pike formation small enough to be enveloped before contact / pike without missile support being shot to pieces while static.
- Examples: Bannockburn 1314 (pike wins), Marignano 1515 (pike wins narrowly), Pavia 1525 (pike loses to combined firearm + cavalry, not to pure cavalry).

**Heavy pike infantry (4) vs. Mass missile infantry (8)** → **Missile wins by default if pike is static.**
- Why: Pike cannot return fire; pike cannot close with missile troops fast enough; sustained volley fire degrades pike formation casualty rate.
- Reversal conditions: pike with missile support of its own (mixed pike-and-shot); pike that can charge before missile attrition matters (Swiss aggressive attack-pace); broken terrain or close range that negates missile range advantage.
- Examples: Falkirk 1298 (static Scottish schiltron vs. English longbow — schiltron loses); Crécy / Poitiers / Agincourt 1346–1415 (French heavy infantry — partly dismounted men-at-arms behaving like pike-equivalent — vs. English longbow + men-at-arms support; longbow side wins each time).

**Mass missile infantry (8) vs. Heavy cavalry shock (1)** → **Cavalry wins by default if it can close.**
- Why: Missile infantry has rate-of-fire limits; a determined cavalry charge can absorb 2–4 volleys and close to lance range before crippling losses; missile infantry in close combat is defeated.
- Reversal conditions: missile infantry behind obstacles (stakes, slope, water); missile infantry supported by pike or dismounted men-at-arms (the longbow battles); cavalry without route to close (e.g. wagon-fortress in front of missile troops).
- Examples: Patay 1429 (French gendarmes catch English archers without men-at-arms support — archers slaughtered); Carrhae 53 BC reversed (here the cavalry is light, but the principle — cavalry catching missile infantry without protection — applies); Inverkeithing-style ambushes (longbow infantry caught by cavalry on open ground).

### Tier 2: The light cavalry asymmetry

**Light cavalry / horse-archer (2) vs. anything but cavalry** → **Horse-archer wins by default through attrition.**
- Why: Mobility prevents close engagement; ranged attrition over time degrades opponent without commensurate losses.
- Reversal conditions: opponent has own steppe cavalry (Byzantine, Han, Mamluk approach); restrictive terrain (mountains, forest, fortifications); fixed defensive position with adequate supply (e.g., wagon-fortress); firearms with effective range.
- Examples: Scythia 513 BC (Darius can't catch them), Carrhae 53 BC (horse-archer destroys legions), Liegnitz 1241 (Mongol horse-archer + heavy cavalry combo destroys mixed European force).

**Light cavalry / horse-archer (2) vs. Heavy cavalry shock (1)** → **Horse-archer wins by default in open ground.**
- Why: Mobility lets horse-archer choose engagement range and direction; heavy cavalry charge into horse-archer formation has no effect once the archers withdraw; sustained attrition wears the heavy cavalry's horses and riders down.
- Reversal conditions: terrain that forces close engagement; opponent's heavy cavalry catches the horse-archer disordered or pinned; mixed-cavalry formation (heavy + bow) that can both attrite and close.
- Examples: Hattin 1187 (Saladin's mixed force destroys Crusader heavy cavalry); Manzikert 1071 (Seljuk horse-archer + Byzantine command failure).

### Tier 3: The combined arms dominance

**Mixed cavalry (3) vs. Heavy cavalry shock (1)** → **Mixed wins by default.**
- Why: Mixed cavalry can choose to skirmish or to close; heavy cavalry is committed to closing. Mixed cavalry can attrite the heavy at distance, then engage at close range with heavy components of its own.
- Reversal conditions: heavy cavalry catches mixed disordered; mixed cavalry's leader-quality or unit cohesion is much weaker.
- Examples: Saladin's Ayyubid forces routinely defeating Crusader heavy cavalry; Mongol heavy cavalry against opponents who lack mixed-mode capability.

**Wagon-fortress (10) vs. anything mobile-attacking** → **Wagon-fortress wins by default.**
- Why: Provides fortified base for firearms / missile fire; cannot be overrun by cavalry; mass infantry attack against wagon-fortress takes prohibitive casualties.
- Reversal conditions: starvation (wagon-fortress cannot move; siege-it-out works); flanking around the wagon-fortress; artillery superior to the fortress's own firepower; ambush before the wagons are chained.
- Examples: Hussite victories at Vítkov, Sudoměř, Domažlice; Panipat 1526; Mohács 1526; Ottoman engagements throughout the 16th century.

**Heavy cavalry shock (1) vs. Wagon-fortress (10)** → **Wagon-fortress wins decisively.**
- Why: Cavalry cannot displace chained wagons; charge breaks against the wagon-line; firearm/missile fire from inside the fortress devastates the charge.
- Reversal conditions: very few; this is one of the most one-sided matchups in pre-firearm history. Possibly: cavalry that maneuvers around the fortress and attacks supply, forcing the fortress to move and break.
- Examples: Mohács 1526 (Hungarian heavy cavalry charges Ottoman tabur cengi and is destroyed); Panipat 1526.

### Tier 4: The shield-wall / pike-circle defensive systems

**Shield-wall (5) vs. Heavy cavalry shock (1)** → **Shield-wall wins by default if it holds.**
- Why: Tight shield-and-spear formation cannot be penetrated by cavalry charge; horses balk; riders are pulled down or speared.
- Reversal conditions: shield-wall on bad ground or broken; shield-wall worn down by sustained missile fire (the Hastings pattern); shield-wall flanked or cycled by combined arms.
- Examples: Hastings 1066 (shield-wall holds for hours, eventually attrited; cavalry alone could not have broken it); Stamford Bridge 1066.

**Shield-wall (5) vs. Mass missile infantry (8)** → **Missile wins by default through attrition.**
- Why: Shield-wall is static; cannot reach missile troops; sustained volley fire eventually opens gaps.
- Reversal conditions: shield-wall has its own missile arm; shield-wall can charge before attrition matters; shield-wall on ground that breaks missile line-of-sight.
- Examples: Falkirk 1298 (functionally a shield-wall — the schiltron — loses to longbow); Hastings later phases (longbow attrition eventually contributes).

**Schiltron (7) vs. Heavy cavalry** → **Schiltron wins by default.** (Same as pike vs. cavalry; the schiltron is a pike-circle.)

**Schiltron (7) vs. Mass missile infantry** → **Missile wins by default (severe).** Static schiltron is the worst-case static target.
- Examples: Falkirk 1298.

### Tier 5: The modular Roman / Spanish system

**Sword-and-shield modular infantry (6) vs. Heavy pike infantry (4)** → **Modular wins by default on terrain not perfectly flat.**
- Why: Pike phalanx cannot maneuver after committing; gaps open between blocks on broken ground; modular infantry pours into gaps; pike is useless laterally.
- Reversal conditions: perfectly flat open ground (pike can maintain monolithic line); modular infantry caught in open before maneuvering room; pike with effective screening force or supporting cavalry that protects flanks.
- Examples: Pydna 168 BC (Roman maniple destroys Macedonian phalanx on broken ground); Magnesia 190 BC (similar pattern).

**Sword-and-shield modular infantry (6) vs. Heavy cavalry shock (1)** → **Cavalry wins by default if it can close.**
- Why: Cavalry mass + speed against unbraced infantry breaks them.
- Reversal conditions: infantry on prepared ground; infantry behind obstacles; infantry can volley fire (Roman pilum on incoming cavalry); cavalry attacking uphill.
- Examples: Cannae 216 BC (Carthaginian Numidian cavalry + Spanish heavy cavalry vs. Roman cavalry, then enveloping Roman infantry); Roman wins over barbarian cavalry are mostly Roman cavalry winning the flanks, not Roman infantry resisting cavalry charges.

### Tier 6: Skirmishers, elephants, and chariots

**Skirmisher light infantry (9) vs. Heavy infantry of any kind** → **Skirmishers win by default through attrition** (in open ground without missile defense).
- Why: Same logic as horse-archer asymmetry, scaled down.
- Reversal conditions: heavy infantry has missile support that out-ranges or out-volumes skirmishers; restrictive terrain; skirmishers caught in pursuit by mounted forces.
- Examples: Sphacteria 425 BC (Spartan hoplites vs. Athenian light troops on rocky ground — Spartans worn down and surrender); various Hellenistic and Roman engagements with peltasts.

**War elephants (11) vs. Most infantry (early period)** → **Elephants win by default if infantry has no countermeasures.**
- Why: Horses panic, infantry morale collapses, shock + missile platforms from howdahs.
- Reversal conditions: trained infantry that doesn't panic (Roman maniples at Zama 202 BC); caltrops, fire, lanes opened for elephants to pass through; missile fire panicking the elephants themselves (Hydaspes 326 BC).
- Examples: Hydaspes 326 BC (Alexander defeats Porus's elephants); Zama 202 BC (Romans open lanes); Panipat 1526 (firearm fire panics elephants disastrously).

**War elephants (11) vs. Mass missile / firearms** → **Missile / firearms win decisively.**
- Why: Elephants panic under missile fire and trample their own side.
- Examples: Hydaspes 326 BC; Panipat 1526. **After ~1526, war elephants are no longer a battle-deciding arm.**

**War chariots (12) vs. Trained heavy infantry** → **Infantry wins by default.**
- Why: Chariots can be opened lanes for, archers can shoot drivers, and disordered chariots are vulnerable.
- Reversal conditions: chariots used as massed mobile missile platforms against unprepared infantry; chariots in their proper era (Bronze Age, when no countermeasures yet exist).
- Examples: Gaugamela 331 BC (Persian scythed chariots are useless against Macedonian phalanx). Persian chariots were obsolete by their last documented use.

### Tier 7: Pre-Columbian special cases

**Pre-Columbian elite infantry (13) vs. Spanish combined arms (heavy cavalry shock + steel + arquebus + war dogs)** → **Spanish-with-allied-indigenous-mass wins decisively.**
- Why: Doctrinal mismatch (capture-priority vs. kill-priority); weapon asymmetry; cavalry as unprecedented arm; epidemic disease as concurrent factor; political fracture exploited.
- Reversal conditions: Spanish without indigenous allies and without disease backdrop probably fail; restrictive terrain (the Spanish-Maya wars in Yucatán dragged on for decades; the Itzá kingdom didn't fall until 1697).
- Examples: Cortés 1519–1521; Pizarro 1532–1533.

**Pre-Columbian elite infantry (13) vs. similar pre-Columbian opponent** → **Roughly even, decided by numbers, terrain, command, alliance.**
- Examples: Aztec vs. Tlaxcalan (chronic Flower War without decisive resolution); Aztec vs. Tarascan (Tarascan defensive victories repeatedly).

### Generic conclusions from the matchup matrix

**Conclusion 1: No formation wins all matchups.** Every formation class has matchups in which it loses by default and matchups in which it wins. The "best" formation is the one whose matchups span its enemy menu most favorably — which is to say, **combined-arms forces dominate because they have favorable matchups across the widest range of opponents.**

**Conclusion 2: The rock-paper-scissors of pike-cavalry-missile is real but qualified by terrain and command.** In symmetric "fair fight" conditions, pike beats cavalry, cavalry beats missile, missile beats pike. In actual battles, terrain or command quality often dominate this default — Swiss pike beats cavalry, but Swiss pike attacking uphill against fortified arquebus wins less than half the time (Bicocca).

**Conclusion 3: Combined arms beats mono-culture except in specific terrain.** Mongol vs. European feudal: combined Mongol arms wins on open steppe; loses or stalemates in mountains/forests/Vietnamese terrain. Spanish coronelía vs. Swiss pike: combined arms wins on prepared ground; Swiss pike wins in open meeting engagements where charge tempo dominates.

**Conclusion 4: Wagon-fortress + firearms is the dominant late-period formation against all pre-firearm combatants.** From Hussite (1420s) through Panipat (1526), the wagon-fortress + missile fire + cavalry-on-flanks combination wins virtually every engagement where it is properly deployed against a non-firearm-integrated opponent. The combination is the highest matchup-coverage formation in the catalogue.

**Conclusion 5: Steppe horse-archery is the dominant pre-gunpowder asymmetric formation.** From ~700 BC to ~1500 AD, horse-archer doctrine on open terrain defeats most sedentary doctrines. Counters require terrain restriction, mirror-cavalry, or — eventually — gunpowder.

**Conclusion 6: The decisive arm is usually the *fixing* arm's complement.** Across hammer-and-anvil engagements, the anvil (infantry, pike, fortification) is necessary but rarely sufficient; the hammer (cavalry, sortie, flanking reserve) usually delivers the decisive moment. **An army with a strong fixing force but no decisive striker grinds; an army with a strong striker but no fixing force commits prematurely and is consumed.**

**Conclusion 7: Single-arm matchups poorly model real warfare.** Real battles involve multi-arm interactions, terrain, command, and timing simultaneously. The matrix above is a useful abstraction but should not be read as predictive of actual battle outcomes. **Battles are won by armies that achieve favorable local matchups across their entire front simultaneously**, not by armies that have one "best" matchup.

### Composite system rankings (synthesizing across matchups)

Based on the breadth of favorable matchups and historical performance, the late-period composite systems rank (approximately):

1. **Ottoman mature system** (~1450–1566) — combined Janissary firearm + sipahi cavalry + tabur cengi + artillery + akıncı + logistics + slave-soldier elite. Best matchup coverage in the catalogue.
2. **Spanish coronelía / early tercio** — comparable doctrinal sophistication at smaller scale.
3. **Mongol decimal-organization combined arms at peak** (~1230s) — best matchup coverage for the pre-firearm era; loses to Mamluk-mirror only on specific terrain.
4. **Roman mature imperial legion + auxilia** (1st–3rd c. AD) — best matchup coverage of the classical era; the durable institutional case.
5. **Babur's hybrid (Timurid cavalry + Hussite-pattern wagons + Anatolian-Ottoman firearms)** — small force, won decisively at Panipat.
6. **Byzantine combined arms at peak** (10th c.) — Phokas-era integrated infantry + cavalry + bow.
7. **Swiss pike (offensive)** — peak doctrine 1480s–1510s, then declines.
8. **Mamluk *furusiyya* combined cavalry** — peak through ~1450s, then exposed to firearms.
9. **English longbow + dismounted men-at-arms** — peak doctrine 1340s–1410s, then declines.
10. **Hussite wagenburg** — peak through 1430s, doctrinally durable in adapted form.

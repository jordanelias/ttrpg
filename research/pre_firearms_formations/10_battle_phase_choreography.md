# Chunk F2 — Battle-Phase Choreography Synthesis (Task 4)

**Scope:** The granular synthesis of unit position, trajectory, timing, and speed through the phases of pre-firearm battle. Distilled from the phase walkthroughs documented across the catalogue (Gaugamela, Cannae, Carrhae, Hattin, Arsuf, Liegnitz, Hastings, Bannockburn, Crécy, Poitiers, Agincourt, Grandson, Murten, Nancy, Bicocca, Pavia, Cerignola, Nicopolis, Varna, Mohács, Ankara, Marj Dabiq, Chaldiran, Panipat, Tumu, Yarmouk, Kadesh, Sempach, Hydaspes).

This is the highest-value deliverable in the project for game-design extraction. The catalogue chunks describe *what* doctrines existed; this chunk describes *what units did, where they stood, where they moved, how fast, and when*.

The structure:
1. **The generic 8-phase framework** — the standard sequence of pre-firearm battle.
2. **Per-phase unit choreography** — what each formation class does at each phase, with position, trajectory, timing, and speed.
3. **Reference tables** — speed, range, frontage, timing.
4. **Battle archetypes** — how different doctrines arrange the phases.
5. **Failure modes per phase** — what goes wrong, when, and why.
6. **Game-design extraction** — what mechanics would capture these dynamics for Valoria.

---

## Part I — The Generic 8-Phase Framework

Pre-firearm battles, despite enormous doctrinal variation, follow a recognizable phase sequence. Specific battles compress, expand, or omit phases — but the underlying structure recurs.

| # | Phase | Typical duration | Defining activity |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | **Approach / pre-battle maneuver** | Hours to days | Strategic positioning; ground selection; intelligence; refusing or imposing engagement |
| 1 | **Deployment** | 30 min – 3 hours | Order-of-march converted to order-of-battle; formations assembled; positions assigned |
| 2 | **Skirmish / screening** | 30 min – 2 hours | Light troops engage; reconnaissance by fire; opening missile exchange |
| 3 | **Missile attrition** | Highly variable (sometimes absent, sometimes hours) | Massed missile arm degrades enemy formation at range |
| 4 | **Main contact** | 30 min – 4+ hours | Heavy formations engage; close combat begins |
| 5 | **Decisive commit** | Minutes to ~30 min | The reserve / cavalry / fresh force is committed to break the deadlock |
| 6 | **Decision / collapse** | Minutes to ~1 hour | One side breaks; cohesion fails; rout begins |
| 7 | **Pursuit** | Hours to days | Mounted arm runs down fleeing enemy; casualty multiplication |

Total combat-time for a decisive battle is typically **2–6 hours** of phases 2–6, plus deployment before and pursuit after. Hastings (~9 hours of active combat) and Bouvines (~3 hours) bracket the typical range. Yarmouk (six days) is exceptional.

Some battles never reach phase 5–6 — they end at phase 1 (the army that arrives second and finds the first deployed simply refuses engagement) or at phase 3 (missile attrition produces such asymmetric loss that one side disengages without main contact). Some battles compress phases 1–4 into a single confused engagement (Liegnitz, Mohi, ambushes generally).

## Part II — Per-Phase Unit Choreography

### Phase 0 — Approach / Pre-Battle Maneuver

This is operational rather than tactical, but it determines what tactical conditions will exist.

**What happens:**
- Armies move toward expected engagement, sometimes for days or weeks.
- Scouts (light cavalry, akıncı, hobelars, mounted serjeants) range 1–20 km ahead and on flanks, reporting enemy position and ground.
- Engineers (Mongol, Roman, Byzantine, Ottoman) construct bridges, roads, fortified camps, or prepare battle ground.
- Commanders attempt to **impose engagement** on advantageous ground or **refuse engagement** on disadvantageous ground.

**Position and trajectory:**
- Marching order is column, typically: vanguard (light cavalry + elite infantry detachment), main body, baggage train, rearguard.
- Distances between elements: 1–5 km typical; baggage train sometimes 10+ km behind the vanguard.
- Direction is toward the planned engagement area, with multiple route options for tactical flexibility.

**Timing:**
- Average operational pace: **15–25 km/day** for European medieval armies; **25–40 km/day** for well-led classical armies on prepared roads; **40–60 km/day** for Mongol force in core territory; **forced marches** up to 100 km/day for elite cavalry (Mongol couriers via *yam* relay, Crusader fighting-march in emergency, Roman emergency reinforcement).

**Speed (units within column):**
- Heavy infantry sustained march: 4–5 km/h with brief halts.
- Cavalry sustained march: 5–7 km/h (walking gait to spare horses).
- Mongol cavalry with remounts: 10–15 km/h sustained, possible faster bursts.

**Failure modes:**
- Wrong ground (Hattin: army out-of-water; Tumu: army without resupply).
- Surprise (Liegnitz: army not deployed; Bannockburn: English caught between burns; Talas: Karluk defection mid-march).
- Foraging dispersal (Mongol horse-archers love this — an army spread out for forage is doctrinally already half-defeated).

### Phase 1 — Deployment

The transition from order-of-march to order-of-battle. **This is when the army "becomes" a battle force** rather than a column.

**What happens:**
- Vanguard arrives at deployment area; halts; begins forming line.
- Main body arrives in sequence; takes assigned positions (left wing, center, right wing, reserve).
- Light troops form skirmish screen ~100–300 m forward.
- Missile troops (longbow, crossbow, sparabara, Janissary) form behind or interspersed with heavy infantry, with prepared positions where time permits (stakes at Agincourt, ditches at Cerignola, wagons at Panipat).
- Cavalry positions on wings or in reserve.
- Commander establishes command position (typically center-rear or with elite reserve).

**Position and trajectory:**
- Center: heavy infantry, dismounted men-at-arms, pike block, Janissaries, sparabara, etc. — **the fixing force**.
- Wings: cavalry (heavy and/or light), with mixed-mode cavalry on the more critical flank.
- Reserve: elite shock arm (Companions, household knights, *cuachicqueh*, Mongol heavy cavalry) held centrally or behind the line.
- Frontage: scales with army size. 1,000 men ≈ 200–300 m frontage (4–5 men per meter typical density). 20,000 men ≈ 2–3 km frontage. Mongol forces often deploy on wider frontage (3–5 km) to enable encirclement; Roman / Spartan / Macedonian forces deploy on tighter frontages (1–2 km) to maximize depth.
- Depth: hoplite 8 (sometimes deeper), Macedonian sarissa 16, Swiss pike 16–40, Roman maniple/cohort 6–10 effective depth, Mongol 5, English longbow archers 4–6 deep in herse formations.

**Timing:**
- Deployment from column to line typically takes **1–3 hours** for armies of 10,000–50,000.
- Slower for less-trained armies (4+ hours).
- Faster for elite professional forces with rehearsed drill (well under 1 hour for tagmatic Byzantine or Mongol contingents).

**Speed (movement during deployment):**
- Walking pace; no charging movement. Heavy formations move at 3–4 km/h while forming line.
- Light troops move faster (5–7 km/h) to take screening position quickly.

**Failure modes:**
- Caught mid-deployment (Mohács: Hungarian late-arrivals never reach proper positions; Pavia: French troops caught in their tents).
- Wrong deployment for the actual battle (Burgundian artillery facing the wrong direction at Nancy 1477).
- Misalignment between divisions (Macedonian phalanx gaps on broken ground at Pydna).
- Subordinate refusal to take assigned position (Andronikos Doukas at Manzikert).

### Phase 2 — Skirmish / Screening

Both armies test the other while developing intelligence about strength and disposition.

**What happens:**
- Light cavalry (akıncı, Numidian, hobelar, prokoursatores, jinetes) ride between the lines, exchanging missile fire with the opposing skirmish screen.
- Light infantry (velites, peltasts, slingers, ashigaru bowmen, Aztec atlatl-men, azab) advance to 50–100 m of the enemy and engage with missile fire.
- The skirmish screen "feels out" the enemy's strength, missile capability, and weak points.
- Skirmishers withdraw before main contact, typically through gaps in the heavy infantry line.

**Position and trajectory:**
- Skirmishers operate in a **forward zone 50–300 m in front of the main line**.
- Light cavalry ranges out to 500–1000 m, particularly on wings.
- Movement is fluid — skirmishers move continuously, advancing to shoot then withdrawing, never standing in place.

**Timing:**
- Skirmish phase typically **30 min – 2 hours**.
- Sometimes prolonged (Yarmouk's days-long engagement begins with extended skirmish).
- Sometimes brief or absent (ambush, encirclement, surprise attack).

**Speed:**
- Light cavalry skirmish: trot to canter, **12–20 km/h** in continuous motion.
- Light infantry: jogging pace, **6–10 km/h**.

**Failure modes:**
- Premature skirmisher retreat exposes the main line to a charge it isn't ready for.
- Skirmishers caught by cavalry while still forward (peltasts can usually outrun heavy infantry but not cavalry).
- Skirmish phase exposing the main commander's position (Crécy: French cavalry charges *through* their own retreating Genoese, destroying both).

### Phase 3 — Missile Attrition

In doctrines with significant massed missile capability (English longbow, Han/Song crossbow, Mongol horse-archer, Ottoman Janissary, sparabara, Hussite/Babur firearm-and-bow combination), this is a major phase. In doctrines without it, this phase is brief or absent.

**What happens:**
- Massed missile fire from prepared positions or formed missile units.
- The opposing army either absorbs (heavy armor, shields, fortification) or returns fire (if it has missile capability of its own).
- Sustained attrition. Casualties accumulate gradually but compound over time.
- Casualty rates depend on protection: armored heavy infantry might lose 5–10% per hour of sustained fire; unarmored or partially armored troops 20–40% per hour.

**Position and trajectory:**
- Missile troops in formed ranks (longbow 4–6 deep in herse; sparabara 9 ranks behind wall-shields; Janissary 4 deep behind tabur cengi; crossbow lines in alternating fire-and-reload). Position is **stationary or slowly advancing**.
- Target army is forced to either advance (to close range and end the attrition) or withdraw (admit defeat without main contact).

**Timing:**
- Variable: 30 min to several hours.
- Mongol horse-archer attrition can extend through an entire day at full operational scale.
- Longbow attrition typically 30 min to 1 hour before the opposing line either commits to close or breaks.

**Speed:**
- Missile arm largely stationary. Movement of ~1 km/h if advancing slowly under fire.
- Rate of fire varies: longbow 6–10 arrows/min (sustained), composite bow 8–12, crossbow 1–2, early arquebus 1–2, sparabara archers 6–8.
- **Volume of fire at 6,000 longbowmen × 8 arrows/min = ~50,000 arrows/min** — saturating any frontage they face.

**Failure modes:**
- Missile attrition without ability to follow up (longbow without supporting men-at-arms vs. fast-closing cavalry — Patay 1429).
- Insufficient ammunition (Hastings end-phase: English archers running out of arrows; the Crusader supply limits at Hattin).
- Missile platform overrun before the engagement can develop (Carrhae reversal would have required this; the Romans didn't manage it).
- Mass missile fire that hits friendlies in front (Crécy: Genoese disorder).

### Phase 4 — Main Contact

The heavy lines meet. **This is the iconic image of pre-firearm battle**, but it is often *not* where the battle is decided.

**What happens:**
- Heavy infantry lines close to contact, typically at walking-to-trotting pace.
- Pike formations level pikes; sword-and-shield formations close shields; cavalry on wings positions for the decisive commit.
- Main lines engage at contact. Fighting is along the entire frontage.
- Casualty rate is high but distributed — the front rank takes most of it; ranks behind take less; the formation degrades from the front.
- **The engagement is often prolonged.** Many battles see the main lines locked in combat for an hour or more without decisive movement.

**Position and trajectory:**
- Heavy infantry: stationary or slowly moving (~1–2 km/h while in contact).
- Cavalry: positioning for commit, often moving laterally to find the right moment / angle of attack.
- Reserve: held in position behind the line, watching for the moment to commit.
- Missile troops: shoot over heads of friendly infantry into the enemy rear ranks where possible; otherwise fire on flanks.

**Timing:**
- Main contact often **1–3 hours** in major battles.
- Some battles have multiple distinct main-contact engagements (Hastings: 6+ separate cavalry / infantry / archer cycles); some have one prolonged engagement (Crécy: continuous French commitment through the afternoon).

**Speed:**
- Heavy infantry in contact: <1 km/h forward movement; often static or yielding ground in small increments.
- Cavalry on wings during main-contact phase: lateral or screening movement at trot.

**Critical sub-dynamics:**
- **Rotation / line relief.** Roman manipular / cohort line replaces fresh troops mid-engagement. Macedonian phalanx cannot. **The army with line-relief capability fights longer effectively than one without.**
- **Local breakthroughs.** Pike-points or wedge formations can punch holes in the opposing line. The breakthrough either widens (decisive) or is contained (battle continues).
- **Morale check timing.** Front-rank casualties propagate morale effects backward. Sustained heavy losses + visible command failure + flanking pressure = morale collapse becomes possible.

**Failure modes:**
- Premature retreat: army interprets unfavorable position as defeat and breaks before the decision is forced (Grandson 1476: Burgundian retreat order interpreted as rout).
- Premature commit of the reserve: nothing left for the decisive phase (Pavia 1525: Francis I's gendarmes commit too early, blocking French artillery).
- Failure to commit reserve at all: army grinds to attritional defeat (some descriptions of Hastings — Harold has no real reserve).
- One wing breaks before the other engages: the broken wing exposes the rest of the army to flanking commit by the enemy (Cannae cascade).

### Phase 5 — Decisive Commit

**The most important single phase of pre-firearm battle.** The phase where the battle is won. Lasts only minutes, but everything before it has been setup and everything after it is consequence.

**What happens:**
- The commander identifies that the conditions for the decisive commit have been reached: the enemy is degraded enough to break, the right flank is open, the reserve is positioned, the moment is now.
- The reserve / cavalry / fresh force is committed.
- **The commit must be aimed at a specific objective**: enemy command position, exposed flank, gap in enemy line, retreat route.
- The committed force charges, attacks, or maneuvers at maximum tempo.

**Position and trajectory:**
- For a heavy-cavalry commit: the conroi / wedge / lance / kataphraktoi formation accelerates from walk to trot to gallop over ~100–300 m, striking the chosen target at maximum velocity.
- For a Mongol *tulughma*: the encircling force has already moved into position during phases 2–4; phase 5 is when it closes the encirclement (cavalry now strikes from rear or far flank, simultaneously with frontal pressure).
- For a Swiss pike commit: not a separate reserve; the entire square is the commit, with timing being the speed of the advance pulling the enemy's reaction out of phase.
- For a Hussite wagon-sortie: cavalry and flailmen exit through prepared gaps and attack the now-exhausted attacker.

**Timing:**
- The commit window is often **2–10 minutes**. Late by 30 minutes = missed; early by 30 minutes = wasted.
- This is the human-skill bottleneck identified in F1's M-4 (the discipline-timing axis).

**Speed:**
- Heavy cavalry final charge: gallop, **30–40 km/h** for the last 30–50 m.
- Earlier approach in the charge: trot to canter, **12–20 km/h**.
- Encircling cavalry closing the trap: trot to canter, **15–20 km/h** sustained over the closing 500–1000 m.
- Swiss pike at attack pace: **5–7 km/h** sustained, the *cohesion* being the asset rather than peak speed.

**Critical mechanics:**
- **The single-shot character of the heavy-cavalry charge** (T-26): cavalry is functionally spent after contact. The commit either decides the battle or wastes the cavalry for the rest of the engagement.
- **The wedge formation concentrates impact**: a 20-front × 12-deep kataphraktoi wedge strikes the enemy on a 20-man frontage, then widens behind to exploit the breach. Pure mass on a small area.
- **The Mongol commit is multi-directional**: the encircling force strikes from multiple directions simultaneously, denying the enemy a clear front to defend.

**Failure modes:**
- Premature commit (Hospitaller charge at Arsuf — barely converted to success by Richard's instant commitment of full force; would have been catastrophe otherwise).
- Late commit (Manzikert: Andronikos Doukas's withdrawal denies the Byzantine commit ever happening; Hattin Phase 4: Crusader infantry breaks before the cavalry commit can be made).
- Wrong direction (gendarme charges at Crécy and Agincourt: against the wrong target, into prepared killing ground).
- Charge through own troops (Crécy: French cavalry rides over their own retreating Genoese crossbowmen).
- Failure of the *fixing* force to fix (Cannae: the Roman center is fixed too well; it advances into the trap. The Carthaginian wings then commit. The fixing fails by working too well — Roman pressure breaks the Punic center, but the wings now fold inward).

### Phase 6 — Decision / Collapse

The losing side's cohesion fails. **Most of a battle's casualties typically come from this phase and Phase 7 (pursuit) combined — not from Phases 4–5.**

**What happens:**
- Local breakthroughs in the losing army's line compound: troops who see their neighbors break themselves break.
- The morale field collapses. Individual decisions to flee become collective.
- Officers attempt to rally; sometimes successful at small scale; rarely successful at army scale.
- Command authority degrades — orders cannot be passed; commanders cannot see what's happening; the army becomes an unorganized mass.

**Position and trajectory:**
- Losing army: dispersing in many directions, but typically away from the enemy and along the line of retreat.
- Winning army: pressing forward, with cavalry pursuing and infantry consolidating ground.
- Surviving cohesive units of the losing army: forming "last stand" knots — typically at high ground, at fortified points, or around the army's commander.

**Timing:**
- Collapse phase typically **15 min – 1 hour** from first major breakage to full disintegration.
- Sometimes briefer (Bouvines: Otto's banner falls and the imperial center collapses within minutes).
- Sometimes prolonged (Hastings: the shield-wall around Harold's body persists for hours after the main line breaks).

**Speed:**
- Routing infantry: running, **8–12 km/h** (faster than they ever moved during the battle).
- Routing cavalry: gallop, **30+ km/h**, but horse condition limits sustained speed.

**Critical sub-dynamics:**
- **Cohesive units that don't break can sometimes withdraw intact.** Spartan rearguard at Plataea, Templar / Hospitaller rearguard at Hattin, Roman center at Carrhae for a while.
- **Command-position survival vs. death** changes the political consequences. Battle losses with the commander captured (Edward II at Bannockburn — he escapes; Bayezid I at Ankara — captured; King John II of France at Poitiers — captured; Francis I at Pavia — captured; King Louis II at Mohács — killed) become political-strategic catastrophes beyond the military loss.

**Failure modes (these are mostly for the winning side):**
- Winning side breaks discipline to loot prematurely (Persians at Marathon; Mongol baggage was looted at one of the Mamluk-Mongol engagements, allowing Mongol regroup).
- Winning side over-extends pursuit and is counter-attacked by reserves (Manzikert: Byzantine right-wing victory not consolidated, then central collapse).
- Winning side miscalculates the threat from broken enemy (Henry V at Agincourt: the prisoner-killing order responds to a fear of renewed enemy attack — debatable in retrospect).

### Phase 7 — Pursuit

The cavalry runs down the routed enemy. **This is where the casualty ratio gets extreme.**

**What happens:**
- Light and heavy cavalry pursue routed enemy.
- Infantry consolidates the battlefield, secures prisoners, collects equipment.
- Pursuit continues until horses tire, terrain prevents continuation, or night falls.
- In some doctrines (Mongol), pursuit is doctrinally institutionalized and can extend over days at strategic scale.

**Position and trajectory:**
- Cavalry pursuit fans out in the direction of the enemy retreat — typically along multiple parallel routes if the retreat is over wide ground.
- Pursuing forces try to **get ahead of the retreat** to cut off escape (closes the encirclement at strategic scale).

**Timing:**
- Tactical pursuit: hours.
- Operational pursuit: days (Mongol; Roman after Cannae's reverse, etc.).
- Strategic pursuit (e.g., Mongol pursuit of Hungarian survivors after Mohi): weeks.

**Speed:**
- Cavalry sustained pursuit: trot to canter, **12–18 km/h** sustainable.
- Pursued infantry: running and tiring, **8 km/h** initially declining to walking pace within an hour.

**Casualty asymmetry:**
- Most pre-firearm battles produce winning-side casualties of 5–15% and losing-side casualties of 30–60%+.
- The disproportion is almost entirely produced in phase 6–7 (collapse and pursuit), not in 4–5 (main contact and commit).
- **An army that disengages cleanly without breaking suffers proportionally low casualties; an army that breaks under pursuit suffers catastrophic ones.**

## Part IV — Battle Archetypes

Different doctrines arrange the phases differently. Six common archetypes from the catalogue:

### Archetype 1: The classical decisive battle (Macedonian / Roman / Hellenistic)

Phases 0–7 sequenced cleanly. Both armies deploy in line, skirmish, missile-exchange briefly, engage main lines, the deciding commit on a wing decides, opposing army breaks, pursuit.

Examples: Gaugamela, Cannae, Pharsalus, Magnesia, Pydna.

### Archetype 2: The defensive attritional battle (English longbow / Spanish Cerignola)

Defending army deploys on chosen ground with obstacles. Attacker engages; long missile attrition phase; attacker's morale or cohesion fails before main contact develops fully; defender counter-attacks the broken attacker.

Examples: Crécy, Poitiers, Agincourt, Cerignola, Murten.

### Archetype 3: The maneuver-decision battle (Mongol / Steppe)

Phases 2–5 overlap: skirmish, attrition, encirclement maneuver, and decisive commit happen simultaneously across the battlespace rather than sequentially. Main-contact phase often telescoped or absent — the enemy is degraded by attrition and encirclement before it can effectively engage.

Examples: Liegnitz, Mohi, Manzikert, Carrhae, Hattin (partly).

### Archetype 4: The set-piece pike-and-shot transition (early 16th century)

Phase 1 (deployment) extended into fortification preparation; Phase 3 (missile attrition) elevated to *the* central phase, with arquebus + artillery as the decisive arm; Phase 4 (main contact) often absent or one-sided.

Examples: Cerignola, Bicocca, Mohács, Panipat.

### Archetype 5: The siege-as-battle (Constantinople 1453, various)

A different structure entirely: phases extend over weeks. Phase 1 (deployment) = artillery emplacement, mining, circumvallation. Phase 3 (attrition) = continuous bombardment. Phase 4 (main contact) = breach assaults. Phase 5 (commit) = final storm. Phase 7 (pursuit) = sack of the city.

Examples: Constantinople 1453, various Crusader and Reconquista sieges.

### Archetype 6: The Hussite wagenburg defensive

Phase 1 (deployment) = wagon-fortress assembly. Phase 2–4 telescoped — the attacker engages the wagon-line; missile fire kills attackers; the attack breaks; cavalry sortie counter-attacks. Phase 5 (commit) is the sortie. Phase 6–7 standard pursuit.

Examples: Vítkov Hill, Sudoměř, Panipat (variant), Mohács (variant).

## Part VI — Game-Design Extraction for Valoria

Since the project is research for Valoria, this section translates the phase choreography into game-mechanic implications. The translation is offered as observations, not as design specification (per the project-owner contract: "Jordan specifies; Claude executes" — these are observations Jordan may or may not specify into the game).

### Observation 1: The phase structure is the player's UX scaffolding

Pre-firearm battles in real history are not "one combat" — they are a sequence of distinct phases with different unit roles, timing, and stakes. A game that compresses everything into a single melee misses the dynamic. **Phases as discrete game-states** — approach, deployment, skirmish, attrition, main contact, commit, collapse, pursuit — give the player different decisions at different moments.

Each phase has its own "decision space": deployment is about positioning; skirmish is about reconnaissance; attrition is about timing the close; commit is about the single most important action of the battle.

### Observation 2: The decisive-commit window is the *one* mechanic that captures pre-firearm tactics

If a single mechanic must represent the battle, it's the **timing of the decisive commit**. Everything before it is setup; everything after is consequence. The player should feel that:

- They have a high-investment decisive arm (cavalry / sortie / reserve / breakthrough unit).
- Committing it too early wastes it.
- Committing it too late misses the window.
- The window is short (game-time scaled).
- The window depends on conditions the player must read in real time (enemy posture, friendly degradation, terrain access).

This single dynamic captures **M-4 (the discipline-timing axis)** — the universal command skill across all doctrines.

### Observation 3: The rock-paper-scissors needs to exist but be qualified

Pike-cavalry-missile rock-paper-scissors is real (per the matchup matrix). **Implementing it as pure RPS over-simplifies.** The conditions matter:

- Pike beats cavalry, BUT pike on broken ground loses cohesion.
- Cavalry beats missile, BUT cavalry against missile in fortified position can't close.
- Missile beats pike, BUT missile against pike with its own missile support is neutralized.

The qualifications are where the game-design depth lives. A pure RPS game is boring; a conditional RPS game with terrain / position / supporting-arm modifiers is interesting.

### Observation 4: Combined arms should beat mono-culture by matchup coverage

Per the matchup matrix and M-2 (Institution > tactics > weapons), combined-arms forces win by having favorable matchups across the wider opponent menu. The game should reward force-composition that covers multiple unit-class threats, not force-composition that maxes a single arm. **"Spam pike" or "spam cavalry" should lose to "balanced force with appropriate doctrine"** — the way it historically did.

### Observation 5: The fixing/decisive duality should be visible as roles

Hammer-and-anvil structure runs through almost every successful doctrine (M-4 / T-24). Some units should feel like *anvils* — they take damage, hold position, occupy enemy attention. Other units should feel like *hammers* — they're the closing-the-deal arm, fragile if used wrong, decisive if used right.

The role-distinction lets the player understand why they have multiple unit types and why losing the hammer is worse than losing the anvil even if numerical losses are similar.

### Observation 6: Terrain dominates symmetric matchups

In real battles, terrain often decides matchups more than unit class. Bannockburn, Hastings, Cerignola, Crécy, Agincourt, Murten, Marignano — all decided in significant part by ground.

The game should make terrain selection and exploitation a major decision. Defensive ground should significantly favor the defender, even if doctrinally inferior. Cavalry on bad ground should lose to infantry it would defeat on flat ground.

### Observation 7: Force-generation as a strategic-level mechanic

Per T-5 / M-2: institutional depth outlasts tactical brilliance. The game should reward investment in **persistent force-generation institutions** (training schools, recruitment systems, supply networks) over **maxing out a single elite unit**. Burgundian-style "build the best army you can and lose it once" should fail strategically; Roman-style "field adequate armies repeatedly" should succeed.

This is a strategic-layer mechanic distinct from tactical-layer mechanics. The game's strategic layer should make institutions visible and investable.

### Observation 8: Doctrine-substrate-opponent triangle as design constraint

Per M-1: every doctrine has a triangle. **In game terms: the player should not be able to slot any doctrine into any context.** Each doctrine should have:

- A substrate requirement (population / economy / technology / institution).
- An opponent profile it works against.
- A cultural-values implication (what counts as victory for this doctrine's society).

This is the most distinctive game-design implication of the project. Most strategy games let the player mix doctrines freely; the historical record suggests doctrines are package deals with the political-economic-cultural systems that produced them.

### Observation 9: Compound-failure stacking

Per M-8: catastrophic outcomes require a stack of failure modes simultaneously. A game with single-cause "you lose" failure conditions doesn't capture historical reality. **Disasters of scale require multiple compounding factors**: a doctrinally mismatched force *plus* command failure *plus* logistical strain *plus* political vulnerability.

This implies: most engagements should produce moderate outcomes; rare engagements with multiple failure-mode stacks should produce catastrophic outcomes. The player can be defeated routinely; they should be destroyed only when multiple things go wrong simultaneously.

### Observation 10: Most casualties come from phases 6–7

Per the failure-mode analysis: 5–15% loss on winning side, 30–60% on losing side, with most losses concentrated in collapse and pursuit. **The game's casualty model should reflect this — fighting itself is dangerous but survivable; routing is devastating.**

Implications for mechanics:
- An army that withdraws in good order should be reusable. A broken army should not be.
- "Decisive battle" should have stronger campaign consequences than "moderate engagement."
- Pursuit should be a separate game-mechanic decision, with its own risks (cavalry committed to pursuit can be ambushed by reserves; pursuit through unfamiliar terrain costs cohesion).

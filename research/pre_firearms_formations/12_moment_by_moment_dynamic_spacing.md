# Moment-by-Moment Dynamic Spacing — Primary-Source Reconstruction

**Scope:** A granular reconstruction of the dynamic relative spacing and timing between two opposing armies through the phases of selected pre-firearm battles, drawing on primary military treatises, manuscripts, and chronicles. The focus is on **measurable, primary-source-cited spatial and temporal information** rather than on doctrinal abstraction (which the prior chunks covered).

**Author note on epistemics:** Primary-source numbers are often unreliable. Ancient army sizes are routinely exaggerated; medieval chronicler distances are imprecise; "the distance of a bowshot" varies by source. Where I cite a measurement, I cite the source and flag the confidence level. Where modern scholarship has revised the primary-source figure, I note it. Where I extrapolate from manual primitives (e.g., calculating frontage from file-spacing × number of files), I flag the extrapolation as derivation, not source data.

**Citation convention:** Author + work + book/chapter when possible. Quotations are kept under 15 words and used only where exact wording matters. The vast majority of source content is paraphrased.

## Part I — Primary Sources Consulted

This is the substantive corpus I draw on for spatial-temporal measurement. Annotations indicate what each source contributes that the others do not.

### Greek and Hellenistic theoretical / drill manuals

- **Xenophon**, *Anabasis* (early 4th c. BC) — march-of-the-Ten-Thousand; gives orderly retreat formations, route columns, hollow-square defensive marching against Persian harassment.
- **Xenophon**, *Hipparchikos* and *On the Cavalry Commander* — Greek cavalry doctrine and training; explicit on file-and-rank spacing for cavalry units, scout intervals, drill evolutions.
- **Asclepiodotus**, *Tactica* (1st c. BC) — the most explicit single source for Hellenistic phalanx file-spacing: open order, compact order, and *synaspismos* with named intervals (covered in Part II).
- **Aelian Tacticus**, *Tactica Theoria* (~AD 100) — Aelian builds on Asclepiodotus and adds drill evolutions: facing-changes, doubling, counter-march mechanics. Heavily influential through Byzantine military literature.
- **Arrian**, *Tactica* (~AD 130s) — Roman-era summary of Greek tactical theory + Roman cavalry drill (the *hippika gymnasia*). Gives cavalry maneuver speeds and procedures.

### Greek and Roman narrative historians (battlefield-level detail)

- **Herodotus**, *Histories* (5th c. BC) — Persian Wars; numbers wildly exaggerated, but positional descriptions usable. Marathon, Plataea.
- **Thucydides**, *Peloponnesian War* — Mantinea, Delium; first explicit description of phalanx right-flank drift (Mantinea 418 BC, Book V).
- **Polybius**, *Histories* (2nd c. BC) — the indispensable source. Book 6 gives detailed Roman army composition and spacing. Book 18 contrasts Macedonian phalanx with Roman manipular system explicitly, with file-spacing measurements. Books 3 and 15 cover Cannae and Zama with positional detail.
- **Diodorus Siculus** — sometimes preserves source material from lost Hellenistic historians.
- **Livy** (1st c. BC) — *Ab Urbe Condita*. Cannae in Book 22. Less mechanically precise than Polybius but more dramatically detailed.
- **Plutarch** (~AD 100) — *Lives*. Often preserves details from lost sources; especially useful for Alexander (Life of Alexander) and Marius/Sulla/Pompey/Caesar.
- **Arrian**, *Anabasis* — the most reliable narrative source for Alexander's campaigns. Gaugamela in Book III.
- **Quintus Curtius Rufus** (1st c. AD) — *Histories of Alexander*. Less reliable than Arrian but preserves alternative details.
- **Caesar**, *Commentaries on the Gallic War* and *Civil War* — eyewitness commander's narrative. Pharsalus in *Civil War* III. Highly mechanical in describing relative positions, distances, and timing.
- **Tacitus** — *Annals*, *Histories*, *Agricola* — Roman imperial-era engagements.
- **Onasander**, *Strategikos* (~AD 50) — Greek general-officer's manual on the duties of the general; pre-battle ceremonial timing, harangue, formation deployment procedure.
- **Frontinus**, *Stratagems* (~AD 90) — a collection of stratagems by category, useful for tactical-primitive identification.
- **Vegetius**, *De Re Militari* (late 4th – early 5th c. AD) — late-Roman / proto-Byzantine compilation; codifies legionary spacing, drill, march procedures. Most-copied military manual in medieval Europe.
- **Ammianus Marcellinus** (4th c. AD) — *Res Gestae*. Eyewitness or near-eyewitness for Adrianople (378 AD); precise on phase choreography.

### Byzantine military manuals

- **Maurice's *Strategikon*** (~AD 600) — the most detailed Byzantine military manual; specifies cavalry deployment in two lines with reserves, names every drill evolution, gives intervals between formations.
- **Leo VI's *Tactica*** (~AD 900) — synthesizes Strategikon material and updates for Macedonian-dynasty practice. Detailed on combined-arms deployment.
- **Nikephoros II Phokas's *Praecepta Militaria*** (~AD 965) — the most operationally specific Byzantine manual. Gives explicit formation dimensions: 504-man infantry hollow-square formation; kataphraktoi wedge of 20-front widening to 64-rear, 12 deep.
- **Sylloge Tacticorum** (~10th c.) — tactical compendium.
- **Anna Komnene**, *Alexiad* (~12th c.) — narrative history with strong military detail on Norman, Pecheneg, and Turkic warfare from Byzantine perspective.

### Islamic / Mamluk furusiyya literature

- **Al-Aqsara'i**, *Nihayat al-Su'l wa-l-Umniyya fi Ta'lim A'mal al-Furusiyya* (~1371) — late-Mamluk training manual; details cavalry drill, lance handling, mounted archery, formation maneuvers. The peak of pre-Ottoman cavalry-doctrine writing.
- **Various 13th–15th c. furusiyya manuals** — preserve drill procedures, training programs, weapons handling. Less battle-narrative than European chronicles.

### Late-medieval European chronicles and manuals

- **William of Poitiers**, *Gesta Guillelmi* (~1070s) — pro-William narrative of Hastings; some positional detail.
- **Bayeux Tapestry** (~1077) — visual primary source for Hastings; not a treatise but illustrates relative positioning of troops.
- **Anonymous**, *Gesta Henrici Quinti* (~1416–1417) — anonymous eyewitness chaplain's account of Agincourt; the most useful single source for the battle's phase choreography.
- **Jean Froissart**, *Chroniques* (late 14th c.) — Crécy, Poitiers, and other 14th-c. Anglo-French engagements; rich in episode but often imprecise on numbers.
- **Jean le Bel**, *Chronique* (mid-14th c.) — earlier and often more sober than Froissart, who used him as a source.
- **Geoffrey le Baker**, *Chronicon* — English Crécy and Poitiers source.
- **Geoffroi de Charny**, *Livre de Chevalerie* (~1350s) — chivalric manual with passages on practical combat.
- **Honoré Bonet (Bouvet)**, *L'Arbre des Batailles* (~1387) — law-of-arms treatise with discussions of battle conduct.
- **Christine de Pizan**, *Livre des Faits d'Armes et de Chevalerie* (~1410) — military treatise drawing on Vegetius and contemporary practice.
- **Jean de Bueil**, *Le Jouvencel* (~1466) — French knightly memoir-manual; the Hundred Years' War from a serving officer's perspective.
- **Philippe de Commynes**, *Mémoires* (~1490s) — the Burgundian wars (Grandson, Murten, Nancy) from a participant who switched sides; politically and tactically observant.
- **Jan Žižka**, the so-called *Žižka's Military Ordinance* (1423) — Hussite formation discipline and wagenburg procedure; one of the few "rule books" from a serving commander of the era.
- **Polydore Vergil** and other Tudor historians — for late-15th c. English warfare.

### Renaissance military writers

- **Niccolò Machiavelli**, *Arte della Guerra* / *The Art of War* (1521) — proto-modern military theory drawing on Roman models; explicit on infantry formation drill, pike-and-shot integration as theoretical exercise.
- **Francesco Guicciardini**, *Storia d'Italia* (~1540s) — narrative history of the Italian Wars including Marignano, Bicocca, Pavia; participant or near-participant for the later engagements.
- **Various Italian War commentaries** by Florange, Du Bellay, Brantôme, etc.
- **Babur**, *Bāburnāma* (~1530, written in Chagatai Turkic) — Babur's own memoir including a detailed first-person account of the First Battle of Panipat (1526). One of the very few first-person primary sources by a victorious commander at this scale, anywhere in the period.

### Chinese military writings

- **Sun Tzu (Sunzi)**, *The Art of War* (~5th c. BC) — philosophical and theoretical; less granular on spacing/timing than Greek manuals.
- **Sun Bin**, *Sun Bin Bingfa* (~4th c. BC) — more operational than Sun Tzu; some formation discussion.
- **Wuzi (Wu Qi)** (~4th c. BC) — practical treatise; discusses formation depth and command.
- **Six Secret Teachings of the Tai Gong (Liutao)** — Han-period text in archaic frame; some formation material.
- **Wujing Zongyao** (1044 AD) — Song-period military encyclopedia commissioned by the state; gives formation diagrams, equipment specifications, fortification engineering, early gunpowder devices. The Chinese equivalent of a fully-illustrated military manual.

### Modern critical reconstructions (used as cross-check, not as primary)

- **Hans Delbrück**, *Geschichte der Kriegskunst im Rahmen der politischen Geschichte* (1900–1936) — the modern foundation of rigorous primary-source-critical military history.
- **Philip Sabin**, work on Cannae, Roman manipular tactics, and "battle reconstruction methodology."
- **Adrian Goldsworthy**, *Roman Army at War*; *Cannae*; *Punic Wars*.
- **John Keegan**, *Face of Battle* (Agincourt section especially).
- **Bernard Bachrach**, on early medieval and Carolingian warfare.
- **Clifford Rogers**, on the Hundred Years' War and the longbow.
- **Kelly DeVries**, on infantry warfare in the Middle Ages.
- **John Lynn**, on early modern European warfare.

## Part II — The Spatial Vocabulary of the Manuals

Before reconstructing battles, the unit-formation primitives themselves need to be specified. The manuals give precise file-spacing for several formation types.

### Hellenistic phalanx file-and-rank intervals (Asclepiodotus, Aelian, Polybius)

Asclepiodotus, *Tactica* §IV, distinguishes three formation densities by the lateral spacing (the distance between file-centers), measured in cubits and converted to feet:

- **Open order (*araiotēs*):** 4 cubits per file ≈ 6 Roman feet ≈ 1.8 m. Used for the march and for any non-combat formation.
- **Compact order (*pyknōsis*):** 2 cubits per file ≈ 3 Roman feet ≈ 0.9 m. Standard combat order — both Greek hoplite and Macedonian sarissa-phalanx.
- **Locked-shields (*synaspismos*):** 1 cubit per file ≈ 1.5 Roman feet ≈ 0.45 m. Used to receive a charge; shields overlap; the formation is functionally immovable laterally.

**Rank depth (front-to-back interval) is typically half the file interval** — so at *pyknōsis*, files are 0.9 m apart laterally but only ~0.45 m apart front-to-back. This produces a denser-than-square spacing in the close-combat formation.

**Standard Hellenistic phalanx depth:** 16 ranks (one *syntagma* = 16 × 16 = 256 men).

**Implication for frontage:** a 4,096-man taxis at *pyknōsis* has a frontage of 256 files × 0.9 m = **~230 m**. A 16,384-man "grand phalanx" has a frontage of 1,024 files × 0.9 m = **~920 m** — nearly a kilometer.

The first **5 ranks** of a sarissa-phalanx project pike-points beyond the front-rank shield-line; ranks 6–16 carry pikes vertical or angled. This is Polybius 18.29.

### Greek hoplite phalanx — depth variants

Standard 8 ranks deep. Variants used for tactical effect:

- **Spartan hoplites at Mantinea (Thucydides V.68)** are described in detail (with a famous numerical discrepancy Thucydides himself acknowledges). Files of 8.
- **Theban left at Leuctra (Xenophon, *Hellenica* VI.4)** — 50 deep, against a Spartan standard 12 deep on the right. The local mass differential is roughly 4:1.

### Roman manipular and cohort spacing (Polybius, Vegetius)

Polybius 18.30 gives the Roman interval: **3 feet per legionary**, both file and rank. Vegetius 3.14–15 codifies later practice: legionaries deploy 3 ft (~0.9 m) per file frontage, 6–7 ft (~1.8–2.1 m) per rank front-to-back.

**Standard cohort depth:** 4–5 ranks for the imperial cohort, varying by tactical situation.

**Quincunx geometry per Polybius 6.24:** maniples in the first line are spaced one maniple-width apart laterally (i.e., a gap equal to the maniple's own frontage). Principes behind cover the gaps; triarii behind cover the principes' gaps.

**Implication for triplex acies frontage:** a Polybian legion of 4,200 men with 10 maniples × 120 men per maniple of hastati / principes + 10 maniples of 60 triarii deploys, per Goldsworthy's reconstruction, on a frontage of roughly **350–450 m** in three lines (rough — the precise spacing of the lines and the maniple gap-coverage geometry are debated).

### Byzantine *Praecepta Militaria* formations (10th c.)

The most spatially specific medieval manual. Nikephoros II Phokas gives:

- **Infantry hollow square:** the front rank length is typically given as ~500 paces ≈ **750–900 m** depending on which pace measure is used.
- **The "double-line" infantry depth:** 16 deep, with archers interspersed at specified ratios (typically 1 archer per 3 or 4 *skoutatoi*).
- **Kataphraktoi wedge** for the cavalry charge: 20 men in the front rank, widening to 64 in the rear rank, 12 ranks deep. The wedge contains ~504 horsemen.
- **Sortie gaps** in the infantry square deliberately preserved at the corners for cavalry sortie.

### Hussite *wagenburg* dimensions (15th c. sources, including Žižka's Ordinance)

Hussite reconstruction sources suggest:
- War wagons ~3 m long, ~1.5 m wide.
- A wagenburg of 200 wagons in a roughly circular or oval shape has a perimeter of roughly **~700–800 m** (varies with shape).
- Internal organization: lanes of 3–5 m for movement of reserves; a central command position.
- Per-wagon crew of 16–22 men distributed across missile, melee, and cavalry roles.

### Swiss *Gewalthaufen* (15th–16th c. sources)

Swiss pike squares typically deployed with:
- File interval at attack pace: ~0.9 m (similar to Macedonian *pyknōsis*).
- A square of 5,000 men formed on roughly **65 × 80 (or 70 × 70) files** — frontage and depth both ~**60–70 m**.
- 16+ rank depth; the first 4 ranks present pikes horizontally.

### Pace conversions (because primary sources use ancient units)

For reference (these conversions are approximate and source-by-source variable):

- Roman *passus* (full pace, left-to-left foot) = 5 Roman feet = ~1.48 m
- Roman *gradus* (half pace, one step) = 2.5 Roman feet = ~0.74 m
- Greek *stadion* (stade) ≈ 185 m (varies)
- Greek *plethron* ≈ 30 m
- Greek cubit (*pēchys*) ≈ 0.45 m
- Roman cubit (*cubitum*) ≈ 0.44 m
- Late medieval "pace" — variable (~0.75 m to ~1.5 m by region/period)
- Late medieval "bowshot" — variable; typically ~200–300 m for a longbow at maximum range, less for crossbow at lethal range
- Late medieval "league" (French *lieue*) — variable, typically 3–4.5 km

## Part III — Moment-by-Moment Battle Reconstructions

For each battle, I reconstruct the dynamic relative positioning of the two armies through the phases. Spacing and timing are given where extractable from primary sources; estimates are flagged.

### 1. Cannae (2 August 216 BC)

**Sources:** Polybius 3.107–117 (the principal source, ~50 years after the event using earlier accounts including possibly Hannibal's own staff); Livy 22.42–49 (Augustan-era, more dramatic but generally derivative of Polybius and other sources).

**Forces:**
- **Roman** (consuls Varro and Paullus): ~80,000 infantry, ~6,000 cavalry. (Polybius 3.107: 8 legions doubled to ~5,000 each plus allies; modern scholarship reduces this estimate by some 10–20%.) `[CONFIDENCE: medium — numbers debated; the *order of magnitude* is solid.]`
- **Carthaginian** (Hannibal): ~40,000 infantry, ~10,000 cavalry. Composition: Spanish and Gallic heavy infantry in center (~20,000); African heavy infantry on wings (~8,000 each, equipped from Roman dead of Trebia/Trasimene); Numidian light cavalry on right; Spanish/Gallic heavy cavalry on left.

**The deployment (~early morning):**

The Roman army deploys on the south bank of the Aufidus River near Cannae. **Roman frontage is extraordinarily compressed:** Polybius 3.113 specifically notes that Varro deepens the maniples beyond their normal interval. Modern estimate: legionaries packed at perhaps **0.6 m per file** instead of the usual ~0.9 m, with depth doubled or tripled. Roman front: ~1.5 km. Depth: perhaps 50+ ranks at the densest. **This is a deliberate Roman choice for a battering-ram center, not a Hannibalic ambush.**

Hannibal's deployment matches the Roman frontage at the **front** but is much shallower:
- **Spanish and Gallic infantry in center** — drawn forward into a convex bulge ("crescent" arc), perhaps 200–300 m ahead of the African wings at the apex.
- **African heavy infantry on flanks** — held back in columns, NOT advanced. These are the formation's hinges.
- **Numidian light cavalry** on the right (river side, anchored against the Aufidus). ~3,000.
- **Hasdrubal's heavy cavalry** on the left. ~6,000.

**Hannibal's center protrudes forward, his wings refused.** Polybius is explicit on this geometry (3.113). The visual: the Punic army is an arc with the center bulging toward Rome and the wings curving slightly back. From the Roman perspective, the center is closest to them; the wings are farther away.

**Initial inter-army distance: ~1.5–2 km** as the lines complete deployment.

**Phase 1: skirmish (~mid-morning):**

Light troops from both sides exchange missiles in the gap between the main lines. Romans deploy *velites* in front; Hannibal has Balearic slingers and Numidian javelinmen. Skirmishing is brief. Estimated duration: **30–45 minutes**.

**Phase 2: heavy line approach.**

Both heavy lines advance at the standard infantry pace — roughly **4 km/h sustained, slowing to ~2 km/h in the last 100 m as cohesion tightens**. The center collision is reached first, because Hannibal's center is already forward in the convex bulge.

**Estimated time from "advance" order to first heavy contact in the center: ~15–20 minutes.** The Roman center has perhaps ~1 km to cover; Hannibal's center bulge has perhaps ~700 m. Both close at ~3–4 km/h.

**Phase 3: center engagement (~midday):**

Spanish/Gallic infantry meets Roman manipular infantry. The Roman weight of numbers — perhaps 4:1 in the center — begins to push the Spanish/Gallic line backward. The **crescent inverts**: where Hannibal's center had been forward, it is now pressing back; where the African wings had been refused, they are now level with the line, and as the center continues to retreat, the African flanks become forward-facing.

**Polybius (3.115) explicitly describes this controlled retreat.** The Spanish/Gallic infantry yields ground while maintaining cohesion — perhaps **100 m of withdrawal over 30–45 minutes**. They are not routed; they are giving ground.

**Phase 4: cavalry engagements on the wings (concurrent with Phase 3):**

On Hannibal's left, Hasdrubal's heavy cavalry charges the Roman right-wing cavalry (~2,400 citizen cavalry under Aemilius Paullus). The numerical mismatch is severe — perhaps 6,000 vs. 2,400. Roman cavalry is destroyed in melee within an estimated **15–20 minutes** of the cavalry charge initiating. Hasdrubal's cavalry now controls the field behind the Roman right.

On Hannibal's right, Numidian cavalry skirmishes with Roman allied cavalry on the Roman left (~3,600 men). The Numidian style is *not* to close — they harass, withdraw, return. This holds the Roman allied cavalry in place without immediately destroying it.

**Critical Hasdrubal decision (~midday or shortly after):** instead of pursuing the broken Roman right cavalry off the field, Hasdrubal **wheels behind the Roman line** and crosses the rear of the entire Roman army to reinforce the Numidians on the Punic right. Estimated traversal distance: **~1.5–2 km along the rear of the Roman infantry**, which is now deeply engaged forward. Estimated time for this maneuver: **20–30 minutes at trot to canter** for ~6,000 horsemen.

**Phase 5: the African flanks turn inward (~early afternoon):**

As the Spanish/Gallic center continues to give ground and the Roman infantry advances into the "pocket," the African heavy infantry on both wings — until now still in column on the flanks of the Punic line — execute a **wheel inward**. They turn 90°, present face toward the now-protruding flanks of the Roman infantry mass, and engage.

**This is the pincer closing on three sides.** The Romans are now engaged frontally (Spanish/Gallic giving ground), left flank (African heavy infantry), and right flank (African heavy infantry). The pocket is roughly **1 km long and contracting**, with perhaps 60,000–70,000 Romans inside.

**Phase 6: the rear closes (~mid-afternoon):**

Hasdrubal's heavy cavalry, having joined the Numidians and broken or driven off the Roman allied cavalry, now wheels into the Roman rear. The encirclement is complete on all four sides.

**Total elapsed time from heavy first contact to full encirclement: ~2–3 hours.** The Romans are now in a pocket they cannot escape, pressed from all sides, with no room to maneuver.

**Phase 7: the killing (~mid-to-late afternoon):**

Polybius 3.117 estimates Roman dead at ~70,000; modern scholarship reduces this to ~50,000–60,000. Most of the killing happens in this phase, which Polybius does not give a clear duration for. The killing rate, by inference: if 50,000+ men die in 3–4 hours, that's ~13,000–17,000 per hour — limited primarily by the physical capacity of Carthaginian sword arms to keep killing. Polybius and Livy both note that surviving Carthaginians were exhausted from sustained killing.

**Battle ends ~dusk.** Roman survivors: perhaps 14,000 escape, including 10,000 who hold a fortified camp later. Aemilius Paullus is killed; Varro escapes.

**Dynamic-spacing observations:**

- Initial relative distance: ~1.5–2 km.
- Initial Roman frontage compressed to ~1.5 km (depth doubled-to-tripled normal).
- Carthaginian center bulge: forward ~200–300 m relative to wings; converges to first contact while wings refuse.
- Roman penetration into the Carthaginian bulge: ~100 m of "pocket-mouth" advance.
- African wing-turn: 90° wheel, ~500–700 m frontage on each flank moving inward.
- Hasdrubal's flank-and-rear traversal: ~1.5–2 km behind the Roman army.
- Total spatial extent of the encirclement at maximum: a "U" of roughly 1 km × 1 km × 1 km, three sides Carthaginian, closing to all four sides.
- **Total tactical duration: ~5–6 hours of decisive combat after first contact.**

### 2. Pharsalus (9 August 48 BC)

**Source:** Caesar, *De Bello Civili* III.85–99. **Caesar's first-person account.** This is exceptional — a primary source by a commanding general at his own great battle, written within years.

**Forces:**
- **Pompeian:** ~45,000 legionaries in 110 cohorts (about double Caesar's infantry), ~7,000 cavalry, ~5,000 light infantry. Right flank anchored on the Enipeus river.
- **Caesarian:** ~22,000 legionaries in 80 cohorts (notably under-strength after Dyrrhachium), ~1,000 cavalry, modest light infantry.

**Deployment:**

Caesar describes both lines deployed in three battle lines (*triplex acies*) with 10 cohorts each per line, plus a special **fourth line of 6 cohorts** held in reserve behind his right wing — drawn from the rear ranks of the third line. This fourth line is the tactical innovation of the battle.

Pompey's right is anchored on the river; his left wing has all his cavalry plus light infantry, intending to drive off Caesar's small cavalry, sweep around Caesar's right, and roll up the Caesarian line from flank and rear.

**Initial inter-army distance:** Caesar (III.92) gives "about a half-mile" between the lines as both deploy — roughly **750 m**, plausible for the moment of completed deployment.

**Phase 1: Pompey holds his line stationary (~midday):**

Pompey orders his infantry to stand and let Caesar's army close — a deliberate counter-doctrine. The reasoning (per Caesar III.92): cavalry will deliver the decisive blow on the flank; Pompey's infantry has only to absorb. By holding stationary, Pompey forces Caesar's infantry to expend more energy in the approach.

Caesar (III.92) notes this is unusual — standard doctrine is for both lines to advance — and that his men, knowing standard doctrine, **stop their advance halfway across the gap** to rest before completing the charge. **The Caesarian infantry advance therefore has two distinct movements**: an initial advance covering roughly half the distance (~350–400 m), a pause, then a final charge across the remaining ~350–400 m.

**Estimated duration:** initial advance ~3–4 minutes at ~3 km/h; pause perhaps 1–2 minutes; final charge ~2–3 minutes at ~5–6 km/h. **Total approach time: ~7–10 minutes.**

**Phase 2: pilum exchange and main contact (~early afternoon):**

At ~30 m range the Caesarian infantry hurl pila, then close to gladius range. Pompeian infantry, having stood stationary, responds with their own pila. Main contact along the entire frontage of perhaps **2.5–3 km** (110 cohorts at ~25 m each in the front line).

**Phase 3: cavalry attack on the Caesarian right (~simultaneous with Phase 2):**

Pompey's massed cavalry (~7,000) advances against Caesar's small right-wing cavalry (~1,000). The Pompeian cavalry succeeds in driving Caesar's cavalry off the field within an estimated **10–15 minutes** of the cavalry charge — the numerical mismatch is decisive.

The Pompeian cavalry, now having no opposition, begins to wheel toward Caesar's exposed right flank.

**Phase 4: the fourth-line ambush (~mid-afternoon):**

Caesar, anticipating the cavalry sweep, has positioned his fourth line (6 cohorts, ~2,400 men) **at an oblique angle behind his right wing**, hidden by the rest of his army. As the Pompeian cavalry wheels toward Caesar's right, the fourth line emerges and charges *frontally* into them.

Caesar (III.93) specifies that his fourth-line cohorts are **instructed to use their pila as thrusting spears against the cavalry's faces** rather than throwing them. This is the tactical detail: cavalry charges into men holding short spears point-forward, lose horses to face-thrusts, and rout.

**The Pompeian cavalry breaks within perhaps 5–10 minutes** of the fourth-line attack. Caesar's fourth line, now free, wheels left to attack the exposed flank of the Pompeian infantry.

**Phase 5: Pompeian infantry envelopment from the flank (~mid-to-late afternoon):**

Caesarian fourth line, plus Caesar's reformed cavalry pursuing the fled Pompeian cavalry, now attacks the Pompeian infantry's left flank and rear. The Pompeian infantry, locked frontally with Caesar's main line and now flanked, begins to give ground.

**Phase 6: Pompeian collapse and pursuit (~late afternoon):**

Pompey himself, observing the cavalry defeat from a vantage point, leaves the field. Pompeian infantry, sensing the command's departure and the deepening flank pressure, breaks and routs toward their camp ~3 km to the rear.

Caesar pursues, takes the Pompeian camp, and continues pursuit through the night. Pompey escapes east; eventually flees to Egypt.

**Casualties (Caesar III.99):** Pompeian killed ~15,000; captured ~24,000. Caesarian killed ~200 (almost certainly a propaganda figure — modern scholarship doubles or triples this).

**Dynamic-spacing observations:**

- Initial inter-army distance: ~750 m (Caesar's own figure).
- Caesarian advance in two stages: ~350 m + pause + ~350 m.
- Total approach time: ~7–10 minutes.
- Pompeian cavalry sweep arc: ~7,000 horse moving from far left flank to mid-line behind Caesar's right, a wheel of perhaps 500–700 m radius before contact with fourth line.
- Fourth-line ambush range: pila-thrust distance (~3–4 m point-to-point); cavalry-decisive at <10 m.
- Pompeian flank-and-rear pressure begins ~30–45 minutes after main contact.
- Total tactical duration from first contact to Pompeian rout: **~2–3 hours.**
- Pursuit distance to the camp: ~3 km.

### 3. Adrianople (9 August 378 AD)

**Source:** Ammianus Marcellinus, *Res Gestae* XXXI.12–13. Ammianus is a near-contemporary, a former soldier, and may have access to military sources.

**Forces:**
- **Roman** (Emperor Valens): ~15,000–30,000; modern estimates vary wildly. Combined heavy infantry, cavalry, light troops.
- **Gothic** (Fritigern and others): ~12,000–20,000; the bulk in a wagon-fortress (*carrago*) atop a hill. Tervingi infantry inside the wagon-laager; Greuthungi cavalry under Alatheus and Saphrax detached and away from the laager.

**The Roman approach:**

Valens marches from Adrianople with a substantial force in heat — Ammianus emphasizes the heat (XXXI.12). Approach distance from Adrianople: ~12 km; the Roman force is **already exhausted by the march** before any deployment occurs.

**Phase 1: skirmish at the wagon-laager (~midday):**

Roman skirmishers and the Roman left wing approach the wagon-laager. Gothic missile fire from inside the laager attrites the Roman left. **Fritigern stalls for time** — sending envoys for hostage exchange and parley while waiting for Alatheus and Saphrax's cavalry to return to the battle.

**Phase 2: Roman left wing engages (~early afternoon):**

The Roman left, having advanced to engage the laager, becomes pinned. The right wing has not yet fully deployed when Roman command (some unit commander not named by Ammianus, possibly the *primicerius* Cassio) **prematurely advances** into the engagement without orders. The Roman right is now committed without coordination.

**Phase 3: the Greuthungi cavalry returns (~mid-afternoon):**

Alatheus and Saphrax's cavalry — perhaps 5,000–8,000 — return to the battlefield from foraging or scouting. They strike the Roman right wing **in flank or rear** — Ammianus is not perfectly clear which, but the effect is unambiguous. The Roman right disintegrates rapidly.

**Phase 4: rolling collapse of the Roman line (~late afternoon):**

With the Roman right gone, the cavalry presses onto the rear of the Roman center. The Roman infantry, now pressed from both front (Tervingi infantry sortieing from the laager) and rear (Greuthungi cavalry), is **compressed into a mass so tight that it cannot use its weapons effectively** — Ammianus describes the soldiers as unable even to lift their arms.

The packed Roman center is killed in place over perhaps **2–3 hours** of close-quarter slaughter. Emperor Valens is killed; his body is never recovered.

**Casualties:** Ammianus says two-thirds of the army was killed; modern estimates suggest 10,000+ Roman deaths.

**Dynamic-spacing observations:**

- Roman approach to the wagon-laager: a march of ~12 km in heat reducing Roman cohesion before any contact.
- Skirmish phase at the laager: probably 1–2 hours of standoff while Fritigern stalls.
- **Geographic separation of Greuthungi cavalry from the laager: estimated ~5–10 km when the battle begins.** Their return — at trot to canter, ~12–15 km/h — would take perhaps 30–60 minutes once they decide to return.
- Pincer pocket dimensions at maximum compression: ~500 m × 500 m, with perhaps 20,000+ Romans inside.
- The "weapons cannot be used in the press" detail in Ammianus: this is a primary-source specific observation about the lethal density of the packed formation. **Casualty rates in such compressed conditions can reach 80% of the trapped force.**

### 4. Hastings (14 October 1066)

**Sources:** William of Poitiers, *Gesta Guillelmi*; the Bayeux Tapestry (visual); the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*; Florence of Worcester; later sources including Orderic Vitalis and Wace (*Roman de Rou*, 12th c., to be used cautiously).

**Forces:**
- **English** (Harold Godwinson): ~7,000–8,000. Composition: thegns and huscarls (perhaps 1,000–2,000 elite, including ~2,000 huscarls with kite shields and Dane-axe), fyrd levy (perhaps 5,000–6,000). Almost no cavalry; almost no archers.
- **Norman** (William): ~7,000–10,000. Composition: ~2,000–3,000 mounted knights; ~3,000–4,000 heavy infantry spearmen; ~1,000–2,000 archers and crossbowmen.

**Deployment:**

Harold deploys on Senlac Hill, atop a ridge running east-west, with the slope falling to the south where William's army deploys. Ridge frontage: estimated **600–700 m**.

Norman deployment: archers / crossbowmen forward (perhaps 100 m in front of the heavy infantry); heavy infantry in line; cavalry in three "battles" behind (Bretons on the left, Normans in the center, French/Flemish on the right). Total Norman frontage: ~600 m matching English.

Initial inter-army distance after both deployments complete: ~250–400 m (the English are uphill; the Normans deploy at the base of the slope).

**Phase 1: Norman archers shoot uphill (~9:00 AM):**

Norman archers advance to ~100 m of the English line and shoot uphill. Arrow trajectory is bow-stretching; effect on the shield-wall is limited. **Sustained for perhaps 30–45 minutes**, with little to show. English return fire is negligible (very few archers).

**Phase 2: Norman heavy infantry advance (~9:30–10:00 AM):**

Norman heavy spearmen advance uphill to engage the shield-wall. The slope is significant — perhaps 1:6 to 1:8 grade for much of the ascent. **Norman infantry close to contact at ~3 km/h on the slope, slower than their level march pace.**

**Phase 3: Shield-wall contact (~10:00 AM onwards):**

English Dane-axes and spears engage the Norman spearmen. **The wall holds.** Norman infantry takes heavy losses; some are pushed back down the slope.

**Phase 4: First cavalry charge (~late morning):**

Norman knights charge uphill into the shield-wall. **The cavalry cannot break the wall.** Some chronicles describe the Bretons on the Norman left actually breaking and fleeing down the slope.

**Phase 5: First feigned retreat / actual rout, with English pursuit (~midday):**

A portion of the English right wing — possibly fyrd levy without huscarl discipline — breaks formation to pursue the fleeing Bretons. They run perhaps **200–300 m downhill** before Norman cavalry on the rest of the line wheels and counter-charges the isolated English pursuers.

**The isolated English are destroyed within perhaps 10–15 minutes of leaving the shield-wall.** The shield-wall is now shorter on the right.

**Phase 6: Repeated cycles (~early afternoon onwards):**

William cycles his arms: archers fire, infantry attack, cavalry charge, withdraw, repeat. The English shield-wall progressively thins on the right where two more feigned-retreat / counter-charge cycles further reduce the line. **The wall holds in the center around Harold.**

**Cycle duration: each archery-infantry-cavalry cycle perhaps 30–60 minutes.**

**Phase 7: High-angle archery and Harold's death (~late afternoon):**

Norman archers shoot at high angle, dropping arrows behind the shield-wall onto faces and necks. **Harold is hit and falls** — the famous arrow-in-the-eye scene on the Bayeux Tapestry. Sources are conflicting; modern scholarship is divided on whether the arrow killed him or whether he was killed by cavalry penetrating the now-weakened line.

**Phase 8: Shield-wall collapse and final stand (~dusk):**

With Harold dead and the line thinned, the shield-wall finally breaks. Norman cavalry exploits the rout. Huscarls form a final knot around Harold's body and are killed in place. **Final action: ~30 minutes of last-stand combat after the wall breaks.**

**Total battle duration: ~9 hours of active combat (early morning to dusk in October).** Exceptionally long for a medieval battle.

**Dynamic-spacing observations:**

- Slope is the defining spatial feature: ~250–400 m of contact-distance, but with significant gradient.
- Cyclical engagement: each archery / infantry / cavalry cycle perhaps 30–60 minutes; 6–10 cycles total through the day.
- English pursuit-rout distance: ~200–300 m downhill, then trapped on level ground by counter-charging cavalry.
- Shield-wall frontage shrinks from ~600 m to perhaps 200–300 m by late afternoon — through casualty attrition and through the feigned-retreat losses.
- The decisive blow is missile-delivered (high-angle archery), not melee — the longbow ancestor doctrine appears even before the longbow's institutional emergence.

### 5. Crécy (26 August 1346)

**Sources:** Jean le Bel, *Chronique*; Jean Froissart, *Chroniques* (drawing heavily on Le Bel for early portions); Geoffrey le Baker, *Chronicon*; Italian and other continental chronicles. Some military-logistical detail from English administrative records.

**Forces:**
- **English** (Edward III, Black Prince): ~14,000–16,000. Composition: ~2,500–4,000 men-at-arms (dismounted); ~5,000–7,000 longbowmen; ~3,000 Welsh spearmen and other foot. Strong defensive position.
- **French** (Philip VI): ~30,000–40,000. Composition: 8,000–12,000 mounted men-at-arms; 6,000 Genoese crossbowmen; large but variable-quality foot levy.

**Deployment:**

Edward deploys on a gentle slope between the villages of Crécy and Wadicourt, frontage of ~2 km. Three "battles":
- **Right (Black Prince):** ~4,000, including longbowmen on a refused wing at oblique angle.
- **Left (Northampton):** ~4,000, similarly with longbow wing.
- **Reserve (Edward himself):** ~4,000 behind the center.
- Baggage train fortified as a wagon-park behind the line — about 100 m to rear.

The position has a windmill behind the center (Edward's command post), with the longbowmen in the **herse formation** (a wedge or chevron, possibly arrayed at angles to deliver oblique fire) on the wings.

**Phase 1: French approach (~mid-to-late afternoon):**

French army arrives at Crécy after a long approach march in heat. Initial deployment chaotic — divisions arriving piecemeal. Estimated time of arrival of the French vanguard: **~3:00–4:00 PM**.

**Phase 2: Genoese crossbow advance (~late afternoon):**

Genoese crossbowmen advance to ~150 m from the English line. **A brief rain shower** preceding the engagement has wet their crossbow strings (which they leave on the bows when not in use — a logistical detail from Froissart and Le Bel). Their effective range is reduced and their rate of fire is poor.

English longbowmen (whose bows are unstrung in damp weather and re-strung when needed) deliver mass volley fire at ~250 m. **Estimated arrow density: 5,000 longbowmen × 8 arrows/min = 40,000 arrows/min** descending on the Genoese line — saturating the ~200 m frontage. Genoese take heavy casualties within minutes.

**Phase 3: Genoese withdrawal (~early evening):**

Genoese begin to withdraw from the impossible position. **Philip VI, seeing the withdrawal and not understanding it, orders his cavalry to ride down the Genoese as traitors.** French knights charge into and over the retreating crossbowmen, fragmenting both contingents.

**Phase 4: First French cavalry charge (~early evening):**

French heavy cavalry, now in disorder from their own command failure, advances uphill into the English line. Distance from the French start to the English line: ~250–300 m uphill. **Charge speed slows from canter (~16 km/h) at start to slow trot (~10 km/h) on the slope.**

English longbowmen fire massed volleys. Estimated:
- At 200 m range: armor-distant fire suppresses but doesn't kill many fully-armored knights.
- At 100 m: horses begin dying; armored knights stagger.
- At 30–50 m: armor-piercing range; broadhead arrows kill horses; bodkins penetrate mail; even plate armor's joints are vulnerable.

**Charge reaches the English line in significantly disrupted state.** Those that arrive are met by dismounted English men-at-arms with poleaxes; knights are pulled from horses, killed where they fall.

**Phase 5: Repeated charges through the night (~evening into night):**

Sources count **15 or 16 separate French cavalry charges** through the afternoon and into the night. Each follows the same pattern: distance uphill, longbow attrition, fragmented impact, dismounted defense holds, charge breaks. Each charge perhaps 20–30 minutes of action; total 5–6 hours of repeated engagement.

**Critical detail from Froissart and Le Bel:** Edward III, asked by attendants to commit reinforcements to the Black Prince's hard-pressed right, replies that the Prince should win his spurs unaided. He does not commit the reserve. The English position holds without reserve commitment.

**Phase 6: Collapse and abandonment (~late night to dawn):**

The French command structure fails. Philip VI is wounded, escapes the field with a small escort. The remaining French knights, exhausted, cease attacks. Many sleep where they fell.

**Phase 7: Morning aftermath (~dawn 27 August):**

English search the field. **French aristocratic casualties:** the King of Bohemia (charging blind with his horse tied to attendants — historically attested), the Count of Flanders, the Duke of Lorraine, ~1,500 knights and squires, plus thousands of foot.

**English losses:** perhaps 200–300 total.

**Dynamic-spacing observations:**

- English frontage: ~2 km, divided into three battles + longbow wings.
- Longbow killing arc: from ~250 m to ~30 m from the English line, with effectiveness scaling with proximity.
- French charge distance: ~250–300 m uphill, with slope slowing the charge from canter to slow trot.
- Charge density: each successive charge committed perhaps 1,500–3,000 horses; the line accumulates dead horses and men, **physically obstructing later charges**.
- Repetition: 15–16 charges over ~6 hours, indicating French cavalry uncommitted to systematic withdrawal.
- The "drift" of dead bodies and obstacles in the killing zone: by the end of the battle, the slope in front of the English line is described as carpeted with bodies — perhaps **3,000–5,000 dead horses and men in a strip ~50 m deep × 500 m wide** in the most heavily contested center.

### 6. Agincourt (25 October 1415)

**Source:** *Gesta Henrici Quinti* (anonymous chaplain, ~1416–17, the closest near-eyewitness account); Thomas Walsingham, *Historia Anglicana*; the Burgundian *Religieux de Saint-Denys* and Monstrelet on the French side; logistical records from English administrative documents.

**Forces:**
- **English** (Henry V): ~6,000–9,000 total. Composition: ~1,000 dismounted men-at-arms; ~5,000–7,000 longbowmen. Exhausted by long march from Harfleur; sick (dysentery); short of supplies.
- **French** (Constable d'Albret, Marshal Boucicaut, et al.): ~12,000–25,000 (the lower figure is modern revised; the upper is from chronicles). Mostly mounted men-at-arms, with significant numbers electing to fight dismounted; lighter contingents and some crossbow.

**Deployment:**

Henry V deploys on a narrow plowed field — recently rain-soaked — between the woods of Agincourt (south) and Tramecourt (north). Frontage: **~750–1,000 m wide**, narrowing slightly toward the French position.

Three "battles" dismounted: Henry's own in center, Duke of York on the right, Lord Camoys on the left. Each battle has longbow wings refused at obtuse angles. Stakes (sharpened wooden pickets ~1.5 m, driven into the ground at 45° angles, pointing outward) are planted in front of the archer positions.

French deployment: three lines, the first two dismounted, the third mounted in reserve. Cavalry detachments on the flanks. **Total French frontage exceeds the field's width — they cannot fully deploy.** Many troops queue behind the front lines.

**Initial inter-army distance after deployment: ~1 km.**

**Phase 1: morning stand-off (4–5 hours, ~6:00–10:00 AM):**

Both armies stand in deployment for several hours. Henry V wants to attack but doesn't want to abandon his defensive position. The French wait for reinforcement and to drag out exhaustion.

**Phase 2: English advance (~10:00 AM):**

Henry orders the English line to **pull up stakes and walk forward** ~750 m, then **replant stakes** and assume defensive posture again. This is a tactical innovation — bring the killing zone to the French rather than wait. The advance closes the distance to perhaps **250–300 m**, the longbow's effective volley range. The advance takes perhaps 30 minutes.

**Phase 3: opening longbow volleys (~10:30 AM):**

English longbowmen open fire. The French, who had hoped the English would attack frontally to be ground down, now must either advance or stand under bombardment.

**Phase 4: French cavalry charges (~11:00 AM):**

Small French cavalry contingents (probably ~600–800 horse) charge the English archer wings. **They strike the stakes**. Horses impale themselves or are panicked by the stakes; archers shoot horses at close range; **wounded horses stampede back through the advancing French main line**, fragmenting it.

**Phase 5: French dismounted main attack (~late morning to noon):**

The main French force, dismounted in plate armor, advances on foot across the rain-soaked plowed field. **The field is muddy** — sources note this explicitly. Heavy plate (~30 kg of armor per knight) plus deep mud (~ankle to calf-deep in places) means:

- Pace slows from normal walking (~4 km/h) to perhaps **1–2 km/h**.
- Energy expenditure per step is dramatically increased.
- Knights tire long before contact.
- Stragglers slow the column.

**Distance: ~300 m of mud-march. Estimated time: 15–20 minutes** at the reduced pace, in armor, under arrow fire.

The narrow field also compresses the French line. The center is forced into a column of perhaps 30 ranks deep, **funneled by the woods on both flanks**.

**Phase 6: contact (~midday):**

The exhausted, oxygen-starved French front line reaches the English position. English men-at-arms meet them at the line; longbowmen on the flanks shoot point-blank into the **flanks of the densely-packed French column**.

**Critical detail from the *Gesta*:** men in plate armor who fall in the mud cannot rise without help. Subsequent ranks press forward and walk on the fallen. **Piles of armored bodies accumulate.**

**Phase 7: the archer melee commit (~early afternoon):**

English longbowmen, ammunition running low and having shot effectively at point-blank into the French press, draw mallets, daggers, and short swords. They **leave their positions and attack the flanks of the French column directly** — the *Gesta* description.

The contrast is critical:
- Longbowmen: lightly armored, agile, fresh.
- French men-at-arms: heavily armored, exhausted, immobilized in mud, dense-packed, can barely use weapons.

**The killing is one-sided.** Casualty rates among the French front-and-flanks reach extreme densities — perhaps **20–30 French casualties per English casualty** in this phase.

**Phase 8: the prisoner massacre (~late afternoon):**

A French baggage-train attack or a perceived threat from an unbeaten French rear force prompts Henry V to order the killing of prisoners. **Thousands of captured aristocrats are killed.** Morally controversial; tactically a recognition that the English can't fight new arrivals while guarding prisoners.

**French aristocratic casualties: 5,000+, including Constable d'Albret, the Dukes of Brabant, Bar, and Alençon, and many senior nobles. English losses: ~100–400.**

**Dynamic-spacing observations:**

- Initial distance: ~1 km.
- English advance: ~750 m to ~250 m of the French line — bringing longbow range to bear.
- French dismounted advance: ~300 m through mud + arrow-fire, taking 15–20 minutes for armored troops.
- Field compression: from probable 1 km wide at the French rear to ~750 m at the English line, **funneled by woods**.
- Density at the contact zone: perhaps **30 ranks deep × 750 m wide of French men-at-arms** trying to reach the English line — a thoroughly inefficient deployment forced by geometry.
- Pile-up of bodies at the contact line: probably **head-high in places**, per chronicle descriptions of men climbing over their fellow dead.

### 7. Marignano (13–14 September 1515)

**Sources:** Various French and Swiss contemporary accounts, including narrative letters from Francis I, Marshal Trivulzio, and others; later integrative accounts in Guicciardini and military memoirs.

**Forces:**
- **French and allies** (Francis I): ~30,000+. Composition: ~6,000–8,000 gendarmes (heavy cavalry); ~20,000 mixed German and Italian foot (including Landsknechte and "Black Bands"); 72 cannons.
- **Swiss** (in Milanese service): ~20,000–25,000 pike infantry in three columns.

**Day 1, deployment (~mid-afternoon, 13 September):**

French army deployed in field-fortified positions south of Milan, around the village of Marignano. The line is anchored with cannons on a ridge, infantry in front, cavalry on the flanks. The position has irrigation ditches that channel any approach.

Swiss approach from Milan in three columns. The Swiss vanguard reaches the French position late on the 13th. **They attack immediately**, despite the late hour and absent the reinforcements that would have arrived by the next day — a Swiss-doctrine decision (the aggressive offensive pike norm).

**Phase 1: Swiss vanguard attack (~late afternoon, 13 September):**

Vanguard column of perhaps 7,000–8,000 Swiss pike advances against the French center at **standard Swiss attack pace** (described as a "quick walk, almost a trot" — ~5–7 km/h sustained, faster than typical infantry advance).

Distance from the Swiss start to the French position: estimated **400–600 m**. Approach time: ~5–7 minutes.

French cannons open fire as the Swiss enter range. **Each cannon shot can rip a furrow through a tight pike column** — sources note casualties in the front ranks but the column does not break. The Swiss continue.

**Phase 2: Swiss contact with French infantry (~17:00–18:00, 13 September):**

Swiss pike reaches the French infantry line. **The pikes punch through the front rank** of the Landsknechte and Black Bands. French infantry begins to give ground.

**Phase 3: French gendarme counter-charge (~dusk, 13 September):**

French gendarmes (heavy cavalry under Francis I in person) charge the Swiss flanks. **Cavalry vs. pike at flank** — the Swiss formation, deep but slow to reorient, takes time to face the new threat. Gendarmes succeed in disordering the Swiss vanguard.

**Phase 4: night engagement (~21:00 onward, 13 September):**

**Fighting continues into the night**, exceptional in pre-firearm warfare. Both sides exhausted; visibility limited to moonlight; engagement is confused. Sources describe fighting by torchlight. **Combatants on both sides cease coordinated action by ~midnight.**

**Day 2 (14 September):**

**Phase 5: Swiss renewed attack (~dawn):**

Swiss main body and rearguard reorganize and renew the attack. French line holds; the gendarmes regroup and re-engage. Fighting through the morning.

**Phase 6: Venetian reinforcement (~late morning):**

A Venetian army of ~6,000 — heavy infantry and cavalry — arrives from the east, having marched through the night. They strike the Swiss left flank.

**Phase 7: Swiss withdrawal (~midday, 14 September):**

With the Venetian flank attack added to the French frontal defense, the Swiss position becomes untenable. **Swiss withdrawal is orderly** — they pull back in formation, not in rout, taking their guns and standards.

**Total battle duration: ~24+ hours of continuous engagement** with night-fighting pauses. **The longest set-piece battle in this entire reconstruction.**

**Casualties:** Swiss losses ~10,000 (including most of their senior commanders); French losses ~5,000.

**Dynamic-spacing observations:**

- Initial inter-army distance: ~400–600 m.
- Swiss attack pace: ~5–7 km/h sustained — markedly faster than typical pike advance.
- Swiss penetration: ~50–100 m into the French line before being stopped.
- Cannon casualty effect on pike column: each shot can clear a furrow ~2–3 ranks deep × ~1 m wide; over a 30-minute approach, perhaps 200–400 Swiss killed by cannon alone before contact.
- Night-fighting break: ~6 hours of paused but not disengaged combat.
- Venetian flank arrival distance: their march approach was perhaps 15 km overnight, then ~500 m into the flank of the Swiss position.

### 8. Pavia (24 February 1525)

**Sources:** Multiple Italian War narratives (Guicciardini), Imperial / Spanish military letters, French recollections. The most-documented Italian Wars battle.

**Forces:**
- **French** (Francis I): ~28,000 besieging Pavia. Composition: ~8,000 gendarmes; ~6,000 Swiss mercenary pike; ~10,000 Landsknechte (the "Black Bands" plus other German mercenaries); ~4,000 mixed French infantry and arquebusiers; 53 guns.
- **Imperial** (Lannoy / d'Avalos): ~23,000 relief army. Composition: ~6,000 Spanish arquebusiers and pike (under Antonio de Leyva); ~7,000 Landsknechte (Imperial); ~5,000 light infantry; ~4,000 cavalry (heavy and light); modest artillery.

**Strategic setup:** the French have besieged Pavia for months; the Imperial relief force has been gathering near the city; both sides know the engagement is coming.

**Phase 1: Imperial overnight breach of the Mirabello park wall (~23 February into 24 February dawn):**

Imperial engineers breach the wall of the Mirabello — a Visconti hunting park north of Pavia ~5 km long, enclosed by a high masonry wall — at the northern end during the night. The Imperial force begins to pour into the park through the breach. **The French don't detect this for hours.**

**Phase 2: French dawn response (~5:00–6:00 AM, 24 February):**

French scouts finally report Imperial troops inside the Mirabello. Francis I and his commanders mobilize hastily. **The French are surprised.**

**Phase 3: French gendarme initial charge (~6:00–7:00 AM):**

Francis I, on a famous war-horse, leads the gendarme charge against the Imperial vanguard inside the park. **The charge initially succeeds** — Imperial advance troops are scattered; the Marquess of Pescara (d'Avalos) is wounded in the early action.

**Phase 4: French artillery interference (~7:00–8:00 AM):**

**Critical command error.** The gendarme charge, in pursuit of the broken Imperial vanguard, sweeps past the position of the French field artillery, which had been doing significant damage to the Imperial advance from beyond the park wall through prepared firing positions. **The gendarmes' position now masks the French artillery's field of fire.** The artillery must cease fire to avoid hitting their own cavalry.

**Phase 5: Spanish arquebusier dispersal into the park (~8:00–9:00 AM):**

Antonio de Leyva orders his Spanish arquebusiers — ~3,000 to 4,000 men — to **disperse from formation into the wooded terrain of the Mirabello park**. They take cover behind trees, hedges, and folds in the ground. The park is broken terrain — woods, brush, hedgerows, ditches — ideal for skirmishing infantry and impossible for the gendarmes' charge.

**Phase 6: French cavalry shot down individually (~9:00–11:00 AM):**

Spanish arquebusiers shoot at the gendarmes from concealment. **The gendarmes — heavily armored, on tired horses, in close country — cannot effectively engage skirmishers in the trees.** Knights are shot down one by one. Horses go down. The gendarme charge that began with promise dissolves into a stationary killing zone.

**Estimated arquebusier effective rate:** ~1 shot per minute per man × 3,000+ arquebusiers = perhaps 3,000+ shots per minute, with each round potentially hitting a high-value target (a gendarme is worth perhaps 100x a foot soldier in equipment cost). Over 2 hours, this is ~360,000 shots total — saturating the park.

**Phase 7: Francis I captured (~late morning):**

Francis I's horse is shot. **He himself is wounded, dismounted, and captured** by Spanish-Burgundian troops — initially not recognized; eventually identified. **The king of France is a prisoner of war.**

**Phase 8: French infantry collapse (~late morning to early afternoon):**

Without the gendarmes (destroyed), without the king (captured), and without artillery support (which had ceased fire and could not effectively re-engage), the French infantry collapses. Swiss pike attempts to hold; Landsknecht pike attempts to hold; both are pressured by Imperial pike-and-shot and cavalry. **The French Black Bands fight to the death** — about 5,000 of these "Bandi Neri" are killed nearly to the last man, an unusually high cohesion-under-defeat record.

**Total tactical duration: ~6 hours from gendarme charge to French collapse.**

**Casualties:** French ~10,000 killed plus thousands captured; Imperial ~700–1,500 killed. **Catastrophic ratio.**

**Dynamic-spacing observations:**

- The defining spatial feature is the **Mirabello park's broken terrain** — woods, brush, ditches, hedges. Cavalry can charge in the open but cannot fight effectively in the park's interior.
- French artillery effective range: ~500 m from emplacement, but blocked by the gendarmes' position after their charge.
- Arquebusier effective range in the woods: ~50–80 m from cover to mounted target — point-blank for arquebus.
- Spatial concentration of casualties: the gendarmes died over a ~1 km² area of the park, perhaps 50–100 dead per hectare in the densest killing zones.
- **First example in major European warfare of "skirmishing arquebus + broken terrain defeating heavy cavalry."** This is the doctrinal future.

### 9. Panipat (21 April 1526)

**Source:** The *Bāburnāma* — Babur's first-person memoir, written in Chagatai Turkic, **the most spatially detailed primary-source narrative of any single pre-firearm battle by a victorious commander**. Babur is precise about his deployment, his use of carts, his expectations of Lodi's response, and the timing of the encirclement.

**Forces:**
- **Babur:** ~12,000–15,000. Composition: Turkic-Mongol cavalry; ~700 chained *araba* (carts); ~5,000 *tufangchi* (Anatolian-Ottoman-trained matchlock arquebusiers); a small artillery train under Ustad Ali Quli.
- **Ibrahim Lodi:** perhaps 30,000–100,000 (chronicle exaggeration; modern estimates favor the lower end). Composition: ~30,000 cavalry; ~100+ war elephants; mixed infantry; limited firearm component.

**Deployment (~21 April morning):**

Babur arrives at Panipat several days before Lodi. Babur's preparation:
- ~**700 araba carts** are chained together in a defensive line along the front of his deployment. Gaps (*aghruq*) are deliberately left every 30–50 m for cavalry sortie.
- Artillery and matchlock arquebusiers deployed behind the chained cart-line.
- Cavalry concentrated on both wings, outside the wagon enclosure.
- Town of Panipat and partial earthworks anchor the right flank; the left is open, with cavalry covering.

**Frontage of the wagon-line: ~800–900 m**, based on 700 carts × ~1.2 m wagon-spacing each.

**Lodi's deployment:** traditional Delhi Sultanate massed cavalry + elephants + infantry, in dense column rather than wide line. Lodi's army is unfamiliar with Babur's tactics; deployment is by traditional norm.

**Initial inter-army distance:** ~2 km at first sighting; Lodi advances toward Babur.

**Phase 1: Babur's flanking cavalry deploys (~early morning):**

Babur sends light cavalry under his sons (Humayun, Askari) to **flank Lodi's wings**. The flanking cavalry rides wide of the main engagement — perhaps **1–2 km out** on each side — to begin the classic Mongol *tulughma* (encirclement).

**Phase 2: Lodi's advance (~mid-morning):**

Lodi's army advances on the wagon-line. **Elephants lead the charge** — the traditional Indian shock arm. Lodi's main cavalry follows; infantry behind.

**Distance from Lodi's start to Babur's wagons: ~800 m.** Lodi's pace: elephant pace ~6 km/h sustained; cavalry behind moving slower to maintain coherence. **Approach time: ~10–15 minutes** from the order to advance.

**Phase 3: arquebus and artillery fire at point-blank (~mid-to-late morning):**

As Lodi's elephants and front cavalry reach the wagon-line, **Babur's matchlock fire and artillery erupt at point-blank range**. Babur's account in the *Bāburnāma* is specific: gunpowder weapons fired in massed volleys.

**Elephants panic.** This is the defining moment of the engagement. The combination of gunpowder smoke, noise, and casualties to the elephants themselves causes a stampede. **Many elephants turn and trample their own troops backward.**

**Phase 4: Wagon-line holds; Babur's cavalry encircles (~late morning):**

Lodi's advance has stalled in front of the wagon-line. Lateral pressure builds as more troops accumulate behind the front line. **Babur's flanking cavalry, having ridden the long arcs around the Lodi flanks, now closes the encirclement from both sides.**

**Spatial arrangement at maximum encirclement:**
- Wagon-line frontage: 800–900 m, facing east (toward Lodi's advance).
- Babur's flanking cavalry: north and south, each enclosing ~1 km of the Lodi force.
- Lodi rear: unblocked initially, but as the encirclement deepens, his retreat options collapse.
- **The Lodi army is now pressed into a pocket of perhaps 1 km × 1 km, with ~50,000–80,000 men inside.**

**Phase 5: Lodi's death and Sultanate collapse (~midday):**

Ibrahim Lodi is killed in the fighting — exact circumstances unclear in the *Bāburnāma* (Babur was not on his immediate position, and the *Bāburnāma* notes Lodi was found dead in a pile of bodies). Without command, the Lodi army collapses.

**Phase 6: Pursuit and the gates of Delhi (~afternoon and onward):**

Babur sends pursuit forces toward Delhi. **Within a few days**, Delhi is in Mughal hands. The Lodi dynasty is gone; the Mughal Empire begins.

**Casualties:** Lodi side perhaps 15,000–20,000 killed; Babur's losses perhaps 1,500–2,000.

**Total tactical duration: ~6 hours from first contact to Lodi's death.**

**Dynamic-spacing observations:**

- The chained wagon-line **physically blocks** an attacking force from closing on the matchlock infantry — at most a few meters of penetration through the cart gaps, where defenders can concentrate fire.
- Elephant panic radius: a panicked elephant can rampage in a 100 m circle around its trigger point; with 100+ elephants panicking simultaneously, the result is a ~1 km² zone of stampede.
- Flanking cavalry arc length: Babur's flankers covered perhaps 1.5–2 km from start to encirclement closure on each side.
- Lodi pocket size at maximum: ~1 km × 1 km, density of perhaps 80,000 men in 1 km² = **8 men per m²** — far too dense to fight effectively, similar in spirit to Adrianople's lethal compression.

## Part IV — Generic Patterns of Dynamic Relative Spacing

Across the reconstructions in Part III, patterns of relative spacing recur. These are the meta-observations.

### 1. The "approach window" is 250 m – 2 km.

Initial deployment-complete distances cluster:
- **Cannae:** ~1.5–2 km.
- **Pharsalus:** ~750 m.
- **Adrianople:** approach over ~12 km, then engagement at <500 m.
- **Hastings:** 250–400 m (slope-anchored).
- **Crécy:** ~250–400 m of killing zone after the French commit.
- **Agincourt:** 250 m after the English advance.
- **Marignano:** 400–600 m at first contact.
- **Pavia:** 0 (Imperial breach of park wall inside French perimeter).
- **Panipat:** ~800 m at first contact.

**Range of typical "first contact" distance: 250–800 m.** The approach phase occurs in this window; outside it, armies haven't deployed; inside it, contact is imminent.

### 2. The skirmish zone is 50–300 m forward of the main line.

Light troops operate in this zone bilaterally. Both armies' skirmishers contest the middle ground; missile fire across the gap is light-troops-on-light-troops or, on occasion, light-troops shooting at the opposing main line as a probe.

### 3. The "killing arc" of mass missile fire is 30–250 m.

- **Longbow:** effective volley 200–250 m; lethal armor-pierce 30–50 m.
- **Composite bow:** effective 150–200 m; lethal 50 m.
- **Crossbow:** effective 100–150 m.
- **Arquebus:** effective 50–100 m.

The deeper the killing arc, the longer the attrition phase. Longbow battles have ~30–60 minute attrition windows; arquebus battles compress this to ~10–20 minute windows of point-blank engagement.

### 4. The "main-contact frontage" matches initial deployment frontages.

A 7,000-man army engaging a 7,000-man army both deploy on similar frontages (~600–800 m by typical 10th-c. or late-medieval norms). Mismatched frontages produce either funneling (the wider army's wings overlap and envelop) or void (the narrower army has empty flanks).

**The Cannae pattern:** Roman frontage compressed to ~1.5 km while Carthaginian frontage starts wider (with refused wings) and ends wider (with the African wings facing inward at the encirclement). Carthaginian *outer* frontage at encirclement is ~3 km — twice the Roman frontage.

### 5. The "lethal pocket" forms when one army is pressed into a smaller area than its formation can use.

- Adrianople: ~500 m × 500 m for ~20,000 men = 12.5 m² per man — too dense.
- Agincourt: ~750 m × 200 m of advance for ~12,000+ men = 12.5 m² per man — same lethal density.
- Cannae: ~1 km × 1 km for ~50,000 men = 20 m² per man — still very dense.
- Panipat: ~1 km × 1 km for ~80,000 men = 12.5 m² per man — same lethal density.

**The recurring number is ~12–20 m² per man at lethal compression.** Below this density, troops cannot effectively use weapons, cannot rotate fresh men forward, and casualty rates spike.

### 6. Cavalry charge final acceleration: ~30–50 m at gallop.

Across all the reconstructions where cavalry charge is described:
- Approach at walk-to-trot (~6–13 km/h) until ~100 m from target.
- Trot-to-canter (~13–20 km/h) from ~100 m to ~50 m.
- Gallop (~30–40 km/h) only in the final 30–50 m.

**The full-gallop window is brief.** Horses cannot sustain gallop for long without collapsing. This is the physical fact that limits cavalry charge length and constrains "final approach" distance to a sub-100 m window.

### 7. The "decisive commit" occupies 5–30 minutes of clock time.

Across the reconstructions:
- Cannae African wing turn: ~30 minutes.
- Pharsalus fourth-line ambush: ~5–10 minutes.
- Adrianople Greuthungi flank attack: ~30 minutes.
- Hastings high-angle archery + Harold's death: ~30 minutes.
- Crécy: no single decisive commit; instead, attritional cycles.
- Agincourt archer melee commit: ~30 minutes.
- Marignano Venetian flank attack: ~30 minutes.
- Pavia gendarme charge into the woods: ~5–10 minutes of the critical decision (the charge that masked the artillery).
- Panipat encirclement closure: ~30 minutes.

**Decisive commit window: ~5–30 minutes.** Outside this window, the battle continues without resolution; inside it, the battle is won or lost. The commander's task is to be ready at this 5–30 minute window with the right force at the right place.

### 8. The full battle duration: 2–9 hours in active combat.

- Cannae: ~5–6 hours.
- Pharsalus: ~2–3 hours.
- Adrianople: ~3–5 hours.
- Hastings: ~9 hours.
- Crécy: ~5–6 hours.
- Agincourt: ~3 hours.
- Marignano: ~24+ hours (exceptional).
- Pavia: ~6 hours.
- Panipat: ~6 hours.

**Typical full battle: 3–6 hours active combat.** Deployment beforehand: 1–3 hours. Pursuit after: hours to days.

## Part VI — Game-Design Notes for Valoria (per project-owner contract: observations, not specifications)

The dynamic-spacing reconstructions in this document suggest several specific game-design considerations beyond the F2 observations:

**O-1:** The **250–800 m "first contact" distance** is a natural battle-scale spatial unit. A tactical map at this scale lets the player see deployment, skirmish, and approach as a single visual frame.

**O-2:** The **lethal density of ~12–20 m² per man** is a recurring physical fact. Mechanical effects of being pressed into a pocket smaller than a doctrine can use should produce step-change casualty multipliers, not linear ones.

**O-3:** The **5–30 minute decisive-commit window** is the single most important game-time interval. A real-time strategy game (or RTS-with-pause hybrid) might compress an entire battle to ~5–10 minutes of player time and concentrate the decisive-commit decision in a 10–60 second window of that.

**O-4:** The **30–50 m final-gallop window** for cavalry is a hard physical fact. Mechanics that let cavalry sustain full charge speed indefinitely are unhistorical and remove a tactical constraint.

**O-5:** The **rain/mud/terrain modifier effects at Crécy, Agincourt, Pavia** are dramatic — armies effective on dry ground can become ineffective on wet ground at the same hour. Terrain × weather × time-of-day interaction is a real combat factor.

**O-6:** The **primary-source treatises themselves** are a thematic resource for the game. If Valoria's strategic-layer factions have access to "doctrinal manuals" as a game-economy resource (a faction can acquire / develop / lose military manuals encoding their doctrine), this maps to the historical reality of training pipelines and institutional knowledge.

**O-7:** The **chronicle / first-person source asymmetry** — Caesar at Pharsalus, the *Bāburnāma* at Panipat — suggests that one option for Valoria is for player-protagonists to author battle accounts that shape later memory of the engagement. Historical battles are remembered through their tellers.

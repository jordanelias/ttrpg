## Part I — Corrections Identified

### Cannae — frontage and depth

**File 12 stated:** "Roman frontage compressed to ~1.5 km. Depth: perhaps 50+ ranks at the densest."

**Authoritative source (Goldsworthy via *Cassell's Fields of Battle*, *Roman Warfare*, *The Punic Wars*):** Goldsworthy's modern reconstruction places the Roman frontage at **~1.95 km** — specifically 360 m for the 2,400 citizen cavalry on the river side, 1,050 m for the infantry center, 540 m for the 3,600 allied cavalry on the inland flank. The reconstruction relies on Connolly's hypothesis of the Aufidus running considerably north of its current course in 216 BC, leaving a 1.3-mile-wide flat for the Roman deployment.

**Authoritative source (Goldsworthy on Polybius on depth):** "It is difficult to see [Polybius's 'many times deeper than wide' descriptor] being applied to a formation much wider than five or six files across and less deep than twenty-nine or twenty-four ranks." Modern estimates range from 50 to 70 ranks per maniple in the densest center.

**Severity:** P2 (significant under-estimate of frontage; depth range was reasonable but could have been more precise).

**Correction needed in file 12:** Roman frontage ~2 km (with constituent dimensions broken out: 360 m + 1,050 m + 540 m). Roman center depth: 24–70 ranks depending on maniple position, not "50+" as a uniform claim.

### Cannae — Roman cavalry numbers

**File 12 stated:** "~80,000 infantry, ~6,000 cavalry."

**Authoritative source (multiple including Goldsworthy):** ~80,000 infantry confirmed; cavalry ~6,000 confirmed (2,400 citizen + 3,600 allied per Cassell's analysis of Livy's source that "in this year the allies provided twice as many cavalry as the Romans" — abnormal for the era).

**Severity:** P3 — no correction needed.

### Cannae — Carthaginian losses

**File 12 stated:** Implied through context, no direct figure.

**Britannica:** "The Carthaginians lost about 6,000 men."

**Correction needed in file 12:** Add explicit Carthaginian casualty figure (~6,000) for symmetry with the Roman figures cited.

### Pharsalus — fourth-line size

**File 12 stated:** "small reserve cavalry force (perhaps ~2,400)" for the fourth line cohorts.

**Authoritative sources (Past Magazine, USMA West Point map, Livius, Academia paper):** The fourth line was **6 cohorts ≈ 3,000 men**, possibly 8 cohorts ≈ 4,000 men per some readings. The phrase "perhaps 3,000–4,000 men" is the standard modern reconstruction.

**Severity:** P2 — under-estimate by ~600–1,600 men.

**Correction needed in file 12:** Fourth line ~3,000–4,000 men (6 cohorts at ~500 men each, possibly 8 cohorts).

### Pharsalus — depth of Pompey's line

**File 12 stated:** No explicit depth figure for Pompey.

**Authoritative source (Wikipedia citing standard reconstruction):** Pompey deployed his infantry in three lines of "10 men deep" total — i.e., a deep formation suitable for absorbing rather than maneuvering.

**Severity:** P3 — augmentation, not correction.

### Adrianople — Roman force size

**File 12 stated:** "~15,000–30,000 (modern estimates vary wildly)."

**Authoritative cross-check:** The wide range I gave is the standard modern reconstruction. Ammianus's narrative implies a substantial force; reconstructions span the range I stated. No correction.

**Severity:** P3 — no correction needed.

### Hastings — phase timing

**File 12 stated:** "Total battle duration: ~9 hours of active combat (early morning to dusk in October)."

**Authoritative cross-check:** Standard reconstruction. October sunset in northern France ~5:30 PM; battle commenced ~9:00 AM per *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*. 8–9 hours is the canonical figure.

**Severity:** P3 — no correction needed.

### Crécy — primary-source citation accuracy

**File 12 stated:** "rain shower preceding the engagement has wet [Genoese] crossbow strings (which they leave on the bow when not in use — a logistical detail from Froissart and Le Bel)."

**Authoritative cross-check:** Jean le Bel mentions the rain affecting the Genoese strings; Froissart elaborates. The detail is widely repeated in modern accounts. However, the "leave strings on bows" interpretation is the standard modern interpretive gloss, not necessarily the literal claim of either chronicler. **Recommend flagging this as modern reconstruction rather than direct primary-source claim.**

**Severity:** P3 — clarification.

### Agincourt — field dimensions and funnel

**File 12 stated:** "Frontage: ~750–1,000 m wide, narrowing slightly toward the French position."

**Authoritative sources (Hibbert via Mechtraveller; battlefieldtravels.com; Britannica):**
- French initial deployment at ~3/4 mile (~1.2 km) wide section.
- Narrowing to ~1/2 mile (~800 m) between the woods of Tramecourt and Agincourt at the contact zone.
- Narrowing is TOWARD Henry's position (south end of the field), not "toward the French" — file 12's wording is wrong about direction.

**Severity:** P2 — directional error and under-estimate of initial French frontage.

**Correction needed in file 12:** "Field width: French initial deployment ~1.2 km wide, narrowing via the wood-funnel to ~800 m at the English contact zone. The narrowing direction is *from French side toward English side*, compressing the French as they advanced."

### Agincourt — longbow effective range

**File 12 stated:** "Effective volley 200–250 m; lethal armor-pierce 30–50 m."

**Authoritative source (Britannica):** "Effective range of 250 yards (229 metres)."

**Severity:** P3 — file 12 figure consistent; lethal range claim is standard modern reconstruction.

### Marignano — phase timing

**File 12 stated:** "Marignano: ~24+ hours of continuous engagement (exceptional)."

**Authoritative cross-check (Wikipedia, Britannica, Musée de l'Armée):** Battle 13–14 September 1515. Started "late afternoon" 13 Sept; "midnight" pause; resumed "at dawn" 14 Sept; Venetian reinforcement "around 11 o'clock" on 14 Sept; Swiss withdrew thereafter. **Active combat ~6 hours late on day 1 + ~5–6 hours morning of day 2 = ~12 hours of combat with ~6 hours of overnight pause.** My "24+ hours" was misleading; actual continuous engagement is closer to 12 hours of combat across a 24-hour clock window.

**Severity:** P2 — misleading framing of "continuous" engagement.

**Correction needed in file 12:** "Active combat ~12 hours across two days (with ~6-hour overnight pause); total clock-window ~24 hours."

### Marignano — Swiss force size

**File 12 stated:** "~20,000–25,000 pike infantry."

**Authoritative source (Musée de l'Armée, NamuWiki):** ~22,000 Swiss infantry; Britannica confirms similar order.

**Severity:** P3 — file 12 estimate brackets the consensus figure.

### Pavia — fundamental phasing

**Authoritative cross-check:** Standard modern reconstruction. No corrections.

### Panipat — sally gap spacing

**File 12 stated:** "Gaps (*aghruq*) are deliberately left every 30–50 m for cavalry sortie."

**Authoritative sources (Wikipedia, Medium IDF article on First Battle of Panipat):**
- "He left sally points 150 riders wide for his cavalry to advance through" (Wikipedia).
- "At intervals of 100 to 200 yards there were intervals with passages for cavalry to sally out" (Medium analysis citing Bāburnāma).
- Each sally point ~150 riders wide; spacing ~100–200 yards (~90–180 m).

**Severity:** P1 — factual error. The 30–50 m figure was simply wrong; correct figure is ~90–180 m between sally points.

**Correction needed in file 12:** "Sally points (each ~150 riders wide) at intervals of approximately 100–200 yards (~90–180 m) along the wagon-line. The structure is fewer, larger gaps — not many small ones."

### Panipat — elephant count

**File 12 stated:** "war elephants — probably 100+, the Lodi prestige and shock arm."

**Authoritative sources (Britannica, Panipat.gov.in, multiple secondary):** Lodi's elephants are estimated at **~1,000**, per the Bāburnāma's own description ("whose elephants and whose begs' elephants were about 1,000"). Britannica and the panipat.gov.in government source both confirm "at least 1,000 war elephants."

**Severity:** P1 — significant under-count, by an order of magnitude.

**Correction needed in file 12:** "War elephants — approximately 1,000, per the Bāburnāma's own description. This is the largest war-elephant deployment in any battle covered in this entire research project."

### Panipat — Babur's cannon count

**Severity:** P3 — augmentation.

**Correction needed in file 12:** Add specific figure: ~20–24 pieces of field artillery.

### Manual primitives — Asclepiodotus

**File 12 stated:** "Open order: 4 cubits per file ≈ 6 Roman feet ≈ 1.8 m. Compact order (pyknōsis): 2 cubits per file ≈ 3 Roman feet ≈ 0.9 m. Locked-shields (synaspismos): 1 cubit per file ≈ 1.5 Roman feet ≈ 0.45 m."

**Authoritative source (Asclepiodotus, *Tactica* IV via LacusCurtius):** All three figures confirmed. The cubit conversion of ~48 cm (per the recent Du Plessis paper using the Olympia stade and Salamis reliefs) is consistent.

**Severity:** P3 — no correction. **This is a strong manual-primitive citation.**

**Note worth adding:** Modern scholars (Walbank, English, Matthew) dispute whether synaspismos was physically feasible given sarissa length and shield grip; experimental archaeology (Du Plessis 2019) supports feasibility if the sarissa is held in a high guard. **The primary-source claim is well-attested; its real-world execution is debated.**

### Manual primitives — Polybius / Vegetius

**File 12 stated:** "Polybius 18.30 gives the Roman interval: 3 feet per legionary, both file and rank. Vegetius 3.14–15 codifies later practice: legionaries deploy 3 ft (~0.9 m) per file frontage, 6–7 ft (~1.8–2.1 m) per rank front-to-back."

**Authoritative source (Goldsworthy via Cassell's):** "Vegetius 3.14–15 allocated a frontage of 1 m (3 feet) and a depth of 2.1 m (7 feet) to a Roman legionary." Goldsworthy also notes: "Elsewhere Polybius claimed that each legionary occupied 1.8 m (6 feet) square, but the passage is heavily stylized and seems improbable; 18.30.5–11."

**Severity:** P3 — file 12 figure consistent with the working modern estimate. Recommend acknowledging the Polybius 18.30 6-ft figure as the heavily-stylized alternative that modern scholarship rejects.

## Part III — Methodological Limitations Worth Stating

`[SELF-AUTHORED — bias risk]` These would be raised by an independent reviewer:

1. **Selection bias toward Western traditions.** File 12 reconstructs 9 battles; 6 are European (Cannae, Pharsalus, Hastings, Crécy, Agincourt, Marignano, Pavia) and 1 is Mediterranean (Adrianople), with only Panipat representing non-European warfare. A more globally balanced selection would include Chinese, Mongol, or Mamluk reconstructions where source detail permits. **However:** primary-source granularity for non-European battles is generally poorer, biasing the sample-of-what-can-be-reconstructed toward Europe and the Mediterranean.

2. **Derived-vs-source-stated measurements not always flagged.** Distances are usually derived from "frontage × file spacing"; durations are usually derived from "distance / movement speed." When file 12 says "approach time ~10–15 minutes," this is *almost always* a derivation, not a source-stated figure. File 12 flagged this in the closing notes but did not flag each individual derivation. A more rigorous version would tag each derived figure inline.

3. **Modern reconstruction is not primary source.** Goldsworthy, Keegan, Sabin, Curry, etc. are themselves making interpretive choices about contested evidence. When file 12 cites "modern scholarship" for a number, that number reflects current scholarly consensus, not objective truth. **The number that "was once correct" can become "now revised" within a decade.** Anne Curry's revisions of Agincourt French numbers from ~30,000 to ~12,000 is a recent example.

4. **Source bias of the originals.** Polybius wrote in service of the Scipionic family; Caesar wrote in service of himself; Babur wrote in service of his own legacy. Surviving narratives are *not* neutral; they are political documents that have survived because some authority valued their preservation. **The "primary sources" I cite are themselves doctrinal artifacts that influence how later scholars reconstruct the events.**

5. **Manual primitives may not reflect actual practice.** Asclepiodotus's *Tactica* is a theoretical drill manual, not a battlefield report. The phalanx in real combat may have deployed at intervals different from the manual specification. Vegetius wrote ~400 years after the manipular legion was obsolete. **The "manual primitives" are normative ideals, not empirical descriptions of every battle.**

6. **I am a single author working from training data.** I cannot verify every primary-source claim from the original texts in real time. Where the LacusCurtius citation is direct (Asclepiodotus IV), confidence is high. Where I'm summarizing from secondary-scholarship paraphrases, confidence is medium.

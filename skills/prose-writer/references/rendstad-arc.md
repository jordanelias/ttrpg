# The Rendstad Assessment

*Arc setting: Rendstad (T7), Hafenmark Highlands. Remote forested valley. Traditional temperament. Late autumn. A Church assessor has arrived to investigate reports of folk practice in the upper valley. The Hafenmark sub-prefect considers this a jurisdictional violation. The practitioner — a woman named Ilse who mends nets and cures smoke-sickness in the timber camps — does not know that what she does has a name the Church uses.*

---

## Scene 1. Arrival.
*Focalization: Vael. Coherence 9. TS 0.*

The road from Gransol entered the Rendstad valley through a stand of larch that had been cut back from the roadside to the depth of two axe-lengths, and then not cut at all. The stumps were old — three seasons, four — and the regrowth had filled in around them so that the road narrowed to a cart's width and then to less. Vael walked the last mile on foot, her horse led behind her, and the sound of the hooves on the packed earth was the only sound until the valley opened and the settlement appeared below: twenty houses, a mill on the river, a timbered customs-house flying the Hafenmark pennant from a pole that leaned seven degrees east.

The customs-house was shut. A woman was sweeping the steps with a broom made from birch twigs bound with a strip of calf-leather, the binding worn through in three places. Vael asked for the sub-prefect.

"Kalder's at the mill," the woman said. She did not stop sweeping. "The assessor's at the inn." She said *assessor* the way she might have said *the rain* — a condition, not a person.

The inn was the largest building. Its roof was shingled with larch bark in the old way, each shingle pinned with an iron nail, and the nails had rusted and stained the bark in vertical lines. Inside, the common room smelled of tallow and spruce sap and the sourness of beer that has been brewed with valley water. The assessor sat at a table near the hearth with a ledger open before him, the pages half-filled in the Church's administrative hand. He was young. His cassock was new. The hem was not yet stained from the road.

"You are from the provincial office," the assessor said. It was not a question.

"I am not," Vael said.

The assessor looked at her. He had the face of a man who had been told what to expect and was expecting it. He closed the ledger. The clasp was brass, and the brass was bright, and Vael thought of Maren's ledgers and the twenty-three volumes of the Greinmar Poor Relief and the difference between a book that had been opened a thousand times and a book that had been opened perhaps six times. This ledger had been opened six times. The spine was not yet broken.

## Scene 2. The Sub-Prefect.
*Focalization: Vael. Coherence 7. TS 0.*

Kalder came from the mill with flour on his cuffs and the particular expression of a man who has been interrupted in something he considered more important than whatever he has been interrupted for. He was tall, thin at the wrists, with the posture of someone who had learned to stand in rooms where other people were shorter. His authority was Hafenmark's — constitutional, procedural, and under Duchess Baralta's writ — and he carried it the way the customs-house carried its pennant, which was to say visibly and at a slight angle.

"The assessor has no jurisdiction here," Kalder said. He sat across from Vael in the customs-house and poured two cups from a bottle whose label was in the Gransol dialect. "Himmelenger can send whoever it likes. Rendstad answers to Gransol. Gransol answers to Parliament. Parliament does not answer to the Confessor."

"And the woman," Vael said.

"Ilse." Kalder drank. "Ilse mends nets. Ilse cures smoke-sickness. Ilse has done these things for — she learned from her mother, who learned from her mother. There is no word for what Ilse does because there has never needed to be a word. She boils bark. She wraps linen around a joint that has been strained. She knows which moss draws infection. The Church calls this something that the Church has a word for, and the word is not — the word does not."

He stopped. He poured again. Vael's cup was still full.

"The word does not describe what Ilse does," Kalder said. "The word describes what the Church is afraid of."

Vael had already known what he was going to say. Not the words — the shape of the argument, the direction. She had heard it before she had heard it, and the hearing-before was the kind of thing she had been noticing lately — a moment arriving before its cause, a sentence assembling itself in her hands before the speaker's mouth had finished. She was quite certain this was simply the quality of having heard similar arguments before. It was pattern-recognition, nothing more.

Outside, the larch. The river. The customs-house pennant leaning east.

## Scene 3. The Assessment Record.
*Focalization: Church Chronicler (Certainty 5, TS 0). Chronicle-mode.*

The assessment at Rendstad (T7, Hafenmark Highlands) was ordered by the Office of Doctrinal Compliance following reports received in the third quarter from a timber-camp foreman whose name is withheld per standard procedure. The reports described a woman, identified as Ilse of Rendstad, performing curative acts of an uncanonical nature — specifically, the application of botanical preparations and manual techniques to ailments that the complainant observed responding at a rate and completeness inconsistent with the natural pharmacological properties of the materials employed.

Assessor Lukas Brenn arrived in Rendstad on the seventh day of the month. He was accompanied by a scribe and carried with him the standard assessment kit: the Compliance Ledger (form C-14, seventh edition), a copy of the *Compendium of Suppressed Practices* (abridged, fieldwork edition), and a sealed letter of authority from the Office bearing the Confessor's counter-seal.

Sub-Prefect Kalder of Rendstad refused to acknowledge the authority of the letter on jurisdictional grounds, citing Hafenmark Parliamentary Precedent 41 (the "Doctrinal Exclusion," establishing that Church compliance assessments in Hafenmark territories require prior approval from the Sub-Prefect's office, which approval had not been sought). This refusal is noted without editorial comment. The Church's position, as stated in the Confessor's counter-seal letter, is that doctrinal matters supersede territorial jurisdiction under the Concordat of the Fifth Renewal (Article 9, §3: "Matters touching the integrity of the faith shall not be subject to territorial limitation").

The assessment was not completed. The subject, Ilse of Rendstad, was not interviewed. Assessor Brenn remains at the Rendstad inn at the time of this report. Sub-Prefect Kalder has dispatched a rider to Gransol.

## Scene 4. The Upper Valley.
*Focalization: Vael. Coherence 4. TS 0.*

Vael went to the upper valley because — she had decided to go. The decision had been a decision and it had been hers and she had made it. She was in the upper valley. The timber camp was to her left, or she had passed it. The smoke from the camp was behind her and also around her because the valley held smoke the way valleys hold — the smoke was there.

Ilse's house was at the end of a path that was not a path but the place where the grass had been walked down by the passage of people who had come to Ilse. The house was small. The door was open. From inside came a smell that was bark and moss and something sharper that Vael's categories reached for and — the smell was what it was. She stood at the threshold.

Ilse was sitting at a table. Her hands were in a bowl of water and in the water were leaves and the leaves were doing something that leaves do not do, or that these leaves did, or that the water was doing and the leaves were in, and Ilse's hands were in the water with the leaves and her hands were — still. Her hands were very still. The stillness was not the stillness of rest. It was the stillness of someone whose hands are doing something that the stillness is part of. Vael sat with this.

Ilse looked up. She had a face that was — it was a face. It was the face of a woman who was Ilse and who had this face and who had been sitting with her hands in the water doing the thing her hands did and who was now looking at Vael. Her expression was. Her expression was the expression of.

"Come in," Ilse said. "Sit."

Vael sat. The chair held her. Her shadow on the wall was the shadow of a person who was sitting and the shadow was — it was correct. The shadow was correct. The leaves in the water were still. Ilse's hands were still in the water. These things were in the room and the room held them and Vael was in the room with them.

The leaves. The water. The bark smell. The light through the door. These.

## Scene 5. What the River Knows.
*Focalization: Vael. Coherence 7. TS 35 (Stirring). After leaving Ilse's house.*

The path back to the settlement followed the river, and the river ran south through the larch, and the larch was the larch she had walked through that morning, and the path was the path. These held. She was walking and the walking was in the right order — left foot then right foot then left — and the order held too, and she was grateful for the order though she did not think gratitude was precisely the right — she was walking.

The river beside her was doing something. It was running south, which was what rivers did in valleys that tilted south, and the running was the running of water over stones, and the stones were — she could point at the stones and say those were the stones the river ran over and she would be right. But the river was also — it was warmer than a river should be in late autumn. Not warmer. It had a quality that she identified as warmth because warmth was the closest — it was the closest. The quality was from the direction of the upper valley, from the direction of Ilse's house, or from beneath the riverbed, or from the water itself.

She was quite certain the quality was the mineral content of the water, which in highland valleys was often — she had heard this, or she knew it, or it was true. Highland water had minerals. Minerals could produce a sensation that the skin interpreted as warmth. This was the explanation, and it was a satisfactory explanation, and the quality continued from the direction of the upper valley and the minerals did not explain the direction.

The larch. The path. The river. The settlement below with its leaning pennant and its assessor and its sub-prefect and its twenty houses. She walked toward them. The quality stayed behind her. Or it was in the water beside her. Or it was in the part of the walk she had already done and the part she was doing now was the part that did not have it.

The customs-house pennant was leaning east. The smoke from the timber camp was in the valley. These held.

Atom Architecture

A pictorial model of formation, movement, and contact engagement

01Shape patterns & per-cell movement speeds

A unit contains one or more atoms. Each atom has a shape, a tier, and a set of cells on the unit's 15×15 grid. Each cell carries a per-cell movement speed determined by its role in the shape. The full atom advances by moving each cell at its own speed every turn — formations deform as they advance.

Patterns shown below in Side A orientation: leading edge (tip / wings / engaged cells) at top of figure, advance arrow pointing up. Side B uses the vertical mirror — see §8 for the mirroring rule.

Why per-cell speeds matter. When all cells of an atom advance at the same speed, the formation is rigid — it preserves shape but can't enact the historical tactical patterns. Horseshoe wings must race ahead of the held center to envelop. Arrowhead's tip should pierce ahead of the base. Per-cell movement makes the shape's tactical role emergent from its geometry, not a special-case rule.

03Contact detection & the pool formula (Bii)

When two atoms have cells within 1 cell of each other (orthogonal or diagonal), they enter an engagement. Each engagement resolves as a separate ED-811 symmetric dice contest. The pool size is calculated per atom using the Bii formula.

single-cell contact between Arrowhead tip and Line front row
fig 3.2
Pool formula · per atom, per contact
pool = base × (atom_troops/unit_troops) × (engaged/atom_max_width) + shape_modifier ─────────────────────────────────────────────── For the Arrowhead's tip atom (single-atom unit): base = min(Size,Cmd) + Cmd = 4+4 = 8 atom_troops = 400 (T3) unit_troops = 400 (single atom) engaged = 1 (just the tip cell) atom_max_width = 9 (T3 base row) shape_modifier = +2 (Arrowhead tip role) pool = 8 × (400/400) × (1/9) + 2 = 8 × 1.0 × 0.111 + 2 = 0.88 + 2 = max(1, 3 dice) For the Line atom (engaged at front row): base = 8 atom_troops = 400 unit_troops = 400 engaged = 1 (single tip-cell contact) atom_max_width = 5 (Line width) shape_modifier = 0 (Line, no mod) pool = 8 × 1.0 × (1/5) + 0 = 1.6 + 0 = max(1, 1 die) ─────────────────────────────────────────────── Engagement (ED-811 symmetric): Arrowhead: 3 dice → likely Success → 4 dmg Line: 1 die → likely Failure → 0 dmg
Why Arrowhead's tip dies anyway. The tip wins the first turn's exchange (3 dice vs 1). But it's only 1 cell. Next turn, the Line's full front row (5 cells) catches up to engage the Arrowhead's wider second row. The Line's pool jumps to 8 × 1.0 × 5/5 = 8 dice. Arrowhead is now outgunned 8:3 because its base hasn't caught up to engage. Result: 92% Line win.

04The Cannae failure mode

In the historical Cannae model, the defender's flank wings curve inward around the advancing enemy, surrounding them. In v5, the wings just race forward in a straight line. The geometry of "intended" vs "actual" makes this clear.

fig 4.1
INTENDED · Cannae envelopment
wings target a point behind the enemy · base closes from behind · arrowhead engaged on 4 sides
fig 4.2
ACTUAL · v5 head-on collision
wings advance straight up · target = arrowhead (whose centroid is straight ahead) · no encirclement geometry
The root cause. When a wing's target is "nearest enemy atom" and that enemy is straight ahead, the wing moves straight ahead. For envelopment to emerge, the wings need to target a point behind the enemy — they need to plan to wrap around, not just react to the closest threat. This is option B-ii from the decision list: wings target a point behind the enemy, not the enemy itself.

05The 21×21 battlefield · why the 3-cell boundary matters

Each unit's atoms are drawn from its 15×15 footprint, but the battle plays out on a 21×21 grid. The 3-cell boundary on each side gives atoms room to maneuver around the unit grid — flanking, encircling, retreating, repositioning. Without it, units are bottled into head-on collision.

07What still needs to be designed

The pictorial model makes visible what the prose summary stated: the v5 architecture works mechanically but fails to capture two historical patterns. Three decisions remain open for v6.

Decision B-ii — curving wings. For Horseshoe to envelop, wings must target a point behind the enemy's centroid, not the enemy itself. This requires either a per-shape target-offset rule (Horseshoe wings target enemy_pos + back_offset) or an explicit "encircle" stance.
Decision C-i / C-ii — pool rebalance. The Bii formula penalizes small atoms in composite units (composite winrate dropped from 85% in v4 to 2.7% in v5). Either drop the atom_troops/unit_troops term (i) or cap the minimum at 50% of base pool (ii). Both preserve atom granularity without crushing diversification.
Decision E — tip support. Arrowhead's tip currently races 4-6 rows ahead of its base and gets isolated. A formation cohesion rule — "tip cannot advance more than N rows ahead of base" — would force the wedge to advance as a coherent body, matching historical Macedonian phalanx tactics.

Decision priority — Jordan to weigh in.

08Side mirroring & 25×25 buffer

Two corrections to the model above. (1) Patterns must be vertically mirrored for Side B so the leading edge (tip / wings / engaged columns) faces the enemy — the original figures show Side B's Arrowhead with its base pointed at Side A, which is geometrically backwards. (2) Battlefield expands from 21×21 to 25×25, giving a 5-cell buffer on each side (was 3-cell) — more room for flank movement and encirclement.

8.1 The mirroring rule

Patterns are authored with row 0 as the forward edge. For Side A (advancing toward lower rows, geographically up) this places the tip / wings / engaged cells at low absolute rows — closest to Side B. For Side B (advancing toward higher rows, geographically down), the same pattern would put the tip away from the enemy. To fix this, Side B's pattern is flipped vertically: (r, c) → (max_r − r, c). Cell-speed and role lookups still use the original pattern coordinates — only positions get oriented.

fig 8.1.a
BEFORE · Side B arrowhead reversed
v5 bug · pattern row 0 = absolute top regardless of side
fig 8.1.b
AFTER · Side B mirrored correctly
pattern flipped: (r,c) → (max_r−r, c) · cell roles preserved
Implementation detail. The flip applies to position, not role. The function oriented_pattern(shape, tier, advance_dir) returns tuples of (orig_r, orig_c, or_r, or_c). Cell offsets are keyed by (orig_r, orig_c) — so an Arrowhead's original tip cell at (0, 4) retains its speed-2 designation and its "tip" role regardless of which side it's on. Only its rendered absolute position differs.

8.2 The 25×25 battlefield with 5-cell buffer

15×15 unit footprints occupy rows 5-19, cols 5-19. The five-cell buffer (rows 0-4, 20-24, cols 0-4, 20-24) is for maneuver during battle — flanking arcs, encirclement, retreat space. Wider buffer = more room for wings to actually wrap around an enemy rather than just close head-on.

8.3 What changes elsewhere in the model

With proper mirroring, the failure modes from earlier sections still hold but the geometry is now correct:

  • Section 2 (deformation): Side B's Arrowhead tip now drives down toward Side A's Horseshoe — same dynamic, but tip & wings genuinely face each other. The "wings race straight up" failure mode still applies.
  • Section 3 (pool formula): Unchanged. The contact column and pool calculation are agnostic to side orientation.
  • Section 4 (Cannae failure): Side B's Arrowhead now drives down with its tip leading; Side A's Horseshoe still doesn't envelop because the wings target the closest enemy (the Arrowhead tip directly ahead), not a point behind it. The fundamental B-ii decision still stands.
  • Section 5 (battlefield): Superseded by §8.2 above with proper 25×25 and 5-cell buffer.
  • The three open decisions (B-ii curving wings, C-i/ii pool rebalance, E tip support): unchanged. The mirroring fix is geometric; the design tensions are mechanical.
The wider buffer makes the encirclement question more answerable. With only 3 buffer cells, the wings literally didn't have room to curve out and around. With 5 cells, the geometry supports a real flanking arc — making the B-ii decision (wings target a point behind enemy) more meaningful, because there's now somewhere to go.
